# Ralph Overhill

- **Role:** Young Badger camp follower and animal-handler in Elmsfield; the party's logistical solution to *getting off the road* without losing their mounts; potential late-game wrangler if the right hands shape his apprenticeship.
- **Species:** Badger. Young — somewhere between eighteen and twenty-two.
- **Lives:** A small room at the back of his uncle's stable on the eastern edge of Elmsfield (the uncle is unnamed; DM-fill if needed). Spends most of his waking hours with the animals, not in the room. (For Elmsfield as place: `doc/places/elmsfield.md`.)
- **First met:** *Asking around Elmsfield turns him up pretty quickly.* He is not hard to find. **Bridget** points the party at him if they ask after a wrangler at the Inn; **Corné Dunham** points at him if the party asks Corné about Badger-clan kin; he turns up at the eastern stable when called.

## Campaign brief

- **Role:** Young Badger horse-handler; the party's *bring-someone-along-to-watch-the-mounts* solution; loves horses more than he loves the idea of the clan, by Corné's diagnostic.
- **Standouts:** *A young Badger in a long apron half a size too big*, sleeves rolled past forearms; **a hoof-pick and a small leather pouch of brushes at his belt**, always; **walks with a particular ease around large animals** — never approaches a horse from behind, always speaks before he reaches; knows every working animal in Elmsfield by gait at distance.
- **Speech tag:** Quiet with strangers; warmer once trusted. Names the animal first when describing a journey. *"That gelding has a stiff off-foreleg in cold weather; I'd take him short stretches."* Will not over-promise. The horses are the conversation.
- **Quests:** Early — *be hired for a specific run, learn what the party actually does, and bring back the mounts; help him acquire one specific pair-of-horses he has been watching for.* Late — *choose where his life goes — Corné's hill-route circuit, Eric Randell's wrangler-line, or his own breeding-and-training operation in Elmsfield.* See §"Personal goals — early and later".
- **Friction:** *Cannot defend a camp against a real threat.* Sent alone against combatants or a bear, he will die — *and he knows this*. The signature move is **flee with the animals**, not stand and fight; a party that misunderstands what they hired loses the wrangler. *Loves horses more than the idea of the clan* — Bridget would have him available for Badger-clan-side moves; Ralph is *willing for the right horse-work* and *not willing as a duty*. The line matters.

## What the party meets

The party walks the eastern stretch of Elmsfield to the stable where the smell of horse-sweat and hay tells them they are in the right place. **A young Badger in a long apron is brushing down a draft-mare in the yard**, talking to her in a low voice about the leg she is not going to be using today. He looks up when the party approaches, finishes the stroke he is on, sets the brush carefully on its hook before turning to them.

His introduction is brief and practical. *"I'm Ralph. You looking for a horse, or someone to watch a horse?"* If the answer is *"someone to watch a horse,"* his interest visibly rises. If the answer is *"a horse to buy,"* he can do that too — the stable trades in working animals — but the watching-work is the conversation he was hoping for.

He asks fair pay; a party offering it gets *real* attention. He asks where they are going; the answer shapes whether he says yes. *"I do not go into the chaos-warped country with mounts. I will go to the edge of it. I will not go past it."*

## Personal goals — early and later

His goals are practical, animal-shaped, and unhurried. He does not narrate them; the party reads them by spending time around him at the stable.

- **Early-game — be hired for a specific run; learn what the party actually does; bring the mounts back.** *What the NPC says* (after the party offers fair pay and names the destination): *"Tell me where, and tell me how long. I do not need to know what you are doing there. I do need to know what the country is — woods, swamp, broken country, road. I will not take horses past the edge of the broken lands; I will take them to Lastwater if the road holds; I will take them up the hills toward Luchebert if Corné says the path is dry. I tend; I do not fight. If something comes at the camp bigger than me, I leave with the horses. You will find a trail. The horses will be alive. I will be too."* — *Where:* the eastern stable for the hire; **wherever the party is going** for the work — Elmsfield's farms, the trade road's skirts, the southern desert as far as Lastwater (`doc/places/southern_desert.md`), Luchebert's hill route via **Corné Dunham** (`doc/npcs/corne_dunham.md`); never into the chaos-warped country itself. — *What:* take Ralph along on a specific run; pay him fair; treat him as crew not as a fighter; come back to find the mounts ready and Ralph ready with them. — *Reward:* a working channel for *every subsequent run* — Ralph will come again, on shorter notice, for the price he charged the first time. The mounts are tended properly; small ailments are caught before they become big ones; the party's ability to *get off the road* expands without their gear vanishing. **Standing rises with Bridget, Corné, and the wider Badger clan** when the party employs Ralph honestly. — *Complications:* he cannot fight; sending him to defend a camp against combatants is a misunderstanding the party should not make. The chaos-warped country is *off-limits* by his rule; pressing him to enter it is the first test of whether the party heard him at the hire. **A party that loses Ralph's mounts through their own carelessness** (failing to leave him a clear withdrawal route; forcing him into terrain he warned about) loses Ralph's standing; he will be slow to hire again, and Bridget will know.

- **Early-game — help him acquire the pair-of-horses he has been watching for.** *What the NPC says* (after a few runs together; over an evening at the stable; the conversation he has been waiting to bring up): *"There is a pair I have been watching at a farm two valleys east of here. Working draught, four years old, broken to harness but not yet to a wagon's full load — and the farmer who has them will not get them to working condition because he does not know what he is looking at. I want them. I cannot afford them. If you carry a letter to the farmer for me, with my offer and his pay, and bring back the pair walking in halter beside the cart you took there — I will run them in for half a season at no charge to you, on any run you make."* — *Where:* the **farm two valleys east** of Elmsfield (DM-fill name; a working farmstead within a day's ride); the eastern stable for the return; the road between for the careful walking-them-back. — *What:* carry Ralph's letter and his pay to the farmer; bring the pair back walking in halter alongside whatever cart the party can spare; help Ralph stable them and begin the work. — *Reward:* a half-season of free runs with Ralph as the wrangler; a pair of horses he is *building into something good* that the party may eventually buy or borrow at favourable terms; standing with Ralph deepens to friendship. — *Complications:* the pair are unbroken to a full load — *Ralph will not lend them out fully trained until he has worked them himself across one season's weather*. A party that tries to use them mid-training risks an injury Ralph will not forgive. The farmer may be honest or may inflate the pay — *Hand* and *Reading* matter on the negotiation.

- **Later-game — choose where his life goes.** *Conditional — opens after Ralph has run with the party across a campaign-year and the choice has become real to him.* *What the NPC says* (in private at the stable, after the second pair-of-horses have been finished and the parry's standing with him is real): *"I have been thinking about what I do after this. Three things in front of me. Corné wants me to walk his hill-route circuit; the work is honest and it is mine if I will take it, but it is *people-work* more than horse-work, and I am not sure that is what my hands want. Eric Randell offered to teach me wrangler-craft on his Dromo line; *that* is horse-work at a register the kingdoms have lost, and the offer is rare, but it would mean leaving Elmsfield. Or — I keep the stable here, take over from my uncle when he steps back, run the breeding-and-training the way I have been wanting to. I do not need to choose this season. I want your read. You have seen what I do. Tell me which one fits."* — *Where:* the **eastern stable** for the conversation; possibly the **hill route** via Corné for the option-by-walking-it (`doc/npcs/corne_dunham.md` late-game *Wilfred is steady but a forge-man, not a path-man — Ralph is young and loves horses more than the idea of a clan*); **Eric Randell's farm** for the wrangler-line conversation (`doc/npcs/eric_randell.md`); the stable's expansion plans for the third option. — *What:* walk one or more of the routes with Ralph at his pace; tell him honestly what you have seen of him; do not push the answer. — *Reward (Corné's circuit):* the valley's hill-route work continues past Corné; the campaign's quiet-good extends. **Reward (Eric Randell's wrangler-line):** Ralph leaves Elmsfield for a year or two and returns having learned a craft no smith-and-blade fighter ever does; the party's later mounts come from Ralph's hand. **Reward (the stable's expansion):** Elmsfield gains a *real* breeding-and-training operation; a campaign that returns to the town across years sees Ralph's pairs walking in halter at every fair. — *Complications:* he will not be pushed; pressuring him toward a particular path costs the standing the party has earned. **The clan's expectations** (Bridget would have him available for clan-side moves; Corné watches him from a distance) sit in tension with horse-work first; if the party chooses to argue clan over horses, they argue against him. *"I am Badger. The horses are the work."*

## How he reads at a glance

- **A young Badger in a long apron half a size too big.** Sleeves rolled past forearms; smudges of liniment, mud, and hay-dust by mid-morning. The apron has been mended *by him*, not neatly.
- **A hoof-pick and a leather pouch of brushes at his belt.** Always. The pick is sharp; the brushes are clean; the pouch is older than he is and was his uncle's.
- **A particular ease around large animals.** Never approaches a horse from behind. Always speaks before he reaches. Reads a flick-of-ear at fifteen paces.
- **Quiet with strangers; warmer once trusted.** A party that has run with him sees the second register; townspeople who only see him at the market do not.

## Visible patterns

- **At the stable from before dawn.** His uncle keeps the books; Ralph keeps the animals. By breakfast he has done two stalls and brushed the working mare for that day's farmer.
- **At the smithy before any farrier work.** Wilfred and Ralph cross paths every few days; Ralph carries shoeing-iron measurements; Wilfred carries the shoes back. They do not chat for long.
- **At the market on trade days.** Sells small pieces (mended halters, plaited rope, a saddle-pad or two) and watches the working animals coming through; updates his head's-list of every horse in Elmsfield and the surrounding valleys.
- **At Bridget's Inn rarely.** A long evening once a season, when his uncle insists. He nurses one drink and listens.
- **Walking the hill-trails with Corné perhaps twice a year.** Not as a regular; as a *checking-in* — Corné's late-game watches Ralph as a possible apprentice the way an elder watches an animal he might saddle one day if it lets him.

## At the eastern stable

A working stable on Elmsfield's eastern edge. Three stalls; a tack room; a small yard with a pump and a worn hitching-post. Ralph's uncle (DM-fill name; older Badger, retiring slowly) handles the trade and the books; Ralph handles the animals.

- **The tack room is *Ralph's room*.** Everything in it is his system: the bridles hung in order of the horses they fit, the brushes by use rather than by size, the medicines on a shelf labelled in his own short hand. *Hand* and *Reading* recover the labels; a party that asks about his system gets a careful walking-tour.
- **The yard's pump runs off a small spring.** Not a working — just good water. Ralph fills the pump's bucket every morning and tops it during the day. He drinks from the same bucket his horses do.
- **A small shelf above his bed in the back room** holds three things: a folded-leather book his mother gave him with names of horses written in (started before he could write; continued in his own hand), a carved wooden horse he made when he was twelve (poor; he keeps it anyway), and a small fistful of horsehair from the first horse he could call his own (a working mare who died last year). A party invited into the back room is invited into his life.

## Stories he tells

When the relevant topic comes up.

### When asked about the working mare he lost — *the mare who died last year*

> *"She was an old draft-mare from a farm half a day west; the farmer sold her to my uncle the spring she could no longer pull a full plough. I had her for three years. She was the first horse I could call mine — not because my uncle gave her to me, he sold her to the next farmer the same season — but because she let me work her. She knew what I knew. She corrected me when I was being a fool. I kept her hair. I named her in the book my mother started. The next mare to be mine will have her own name, and I will not write her in until she has earned it. The mare did not need to earn it; she was already named when I met her."*

### When asked about Corné's circuit — *what he is offering me*

> *"Corné walks the valley so the rest of us do not have to. He sees what is moving on the hill paths; he carries news the way no one else here carries it; the work matters. He has watched me for years without saying anything. I think he wants me to walk it with him for a season and decide. **The honest answer is that I am not sure my hands want people-work**. The horses are quieter in a specific way that I am suited to. Corné's people move faster than that. I am thinking about it. I have not told him no."*

### When asked about Eric Randell — *the offer that surprised me*

> *"Eric came through Elmsfield last autumn with the Dromo bird and stayed at my uncle's stable for three nights. He watched me work the horses. On the third evening he said *if you ever want to learn what wrangling is, come find me*. He did not name a price. He did not name a duration. He just said it. I have been turning that over for half a year. **The offer is rare; the wrangler-line is rare; I do not know if I want to leave Elmsfield**. But I want to know it exists. If the answer turns out to be yes, I will go. If it turns out to be no, I will be glad I was asked."*

### When asked about the chaos-warped country — *why I will not take horses there*

> *"Animals know. A horse walking into the broken lands begins to read everything *wrong* — the wind smells wrong, the ground holds wrong, the night is *louder than it should be*. They become unstable. Some bolt. Some refuse to walk. Some go in and come out wrong, the way the trees come out wrong. I have seen the flock at Field-flock pasture — the one outside Elmsfield's southern fences — and I will not put a horse near it. **I will take you to the edge. I will not take a horse past it.** If you need to go past, you go on foot, and you leave me with the mounts at the last clean ground."*

### When asked about his uncle — *the handover*

> *"He is older than he says. He has been giving me the books in pieces for two years. The trade he still holds; the animals he gave me last winter. By the time he steps back fully I will be running it; he will be in the chair by the pump; he will keep talking and I will keep working. **It is not a sad arrangement.** It is a slow one. He chose it. He has been kind."*

### When asked about Bridget's expectations — *what the clan wants of me*

> *"Bridget would have me available when the clan needs to move. I understand the work; I respect Bridget; I would help if the help fit. But I am not the clan's first answer to *every* question — Wilfred is steadier than I am, Corné knows the valley better than I do, and the clan does well having both of them. **I am Badger. The horses are the work.** If the clan needs a wrangler, I am there. If the clan needs a person to walk into a room and speak for it, that is not me, and Bridget knows that."*

## Mannerisms

- **When pressed** — sets whatever he is holding *down*. Walks two steps to where his horses are, puts a hand on a flank, and answers from there. Does not raise his voice; speaks more carefully.
- **When distraught** — goes to the stalls. Mucks the nearest one entirely. Will not speak until the work is done. The nearby horses *settle* in his presence; a party watching reads the affection from the animal side.
- **When happy** — a quick, surprised laugh. Reaches for the nearest horse's mane, runs his fingers through it briefly, lets go. The smile fades fast; the underlying mood holds.

## What he will not say

- **The exact pay he charges**, before he has read the route. He prices by terrain and length; quoting before he knows is not honest.
- **Which of the three late-game paths he is leaning toward**, before he has decided.
- **What is in the folded-leather book of horse-names** beyond the name of his last mare. The book is private.
- **What his uncle's brother's place across the valley is like.** Family is not for visitors yet.

## What he can teach

- **Animal-handling, basic to intermediate.** How to read a horse's mood; harness work; minor ailments; hoof care; the practical kind. Open to any serious student.
- **Reading a working animal at distance** — gait, ear-position, breath. Slower; teachable through walking with him.
- **Stable-craft.** Mucking, feed, water, the rotation of a small operation. Practical; not romantic.
- **Wrangling — at his current level**, which he is honest about. *"For better than this, you want Eric Randell."*

## DM notes

- **Ralph is the party's logistical solution to *getting off the road*.** A campaign that does not use him forfeits a real friction-easer; a campaign that uses him cheaply (sending him into combat, into chaos-warped country, into terrain he refused) loses him. The two patterns are the same; the rule is *take him at his word*.
- **The signature move is *flee with the animals*.** A DM should narrate this honestly when the moment comes — not as cowardice, as the *function the party hired*. A party returning to find an empty camp and a clean trail with the horses safely on it has the right outcome.
- **The three late-game paths are real choices.** Corné's circuit, Eric Randell's wrangler-line, the stable's expansion. None is the *correct* answer; the campaign should let Ralph choose based on which the party walks beside him first. A DM may use his decision as a quiet calendar — the season Ralph chooses is the season the campaign moves into a new register.
- **Loves horses more than the idea of the clan.** This is *Corné's diagnostic*, not a flaw. Bridget's late-game wants him available; Ralph is *willing if the work fits*. A DM running the Brumal-collapse arc may have Bridget call on him; he will come; he will not lead. That is the right shape.
- **Eric Randell's offer is in canon** (`doc/npcs/eric_randell.md` §"Stories he tells" and crew-stool). A DM running the Eric arc can have Ralph apply; Eric will accept; the campaign loses Ralph from Elmsfield for a year or two and gets him back transformed.
- **The horses are the work.** A DM running Ralph at the table should foreground the animals — describe them, name them, let the party meet them. Ralph in the abstract is a young Badger; Ralph with a specific draft-mare in front of him is the character.
