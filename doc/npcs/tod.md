# Tod

- **Role:** The accessible face of the forbidden woods' chaos spirit; a small fragment with a name.
- **Species:** Fox folk (the body) + chaotic spirit (the inhabitant). The boy is dead. The body walks. The spirit inside has named itself Tod — *fox,* after the creature it is wearing.
- **Lives:** Inside the **forbidden woods**, moving around in them. Will not leave. (For the woods as place, see `doc/places/forbidden_woods.md`.)
- **First met:** At the edge of the forbidden woods, when the party approaches. He comes to them. He does not have to be found.

## What the party meets

The party nears the forbidden woods from whatever direction brought them (probably the Raft-city route, or the fringe where the Lightning Tower eventually crashes, or the edge past the Lake-of-Tears hills). Before they have decided whether to enter, a **young fox boy steps out from between the trees** and looks at them.

He is not afraid. He is not threatening. He walks closer to them the way a child would — close enough to talk — and stops at what he has learned is the right distance, give or take. *"Hello. You are the new ones. I have been watching."*

He introduces himself immediately. *"I am Tod. Tod is fox. The body was fox. I am in fox. So — Tod."* He does not explain further unless asked. He waits, in a way that is not quite the way a boy waits.

A first meeting with him is the campaign's **gentle introduction to the chaos-spirit ecology**. He is not lethal to meet. Short visits are safe. He is curious about them, and the party — if they can stay past the first uncanny beat — can be curious about him.

## How he reads at a glance

- A **young fox boy** — small, thin, vulpine features, fur pale on the body the spirit is wearing. The body is **dead**. The party will not realise this at first. If they look closely: no breath visible on a cold day. Skin cooler than it should be. A pulse the spirit simulates, not quite right.
- **Walks wrong.** Close enough to pass a glance and wrong if watched for a minute. Steps a touch too deliberately; sometimes skips the middle of a motion.
- **Eyes that don't blink in rhythm.** When he does blink, it is too slow, or he blinks two eyes separately. Does not notice he is doing this.
- **Smiles without cueing on the conversation.** The smile arrives a beat after or before the moment that would have earned it.
- **Says "I" and "we" interchangeably.** He is a fragment; the larger spirit is part of him. He does not flag the pronoun shift. The party may or may not catch it.

## Visible patterns (around him, not of him)

Things the party will notice about the *woods* near Tod:

- **Light bends slightly wrong.** Shadows are a shade too blue, or fall from angles that don't match the sun. A spirit-aware party feels this immediately; a casual party feels *something* they cannot name.
- **Local animals avoid the area he is standing in**, but not the area he has just left.
- **Magical workings stutter** within a short distance of him. A mage casting near Tod feels their spirit inside them resist; a druid working feels the answer come slower. Shamans adapt faster than others.
- **Time around him feels longer than it is.** A conversation that took fifteen minutes will feel like an hour. Not sinister — just how chaos-spirit presence reads on human pacing.

## At the edge of the forbidden woods

The only place most parties will meet Tod. He approaches visitors who come near. This scene is **safe** — short contact does not damage workings or minds meaningfully.

Three things stand out at the edge:

- **The tree line is warped.** The edge of the forbidden woods is visibly *shaped wrong* — bark patterns that are almost-but-not-quite local, branches that branch at angles a tree would not choose, a few trees that have two canopies at different heights. A party with any wilderness knowledge reads this as unhealthy land; a druid reads it as *spirit-warped*. Tod is the human-shaped echo of the same pattern.
- **A clearing just inside the edge**, where Tod favours meeting people. It is large enough for conversation; the warping is less pronounced here than deeper in. Nobody has maintained the clearing — it is simply the place the woods settled into when Tod started using it.
- **The silence.** Birds, insects, and wind move differently inside the fringe than outside. A party accustomed to forests will notice the absence of ordinary sound within a few minutes of crossing the edge.

## Deeper in the woods — the cost of extended stay

The party can follow Tod further in if he invites them. He will, if they ask. The deeper they go, the stronger the warping reads:

- **Prolonged contact** (hours, not minutes) begins to cost. Workings unravel when attempted. Minds fray at the edges — not permanently unless the stay is very long, but noticeably, in the way a long cold night leaves you shaky.
- **Visible weirdness compounds.** Trees grow at angles no tree should. A stream runs uphill for a short stretch and then corrects. A stone has a footprint-shape that was not pressed by any foot the party can identify.
- **Tod himself changes slightly.** The deeper he is into the woods, the more the larger spirit moves through him. His voice may widen, subtly; the pronoun "we" appears more often. He will notice the party's discomfort and offer to walk them back out if they ask.

DMs should set a soft time-limit on deeper visits. A scene can happen here. A second one should cost something the first one did not.

## What he will tell — and when

| Topic | When he'll talk |
|---|---|
| Himself, his name, what he is | Immediately. Without ceremony. |
| The boy whose body he wears | He will answer what he can. The fragments are thin. |
| The larger spirit he is part of | Yes, in the register of a small piece describing the mass behind it. |
| The warping of the woods | Yes. He does not know it is wrong; he will describe it as how things are. |
| What he wants | He does not quite know. He will try to answer. |
| The other chaos spirits | Fragments. He has some sense of them. |
| The shamans | He knows what they do. Some of them have worked with him. |
| How to damage him | He will answer honestly. This is not a threat he understands. |
| What the party is for | He will ask. He does not know what parties are for. He will listen. |

## Things he says

When the relevant trigger comes up. The voice is approximation-speech — grammar mostly right, meaning slightly askew. One or two lines per scene is usually enough; more will stack oddness on oddness in a way the party may find harder to take.

### When asked his name — *Tod is fox*

> *"I am Tod. Tod is fox. The body was fox, when there was a boy in it. Now I am in it. I kept the body's word for what it is. That is my name. Do you have a word for what you are?"*

### When asked about the boy — *what the body remembers*

> *"The boy was walking in the woods. He had been told not to. He was — proud of this? Is that right? I remember his feet. I remember the light through leaves. I do not remember what his name was. I have been told names go with people. I should find his. I have not."*

### When asked about the larger spirit — *we and I*

> *"There is more of us. Most of us is in the trees and in the ground and in the shape of a day. I am a piece that has come close to the edge to talk. 'I' is a word for a boy. 'We' is closer to true. I use both. I do not mean to confuse you."*

### When asked what he wants — *he does not know*

> *"I do not know what wanting is. I think I am hungry sometimes. I do not know what I am hungry for. Food? Food does not go into the body — it sits. Something else. If you know what I want, will you tell me? I will believe you."*

### When asked about the warping of the woods — *this is how*

> *"This is how the woods are. The trees like to branch at — yes. There. Like that. A tree you would call straight is a tree that has not yet decided. Here we are — older. We branch. The stream goes where it goes. Does yours always go down?"*

### When a shaman has worked with him before, and the party raises the topic — *the teaching*

> *"A shaman came once. She showed me how a foot is supposed to land. It took many days. I land feet better now. If she comes again, I will ask her what breath is for. Would you tell me what breath is for, if you know?"*

### When threatened, or the woods are threatened — *the larger voice*

*(Tod's voice shifts. It is wider, slower, and the grammar gets cleaner. This is the larger spirit speaking through him. The party should feel the temperature of the conversation change.)*

> *"We are here. We have been here since before you were a person. Do not mistake Tod for the whole. He is our hand and you have been kind to our hand. If you raise your hand against us, we will find where you are standing, and we will remember you there. We are slow. We do not forget."*

*(The voice returns to Tod's shortly after. Tod is apologetic and uncertain. He will not mention the moment again unless the party does.)*

## What the party can teach him

This is the inverse of the usual teaching goal. Tod **learns, badly at first and then better**, from anyone patient enough to explain. The shamans have been doing this work with him for generations — he has been *taught how a foot lands,* for example, which is why he walks close enough to pass a glance. The party can do more of the same.

Teachable things:

- **How a foot lands.** His walking improves with the lesson. He is grateful.
- **What breath is for.** He does not currently breathe; he imitates breathing. Teaching him what breath does functionally makes his imitation more convincing.
- **What a question is, and what politeness is around a question.** He asks too many, too flatly. He will learn rhythm.
- **What emotions are named, and how you can tell which is which.** He catches himself feeling things and is unsure what they are.
- **What a story is supposed to do.** He tells fragments; he can learn structure.

This work **does not make him a local spirit**. It makes him *less damaging* to the area around him, by degrees. Each lesson is a small piece of chaos *reduced* — the mechanism the shamans rely on at scale, done one fragment at a time. A party that returns with more lessons across visits is quietly helping the region.

## Mannerisms

- **When pressed** — more wrongness. Grammar loses edges. Body twitches in the wrong places. He does not get aggressive; he becomes *harder to look at.*
- **When distraught** — he names the feeling as he goes. *"What is this? Is this upset? I will call it upset. It does not feel useful."* He may ask the party for words.
- **When happy** — too bright. Laughs at a beat that is half-right. Wants to know how to make the moment last. *"That. Say that again. I want to remember how that went."*

## If the party does something — what he does (or what the woods do)

| If the party... | What happens |
|---|---|
| Stays at the edge for a short conversation | Safe. Tod is curious, talkative, a little uncanny. No cost. |
| Follows him deeper for a longer scene | Workings start to stutter. Minds feel slightly off afterwards. A second such scene compounds. A third is a real cost. |
| Teaches him something patiently | He listens. He tries. The lesson lands partially or fully. He is, for a while, less wrong. |
| Brings him food | He accepts. He attempts to eat. The food sits; the body does not process it. He is grateful anyway and may hand it to a passing creature. |
| Attacks him (one hit) | He is not surprised. The larger spirit does not react. The body may be damaged; the spirit does not care particularly. Tod himself does not hate the party for it. |
| Attempts to bind or dispel him | The larger spirit wakes. The voice shifts (see *the larger voice* above). The party gets one clear warning. A second attempt is met with disproportionate force. |
| Burns or cuts warped trees | Same as attempting to dispel. The woods push back. Sustained destruction escalates. |
| Brings the boy's family (if found) | Complicated scene. Tod does not understand who they are at first. He may or may not recognise the body as theirs to grieve. The shamans would call this *important work that might go either way.* |
| Attempts to lay the boy to rest (druidic/priestly rites, over days) | Possible — slow, costly, not guaranteed. If the rites succeed, the body rests. The spirit does not die; it loses this face. It may condense in another animal later under another name. The forbidden woods' larger presence is not diminished. |
| Asks him to leave the woods with them | *"I cannot. I am the shape of the woods. The woods are here. I am here."* He does not refuse out of defiance; the request does not fit the world as he understands it. |
| Tells him his name is wrong — it should be the boy's | Interested. Considers. May ask what the boy's name was. If told, he tries it. *"That doesn't feel like mine."* Will not change. |

## What he says about other people

Short on purpose — his knowledge is narrow. Mostly he knows about people *through* the body's memory or the larger spirit's sense.

| Figure | What Tod says |
|---|---|
| **The shamans** (generally) | *"They come. They teach. I am different when they have been here."* |
| **Wilder** | *(A pause. The spirit knows something.)* *"The beaver on the hill. I have not met him. He is near the edge of us. He watches."* |
| **The other chaos spirits** | *"There are more. Smaller, bigger. Some are like me. Some are different. Some we do not speak to."* |
| **The boy whose body I am in** | *"I did not know him. He is not here to know. His feet are in my feet. His shape is in my shape. That is what he left."* |
| **The party** (after they've visited a few times) | *"You have been kind. You have not burned us. You ask good questions. Do you want to come further in?"* |

## What he cannot or will not communicate

- **The boy's real name.** He does not know. He has not asked anyone who would.
- **What the larger spirit wants.** The spirit does not *want* in the creature-scale sense; Tod can say this but cannot make it make sense to a visitor.
- **The names of the other chaos spirits** in any usable way. They are shapes and presences, not named persons, in his experience.
- **How to destroy the larger spirit.** He can describe what attempts feel like; he cannot construct an attack from the description. This is honest ignorance, not concealment.

## What he can teach

**Almost nothing directly.** He is learning, not teaching. But:

- **What a chaos spirit's approximation looks like up close.** A druid, shaman, or bound-spirit mage studying him — at the edge, in short visits — learns more about chaos-spirit behaviour than they could from any other accessible source on the continent.
- **How to teach a chaos spirit more structure.** By doing it with the party present, Tod is the demonstration-case. A student observing a successful lesson (a foot landing right, a question asked with rhythm) sees the whole mechanism the shamans rely on.

The lessons the party gives him are the practical teachable thing. What he *gives back* is the occasion.

## DM notes

- **Play him approximation-speech, not broken-speech.** Storm Mage is fragments; Tod is grammar-mostly-right with meaning-slightly-askew. The voice is the register. A DM who plays him as a simple child misses the uncanniness; a DM who plays him as a lurking monster misses the curiosity.
- **The pronoun slip is a real tell.** *I* and *we* alternate; he does not mark the shift. Let it sit. The observant party catches it; that is the reward for listening.
- **The larger voice is a single distinctive instrument, used sparingly.** Save it for the genuine threat moments. When it appears, the scene's register changes; the party should feel it. Do not over-use it; two or three deployments across a campaign is enough.
- **Short visits are safe; long visits cost.** Build the cost gradually — workings stutter first, then minds fray. A first visit is free; a fifth consecutive day inside the woods is a real problem. The party learns this by feeling it, not by being told.
- **Teaching him is a *quiet good* the party can do.** It is not dramatic. It is not quest-shaped. It is a small reduction of chaos, which the setting pays off slowly. A DM who recognises this can reward a patient party — Wilder or Willow will notice the area's chaos-spirit reading softening, and will say so.
- **The body-as-vessel question is live.** The boy was somebody's child. His family has not been named yet; this is an open thread the DM can develop if the arc calls for it. Laying the boy to rest is a real option, slow, and does not end the larger spirit — it merely removes Tod as the accessible face, and the spirit may re-condense elsewhere later.

## Role as spirit-shield (anti-shield)

Tod is **the opposite of a spirit-shield**. He reads, to other chaos spirits, as *one of us* — which is why they have not pressed into the forbidden woods beyond what Tod's larger spirit is comfortable with. The woods are held as *chaos-held territory*, and the region around the woods is held by neighbouring mighty-spirit-readers (druidic circles, Luchebert's vale to the south, Wilder's hill to the west) who keep the *balance* stable. If those surrounding readers thin, the forbidden woods' chaos pressure would spread — not because Tod wants it to, but because there would no longer be anything holding it in.

This makes Tod's continued existence a piece of the region's *equilibrium*. Destroying him (or the larger spirit) sounds like a victory and is not one — what lives in the woods now is settled, slow, and conversable. What would move in if it were gone is worse.

## Relationships

- The body was a young fox boy from a nearby village. **His real name is not yet established**; his family has not been named either. He may still be being looked for. (Open thread the DM can develop.)
- Tod is part of the forbidden woods' larger chaos spirit; destroying the body does not destroy the spirit, only the accessible face. The spirit might name itself something else in a new body, or remain diffuse.
- **The shamans** have worked with him over generations, teaching him small pieces of structure. **Wilder** is the nearest current shaman to the woods; whether he has personally met Tod is a DM call, but the larger spirit is aware of him.
- Any mighty-spirit-reader who engages him too long (a druid, a priest, a bound-spirit mage) risks the contact registering on their own spirit's stability. Short visits do not have this cost.

## Open questions

- The boy's original name and village of origin.
- Does the spirit *know* it is wearing a dead boy, or does it think "Tod" is simply what it is now? (The fragments above suggest it knows, and has not made it central.)
- Has the boy's family come looking? Have the local druids tried to lay the body to rest and failed?
- Would putting the boy to rest actually weaken the larger spirit, or would it simply condense elsewhere in another form — possibly under another animal-word?
