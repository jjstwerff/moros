# Tod

- **Role:** The accessible face of the forbidden woods' chaos spirit; a fragment with a name
- **Species:** Fox folk (the body) + chaotic spirit (the inhabitant)
- **Location:** The forbidden woods, moving around inside them; he will not leave
- **Status:** The boy is dead. The body walks. The spirit inside it has named itself Tod.

## Summary

A young fox boy walked into the forbidden woods ignoring the warnings. He died there. The **chaos spirit** of the woods took the body and now walks around in it. It has given itself a name: **Tod** — simply "fox," the creature the body used to be. The spirit does not use (or does not know, or does not care about) the boy's real name. It has picked a word that describes what it is wearing and adopted it as identity.

This is characteristic of it. Tod does things the way a chaos spirit thinks those things are done: he walks how the spirit *thinks* a fox boy walks, close enough to pass a glance and wrong if watched for a minute. He speaks by arranging words the way the spirit has observed words being arranged. He introduces himself as "Tod" with no ceremony, because that is the creature he was told he is.

He is only a **small condensed piece** of the full spirit. The bulk remains diffuse in the woods and in the warped trees, and that bulk is **large, slow, and settled** — one of the bigger chaos spirits around, but also one of the least active. The full spirit is like a pond, not a stream; it does not travel, it does not react quickly, and it is correspondingly hard to move or influence at its core. Tod is what it puts forward instead — a small, mobile, talkable fragment, easy to meet precisely because the mass behind him is inert.

Talking to Tod is talking to a narrow strand of a much larger, stranger presence. Answers are partly coherent, partly alien. He will ask questions a boy would not ask. He may be hungry without knowing what for. He may remember the boy's life and also not quite know what a life is.

Tod is not lethal to meet. He is not trying to trick anyone. He is not even *against* this world's rules — he is just not from here and does not yet know them well enough to move through the world without accidentally bending it (see LORE.md §"Why the chaos"). The danger of extended time near him is subtler: contact with a chaos spirit's approximations makes magical workings unravel and minds fray at their edges. Short visits are safe. Long ones cost.

**The spirit behind Tod will lash out if genuinely threatened.** Ritual workings performed in its territory, deliberate destruction of its warped trees, or attempts to bind or dispel it read as active provocation, and the response is disproportionate — the spirit has mass behind its reaction, even if it is slow to deliver. **A normal party is not a threat to it** and can visit, talk to Tod, and leave safely. Only sustained, deliberate, significant acts against the woods' chaos itself trigger the lashing-out.

**A pragmatic consequence.** If the party spends time with Tod and explains things to him — how a foot is supposed to land, how breath is supposed to work, what a question is — he will listen and try. He can learn. The shamans do this work routinely; the party can too. It does not make him a local spirit (he will always be from elsewhere) but it makes him a little less strange, a little less damaging to the area around him. This is how chaos is *reduced*, piece by piece, in the parts of the world the shamans have reached. Tod is the nearest opportunity the party has to do a small version of the same work.

## Role in the campaign

Tod is **the setting's gentle introduction to the chaos-spirit ecology**. A first scene with him — sympathetic, uncanny, educational — prepares the party for the larger stakes:

- Fata morgana's protective arrangement (the mages/shamans/druids/priests hedging out chaos spirits elsewhere in the world).
- The blasted-lands pressure (larger chaotic spirits that have not taken bodies).
- The shamans' long plan (pushing chaos spirits back through the portal).
- The mechanism of "mighty-spirit-readers backing them off" from LORE.md §"Spirit Ecology."

Meeting Tod first is how the party learns what the rest of the setting is quietly protecting itself against. He is also a rare chance to hear a chaos spirit's perspective before the campaign asks them to act on it.

## Relationships

- The body was a young fox boy from a nearby village. The boy's real name is not yet established; his family has not been named either. He may still be being looked for.
- Tod is part of the forbidden woods' larger chaos spirit; destroying the body does not destroy the spirit, only the accessible face of it. The spirit might name itself something else in a new body, or remain diffuse.
- Any mighty-spirit-reader who engages him for too long (a druid, a priest, a bound-spirit mage) risks having the contact register on their own spirit's stability.

## Open questions

- The boy's original name and village of origin.
- Does the spirit *know* it is wearing a dead boy, or does it think "Tod" is simply what it is now?
- Has the boy's family come looking? Have the local druids tried to lay the body to rest and failed?
- Would putting the boy to rest actually weaken the larger spirit, or would it simply condense elsewhere in another form — possibly under another animal-word?
