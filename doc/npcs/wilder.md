# Wilder

- **Role:** Beaver shaman; guardian-watcher at the approach to the blasted lands; the nearest accessible voice for the shamans' long plan.
- **Species:** Beaver (animal-person). Old.
- **Lives:** A hill-top dwelling he built himself, at the **edge of the blasted lands**. He has been there long enough that the land has come to know him back.
- **First met:** On the approach to the blasted lands, when the party's route brings them near his hill. Usually he finds them first.

## Campaign brief

- **Role:** Old beaver shaman holding the line at the western edge of the broken lands; the campaign's default entry point into the shamans' long plan; one of the named pieces of the spirit-shield ring around the hill country.
- **Standouts:** *An old beaver thick-set even for his species*; **eyes that move between sky and ground in equal measure** (a shaman's habit, reading both); old tooth-marks in his tail; **a staff that is clearly his tool as well as his walking aid**. **A small fire kept going outside the dwelling** — more signal than warmth.
- **Speech tag:** Plain, practical, grounded — *not* mystic-performative. Uses *the broken lands* where everyone else says *the blasted lands*. *"I advise against it."* *"That concerns me more than the rest of it."*
- **Quests:** Early — *carry specific contributions to the long plan that are actual tasks, not membership; bring me news of Tod, Ornalis, and Laurent for the notebook.* Late — *broker my meeting with Gerhald; be the carriers when the long plan opens to ritual scale.* See §"Personal goals — early and later".
- **Friction:** *Question quality, not trust quantity.* A party that arrives cold but has *thought* gets real answers; a party that arrives with standing but asks flatly does not. He does not teach as a transaction. Damaging the land around his hill is taken personally and escalates fast.
- *Full character: `doc/npcs/wilder.md`.*

## What the party meets

The party is travelling south — the trade road skirting the blasted lands, or a less-used route that edges closer to them. At some point they come within sight of **a single hill** that stands a little apart, with a small dwelling at its crown and a thin line of smoke above it.

Before they have decided whether to approach, **a figure is already coming down the slope toward them.** An old beaver, thick-set, walking with a staff he uses for balance and for other things. He reaches them on level ground. He does not greet them warmly and does not greet them coldly. He says, quite plainly:

> *"You are heading that way."* *(He gestures south.)* *"Tell me why. I would rather you turned back, but I will want to know either way."*

This is how most travellers meet him. The party that is **deliberately sneaking** — stealth powers, night movement, concealed route — may slip past him and reach the broken country ungreeted. They will have lost his warning and his guidance. The file's default assumes they did not sneak.

## Personal goals — early and later

His goals run on a generational scale; the party intersects with specific pieces of them. He does not offer membership in the long plan on a first visit. He offers *tasks* that the plan needs done — and the tasks are real, not test-pieces.

- **Early-game — carry specific contributions to the long plan that are actual tasks, not membership.** *What the NPC says* (when the party has asked specific informed questions and Wilder has decided they are worth giving real answers to): *"You want to help. Most do not. Most who do, want a place in the plan. The plan does not need new members; it needs work done that members cannot reach. Recover one item from the broken lands a ritual team will use. Carry one message to a shaman whose name you will not learn. Stand on the safe ridge and watch the portal's surface for a day, and bring back what you saw. None of this is membership. All of it is the work."* — *Where:* the **broken lands** themselves (`doc/places/blasted_lands.md`) — at the depth and route Wilder agrees to, never deeper without him; a **named landmark** he points to for each retrieval (warped tree, fallen marker, the safe ridge that overlooks the portal); the **drop-points** he uses to pass messages to other shamans (locations he names without naming the recipients). — *What:* the specific task he has set, executed cleanly. Recovery work means coming back with the item *and* with notes for the notebook on what the route was like this season. Message work means delivering the sealed item to the named drop and waiting only for confirmation, never for conversation. Watch work means a full day on the safe ridge, taking the notes he has shown the party how to take, not staring long at the portal's shimmer. — *Reward:* genuine contribution. The plan moves a step nearer. Wilder writes the work into his notebook with their names. Standing builds toward the late-game offer of joining the ritual push when it opens. — *Complications:* the broken lands are alive with warped local spirits and chaos spirits; Wilder will tell the party what they are likely to encounter and **will not pretend the danger is small**. A single failure does not end the relationship — *"the broken country eats good people; tell me what you saw"* — but a pattern of failures does. The drop-points are real and watched; another shaman's safety depends on the message arriving with no tail. **Staring at the portal's shimmer costs something a notebook cannot record**; the party must trust the warning.

- **Early-game — bring me news of Tod, Ornalis, and Laurent for the notebook.** *What the NPC says* (when the party has met any of the three and Wilder learns of it): *"You met him. Sit. Tell me. The notebook needs what you saw. Tod is the easiest chaos spirit on the continent to teach and is therefore the one most often taught — every visit changes him a little, and I have been writing those changes for years. Ornalis is broken by what I watch for; if Felicia's shrine ever returns him to coherence, the names he could give would feed me for a season. Laurent's wreck eases chaos pressure across the lake region by his existence alone — what the **last flight** would cost the southern frontier is something I need to know before, not after."* — *Where:* the hilltop dwelling for the receiving; the **forbidden woods' edge** for fresh Tod-encounters (`doc/npcs/tod.md`); **Ornalis's hill near the volcano tower** for Storm-Mage news (`doc/npcs/storm_mage.md`); **Laurent's wreck** for the lake-region read (`doc/npcs/laurent.md`); the road in between where Wilder may not be the only one collecting. — *What:* on the way through any of these places, take the notes Wilder has trained the party to take — what was different from last visit, what the warped land around the figure was doing, what was said, what was not. Bring them to him plainly. Let him write while you talk. — *Reward:* his read on what the party encountered, fed back as guidance for the next visit. **The notebook is a generative prop** — over visits it accumulates entries, sketches, names of fallen, small workings the party is welcome to learn from. Standing rises with each clean report. — *Complications:* a party that *performs* a chaos-spirit encounter for him — exaggerates, adds drama, fills gaps with invention — gets caught. He compares against what he already knows and against other visitors' accounts. Caught performers lose the channel. Inventing a Tod-encounter for an entry breaks the relationship; he will tell them so plainly and not raise it again.

- **Later-game — broker my meeting with Gerhald.** *What the NPC says* (in private, after the party has visited both shamans and Wilder learns the second visit happened): *"You have been to him. He has been to no one in years. He and I have not met; I do not believe he knows my hill. We are on the same chaos-spirit axis at opposite edges of what can be held. If we met we would recognise each other in the first minute; we have not met because neither of us has moved. You can carry the question between us. Whether we meet, and where, and what we decide together — that is for him and me to settle. You are the one who can bring the question."* — *Where:* the **hill tower at the Lake of Tears** where Gerhald lives (`doc/npcs/hermit_shaman.md`); Wilder's hilltop; possibly a neutral point neither has held — a druidic circle, **Diederik's** ironwood (`doc/npcs/elder_diederik.md`), **Felicia's shrine** if Willow agrees and the meeting needs the great-spirit register (`doc/npcs/willow.md`). — *What:* carry Wilder's question to Gerhald — verbatim, no editorial. Carry Gerhald's answer back the same way. If both agree to meet, broker the venue; do not stage-manage what they say. Be present, if asked, to carry messages on for the third meeting if there is one. — *Reward:* two old shamans on the same axis, in a room together for the first time in either's life. What they decide — about coordination, about the plan's pace, about whether **Laurent's last flight** is part of the plan or a separate climax — reshapes what the campaign's late game can do. The party becomes the connective tissue between the two oldest practitioners on this register. — *Complications:* both of them are old and neither is mobile in the way they once were; the venue and the season matter. Gerhald has reasons of his own for not having moved (see his file); Wilder does too. **The meeting cannot be forced** — if either declines, the party returns clean and the long plan continues without the conversation. The other shamans in Wilder's network may have views about the meeting happening; Wilder will hear them and decide.

- **Later-game — be the carriers when the long plan opens to ritual scale.** *Conditional — opens once the early-game tasks have been carried cleanly and Wilder has decided the party is ready for the offer.* *What the NPC says* (in his hilltop dwelling, after a clean run of work and the right conversation): *"The plan is opening. The ritual team is being assembled. I am one of the practitioners running it; I am not one of the leaders. We need carriers between the parts of the plan that cannot personally walk between each other — me to the team in the broken lands; the team to a covering druid; the druid to a great-spirit channel; back to me. Names you will not learn except as you need them. Routes you will keep. The work is months, not weeks. Will you carry."* — *Where:* the **broken lands' interior** where the ritual team works at the portal's edge; the **named drop-points** Wilder uses for written messages; the covering druids and great-spirit channels Wilder names as the work calls for them — **Diederik** at the ironwood; **Hank** as second druid in reserve (`doc/npcs/hank.md`); **Willow** at Felicia's shrine if the great-spirit register is needed; **Felicia** through the shrine and through condensation at major rite-points (`doc/npcs/felicia.md`). — *What:* be the connective tissue across months of work — sealed letters between practitioners, sustained presence at the safe ridge during ritual phases, escort for ritual team members travelling the broken country, retrieval of components the team cannot leave the portal to fetch. **Do not improvise on the contents of what you carry**; do not open sealed messages; do not introduce yourselves to other shamans without Wilder's signal. — *Reward:* the long plan moves from generations-of-preparation into *running*. The portal at the centre of the broken lands begins to be opened — carefully, slowly — and the chaos spirits begin to be pushed back through. The campaign's largest possible climax is now in motion; the party is not the leaders, but they are the only people who walked it through. — *Complications:* the chaos spirits notice activity at the portal and respond. The broken lands become more dangerous than they were the season before. **Holding the opening once it begins is the unsolved part of the plan** — Wilder has been honest about this since the early-game story; the party should have heard him. A ritual that goes wrong costs lives, including Wilder's if the wrong piece breaks. The campaign that runs this arc must accept that the bittersweet possibilities (Wilder lost; the team broken; the portal sealed without completion) are real outcomes, not bad-DM scenarios.

- **Later-game — train a successor, when one arrives.** *Conditional — opens if a candidate the shamans' network has surfaced (or a party member with sustained standing and willingness to take a long post) reaches Wilder ready.* *What the NPC says* (at the hilltop, only with someone he has decided is the candidate): *"I have no one in training at present. That concerns me more than the rest of it. The work is the work; the lineage is incidental. If you mean it, the teaching begins now. It is years. You will not leave the hill for stretches. The land will come to know you back. Are you sure."* — *Where:* the hilltop dwelling and the perimeter walk; the safe ridge above the portal; **the broken lands' edge** for the lessons that cannot be done elsewhere. — *What:* take the long-post. Walk the perimeter at dawn and dusk; sit at the table with the notebook; learn the working that is the ring of stones; receive the names Wilder has been protecting all his life. — *Reward:* the line continues at this part of the frontier. The hill stays held past Wilder's life. The continent's spirit-shield does not thin here when he goes. — *Complications:* this is years, not seasons, and not transferrable to a part-time student. A party-member taking it leaves the party, with whatever the campaign does with that change. **Wilder will not press the offer** — he has refused students before and will refuse again rather than train someone unsuited. The candidate must consent knowing what the post is.

## How he reads at a glance

- An **old beaver-folk**, thick-set even for his species. Plain wool clothes, a leather belt with a small pouch on it, a staff that is clearly his tool as well as his walking aid.
- **Eyes that move between the sky and the ground in equal measure.** A shaman's habit — he is reading both.
- **His tail has old tooth-marks in it** — scars from whatever he has encountered out there. Not advertised; visible if the party notices.
- **He walks at an even pace** and does not hurry. The hill does not need him to hurry.
- Calls the country south of him *the broken lands.* Most people elsewhere call it *the blasted lands.* He knows this. He uses his own word.

## Visible patterns the party will notice

- **At the hill in daylight.** He is almost always within sight of his dwelling during the day. Travellers passing within his range trigger his descent.
- **A fire outside the dwelling, kept going.** Small, constant. More signal than warmth.
- **Walks a perimeter around the hill at dawn and dusk.** Slow. The walk is a working, not merely exercise — a party with spirit-awareness will feel it.
- **Listens to the birds.** He corrects his own thinking when birds change behaviour. *"Wait. That was not the right call for that hour. Something moved."*

## At his hilltop dwelling

A small stone-and-wood house he built. One room, efficiently laid out. The party reaches it if he brings them up — or, less commonly, if they climb the hill without him and he is still coming down.

Three things stand out on arrival:

- **The view.** The hill stands clear of other high ground, and **the blasted lands are visible from the door** — a grey-brown stretch that does not look wrong from this distance, which is how it deceives people. He watches the near edge of it all day. A party standing at the door understands why this is his post.
- **A ring of stones around the dwelling** — small, unobtrusive, placed deliberately. Spirit-aware characters will read it as a working; to the untrained eye it is just stones. The ring is part of why the pressure from the blasted lands eases here.
- **A low table inside** with a **notebook on it** and small offerings (bread, water, a pinch of something). Every visitor leaves traces he notes. If asked, he will show the notebook. Party names may be added if the party is significant.

## What he will tell — and when

| Topic | When he'll talk |
|---|---|
| That the blasted lands are dangerous, in general terms | Immediately. His opening warning. |
| What specifically will happen to a party that enters unprepared | Once asked. Plainly. Without drama. |
| The chaos spirits' behaviour in the broken lands | Freely. He has studied them closely. |
| Warped local spirits (highly influenced originals) | Freely. This is his specialty. |
| His shamanic tradition, generally | Briefly. He does not perform it. |
| Other shamans | **Carefully.** Names are protection; he gives few. |
| The long plan to reopen the portal | **Gated by the quality of the question.** Vague questions get vague answers; specific informed questions get real ones. |
| The portal itself — what it is | Once asked specifically. He has seen it from a distance. |
| His own history on this hill | Briefly; he is not interesting to himself. |
| How to travel safely into the broken lands | Only with his agreement and usually only with him helping. Otherwise: *"I advise against it."* |

## Stories he tells

When the relevant topic comes up.

### When asked why the blasted lands are what they are — *the nearest edge*

> *"The chaos came from a door that opened too far, a long time ago. Most of it is gone elsewhere by now — the bigger pieces migrated. What is left here is the smaller chaos and the local spirits the chaos has pulled out of true. They are not malicious. They do not know this world's rules. A tree they stand near grows sideways. A body they enter moves wrong. They are trying. They are also dangerous. I watch for both."*

### When asked what happens to unprepared travellers — *what he has seen*

> *"They go in. Some come back. Many do not. The ones who do come back are different. Some of them understand what happened to them; most do not. I have names in my notebook I wish I did not have. I write them because writing them is the last useful thing I can do for the person."*

### When asked about the long plan — *if the question is specific* (trust-gated)

> *"The plan is generations long. The portal at the centre of the broken lands is physical as well as metaphorical; the first humans built a structure to stabilise it on this side, and it is still there. The shamans mean to open it again — carefully, slowly, this time — and push the chaos spirits back through it, into the place they came from. That is the short answer. The long answer includes: how we reach the portal alive with a ritual team; how we *hold* the opening once it begins; what we do if the ritual goes wrong. None of these are solved. We are working. We have been working for more than my life, and I am old."*

### When asked what the portal looks like — *from a distance*

> *"I have seen it from a ridge, once. It is a glowing field that *shimmers* on some days and is still on others. When it shimmers, glimpses of dimensions unknown are visible in it. **Those glimpses are not worth risking your mind for.** I tell every ritual candidate so. I am telling you so. If you ever stand on that ridge, do not stare long. Look, note, look away."*

### When asked about his tradition — *briefly*

> *"I am a shaman. My people — the beaver folk of a valley I have not named to you — pass the work along. I learned from my grandmother. She learned from hers. The work is the work; the lineage is incidental. I have no one in training at present; that concerns me more than the rest of it."*

### When asked about the local warped spirits — *the ones still of this world*

> *"The chaos spirits came from elsewhere. The spirits you find in the broken lands now are mostly ours — local spirits that lived too long beside chaos approximations. Their shape has bent. They still belong to this world. They are not chaos. They are a third thing, harder than either, because they should know our rules and no longer do. Most of my work here is with them. A student interested in spirit-ecology would find no better teacher on this continent for this specific register."*

## Mannerisms

- **When pressed** — he does not answer faster. He looks at the speaker for longer than the question deserved. May glance at the broken lands. The answer comes when it comes.
- **When distraught** — walks to the edge of the hill and looks south. Will not speak until he has looked long enough. The party should wait.
- **When at peace** — rare; visible only to a party that stays across several visits. A slow nod, a single *"good,"* a double tap of his staff on the ground — his strongest expression of approval.

## If the party does something — what he does

| If the party... | He responds by... |
|---|---|
| Approaches openly, on a reasonable route | He descends the hill and meets them. Warns them. Offers his help. |
| Sneaks past without engaging | Notices after the fact; does not pursue. They are on their own now for what follows. He will receive them on the way back, if there is a way back. |
| Asks to be taken into the blasted lands | Considers. If their reason is serious and they have done the work, he will guide them to the near edge. He does not go deep casually. |
| Asks about the long plan vaguely | Vague answers. The conversation moves on. He notices the shape of the ask. |
| Asks about the long plan specifically (informed questions) | Real answers. He opens up. See *the long plan* story. |
| Demands to be taken to the portal | *"No."* Does not elaborate on first asking. On later asking, if they have earned standing, he will walk them to the ridge where it can be *seen* from a safe distance — nothing further. |
| Damages the land around his hill | Takes it personally. Warns once. A repeat makes him ask them to leave. A third makes him bring the hill's working to bear; he does not enjoy this. |
| Brings him news of a chaos spirit they have met (Tod, for example) | Listens carefully. Asks specific questions about the encounter. May write in his notebook. May offer guidance for the next visit. |
| Brings him news of the Storm Mage | Listens. *"He is — yes. Ornalis."* (He uses the name if the party has told him it exists. Otherwise he calls him *the old storm mage* like everyone else.) Has opinions; will share them if pressed. |
| Tries to trade for teaching | He does not teach for trade. If they are worth teaching, they will be taught. If they come at it as a transaction, they are not worth teaching. |
| Offers to help with the long plan | Welcomed warily. The plan is generations long; a party's useful contribution is usually a *specific task* (recover an item, deliver a message to another shaman, report back on a condition in the broken lands). Joining the plan as members is a long commitment he will not offer on the first visit. |
| Reports a death in the broken lands they could not prevent | Asks the name. Writes it down. Thanks them for bringing it. Does not comfort them; the grief is theirs. |
| Threatens him | The hill responds before he does. Pressure eases or rises depending on what the threat is. He would rather not bring his working to bear on a person; he will if he must. |

## What he can teach

For students who have earned standing:

- **Shaman's practice** — at a depth most students never reach, with an emphasis on the spirit-ecology of chaos-affected regions. This is his specialty; no one else on the continent teaches this specifically as well.
- **Reading warped land** — how to tell a local spirit that has been pulled out of true from one that is merely old, how to tell a chaos spirit's track from a natural one, how to recognise the *almost-right* that is the chaos register.
- **Speaking to a chaos spirit without making things worse** — the patience-craft Tod's shamanic teachers use.
- **Hill-reading** — practical terrain-shaman work. Wind, bird behaviour, light on the ground, the small signs the land uses to talk back.
- **Notebook discipline** — informally, by example. Students who spend time with him begin to write down what they have seen and why; the habit is more useful than it sounds.

He does not teach as a transaction. *"If I think you are worth teaching, I will begin. If I do not, no trade will make me start."*

## What he will not say

- **His age.** Irrelevant to the work; he will deflect.
- **The names of other shamans in the long plan.** Names are protection. Without specific need, he does not give them.
- **What happened at the hill-post he held before this one** (if there was one). Open DM territory; Wilder does not volunteer it.
- **What he would do if the plan had to be abandoned.** He has thought about it. He does not want to say aloud.
- **Which of his students in the past did not return from the broken lands.** The names are in the notebook; the notebook is his.

## DM notes

- **Play him steady, not mystic-performative.** Wilder is an old working shaman, not a sage. His answers are practical; his craft is grounded. A DM playing him as mysterious misses the grain. He *is* mysterious; he does not perform it.
- **He notices before the party notices him.** This is baked into the first encounter. Describe the hill first, the smoke second, the figure already coming down toward them third. The party should feel met before they have decided.
- **The long-plan gate is question-quality, not trust-quantity.** A party that arrives cold but has *thought* can get real answers. A party that arrives with standing but asks flatly cannot. DM notes: reward the specific informed question. Do not reward the generic respectful question at the same level.
- **His hill is a spirit-shield node.** Per `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Spirit Ecology," his presence and his ring of stones hold chaos pressure at bay for a small radius. Losing him thins the cover at this part of the frontier. A campaign that kills him or drives him off should account for the consequence.
- **He is the campaign's default entry to the shamans' long plan.** If the party engages with that climax-option, Wilder is most often their first contact. From him they can learn what the plan is, what it requires, and who else is working on it (without getting names). Further contacts are DM-fill.
- **The notebook is a generative prop.** Every visit produces an entry. A party that returns can ask to see it. It should contain names of the fallen, observations, sketches of spirit-behaviour, small workings the party is welcome to see. A DM can use it to deliver information that would feel stilted as dialogue.
- **He pairs with Tod and the Storm Mage.** All three sit on the chaos-spirit axis in different registers — Tod inside the chaos, Ornalis broken by it, Wilder holding the line against it. Parties that have met any of the three will find Wilder interested in what they know.

## Role as spirit-shield

Per `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Spirit Ecology," an old shaman working at the edge of chaos-spirit territory reads to chaotic spirits as a mighty-adjacent presence. Wilder's hill-top, and a modest radius around it, is therefore **a clear-spot on the edge of the blasted lands**: a place where the chaos-pressure eases, which is part of why a traveller can stop at his hill and think clearly before continuing. The further from his hill, the faster that protection fades.

His presence is also part of why the blasted lands' chaos-pressure has not pushed further north into Brumal and Allondo faster than it has. He is one of several shamans holding the line; losing him — to age, accident, or a chaos-spirit encounter — thins the cover at this part of the frontier.

## Relationships

- Part of the **shamans** as a loose body of practitioners working on the long plan to close the portal. How organised that body is, how they communicate, and who else is in it are DM-fill.
- A fellow old shaman of **Gerhald** (see `doc/npcs/hermit_shaman.md`), though of different species and very different post — both hold difficult territory where most practitioners would not live. Whether they have met, know of each other, or would recognise one another as allies is open. They would almost certainly get on if they did.
- Natural potential ally to **Felicia** (through her hill-country arrangements) and to **Elder Diederik** (druid, ascetic, also holding a spirit-shield post), though none of them have likely ever spoken directly.
- Professional peer of **Willow** at Luchebert and **Rhianna** in Scarlet Vale, in the sense that all three hold different parts of the continent's thinning urban-and-vale spirit-shield. None of them know this about each other in detail.

## Open questions

- Wilder's age, his clan, his shamanic lineage.
- How many other shamans are in the long plan, and how they coordinate.
- What Wilder considers *the right questions* beyond the obvious ones listed above.
- What he would want from a party he decided to invite into the plan.
- Whether he and Gerhald know each other, and if not, what would happen when they met.
- Whether he had a hill-post before this one, and what happened to it.
