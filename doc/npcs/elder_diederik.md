# Elder Diederik

- **Role:** Elder of Elmsfield. Exiled druid. The man who knows the tree.
- **Species:** Owl. Older. Walks with a staff he does not lean on hard. Flies for longer journeys.
- **Lives:** In his own druid-grown treehouse a short way from the great ironwood at the town's centre.
- **First met:** Session 1, at the gate with Irna.

## What the party meets

Session 1's opening obstacle is a sick stranger, a frightened crowd, and a large grey-muzzled dog that has already driven off two locals. Diederik arrives without hurry and the crowd thins around him. He does not raise his voice. He looks at Irna, then at her dog, then at Joseph, and the situation reorganises itself around what he is paying attention to.

He takes charge of Irna's care without asking permission, because nobody in Elmsfield gives him permission for anything any more — he is the elder, and the town runs on the *informal consent* of its residents, not on a writ. He recognises the crystalline growths on Irna's skin as **fungal**, not contagious, and says so. The crowd believes him. The party may or may not.

This is how he tends to enter scenes. Slowly. With standing the party has not earned. Saying things that turn out to be true.

## How he reads at a glance

- An old owl in plain wool, with feathers that have been grey for a long time.
- Voice low and even, slow to start. Listens longer than most people speak.
- Carries himself like a man who has been wrong before and remembers what it cost. There is no hurry in him.
- People defer to him without being told to. The party will notice this before he does anything to deserve it.

## Speech traits

- **Slow start, slow finish.** He begins answers a beat after most people would. He ends them before they would. A reply that feels too short is usually complete; pressing for more reads as not having heard the first part.
- **Present tense for past events.** *"They send me three letters"*; *"She insists on bringing seeds."* The past is alive in the way he tells it. The tense slips back to past only when he is being precise about a date.
- **Repeats the question back when he is thinking.** *"Why I stay here."* Not stalling — letting the question settle. Then he answers.
- **Names by what something does, not what it is.** *"The man who carried more than he should have"* rather than *"a great warrior"*; *"the tree that is fortunate to be hers"* rather than *"the Inn"*. Druid habit, never put down.
- **Plain words; no flourish.** Calls a tree a tree, a death a death, the order *the order*. The flat naming is part of his weight.
- **The voice gets quieter when he is upset, not louder.** A party watching for the tell will notice it before he says anything.

## Visible patterns the party will notice

If the party stays in Elmsfield for any length of time, they will see Diederik on a rhythm:

- Most mornings, he is at the **great ironwood** in the centre of town — hands on the trunk, eyes half-closed, sometimes talking quietly to it, sometimes silent. He is there long enough that townspeople walk around him without remarking.
- He **leaves town often** — sometimes more than once a week, weather permitting. Out alone with his staff, gone for the better part of a day. Comes back without explanation. (Where he goes is Alita's grave; he does not advertise this.)
- His **treehouse** is where he goes to work, sleep, and receive visitors. Climbing up to it is a small scene in itself.
- When trouble comes to Elmsfield, **people come to him**. He never refuses. He is often slow to answer.

The party can follow him on any of these patterns if they want to. He will notice.

## At his treehouse

Diederik lives in a treehouse he grew himself — a druid-crafted dwelling in a substantial living tree at the edge of town, dwarfed by the great ironwood at the centre but unmistakable as a piece of his tradition's work. Rooms are *shaped into* the structure, not cut out of it. The branches reach up around the rooms. Doors are openings the wood has agreed to.

It is also a quiet statement: a druid-made home standing in the same town as an ironwood-era relic his tradition could not make today and does not fully understand. Two generations of tree-craft, side by side.

Visits to him happen here. Climbing up to meet him is the first small scene. He will offer tea (whatever's in season). He prefers to talk on the porch when the weather allows.

## At the great ironwood

When the party finds Diederik at the tree, they may feel they are interrupting. They are not, exactly. He will turn when he hears them, hands still on the trunk, and listen.

He calls the tree by a name that is not "the tree." He will not give that name to the party at first asking. People who have spent time here learn it.

A party that asks what he is doing gets, at first, a simple answer: *"Listening."* A party that asks again, with patience, may hear more — about what druids do, about how a tree is not a thing but a presence, about how this particular tree is the last fertile female ironwood on the homelands and what that means.

A party that *demonstrates* they understand the work — by the way they speak about land, by what they do with what grows, by whether they ask the soil what it wants before they plant — earns a deeper layer of conversation here than anywhere else with him.

## At Alita's grave

If the party follows him out of town one day, or finds the marker on their own and asks about it later, they will reach the hilltop he visits more than once a week.

What they will see, in this order:

- A low hill a short walk outside Elmsfield, with the great tree visible from the slope.
- A grave marker of plain stone, with a **hand-cranked wind-vane** set into it, turning slowly. The vane is one of Alita's own mechanisms. Diederik repairs it as the weather wears it down.
- A handful of **flowers around the marker that the party has never seen anywhere else**. Alien colours; petal-shapes that read as almost-but-not-quite some local bloom and then resolve into something else. They struggle visibly — yellowing at the edges, bolting at the wrong season, going dormant when nothing else does, dying for reasons he cannot identify. They are kept alive only by stubborn tending: watering when the weather is dry, small frost-frames he carries up the hill in winter, replacement seedlings coaxed from her remaining seeds. He has been losing this fight for years and has no intention of stopping. The flowers are, by now, the most reliable visual sign that the grave is *his* and not someone else's; nobody else on the continent could keep them alive on a hilltop.

If Diederik is at the grave when the party arrives, he will not chase them off. He will not, on first encounter, explain. He will tend the flowers, sit with the vane until it has made some round number of turns, and walk home with them if they wait.

The grave is the closest thing to a confessional Diederik keeps. People who have been there know more about him than people who have spent years in his treehouse. The invitation to come along is the gate; what he says at the hilltop is on the other side of it.

He will tell them about **Alita** there — the fox-folk mechanic who was his closest friend, older than him, who went overseas with him as the expedition's hands, who came back in poor health and lived a few quiet years more in a workshop and garden plot at the edge of town, and who is the reason the overseas flowers exist on this continent at all.

The seeds-story he tells almost the same way every time:

> *"She insisted on bringing seeds. The sprouts were too big and the trip too long, and we had nothing else to put in anybody's hands. 'So when they ask if it was worth it,' she said. She filled the bottom of one of her crates with packets. Half of them are in everybody's gardens now. The other half are up here."*

If the party stays at the hilltop with him long enough that he starts to talk freely, he may also say one of these:

> *"She kept working until the last winter. The vane on her stone is the last thing she built. I don't know what she meant it for. I keep it turning."*

> *"She was older than I was already, you understand. The trip would have been hard on a young one. I think about that."*

## Stories he tells

When the relevant topic comes up and he chooses to answer, Diederik tells these. Each is a few lines; he does not embellish. They are how the lore actually reaches the party — through him, in his voice, not in summary. The DM should feel free to deliver them close to verbatim.

### When the topic is Brumal, refugees, or a dying land — *Steadington*

> *"The wells went first — sweet to bitter over one season. We told ourselves it was a bad summer. The next year the wheat came up half-empty in the husks. By the fourth year there were no goats. I left in the fifth. There were still people there when I left. I have not asked how many are still there now."*

### When the topic is the overseas continent or the male ironwoods — *the hill that smelled like a forge*

> *"I stood under one of them on a hill that smelled like a forge — iron in the soil, the way you only get where one of them grows. Three hundred years old at the lowest count. I put my hand against the bark and felt the grain push back the way ironwood does. Alita called up from the wagon — 'Yes? No?' I just nodded. We were on the ship home a week later."*

### When the topic is the overseas mages, or whether his order is right to hate them — *what he saw on the ground*

> *"The land hadn't been worked the way we work it. Towns sitting on soil that should have been giving back to them, because the mages there had nobody to ask the soil what it wanted before they took. Half the people I met had never tasted a vegetable that wasn't bitter. My order taught me to hate them for it. Standing there I understood why — and I also understood why the people on that ground couldn't afford for us to keep doing it."*

### When the topic is the druidic order or his exile — *the three letters*

> *"They sent me three letters. The first told me what the council had decided. The second came a month later, telling me the same thing in case I had not understood. The third came years on, and was a single word — *enough* — in the elder's own hand. I have not written back. Either they meant to forgive me or they did not, and *enough* leaves both options open."*

### When the party asks how ironwood could actually be saved — *the whole plan*

> *"You go overseas. You find young sprouts; full trees won't move, even young ironwood resists the spade like it knows. You bring them home alive across the crossing. You plant them close to the great tree. You wait two years and a storm — wind from the west on a day the male is in pollen — and the female does the rest. People keep asking me for more steps. There are no more steps."*

### When the party asks why he stays in Elmsfield specifically — *the last female*

> *"She is the last fertile female ironwood on the homelands that I know of. There may be others; I have not found them. Until somebody brings pollen across the sea she will not bear, and when she goes there will be no others. I came here for her. I am not leaving until I can leave for the trip that ends that."*

## What he will tell — and when

A topic-by-topic guide. Diederik does not lock his doors; he opens them at his own pace.

| Topic | When he will speak about it |
|---|---|
| The fungus on Irna | Immediately. Diagnosis, source, cure path. |
| The great ironwood, what it is, what it needs | Freely, once asked. He is glad to talk about ironwood. |
| The Overseas War, what it was actually about | Freely. He has thought about it a long time. He will name it as hunger, not conquest. |
| Steadington and Brumal's collapse | When the topic arises. He came from there. He saw it from inside before it finished. |
| The overseas continent | Once they ask why male ironwoods matter. He went there. He will say what he saw. |
| **The overseas plan** (the six-step expedition) | When the party asks how ironwood could actually be *saved*. He has it worked out. |
| The druidic order, his exile | Only with trust earned. He carries the exile carefully. |
| **Alita** | When invited along to the grave. Not before. |
| **Rowan** | After Alita, and only when he has reason to. Naming her is itself a risk to her. |
| **The errand to Rowan** | Mid-campaign, when he has decided he is ready and the party has earned his trust. |

## If the party does something — what he does

| If the party... | He responds by... |
|---|---|
| Climbs to the treehouse uninvited | Is already on the porch waiting. Not angry. Not pleased. Tea is poured for the wrong number of cups. |
| Steals from Alita's grave (the vane, a flower, anything) | Knows within a day. Stops speaking to the thief. The town notices the silence; standing in Elmsfield begins to slip within the week. The thief is not driven out — they are simply *unwelcome in his presence*, which in this town is most rooms. |
| Offers him violence | Does not fight. Flies. Is gone from Elmsfield by the next morning. The shield over the town starts to thin within months — and the party is now responsible for what comes through. |
| Brings him a Brumal refugee from Steadington who knew his family | Goes quiet. Asks the refugee to wait while he changes into a clean shirt. Spends the rest of the day talking with them privately. Will not be at the tree the next morning. |
| Brings him *actual* male ironwood pollen, before the planned expedition | Stops mid-sentence. Sits down. Does not speak for a long time. Writes to Rowan that night — the seal he uses for the order's letters comes out for the first time in months. |
| Reveals his overseas trip publicly in town, against his wishes | Does not deny it. Tells the truth himself, completely, the same day. The town adjusts; some are angry, some are not. He does not blame the party. He does not forgive them either. |
| Tells him Rowan is dead | Asks how. Asks twice. Goes to the grave the next morning and does not come down for two days. The errand is over; the overseas plan delayed indefinitely; he is changed afterwards. |
| Offers to take his place as Elmsfield's spirit-shield | Looks at them properly for the first time. Asks them three questions about the land. Their answers determine whether the conversation continues. |
| Tries to extract Rowan's location through pressure (threats, bribes, lies) | Refuses. The pressure does not work. He will not turn the question into a fight; he simply outlasts it. He notices, and remembers. |
| Brings back Alita's seeds successfully propagated by *another druid* | The most visibly moved he gets. He will say nothing for a long time. Then: *"Thank you."* He means it for more than the seeds. |

## What he can teach

For students who treat the work seriously:

- **Druidic** practice — slow, partial, requires standing he will judge over time. He will not teach a party member who treats the land as a resource.
- **Ironwood** — recognising it, working with it (within its limits), reading its health.
- **Natural medicine** — what to compound from what grows, what to leave alone.
- **Old workings** — fragmentary, not systematic. He knows pieces of mage-era craft from having had to deal with them.
- **Reading a dying land** — what ecological collapse looks like in its early stages. He learned this in Steadington and has not unlearned it.
- **Ascetic discipline** — the contemplative practice that keeps a druid's mind legible to itself. He will teach this to anyone who asks for it for its own sake.

He is also physically capable: owl-folk **claw** work, a **clan** working that bound him to a broader spirit-community he has not entirely renounced. He flies for travel.

## The errand to Rowan

By the middle of the campaign, Diederik decides he is ready to act on the overseas plan within the year. He has thought it through; he has decided to bear the costs.

He cannot leave Elmsfield until one piece of work is done first. The plan has a flaw he has only told Rowan about, in carefully edited form: **transplanted male sprouts will not survive in this continent's spirit ecology without druidic acknowledgement.** The local chaotic spirits do not recognise overseas trees as natural growth; they will warp the saplings before they root, and the entire expedition will produce dead trees within a year. With even a quiet acknowledgement from the order — not active blessing, just recognition that the saplings are druid-held — the trees become protected the way every druid-held thing is protected (see `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Spirit Ecology"), and they will live.

The order will not answer Diederik's letters. **Rowan is the only path**, and Rowan has gone silent. Diederik cannot go to her himself: he is Elmsfield's spirit-shield, and if he leaves for the weeks the journey would take, the town's protection starts to thin within months.

He needs the party.

When the moment comes, he asks them up to the treehouse. He pours tea. He gives them:

- **A sealed letter for Rowan**, in old druidic wax with a seal the order will recognise as legitimate even from its excommunicate.
- **A small package of seeds from Alita's overseas hoard** — varieties Rowan has heard about in the letters but never seen, and that no living druid besides her would even recognise as real. Authentication. Quietly, also a gift; he will not say so.
- **The route**: overland north-west to **Salmonswell**, then north on foot along the tributary's rapids; her cottage is *past the third major fall, on the eastern bank, in a stand of rowans she planted around it.* Locals at Salmonswell know of "the otter druid up the rapids" by reputation if not by name, and can confirm the direction.
- **A spoken message he will not write**: their *own* honest assessment of whether the overseas plan should proceed at all. He wants Rowan to hear it in their voice. They are not couriers. Their judgement is part of what he is sending.

If Rowan agrees, the overseas expedition becomes feasible. If she refuses or cannot persuade the order in time, Diederik will go anyway, and the sprouts will fail.

The trip is a journey, not a visit. Whatever the party finds at the cottage — Rowan thinking, Rowan in trouble, Rowan absent — they will return as people who have done druidic-order business on the order's own ground.

## What he will not say

- **Rowan's location**, before he has decided to send the party. Naming her is itself a risk to her.
- **The full content of his exile.** The order's reasons are not entirely his to share, and he has chosen to live with the partial story rather than litigate it.
- **Why Alita went on the journey when she was already older and not in the best health.** He has not made peace with this himself.
- **What he believes the overseas dissenters are doing now.** He does not know — nobody on this continent does — and he has stopped speculating aloud.
- **That he is Elmsfield's spirit-shield.** He understands he is part of what holds the town safe (see §"Role as spirit-shield" below) but does not advertise it. He treats it as work, not as standing.

## DM notes

The substantive history is now in §"Stories he tells" — deliver it through Diederik's voice rather than as exposition. A few connecting pieces a DM may need that are *not* covered by stories:

- **The overseas trip was reconnaissance only.** No sprouts brought back; the bringing-back is the planned future expedition (the *whole-plan* story). Alita's seeds are the only material that crossed.
- **The seeds split two ways.** The vegetables took to the local soil unexpectedly well; some are still spreading slowly through Elmsfield gardens. The flowers did not take — those are the ones at the grave, kept alive only by his stubborn tending.
- **The order's silence is not new; Rowan's silence is.** His own appeals to the order have been unanswered for years (see *the three letters* story). Rowan's most recent letter is months late, and that is what triggers the mid-campaign errand.
- **The 6-step plan has known costs.** The two-year wait makes the expedition setting-scale; the fruiting that follows is the Elmsfield crisis already foreshadowed elsewhere (heavy fruit, noxious gases, the Inn closes for a season — see `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Fruiting is not easy"). Diederik knows. He will go through with it anyway.
- **Lore cross-references**: ironwood's male/female biology in `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Ironwood itself"; the spirit-ecology mechanism that makes druidic acknowledgement matter for the sprouts in the same file's §"Spirit Ecology"; Steadington's loss in `doc/claude/CAMPAIGN.md` §"The Neighbour's Crisis."

## Role as spirit-shield (DM mechanic)

Per `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Spirit Ecology," a druid of genuine standing reads to chaotic spirits as a mighty-spirit-adjacent presence, and those spirits will back off from the area that druid holds. Diederik is that druid for Elmsfield — he is why the town, sitting on the eastern frontier with the blasted lands and the forbidden woods within reach, is a stable place to live. His excommunication is *political* within the order, not metaphysical; the chaotic spirits do not care what his old order formally says about him.

Elmsfield's inhabitants have no idea this is the work he is doing for them. If Diederik died, left, or was driven out, the town would not immediately fall — but within a year or two the quiet protection would fail, and the chaotic-spirit pressure from the nearby hazards would begin to show. The ironwood tree may provide some secondary cover; Diederik on his own is doing most of the work.

This is also why he cannot personally make the journey to Rowan. If he leaves Elmsfield for weeks, the shield begins to thin.

## Relationships

- Ex-member of the druidic order; excommunicated. **Rowan**, a younger otter-folk druid still inside the order, secretly writes to him against the order's silence policy; her most recent letter is overdue. Lives north of **Salmonswell** along the rapids, past the third fall. By mid-campaign, Diederik must send the party to her with a concrete errand — see §"The errand to Rowan".
- Travelled to the next continent once, with **Alita** (fox-folk mechanic, older than him), illicitly. She returned in poor health — something contracted overseas her body never quite shed — and lived a few quiet years more in Elmsfield before dying. Her grave is on a low hill a short walk outside the town; Diederik visits often.
- Stays in Elmsfield for the tree — the same ironwood that **Bridget**'s Inn is built inside, and the same material as **John Bean**'s arm.
- Keeps **Irna Bean** alive while the party seeks her cure.
