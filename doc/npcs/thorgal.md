# Thorgal

- **Role:** Overseas mage; led the faction that ordered the Overseas War
- **Species:** Human (overseas mage tradition)
- **Location:** Historical — killed in the Overseas War, on this continent
- **Status:** Deceased

## Summary

Thorgal was the leader of the overseas mages at the time of the Overseas War. He is the one who ordered the invasion of the homelands — not as a conquest, but as a mass demi-human emigration under arms: the overseas continent was starving, there was a surplus of demi-humans it could not feed, and Thorgal's solution was to ship them here as soldiers to bring back food.

He was not unopposed. Other mages in his circle disagreed with the decision, but none of them had a workable alternative. The overseas lands were never properly cultivated — the overseas mage tradition never had druidic partnership, and the druids who know about them hate them — and Thorgal's faction could point to the hunger and demand an answer. No one had one that did not involve someone else feeding them.

He *did not* come to the homelands with the invasion force. He ran the operation remotely, from overseas, through a **scrying mirror** that connected him to his forward commander **Raul** (the ox-tauren leader who captured Hannes). The mirror was a spell-conduit, built to carry workings both ways. It was through that mirror that he was killed. When his mind reached through the mirror into **John Bean**'s head — to study a homelander the forward band had encountered — it encountered not the ordinary thought-space it expected but a **phantom arm**, a mental projection of the limb John had recently lost but had forgotten he had lost. That phantom was spell-shaped enough to travel the mirror the other way. It recoiled from the probe; John, still thinking he had both arms, reached back along the connection with that phantom and **pulled**. The pull carried through the mirror. Something fell in Thorgal's overseas chambers, among the shards of the mirror on his end. He died there, not here. The war ended in that moment because the command and control of the whole invasion had been inside his head.

(John's **ironwood prosthetic** came after this — Rhianna fitted it later. The killing blow was a phantom, not an ironwood fist. The prosthetic amplified a latent telekinetic capacity that had, in the moment, briefly expressed itself through pure mental projection.)

## The mirror — still out there, still haunted

The mirror on the **homeland side** — the one in Raul's forward camp — did not simply break in the way a glass shatters. It went dark when Thorgal died; Raul's forces, cut off from command, fell back in confusion and did not carry it with them. What happened to it after the retreat is imprecise: nobody tracked it through the war's end, nobody catalogued it. **It is still out there, somewhere in a forest**, and it is **haunted by Thorgal's presence**.

Not Thorgal as a living mind with plans — that was pulled through the glass and killed on the other continent — but a residue. A lingering awareness. The kind of trace the LORE.md §"Mage ghosts" material describes: a dead mage's connection to an object dense enough with his working that something of him stays anyway. The mirror remembers Thorgal. When conditions are right, the mirror watches back.

**The location is the hills between the Great Swamp and the kingdom of Allondo** — where Raul's forward war-band had settled, well off any patrolled road, in terrain neither kingdom crosses often. The band fell back from there after the mirror went dark; the mirror was among what they did not carry out, and it has remained where it was dropped.

### Who knows the location

Five living people know where the war-band's settlement was. Most will not volunteer it; one will push it into the party's hands actively.

- **Rhianna Linthrope** knows. She has no direct reason to send anyone there — it is a closed chapter from her perspective, and she does not want the kingdoms pulling the residue of a dead overseas mage back open. A party that asks pointed, informed questions (having already learned the mirror exists from another source) can get it out of her; a party that asks vaguely will not.
- **Felix** knows. He was there. He will mention it if the conversation goes there — Felix does not hide information he does not see a reason to protect — but he will not bring it up unprompted.
- **King Hannes** knows. He was held there. He **will not speak of it**: doing so means admitting the full shape of his captivity, which he has never been able to say aloud. A party that somehow gets him to confirm the location has separately gotten the king of Allondo to say the thing he has spent years not saying.
- **John Bean should know but does not.** He was present at close range for the war's end, but his attention in those days was task-focused — the phantom-mirror killing, Rhianna fitting his arm afterwards, the processing of what he had done — not geographical. He never catalogued where the camp was. Pressed, he will say honestly that he cannot say; he is not hiding it.
- **Raul** knows, and **will actively direct the party to the location** if the party crosses paths with him. He wants the mirror delivered to him — it is his connection back to the overseas mage-tradition, and its residue is his former commander. For Raul the mirror is unfinished business. If the campaign brings Raul and the party into the same room, he is the breadcrumb that pushes rather than the one that holds.

The DM picks how and when the knowledge crosses to the party.

### The chain, not the direct ask

In practice, the party does not walk up to Rhianna and say *where's the mirror?* — they have no reason to know to ask. They reach the mirror by **asking NPCs their war stories and following each story to the next person it names**. A conversation with Diederik or any Elmsfield veteran about the Overseas War will surface Felix, John, or Rhianna as figures worth finding. Felix (best found at a local fair; see `doc/npcs/felix.md` §"Where to find him") tells the story that leads to Rhianna. Rhianna, asked pointed informed questions about what Felix referenced, can confirm the location. John fills in the shape of the war's end but not the place. Hannes confirms nothing he is not pressed to. Raul, if the campaign brings him in, short-circuits the chain with a direct pointer.

The discovery is a **chain of stories**, each leading to the next teller. That pattern is intentional — it rewards a party willing to listen to veterans rather than interrogate them, and it keeps the mirror behind the right kind of work.

### Campaign implications of the mirror

- **A communication channel.** If anyone reactivates the mirror — or even gets the haunting to respond to them — it is the closest thing to a direct line to an overseas mage that exists on this continent. Thorgal-the-residue can be *talked to*, if carefully, and what he can tell the party about the overseas continent, about the dissenters, about Raul, about the war's real motives, is information no other source on this side has.
- **A closure for John Bean.** If John ever meets the residue of the man he killed, he learns — perhaps for the first time — that he killed someone trying to feed his people. That is a weight John has so far been protected from carrying. The mirror could deliver it.
- **A danger.** Thorgal's probe killed its target the last time it reached into a mind through glass. His residue is weaker, but a party that approaches the mirror without care may still be probed in turn. Whether the residue *wants* to harm them, or simply does what its old shape still does by habit, is open.
- **A bridge to the DM-fill overseas content.** If the campaign ever needs to render the overseas continent legible, the mirror is the in-world mechanism through which knowledge about it could start crossing the sea again — one careful conversation at a time.

### The bond (major campaign opportunity)

The mirror is **heavy**. Carrying it is a commitment — not impossible, but it takes real effort and planning. A party that chooses to bring it with them has decided the mirror is worth the load.

For an **aspiring mage** (party member or trusted NPC), carrying the mirror opens an unusual possibility: **a bond with Thorgal's residue**, similar in form to the spirit-bindings the mage tradition uses for initiation (see LORE.md §"Mage Initiation") but with a dead mage's lingering awareness instead of a free elemental spirit. The bond is **reciprocal**, not extractive:

- **What the aspiring mage gains:** Thorgal's magical knowledge. That is *overseas-tradition* mage-craft — a body of practice that developed under different conditions (fewer chaotic spirits, unchecked power, no druidic partnership) and has shapes and techniques the homeland tradition does not have. It is a rare and valuable inheritance, the closest any mage on this continent could come to overseas schooling without crossing the sea.
- **What Thorgal's residue gains:** the chance to **see the world he had longed to see** and never did. He commanded the invasion from his chambers overseas, through a glass. He died before setting foot on this continent. His residue, bonded to a carried mirror, finally *gets* the journey he was killed for: mountains, forests, rivers, towns, the people he had only ever known as targets. The mage carries him. He watches.

Over time, as the residue accompanies the mage through the world, **Thorgal will come to an awareness of his own follies**. The war he ordered cost lives on both sides; the reason he ordered it (famine) is real, but the method (sending demi-humans to die for food) was a failure he had not reckoned with while alive. His residue *will*, given enough of the world to see, settle into a calmer and more rueful presence. Early in the bond he is hostile, confused, possibly still dangerous. Late in the bond he is a quiet scholar in the mage's head, pointing out what he finally understands.

This is a **major late-campaign arc** available to a party willing to carry the heavy mirror and willing to let an aspiring mage in their number — or an aspiring mage they befriend — enter a bond with a dead overseas commander. The reward is genuinely rare magical knowledge. The cost is the duration of the bond's rough early months and the weight of the glass itself.

## Relationships

- Killed by **John Bean** (inadvertently, at a distance, through the scrying mirror that connected him to his forward commander; see `john_bean.md`).
- Commanded **Raul** (his ox-tauren forward commander) by scrying mirror. Raul survived.
- Led an overseas mage faction that was itself **not unanimous** — other mages opposed the war but could not offer an alternative. Those dissenters may still be alive.
- Ideological successor, several generations removed, to the old-order mages who crashed **Laurent**'s flying city (see `laurent.md`). Similar in flavour, not the same organisation.

## Open questions

- Did Thorgal know about the chaos-spirit pressure building in Fata morgana's region, or was his invasion entirely about food?
- Did he understand Laurent's situation (demi-lich in a crashed flying city), and did he have a position on the air elemental trapped at the Lake of Tears?

## Overseas politics after Thorgal — DM-fill

The overseas continent's current political structure is **deliberately not specified** in this setting. No one on this continent knows who succeeded Thorgal, how the dissenters fared, or what the current leadership looks like. That ignorance is realistic — the continent went quiet after the mirror broke, and there is no diplomatic channel across the sea to find out through — and it is also a DM convenience. When a campaign actually moves toward the overseas side, whichever DM is running it fills in the political landscape that makes the arc work. Until then, "who runs the overseas mages now" is intentionally blank.
