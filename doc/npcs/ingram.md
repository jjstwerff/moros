# Ingram

- **Role:** Very old Male Mouse; long-time keeper of the Salmonswell orphanage that sheltered Hanny and Egbert after the war. The man who held Brother Darrel's wages untouched for years and returned them to the pair when they came of age.
- **Species:** Mouse folk. Small, silver-furred, hands that shake a little now but were once steady. Bright eyes that miss less than they seem to.
- **Lives:** The orphanage, in the refugee quarters on the valley side of Salmonswell (see `../places/salmonswell.md`). He has not travelled in a decade.
- **First met:** When the party reaches Salmonswell carrying Hank's money and Hank's ask. Also reachable separately if the campaign goes that way first.

## What the party meets

The orphanage sits on one of the valley slopes above the wharf — a building that used to be a fisher-family's home, expanded twice, now holding more beds than its original rooms. The party finds Ingram at a small desk in the front room, working through a pile of mending or a ledger of who is sleeping where this week. He looks up, takes them in, and does not stand. *"Tell me what you've come for. Sit if you need to."*

He listens before he speaks. He speaks carefully, with the habit of an old man who has spent decades being the only adult in a room full of children. His answers are short. He is not withholding; he is saving his voice.

## How he reads at a glance

- **Small, silver-furred, stooped** — old even for mouse-folk, and mouse-folk do not usually make this age. His whiskers have gone white at the tips. Hands tremble slightly at rest; steady when he is writing.
- **An old woollen waistcoat**, patched carefully at the elbows. The orphanage children do the mending now, and the stitches show which child did which patch.
- **A fine thin chain** around his neck with a small key on it — the key to the chest where the orphanage keeps its money and its important papers.
- **Eyes on everyone who enters his room.** He does not rise; he sees.
- **A cup of weak tea at his elbow, always.** The children refill it.

## Visible patterns

- **Front desk in the morning**, ledger work. He tracks every child who has ever come through the door, by date of arrival and date of leaving, in a hand that has gone spidery.
- **In the kitchen at midday**, sitting while the older children cook. He eats with them.
- **A round of the dormitories at dusk** — slow, cane-assisted, every bed checked, a word or two for each child. This ends when the last lamp is out.
- **He does not go down to the wharf any more.** It has been three years, by his own count. The children run errands for him.

## The orphanage — what it is

A post-war refuge that took children who could not be placed — war-orphans from both sides, a few from the Upwelling fishing families who had lost parents to the river, now a growing number of Brumal refugees. Salmonswell's council funds it modestly; the fisher-families send what they can; Ingram runs it on very little.

Its scale is smaller than Hank's farm in Elmsfield, but the logic is the same — a door that does not close. A party that has been to Hank's will recognise the shape of this place and may comment on it. Ingram, told, will not be surprised. *"Hanny was always going to build one of her own."*

## The story of Darrel's coin

This is the core of what Ingram carries, and the reason the party has come to him.

- **After the war, a Beaver-folk sergeant came to the door** with a rabbit-folk girl-child — **Hanny**, his own side — and a wolf-folk girl-child he had brought along from the other side and whose name he would not take.
- **He brought money.** A leather purse of his wartime wages — a substantial sum. He gave it to Ingram and asked him to shelter the children. Ingram asked what he was to tell the children if they asked where the money had come from. The sergeant said *"From a relative. Do not say it was mine. Do not give them my name."* Ingram agreed.
- **Ingram did not use the money.** He had a running orphanage, modestly funded; the sergeant's bag sat in the chest under his desk, untouched. He could have spent it many times and did not. He kept it for the children.
- **When Hanny and the wolf-folk girl came of age and left for the road** (they had by then chosen road-names of their own — Hank and Egbert), Ingram called them into his office, opened the chest, and gave them the bag. *"This was set aside for you by someone who asked not to be named. Take it for what comes next."* He described the man carefully — **stocky beaver-folk, heavier in the shoulder than most, a sergeant's carriage, hands with scars across the knuckles** — so they would know him if they saw him. He did **not** give the name. He had been asked not to, and he had the habit of keeping what had been asked of him.
- **They took the money** and used it to start the farm at Elmsfield.
- **Hank has been saving to return it** ever since — coin by coin, year by year, replacing what she spent. When she had enough, she wanted the bag put into the benefactor's hand. She has written to Ingram a handful of times asking if he had heard where the man was. Ingram has written back in pieces — *"I have heard a rumour of a monastery"* — without confirming the name. The name has been his to hold, under the sergeant's original asking, until Hank's own decision to return the money releases him from it.

## What he will tell — and when

| Topic | When he'll speak |
|---|---|
| That the orphanage sheltered Hanny and Egbert | Immediately, if asked by name. |
| That the money came from a Beaver-folk sergeant, not a relative | Carefully, to the party — only once Hank's sealed letter is on the desk in front of him. That letter is the release he has waited for. |
| **The sergeant's name — *Darrel*** | **Only to party carrying Hank's sealed letter.** He has held the name at Darrel's request since the day the bag was left. Hank's decision to return the money is what finally allows him to speak it. He has never spoken it to Hank by letter. |
| The sergeant's description in detail | Freely. He remembers the man with unusual clarity; he kept the picture for exactly this eventuality, and gave it to Hanny and Egbert at their coming of age. |
| That he has heard the sergeant may be at Luchebert Monastery | Carefully. A traveller told him once; he has not confirmed. He will send the party onward with the name of the monastery, not a certainty. |
| What he thinks Darrel will do when offered the money | Honest guess. *"He will not take it. He did not want it when he gave it; he will not want it now. You will have to think about what to do when he refuses."* |
| How many children have passed through | Freely. He will show the ledger if asked. |
| The orphanage's current needs | Once the conversation has settled. He does not lead with it. |
| His own age and how much longer he expects to be here | Only with gentle asking. He is clear-eyed about it. |

## Stories he tells

### When asked about the day Darrel came — *the sergeant and the two children*

> *"He came up the road from Linar, carrying one child and walking beside the other. He had not slept in days. He did not say what he had seen; he did not need to. He put the bag on my desk and he asked me to shelter them. He said he had put a weapon down and would not pick it up again, and he did not want his name attached to their lives. I took the bag. I kept it in the chest. I have always thought he would come back for the children or the money or both, and I have always been wrong."*

### When asked about the day Hanny and Egbert left — *the chest opened*

> *"They were of age and the road was open and they had decided on the farm together. I called them in and opened the chest and I gave them the bag. I told them what I had been allowed to tell them — that it had been set aside for them by someone who asked not to be named. I described him as carefully as I could, because I thought if the road gave them back to him, they should be able to know the face. I did not give his name. He had asked me not to. Hanny took the description to heart. Egbert took the bag. They left the next morning."*

### When asked why he kept the money untouched — *the quiet accounting*

> *"The orphanage runs on what the wharf sends us. The bag was not ours to spend. The sergeant gave it to shelter these two specifically. When they were sheltered and grown, the bag was theirs. I do not think I did anything clever. I think I did not make a mistake, which is what keeps this house standing."*

### When asked about Hank's letters — *the years of saving*

> *"She writes once or twice a year, in a plain hand that does not say much. She tells me the weather and the children she has taken in. Once she wrote that she had saved back half. Once she wrote that she had saved back all of it. The last letter asked if I had heard where the sergeant was. I wrote back that I had heard a rumour of a monastery. I have not heard more. If you have come with her money, she is a serious woman, and you are carrying something she has worked for for ten years."*

## Mannerisms

- **When pressed** — sets his pen down. Looks up. Waits. The room becomes his room. He will wait a very long time in silence. When he answers, the answer is shorter than the asker expected.
- **When distraught** — his hands become still. The normal tremor stops. A party who knows him sees this as the tell.
- **When happy** — a short dry laugh that sounds like a cough. A child will usually be invited to his lap; the old habit. He pats them once and lets them go.

## If the party does something — what he does

| If the party... | He responds by... |
|---|---|
| Arrives with Hank's money and Hank's sealed letter | Opens the letter. Reads it slowly. Sits with it a long moment. Then — for the first time in decades — speaks the sergeant's name aloud: **Darrel**. Gives them the full description again for confirmation, names the monastery where he believes the man is (Luchebert), and tells them plainly *"He will refuse the bag. Come back to me with what he says."* |
| Arrives with Hank's money but *no* letter | Does not name the sergeant. Will tell them to go back to Hank for a written request, or to write one there at his desk that he can countersign. The name has been held under a request; he will not release it on a spoken claim. |
| Arrives *without* the money and asks about the orphanage | Welcoming. Will talk about his work. Will not volunteer the Darrel story unless Hank's name is raised or the party demonstrates they already know a piece. |
| Offers him coin for the orphanage | Accepted gracefully for the house, declined if pressed as personal charity. The orphanage has real needs; he is not proud. |
| Offers to carry a letter from him to Hank | Accepted gratefully. He writes it there and then. Take care of it; it matters to him. |
| Brings him news of the wider world he has not heard | He will sit with them for an hour. The wharf does not always bring him what he wants to hear. |
| Is rough with the children in his house | One warning, quiet. If repeated, the older children close around and the party is asked to leave. Salmonswell will hear about it. |
| Asks him if Hank's choice is the right one | *"It is hers. I kept the bag; she kept the intention. Both of us were only ever waiting for a way to put it down. If the sergeant will not take it, what you do next is yours, and hers."* |
| Asks what he would do if Darrel refuses | *"I would leave it with the monastery, marked for its sheltered. The sergeant might let that stand where he would not let his own bag stand. But that is my instinct, not his."* |

## What he can teach

- **Letters and ledger work** — in the manner of a career keeper of written records. Patient; exact. He will teach any child who stays long enough, and any adult who asks in a way he respects.
- **The welcome-three-questions** — the practical craft of receiving a new arrival. Hank learned it here; Ingram still uses it on every newcomer. He will pass it on to a party that wants to build something similar elsewhere.

## What he will not say

- **The running names of children who have passed through** beyond the orphanage ledger itself. The ledger is not secret; he will show it. The individual histories are the children's.
- **What he thinks of Father Elliot.** They have not met. Ingram considers it wrong to have opinions of a man he has not met.
- **Whose descendants fund the orphanage privately, beyond the wharf's public contributions.** A merchant-house at Salmonswell has quietly underwritten the shortfall for twenty years; Ingram keeps their name.

## DM notes

- **Ingram is the confirmation stop before the monastery, and he is the one holding the name.** Hank has the description and the resolve; Ingram has the witness *and* the name Darrel asked him to keep. Hank does not know the name. The party arrives carrying Hank's sealed letter — the release Ingram has been waiting for. What he gives them: (1) the name, spoken aloud for the first time in decades — **Darrel**; (2) confirmation of the description; (3) the pointer to Luchebert; (4) his prediction — *he will refuse it* — so the monastery scene is a test, not a surprise.
- **Without Hank's letter, Ingram does not name him.** A party who arrives with the coin but without Hank's words will be sent back, or asked to write a request Ingram can countersign. The name has been held under a request; Ingram will not release it on a spoken claim alone.
- **Ingram is old and does not have many years left.** The DM can use this quietly. If the party returns later in the campaign, he may be gone, the orphanage may have a new keeper, and what Hank kept between them — the letters, the bag's history, the decade of plan — survives only in Hank's hand and in the orphanage ledger Ingram leaves behind. Do not *force* this, but let it sit as a fact of his age.
- **The orphanage parallels Hank's farm.** It is the other end of the same impulse. A party who has been to both places can feel Hank learned her welcome here. Ingram is the closest thing Hank has to a founder-figure who is not Darrel.
- **The merchant-house underwriting line is a DM hook.** If the campaign wants a Salmonswell merchant-house to have a *specific* relationship to the orphanage, this is the thread. Ingram's silence is genuine.
- **If Ingram dies during the campaign**, Hank will want to go. A party who can take her to Salmonswell for a funeral walk is giving her something she cannot give herself.

## Relationships

- **Hanny (Hank)** — the rabbit-folk child he sheltered. They have corresponded by letter for a decade.
- **Egbert** — the wolf-folk child he sheltered. Hanny is the letter-writer; Egbert rarely writes, but reads what Hank reads.
- **Brother Darrel** — the beaver-folk sergeant he has not seen since the day the money was left. He has always assumed Darrel would come back. He has been wrong.
- **The Salmonswell council** — funds the orphanage modestly; he attends sessions when the funding is discussed, not otherwise.
