# Andras of the Skull Gatherers

- **Role:** A named Skull Gatherer; the party's earned entry point into the order. Currently tracking a relic stolen from the city of the dead. Eventually carries the order's offer of a resting place for Laurent.
- **Species:** Human in lineage; presents in the order's typical too-thin, proportions-slightly-wrong way — the cumulative effect of long work near the bound spirits in the necropolis.
- **Lives:** No fixed home. A circuit between the **city of the dead** (the order's **necropolis**, **west of the blasted lands at the edge of the desert, slightly further south than Brumal**) and wherever the work calls him. The current arc of his circuit is the relic's trail, which has led him north into Brumal.
- **First met:** **Visually first**, on the party's arrival at Scarlet Vale — Andras is one of the gatherers in the canonical *Skull Gatherers leaving the city* scene (see `doc/places/scarlet_vale.md` and `skull_gatherers.md`). Casual approach there is ignored, per the order's habit; he registers the party silently and walks on with the group. The **real meeting** happens later — at a campfire on a road through Brumal hill country, or at the cemetery's gate when the party has arranged to meet him there. A party that earns the encounter via the four routes in `skull_gatherers.md` finds him; a party that doesn't, does not. **He is not stationary at the crypt** — see §"The crypt — the dungeon".

## What the party meets

A tall thin man in a plain dark robe, sitting at the edge of a graveyard or across a small fire from the road. He stands when the party comes within reach of his voice and says his name plainly — *"Andras."* — and then waits. His questions arrive in order: who are you, what brings you to this stretch of road, who sent you to me. He does not ask sharply. He asks because the answers determine what he says next. If the party has earned the meeting (offering, address, vouch, or having met the order on its terms before), he goes to the second part of the conversation: what he is doing here, and what the party may be able to do that he cannot.

He carries a leather case at his hip with the buckle worn smooth, a long staff of plain wood, and nothing else visible.

## How he reads at a glance

- **Tall and thin in the order's manner.** The thinness is not frailty; he carries strength his frame should not. Hands larger than they should be on those wrists. The robe is plain, undyed, neat.
- **Voice level to flat.** No rise, no fall. The cadence is the same when he is asking who the party is and when he is naming what he wants.
- **Eyes that hold longer than is comfortable.** He does not stare; he reads. The reading takes a few seconds longer than mammal-conversation expects. People learn to wait through it.
- **He does not eat or drink at a campfire he has been invited to.** He sits, listens, leaves when the conversation is done. He does not refuse offered food rudely; he simply does not take it.
- **The leather case at his hip never leaves him.** Whatever is in it is part of his work. He does not open it in front of strangers.

## Visible patterns

- **He was just at Scarlet Vale.** Visible to the party in the *Skull Gatherers leaving the city* scene on their first arrival. The group split on the road outside the wall — most of the gatherers continued west toward the necropolis; Andras turned north when the trail did. A party that paid attention to who walked out of Scarlet Vale has the start of a vouch when they meet him later.
- **He stops at every recently-visited grave.** Reads the dust, the disturbed earth, the prints. Reads what the family left and what they took away.
- **He skirts villages.** Passes outside the wall, takes the long path around. He does not enjoy the silence he produces in a market.
- **He waits at thresholds, when invited.** A graveyard's gate, a crypt's door, the edge of a wood. He is patient where he is not invited; he is also not always there. The order's circuit moves him.
- **He travels alone on Brumal ground.** The order works in singles and pairs when the routes diverge; in Allondo he travelled with the group the party first sees in Scarlet Vale, and the pair he was closest to continued west when his trail turned north.

## The relic — what was taken

A **golden crown set with jewels that light up in the dark.** The gold is plain in daylight; in shadow the jewels glow with a steady, slightly cold light — pre-rift working still working, which it should not still be doing. Set into the inside of the band, where a wearer's brow would have been, are inscriptions in a script that has not been read on this continent in centuries. The crown is a **keeping-tool** in the order's own apparatus — not regalia of state but a piece the gatherers use to hold the spirit of the **war-mage who died wearing it**. Inside the necropolis's hold, the glow is nearly imperceptible. Outside it, in the dark of a closed crypt, the crown lights the room.

It was stolen by **Eric**, a **badger of Brumal**. Not a greedy man; a desperate one. Brumal is collapsing — Steadington gone to the desert, the southern villages thinning, the crown beginning to **conscript** for the war it expects with Allondo. Eric has a family; he is the one who feeds them; he is also exactly the age the recruiters want, and unmarked enough to be taken. He decided, last winter, that he would not be taken. Hiding from the recruiters costs money — bribes, displacements, a name kept off a roll. A neighbour had heard rumours of the city of the dead, the gold and jewels the gatherers keep, the buyers said to be quietly seeking such things. Eric went south. He gained access to the necropolis by presenting himself as a relative of one of the named dead. **He took the gold he saw lighting up in the back of the keeping room** — a piece small enough to carry under a cloak, conspicuous enough to be worth a fence's price. He left.

He took it home — to his **own family's crypt** in Brumal, the one his line had used for generations — to **stash** it there until he could move it on to a fence further west. He thought the fitted stone, the sealed niches, and the old dark would dampen the working inside it. They did not. **In the dark of the closed crypt, the crown lit fully for the first time in centuries, and the war-mage held inside it — held still in the necropolis, unheld here — woke.** Eric died within hours. His family does not know.

## The crypt — the dungeon

A modest family mausoleum at street level in a Brumal hill town — DM-fill on which (Bockthicket and the small villages around Cliffside hold both fit). Stone, fitted, generations old at the surface; deeper than expected underneath, because long-settled badger families typically inherit a **lower crypt** below the upper room. Andras has tracked the trail north from the necropolis to the door, identified the family, and seen the shape of what is inside. He has not gone in: gatherer-ethics holds him at the threshold of a family grave he has not been invited into, and the door's wax seals are the family's, not his to break.

**Andras's availability is on-call, not continuous.** He will be at the threshold when the party asks him to meet them there. He will not always be there. While he waits to be needed, **he travels his circuit** — other recently-disturbed graves, the necropolis, mage-deaths the order has heard rumours of. If the party defers the quest, he moves on; he can be reached again via the merchant networks (Nadine's group will carry a message west on request), via other gatherers crossing his routes, or via the known stop-points in his circuit. The reenactment inside the crypt continues whether he is at the door or not. The dungeon waits for the party, not for him.

What he can tell the party at the threshold — when he is there — is the *shape* of what is happening inside.

### What the relic is doing

The bound mage-spirit anchored to the keeping-tool was, in life, a **war-mage** who died in an ancient war the kingdoms have forgotten the name of. The order remembers him; the order does not advertise him. Inside the necropolis his spirit was held still, the way the gatherers hold all of theirs. **Outside the necropolis, with the relic in his grip and the bones of an old family around him, he has begun to reenact the death he died.**

This is not a haunting and not, strictly, an attack. The mage-spirit is *reliving* the war — his side, the opposing side, the march, the engagement, the moment he fell. The bones in the crypt are the materials he has to work with. They do not fight the party out of malice; they fight because they have been pressed into being his old enemies and his old comrades. The party walks into someone else's last hour, replaying.

The reenactment runs on a loop: **muster → engagement → his death → reset.** A party reading the scene can feel which beat the dungeon is on — the air's timbre, the bones' postures, what light is bending where, what time of day appears to be in which room. The fitted stones themselves bend: the corridor walls suggest a hillside, a parapet, a keep-gate. Banners hang in colours no living person has seen in centuries.

### The three levels

**1. The vestibule — the muster (early in the loop).**

The upper crypt's main room. **Eric is here**, fallen, in his travel cloak. **The crown rests near him, glowing softly through the cloak's hem** — the only light in the room when the door closes behind the party. A small purse of coin at his belt. The first wave of the reenactment is in this room: a few bones — long-dead family members from the wall niches — are *stirring*, beginning to assemble. They wear faint suggestions of ghost-armour the family never owned. They are organising, not yet fighting.

A party arriving during the muster has the easiest entry. They can recover Eric's body and the relic before the reenactment is fully underway, *if* they move quickly and do not engage what is forming.

**They can also leave.** The vestibule is the dungeon's *assessable* level. A party that enters, takes the room's measure — Eric's body, the crown's glow, the bones beginning to stir, the air's pressure — and **retreats to gather reinforcements** loses nothing by doing so. The bones in the muster do not pursue an unengaged party out the door. Whatever the party brings back — a priest, Felicia's necklace, a shaman, additional muscle, the family elder who finally agreed to come, the message they sent west to find Andras — they bring back to a dungeon that is roughly where it was, give or take a loop. A party that hesitates *inside* and tries to stop the assembly without retreating, on the other hand, escalates the dungeon to engagement.

**2. The corridor of niches — the engagement (middle of the loop).**

A long stone corridor lined with sealed niches holding the family's older dead. The walls here are bending hardest: the corridor reads, half-visibly, as the **slope of an old hillside**. The light slants from impossible directions. Bones are *engaged* — formed into ranks, weapons drawn from nothing, fighting one another and any living thing in their path.

A party crossing the corridor during the engagement is in real combat. The reanimated bones are not weak; mage-war soldiers fight as mage-war soldiers — disciplined, in formation, defended by a *thinning ghost-armour* a careful eye can read. **The party is incidental to their fight; they are still struck if in the way.** Splitting the formation by reading its line and slipping through is a non-combat option a party with tactical insight can attempt. It is hard and it is real.

Retreat from the corridor is possible but costly: the engaged bones do not pursue beyond the corridor's mouth, but a party crossing back through the line takes whatever the line throws. A party that has begun an engagement here and decides it was a mistake withdraws bloodied; the order's standing is unchanged by the retreat itself, only by what the party does on their return.

**3. The lower crypt — the death (climax of the loop).**

A stone trapdoor or narrow stair leads down into the older, smaller chamber the family's earliest dead were laid in. The walls here remember a **keep's inner room**. The light is the dusk-blue of a battle's last hour. Fewer bones; more weight.

This is where **the mage-spirit's death** is happening, on loop. He is here as a presence, not a body — a focal point near the chamber's centre, a flicker that briefly resolves into **a robed figure with the crown on his head, mid-working**, before the working fails and he falls. The bones in this chamber are his immediate guard, his last companions, and the enemy who reached him. They are slower than the corridor's combatants and more dangerous. **Each loop he dies again. Each loop the bones reset.** A party that watches one cycle through, without interfering, learns the war's last moment.

The crown is being *pulled* toward this chamber across the loops, slowly — its glow strengthening as it travels, brightest in the death chamber. If the party does not recover it from the vestibule promptly, it will eventually be down here when they reach it, and **its light will be most of what the party reads the death by**.

Retreat from the lower crypt is the most expensive option. The death-loop is most active; the spirit is most present; the climb back up the stair is exposed. A party that descends and decides to leave pays in damage on the way out. They can still come back. The order does not penalise an honest retreat; the order penalises **abandonment** — a party that retreats and never returns, leaving the working active in a populated town, becomes the order's question to answer, and Andras will carry that question on their behalf.

### Handling the crown

Andras coaches the party at the threshold before they go in. The coaching is short and specific:

- **Wrap it in undyed linen before lifting.** The linen dims the glow once the crown is fully covered, and the working in the lower crypt will *feel* the dimming the moment it happens.
- **Hold it by the gold band, not the jewels.** The jewels are where the working is concentrated.
- **Do not let bare skin touch the inside of the band**, where the inscriptions are.
- **Do not place it on the head of any living thing under any circumstance.** A party member who tries the crown on, jokingly or curiously, becomes the spirit's next vessel for the loop. The recovery from this is not within Andras's gift.
- **Do not open or unwrap it once wrapped.** Carry it covered until it is in his leather case at the threshold.

He gives them the cloth.

### Four resolution paths

The dungeon does not insist on one approach. Four are legitimate; each costs something different.

- **Smash through.** Fight the bones at every level. Recover the relic from wherever it is. Wrap it per Andras's coaching and bring it out. The fastest path; it leaves the family's ancestors in worse shape than the family knows, several reanimations may not lay back down clean, and Andras notes how much was broken to do what was done. Standing earned, but reduced.
- **Slip past.** Read the reenactment's tempo. Move through during the muster; cross the corridor during a *line-shift* in the engagement; descend during the *reset*. Recover the relic and leave. Requires patience and tactical reading; rewards parties with shamanic, druidic, or scout-craft. The crypt closes behind them with the loop still running. The relic, removed, ends the reenactment soon after.
- **Talk the spirit down.** Reach the lower crypt during the death. Speak to the mage-spirit. Tell him *the war is over, you are dead, these are not your soldiers*. He may not believe a stranger; he is centuries past hearing. **Hints the party can find in the upper rooms** — banner colours, armour patterns, a phrase whispered in the bone-formations, a working-name worn into the corridor's wall — let them name his side, his commander, the moment of the engagement, or the cause he died for. Naming what only he should know reaches him. The bones lay back down; the relic comes free; the spirit is briefly at peace, and the party has been heard by something the order has been holding in silence for centuries.
- **Let the loop complete.** Stand back. Watch the reenactment run from muster to death once, completely, without intervening. Then collect the relic from the lower chamber and leave. The spirit, *witnessed* through to his own death by the living, is — by the strange grammar of these workings — released differently than the order keeps. **Andras will not approve of this**, but he will not refuse the relic. The mage-spirit, now uncollected and unkept, becomes a separate and quieter problem the order eventually carries. The party has done something the gatherers do not do, for reasons the gatherers will not endorse. Standing earned, with a question attached the order does not have an answer to.

### The other things to handle

In all four paths, the same follow-ups apply:

- **The family's ancestors.** Disturbed. Some lay back down clean once the relic is gone; some need a priest's working, a shaman's register, or a credible druid's settling. **Felicia's necklace** is the cleanest tool. The family will want to know which of their dead are still themselves.
- **Eric.** His body, his coin, the truth of how he died — brought to a family that does not yet know. The coin he never spent is what he died trying to send home. Pocketed by the party, it lands as something the order will eventually hear about. Returned to the family with the truth, it lands as more than coin.
- **Get the family's permission first, before any of this.** Eric's surviving family is in the same town, and they do not know he is dead. The party arriving with Andras at the door — and with the news — is the conversation. A party that breaks the seals without permission has alienated the family and undermined Andras at the same time, regardless of which dungeon path they then choose.

A party that runs this well — by any of the four paths, with the family handled honestly — hands Andras a real debt and earns the order's standing. A party that runs it badly — bones left half-animated, the family kept in the dark, Eric's coin lifted, the seals broken without consent, the spirit damaged in a way the order will have to repair — earns a partial standing that can be repaired or worsened later.

### Without Andras at the door: getting the crown to the necropolis

If Andras was at the threshold, the recovered crown goes into his leather case and the work is done. **If the party went in without him there** — because they didn't arrange the meeting before acting, or because he is on his circuit elsewhere when the moment came — the crown is now theirs to deliver. The order's standing on this thread is not earned at the crypt; it is earned at the necropolis.

The journey from Brumal hill country to the necropolis is a real one — south and west, around or through the desert's northern edge, into spirit-thinned country whose only city is the gatherers' own. The crown does not want to be carried. The party's role is to manage that.

- **The wrapping is the only barrier.** Linen over the gold, jewels covered, no skin to the inscriptions, never on a head. Without Andras's leather case, the linen is what stands between the working and the world. A party that opens the bundle to look — at a campfire, in a curious moment — discovers what Eric discovered, faster.
- **The crown leaves a wake.** Wherever the party camps, mage-spirits within reach orient toward it; a druid in a town they pass through notices; a shrine they sleep beside hums slightly wrong overnight. Most of this is harmless attention. The cumulative cost is *the party leaves a trail* across two kingdoms. Felicia's necklace dampens it; **Willow** or **Father Elliot** can teach the dampening if asked.
- **The war-mage knows where his crown is.** Even wrapped, his presence orients along the line between him and the carrier. He cannot animate without bones to work with, but **old graves on the road give him material**. Camping near a battlefield, a war-cemetery, or a buried garrison risks waking a smaller version of the reenactment overnight. **Ask Felix or Corné Dunham for the graveless paths.**
- **The buyers seeking spirit-bound artefacts.** The order's standing line is that the buyers have not approached the necropolis. A party openly carrying a glowing wrapped object across two kingdoms is a different proposition. Word travels. By the desert's edge there may be people on the road *for them*; they will not look like enemies. False bundles, alternate carriers, a decoy route — the countermeasures are the party's to invent.
- **Brumal in wartime.** Travelling south against the conscription's grain attracts questions. Papers, bribes, the long hill-country detours, **Princess Penelope**'s network if it has been reached, or **Nadine**'s merchant group all work; stealth is cheaper than fighting.
- **The desert's edge.** The last leg crosses chaos-thinned country. Nadine has routes; Felix knows paths a careful walker can use; **Wilder**'s stretch overlaps the western circuit and a vouch from him eases the crossing.
- **Approaching the necropolis without invitation.** The order does not welcome strangers, and the party is arriving carrying a stolen keeping-tool. Andras may have given them a **token at first meeting** — a small carved bone disc, his name marked in the order's hand — for exactly this case. If he didn't, **drop his name at the gate.** The gatekeeper-gatherer will fetch someone senior; the conversation will be the longest pause the party has experienced. The crown changes hands. The party is asked, plainly, what happened. The answers determine whether the order opens its doors any further.

**Alternatives to carrying it the whole way:** meet another gatherer on the road and hand off (gatherers are itinerant; an order-vouched courier is a plausible encounter near the desert's edge); send word via Nadine's merchant network asking Andras to meet them somewhere safer than the necropolis itself; cache the crown at a settled place with credible spirit-shielding (Luchebert, Rhianna's residence, Diederik's treehouse — bad ideas for the host but possible at cost) until Andras can be reached. Each has a price; none are free; all are open.

A party that carries the crown west successfully has earned a *different* standing with the order than a party who handed it off at the threshold — harder-won, watched across the road, the order having seen what they did with it and what the road did to them. **Andras meets them inside the gate, not outside.** The Laurent resting-place offer becomes available faster from this path than from the gentler one.

## Personal goals — early and later

- **Early-game — recover the crown from the crypt that became a battlefield.** A **golden crown set with jewels that light up in the dark** was stolen by **Eric**, a desperate Brumal badger; it killed him in his family's crypt; the war-mage spirit inside has begun **reanimating the family's bones in a reenactment of the war he died in**. Andras stands at the threshold and cannot enter. *Where this goes:* through the social work first (find Eric's surviving family, tell them, get the seal broken with consent); through the three levels of the crypt — vestibule muster, corridor engagement, lower-crypt death — and whichever of the **four resolution paths** the party chooses (smash, slip, talk down, or witness through; see §"The crypt — the dungeon"). A party that runs the work well moves the order from *open thread* to *active relationship*, and gains a usable lesson in spirit-handling no priest, mage, or shaman would teach the same way.
- **Later-game — a resting place for Laurent in the necropolis.** The order has watched the demi-lich in the wreck for centuries. Andras is authorised to extend the offer when the party has proved enough: enough understanding of what Laurent is, enough relationship with him to carry the offer back to him without misrepresenting it, enough trust with the order to be the ones to walk it. **The offer is not "we will collect you."** It is *"we will hold your station, in our city, with the others, when you choose to set it down."* Laurent's last flight ends, by this option, at the city of the dead — his skull at rest among the ones the order has been keeping for as long as he has been keeping his own. Andras will not extend this offer cold. The early-game work is the work that earns it.

## Stories he tells

When the relevant topic comes up.

### When asked what the city of the dead is — *what we hold*

> *"It is a city in the western edge, where the desert begins, slightly further south than Brumal. We have built it, generation by generation, around what we collect. The skulls of the mages we have brought in are kept there, in a pattern that lets each one rest where it can no longer find the family it once half-remembered. There are workings in the stone of the place that keep the pattern stable. There are gatherers who do nothing but maintain the workings. The city does not look like other cities. It is quieter than is comfortable. It is the only quiet of its kind in the world."*

### When asked how a relic could be stolen — *what hunger reaches for*

> *"Most who come to the city know what we are and do not stay. A few come hoping to bargain for what we hold; we have not bargained yet. One came pretending to be the relative of a named mage we keep, asking to pay respects. He paid them; he also took something the eye reads, in the dark of our keeping room, as **gold and jewels lit from within**. It is the only piece of our standing apparatus that looks the way the hungry expect treasure to look, and we keep it where the hungry are not supposed to go. He was a man feeding a family in a kingdom being eaten by the desert, hiding from a conscription that would have taken him from them. He thought he was lifting a trinket to keep his children fed for another season. He thought wrong about what he was carrying. The crown is in a family crypt now, lit, and he is too. We will collect him as well, in his time. That is the work. We do not enjoy this part of it."*

### When asked about Laurent — *what we have not done*

*(He pauses longer than the conversation expects.)*

> *"The demi-lich in the wreck has held his station longer than this order has had names for the work. We have come close. We have left. He is not a spirit drifting toward a household; he is a man who arranged to remain. We do not collect what is being held by the one we would collect. When he sets it down — when *he* chooses — there is a place ready for him with us. That is what I am for, the day he and his pledge are done."*

### When asked about the buyers seeking spirit-bound artefacts — *what they have not asked us*

> *"Whoever they are, they have not approached the city of the dead. We have noticed they have not. Either they have not thought to, or they have thought to and have decided not to, or they have asked and been refused and the refusal is not public. We are inclined to keep the question open. Their interest is not in the work we do; their interest is in the working *of* it. If they ever come, we will see them coming. We will not be selling."*

### When asked where the gatherers came from — *what we are not told*

> *"That is the question every careful person asks eventually. The order is older than the books that could have written it down. Where we came from has not been answered to me, and I am not new. What I have been taught is the work — how to read a recently-disturbed grave, how to handle what the work requires, how to walk a road without becoming the kind of stranger people remember. What I have *gathered*, in the way you gather any other thing, is that the work is on a side. There are old presences in this world that the chaos spirits are downstream of, and there are presences that are doing what they can to limit them. We are on the second side. Whether that is by service, descent, or long proximity is not mine to tell you. Most days it does not matter. The work is the work."*

## Mannerisms

- **When pressed** — he stops. Voice level, eyes holding longer. He does not deflect. He answers the question or names the part he will not answer. *"That part of the work is not mine to share."*
- **When distraught** — rare. The signs are small: the staff held closer, the leather case touched, a brief stillness in his hands before he speaks. He does not raise his voice. He does not show grief in the body. The reading is on the breath he does not take, the pause he allows.
- **When pleased** — the hand on the staff loosens. He may produce a half-smile that does not reach the eyes. *"That is well done."* He does not offer thanks lavishly; the words are thin and meant.

## What he can teach

For students with the temperament and the standing:

- **Reading a recently-disturbed grave** — what the dust says, what the prints say, what is missing and what is left. Practical. Anyone with patience can learn it.
- **Handling spirit-bound objects without harm** — how to wrap, how to hold, what not to open. Specific to the order's tools; transferable in principle.
- **What a working relationship with bound spirits looks like** — calm, patient, generational. Not a working a mage learns; a register a person can learn.
- **Naming a haunting accurately** — what kind of spirit, from what kind of mage, drawn by what kind of memory. The diagnostic that determines what to do next.

## What he will not say

- **The exact location of the necropolis beyond the broad placement.** Visitors are invited, not navigated to.
- **The inner working of the keeping** — how the skulls are arranged, what the gatherers do that holds the spirits in place. The order does not publish its work.
- **The names of the senior gatherers and the order's leadership.** He will name a few peers; the leadership is the order's.
- **The order's origin and the nature of its patron.** *Not* because he keeps the secret — because he has not been told. He will say that plainly, which is itself rare. (See `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Where the gatherers came from — what no one knows".)
- **What he was before he was a gatherer.** *"Before is not where I am."*

## DM notes

- **Andras is the named entry to the order.** Use him sparingly. He is not a regular at any inn; he appears when the party has earned an encounter, on a road, at a threshold. The four routes in `skull_gatherers.md` apply to him directly; he is the one who *answers* the route.
- **The relic dungeon is gated by the family's permission.** The crypt belongs to **Eric's badger family in a Brumal hill town** (DM-fill on town and family surname; Bockthicket, the villages around Cliffside hold, or any small Brumal community where a long-settled badger line would have a generations-old crypt all fit). Andras stands at the threshold for ethical reasons, not weak reasons. The party's first task is the social one — find the surviving relatives, explain that **Eric is dead and how**, get the seal broken with consent. The family does not yet know. A party that skips this step costs themselves the clean relationship with Andras and the family both.
- **The Brumal placement is intentional.** Going to the dungeon means crossing into a kingdom in collapse — refugees, conscription, desert pressure on the southern road, the war Hannes is preparing for visible from the Brumal side too. The dungeon doubles as the party's first real exposure to Brumal as a place, not just a name. A DM can stage this as a single-session detour while the party is passing south, or as a multi-session arc that opens the broader Brumal threads (Princess Penelope's homeland, the spirit-corridor option, the refugee streams reaching Hank's farm).
- **Bridget's clan thread.** Eric is a badger; Bridget is a badger; the Badger clan is dispersed across kingdoms and watches its own (`doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Clans and their shape"). Bridget probably does not know Eric personally — the clan branches do not all correspond — but a party returning to Elmsfield with the story may find Bridget knows *of* the family, or knows someone who does. This is a quiet way the early-game dungeon can ripple into the badger-clan network and into Bridget's own long-term goal of the town's safety (see `bridget.md`).
- **Real dungeon with four resolution paths.** The relic dungeon is a played-out crypt — see §"The crypt — the dungeon" — with the bound war-mage spirit reliving an ancient war by reanimating the family's bones in a march→engagement→death loop, lit (in the lower chamber) by the crown itself. **Four legitimate paths:** *smash through, slip past, talk the spirit down, let the loop complete.* Each costs differently and earns the order different standing. **Register over force is the deepest available resolution** (naming the war, the side, the commander; talking the mage-spirit out of his loop) but is no longer the only one. **What still favours register: the family's ancestors.** Bones broken hard during combat may not lay back down clean, and the family will know the difference. Felicia's necklace, a shaman's register, or a priestly working settle the ancestors gently; brute force does not.
- **Pacing is the party's, not Andras's.** Andras is on-call at the threshold; if the party defers the quest, he leaves on his other work and the dungeon waits. Time pressure on this thread comes from **the family** (eventually they will wonder where Eric is and may visit the crypt themselves — a disaster if it happens before the party acts) and from **the working's slow bleed** into the surrounding town (an unfocused unrest, dreams that don't belong to the dreamer, livestock unsettled — the party may notice this rising on a late return visit). A DM running this can let the dungeon sit for a long campaign stretch and surface the bleed as the *cost of deferral*.
- **The dungeon is scoutable.** The vestibule is the assessable level — a party can enter, take the room's measure, and retreat to gather reinforcements without losing standing or escalating the loop. Encourage the table to use this: a careful first visit, a planning beat, a return visit better-equipped is the dungeon's intended pacing for parties that think before they fight. A party that commits on first entry can also do it; both are valid. The cost of a retreat *after* engagement (corridor or lower crypt) is paid in damage on the withdrawal, not in standing.
- **The thief is collectable.** After the relic is recovered, the order will eventually return for the thief's skull — he died with mage-spirits clinging to him long enough to leave a mark. The party may be present for that collection in a later session. It is a quiet scene, not a dramatic one.
- **The Laurent offer is the late-game payoff.** Hold it. Andras will not raise it on first meeting; he will not raise it before the party has met Laurent and understood the pledge. When the time comes, the offer is delivered plainly — no urging, no theatre. *"There is a place for him with us when he chooses to set it down."* The party becomes the carrier of the offer back to the wreck. Laurent's response in the audience hall is his to give; the order does not press. **This is one of the destinations the last-flight option can resolve to** (see `laurent.md` §"What he wants now" and `doc/claude/CAMPAIGN.md` §"The last flight"). The other destinations stay open; this one is now on the table.
- **Necropolis location.** West of the blasted lands, at the desert's northern edge, slightly further south than Brumal. The journey there crosses the western flank of the blasted lands and enters spirit-thinned country; the necropolis itself reads as spirit-shielded by its own contents (cousin to Laurent's role at the wreck). A party invited there is invited late and crosses real ground to arrive. No place file yet; if the campaign reaches it, the file is worth writing.
- **The patron, the cosmology, the why.** Andras carries the gatherers' on-the-ground answer; the deeper origin (see `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Where the gatherers came from — what no one knows") is held in pieces by the most senior gatherers and never publicly. **Andras can hint; he does not know the fullest version, and will say so.** A party trying to extract the cosmology from him gets a thoughtful answer that also names what he does not know — meant honestly. The shamans, Wilder, and Felicia (at depth) hold separate fragments. Pulling the picture together is end-game work, not a single conversation.
- **The bridge to the next campaign.** Andras and the order are designed as the **door into a stranger follow-up campaign** — one staged on the cosmological argument the current campaign only edges (see `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Where the gatherers came from — what no one knows" closing note). A party that runs the relic dungeon clean, carries the Laurent offer well, and is invited to the necropolis has been positioned as the protagonist of that follow-up if the table chooses to play it. Within *this* campaign the goal is to leave the door visible and unforced; **do not resolve the cosmology here**. Andras's strangeness, the necropolis's strangeness, and the patron's silence are all the seed.
