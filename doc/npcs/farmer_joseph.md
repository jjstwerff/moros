# Farmer Joseph

- **Role:** Raccoon farmer in Elmsfield; post-war refugee; the town's loudest voice of post-war fear; **Session 1's opening obstacle**.
- **Species:** Raccoon. Stocky, scarred across one hand, voice carries further than his frame.
- **Lives:** His smallholding on the south side of Elmsfield — a modest farmhouse, a barn, and the **plot he bought from [Hank](hank.md)** on which he has just emptied his savings.
- **First met:** At Elmsfield's gate in Session 1, when [Irna Bean](irna_bean.md) arrives covered in crystalline growths. Joseph is the first voice raised in fear.
- **Specialisation:** Sling.

## What the party meets

Session 1 opens at Elmsfield's gate. A cart, a carry, or a figure staggers in — **Irna**, fungus-crusted and feverish — and before the party has quite arrived at the scene, a **stocky raccoon in working leathers is already there, sling loose in his hand, voice raised**. *"Keep her outside. Keep her outside the palisade until someone who knows can say what that is."*

He is not shouting at the party specifically. He is shouting at the **gate**, at the **town**, at anyone who might listen. He is the voice the other residents need somebody to speak first so that they can either back him or not. If the party says nothing, Joseph's reading becomes the town's reading — Irna is held outside, Diederik is fetched, and a tense hour follows. If the party speaks, Joseph hears them *before* he argues with them; his fear is not deaf.

## How he reads at a glance

- A **stocky raccoon in mended work-clothes**, sleeves rolled, **a sling tucked into his belt and wrapped so it can come free fast**. The wrap is practised. The sling is a tool he has used under pressure.
- **A voice that projects.** Outdoor voice, farm-yard voice, refugee-line voice — he learned to make himself heard when the people around him were frightened, and the habit did not leave.
- **Eyes that check faces for sickness first.** A party arriving at Elmsfield for the first time will be read for pallor, sweat, crystalline growths, tremor — before he reads them for friendliness.
- **Fingertips stained with garden dirt year-round.** The soil of his new plot lives under his nails; he works it every hour he has.
- **A visible tiredness.** This is a man who has just finished settling and has no margin left. It shows in the shoulders, in the pace, in the way he sits when he finally does sit.

## Visible patterns the party will notice

- **At the gate whenever a new arrival is announced.** The children bring him word; he is there within minutes. He has appointed himself the welcoming question-asker for this town, and nobody has formally given him the job.
- **On his new plot from first light to dusk.** Every spare hour is going into making the purchase pay off. He is behind on the rotation he bought it for; he knows; he is working the backlog.
- **At Bridget's Inn on seventh evenings**, never oftener. One drink, extended. Bridget keeps his tab open longer than most; he has not missed a payment yet.
- **Never at Hank's farm casually.** He bought the plot from her cleanly; he does not spend social time there. This is not hostility; it is not wanting to see the acreage he can no longer afford.
- **Voice at town meetings is early and loud.** He speaks first; other fearful voices use his words afterwards. Diederik lets him speak first for exactly this reason.

## At the gate — Session 1

The opening scene. See `../claude/CAMPAIGN.md` §"Session 1 — An Unexpected Guest" for where this sits in the campaign arc.

- **Joseph arrives before most of the town does.** The gate sentry sent for Diederik, but Joseph lives close and heard the commotion.
- **His sling is in his hand, not in his belt.** Not pointed; not loaded; out. The signal.
- **His fear is not irrational.** He is not acting on prejudice against Brumal refugees, foreigners, or the Bean name. He is acting on a specific memory of the post-war cholera outbreak that killed people he knew. That memory does not distinguish between contagions; it has one lesson, and the lesson is *keep it outside*.
- **[Egbert](egbert.md) arrives second — a beat before Diederik.** Hank sent her, or she was close; either way, it is the person the town is wariest of who walks in with the first competent hands. She does *not* name the crystal; she cannot. What she does is **smell**. What her smell tells her is that Irna **does not smell sick** — no cholera-sourness, no fever-wrongness, nothing that a spreading sickness leaves on a body. The crystal itself is almost scent-free. To Egbert's nose, Irna reads as *carrying something, not spreading something*. She says as much, shortly, and the town listens despite wanting not to.
- **Addressing Joseph correctly moves the scene.** Acknowledge the town's safety — *not Irna's personhood yet* — and he will listen. Egbert's smell-read is the kind of concrete answer his fear can hear; a party that *backs her on it* brings him down significantly. A party that dismisses Egbert because the town dismisses her leaves Joseph holding his line, and Diederik's arrival has to do more work to close the scene.
- **[Elder Diederik](elder_diederik.md) arrives last.** He gives the full diagnosis — the crystalline growth is a fungus from the **Lake of Tears**, not contagious through air or contact. He confirms what Egbert's nose already ruled out and adds the source and the cure path. Egbert is usually gone by the time he is speaking; the town does not want her at a formal scene, and she does not want to be at one.

## At his smallholding

South edge of the palisade, a farmhouse that has been added to twice in the last year, a barn that is still missing a roof patch, and the new plot beyond — the one he spent everything on.

- **The farmhouse smells of preserved food.** He puts up anything that can be preserved, obsessively, as a hedge against the year going wrong. A party visiting at meal time will be offered from the stockpile and should understand what that gift costs him.
- **The barn is half-patched.** He is doing the work himself as he can afford the materials. A party offering labor on the roof earns more goodwill than coin would.
- **The new plot is visibly behind.** The rotation he bought it for needs a full year to kick in; he is in the lean middle. The plot's edges are weedier than his older plots because he has not had the hours.
- **A small chapel corner** in the farmhouse's main room, with a folk-carving of a raccoon-clan ancestor figure. He is not devout; he keeps it because his grandmother did. A party that asks sympathetically about it gets the most personal of his answers.

## In Elmsfield's square — on market days

He sells his surplus (what there is of it this year), buys what the farm does not grow, listens. He does not shop long; the money is tight and the hours are needed elsewhere.

- **He is civil to everyone and warm to few.** Bridget gets a nod; Hank gets a polite greeting that is shorter than neighbourhood would warrant; the party is sized up in a glance.
- **He catches up on news with the vendors.** Who has arrived; who has left; who is sick; who is not. The fear at the gate is partly fed here.
- **He pays in cash, not barter**, when he can. A habit from his old town — barter is a relationship, cash is a transaction that ends. He wants fewer relationships than he has.

## What he will tell — and when

| Topic | When he'll talk |
|---|---|
| The farm, the rotation, the plot | Freely. A fellow farmer gets a long answer; the party gets the short version unless they show real interest. |
| The cholera outbreak | Once asked, plainly. He does not hide the trauma; he uses it to explain his decisions. |
| Where he was before Elmsfield | Once asked. The old town's name; what happened there; why he left. He does not volunteer details that don't add up to his current view. |
| The sling | Freely. A tool, a craft, a habit. He will demonstrate if asked politely. |
| The plot purchase | Honestly. He paid fair; Hank was fair; it cost him everything; he does not blame her. |
| Brumal refugees | Carefully. His view is complicated; he will speak it but not loudly. |
| Hank's orphanage-farm expansion | Once he trusts the asker. *"She's taking on more than the town is ready for. I do not know what to do about it."* |
| Why he was loud at the gate | Once the moment has passed. He can explain himself; he would rather be asked than not. |
| What he left behind in the old town | **Reluctant.** There are people there he could not save. He does not tell this to the party without earned standing. |

## Stories he tells

When the relevant topic comes up and he chooses to answer.

### When asked about the old town — *where I was before*

> *"I farmed a plot outside a town half a week south of here. A good town. Then the war came through, and then the soldiers stayed, and then the refugees arrived, and then the sickness followed — the three together are what happens, I have learned. I lost neighbours. I stayed through the first year of the sickness because I thought it would pass. It did not pass quickly enough. When I left, the town was still there. I could not stay in it any more."*

### When asked about the cholera — *who I lost*

> *"Three specific people I will not name, because naming them is a thing I do alone. A woman who had fed me the first winter. A young man I had taught to slog a plot. A child who was not mine who used to come to the fence. The lesson I took is that a sickness will not care how good a person you were. It comes in through the gate and it goes out through every door."*

### When asked about the plot purchase — *what it cost me*

> *"Hank sold me good land for a fair price. I bought it with the savings I had spent seven years making. I now have the land and no margin. I will not blame her; she needed the money, I needed the acreage, the rotation will make it back. I will also tell you I am sleeping worse than I was and I am loud at meetings. Those are not coincidences."*

### When asked about the gate — *what I was afraid of*

> *"A stranger fell at the gate covered in something none of us had seen. I saw people die of something none of us had seen once. I do not know what the crystal-fungus was. I did not need to know. I know that the town standing here tonight is thinner than it was a year ago and will be thinner next year. I would rather be loud and wrong than quiet and burying more people. I am willing to apologise if I was wrong. I still would have spoken."*

### When asked about the sling — *a tool for my kind of farming*

> *"Crows, rats, the occasional fox. A sling is the farmer's weapon; it does not draw the soldier. I was not in the war. I use it because the plot pays for itself better without crows. I will teach it if you want to learn; you will not learn it in an afternoon."*

## Mannerisms

- **When pressed** — raises his voice before he means to. The sling-hand drifts to his belt. He does not touch it; he does not have to. The voice gets the room first; the tool is second, and not used.
- **When distraught** — goes to the plot. Works until his hands shake. Talks to no one. Does not accept help; this is how he regulates.
- **When happy** — a wide grin, surprising for the size of his face, that lasts a beat too long. Offers food from his stockpile — *which costs him something* — as the expression of it.

## If the party does something — what he does

| If the party... | He responds by... |
|---|---|
| Argues with him at the gate in Session 1 | Listens, actually. Counter-argues. Does not walk away. His fear is specific; meet it with a specific answer (Diederik will confirm Irna is not contagious, the party can offer quarantine, etc.) and he will back down. *Do not* dismiss the fear; do not mock it. |
| Acknowledges his fear is about the town's safety, not Irna's personhood | Significantly lowers the temperature. *"Then you know what I am asking."* He is willing to accept a careful path from there. |
| Overrides him at the gate without engaging | Backs down publicly. Backs down *reluctantly*. Becomes a quieter but steady opponent on every future refugee question, and the party has earned him as an obstacle for the rest of the campaign. |
| Mocks his fear | Stops listening. The sling-hand goes to his belt and stays. The scene becomes dangerous; Diederik will step between. The party has lost him and made the opening of the campaign harder than it needed to be. |
| Helps him on the plot (a day's labor, a roof patch, a new fence) | Goes very quiet. Does not thank them aloud at the time. Brings food to their lodgings that evening. His standing with them rises; he will speak for them at meetings afterwards. |
| Offers him coin for the farm's cash-crunch | Declines charity that costs the giver nothing. Accepts a loan offered with plain terms. Accepts *work* offered as a transaction — *"I'll pay you to help me cure the winter store"* — and is more grateful for work than for money. |
| Brings him useful farming intelligence | Sits with them for a full conversation. Wants specifics; will swap his own observations. |
| Threatens him | The sling comes out. He does not attack first. A party that escalates here has picked a fight with a man who *can* use the tool; Elmsfield will close around him. |
| Reveals Irna was not contagious — later, with proof | He will accept the proof plainly. He will not apologise unprompted; asked for one, he gives a short honest one. His fear adjusts rather than disappearing. |
| Brings more refugees to Elmsfield | Engages Diederik at the meeting. *"How many more. How long. Who's feeding them."* Not cruel; exhausted. A party that has specific answers moves him; a party that shows up with people and no plan hardens him. |
| Asks him to teach the sling | Yes. Over weeks, not an afternoon. The student muckings-in on the plot in exchange for the lessons. |

## What he can teach

- **The sling.** A working farmer's weapon; accurate at range for crows, rats, small game. Taught over weeks to a student willing to help on the plot.
- **Crop rotation — the reason for the new plot.** He is not a book-reader like Hank; he has been taught the rotation he uses, and he can pass it on plainly. Useful for a party with a Farming specialist or a general interest.
- **Reading a gate-scene.** Informal; by example. Who he lets in quickly, who he holds, who he refuses. A party watching him at the gate over multiple arrivals picks this up.
- **Preserving under pressure** — the paranoid stockpiler's craft. Salting, drying, pickling, keeping a pantry that outlasts a bad year.

## What he will not say

- **The names of the three he lost to cholera.** He has told the party the count; he will not give the names. They are his to carry.
- **The specific thing he could not do in the old town.** There is one moment from the outbreak that he thinks about most. He will not describe it.
- **That he sometimes resents Hank for the scale of the orphanage.** He knows the resentment is not fair — the sale was clean, the orphans are not the cause of his stretch — and he refuses to give it a voice. It leaks anyway, at meetings, carefully.
- **How much of his sling-work is farm craft and how much is a war-era habit he wants to deny.** He uses the tool; he does not dwell on where the speed came from.

## DM notes

- **He is Session 1's opening obstacle and he is not a villain.** A party that defeats him in Session 1 has defeated the wrong thing. The correct win is *understanding* him — his fear is earned, his voice is the voice of a real town-problem the campaign will return to, and getting him onto the same side as the party opens the rest of the campaign. A party that steamrolls him makes the campaign's "refugee-pressure" thread harder at every later step.
- **His stretched finances are the reason he is loud.** A party that addresses the finances — with labour, a loan, a commission — quiets him more than any argument will. This is a legitimate lever and the DM should let it work.
- **Hank is the quiet edge of his arc.** The sale was fair; the cost was real. A party that brings Hank's *and* Joseph's perspectives into dialogue — without forcing reconciliation — does the campaign's refugee-thread more work than any speech.
- **Do not let him stay loud past his use.** Joseph is the voice of Session 1 and the voice of the refugee-debate at key meetings. Between those, he is a quiet stretched neighbour. A DM who plays him loud all the time flattens him into a caricature; play him at the volume the scene actually calls for.
- **The cholera trauma is not for dramatic revelation.** He will tell the party about it once, plainly, if asked. Do not build a mystery around it. The trauma is *context for his behaviour at the gate*, not a twist.
- **He can become an ally.** A campaign that treats him with care across Sessions 1–10 can earn him fully: the farmer who speaks *for* careful welcome at town meetings, the sling at the party's side for a defensive job, the voice that reconciles the post-war settlers with the newer waves. This is a real arc; it is not guaranteed.

## Background — for the DM

*Reference, not script.*

- **Post-war Raccoon refugee**, from a town half a week south of Elmsfield. Not a soldier; a farmer who lived through the occupation and the post-occupation settling.
- **Watched the post-war cholera outbreak** kill neighbours. Lost three specific people whose names he keeps. Stayed a year too long trying to help; left when he could no longer stay.
- **Arrived in Elmsfield** some years ago; has been building his smallholding ever since. The plot bought from Hank is the *extension* of a farm he had already made work — the ambition step, the one that would secure the next decade if it paid off.
- **The plot purchase emptied him.** He is in the lean middle; the rotation will kick in inside the year; he will survive if nothing else breaks.
- **The secret** — the one he will not name — is the specific thing he did or did not do during the outbreak in the old town. A DM can define it to fit the campaign's needs; a strong candidate is a moment where he chose not to let someone through his own gate, and they did not survive the night. He has made peace with his decision but not with the shape of it.

## Relationships

- Bought a plot from **[Hank](hank.md)** — one of the transactions that has helped her farm expand into an orphanage-of-sorts. He does not blame her; the transaction hollowed him out; it will colour his stance on anything that asks more of him in the short term.
- Part of Elmsfield's **post-war settler community**, with the specific edge of someone who *just* finished settling and whose margin is now zero.
- Lost neighbours to post-war cholera in his old town; his fear at Irna's arrival comes from that loss, not from generic xenophobia.
- Has a **tab at Bridget's Inn** that she keeps open longer than most; he has not missed a payment.
- Received **a scythe sold below cost** by Wilfred one bad season; he noticed, never thanked him, never forgot.
