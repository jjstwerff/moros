# Corné Dunham

- **Role:** Elmsfield's valley-and-hills man — the local who knows the paths, the seasons, and the way *over the ridges* to Luchebert Monastery. Part of the Elmsfield **Badger-clan** concentration.
- **Species:** Badger. Lean, weathered, built for long days afoot.
- **Lives:** A small stone-and-timber cottage on the north-western edge of Elmsfield, at the point where the tilled land gives up and the hill-track begins. The cottage is his own work, extended twice, and is *closer to the hills than to the town*.
- **First met:** The party comes looking for him — directed by **[Elder Diederik](elder_diederik.md)** or **[Bridget](bridget.md)** when the topic of *how to get to Luchebert* comes up. Or they see him on the track in or out of town on a day he has been guiding somebody.

## What the party meets

The party walks the north-western track. Elmsfield thins; the fields stop at a sharp field-edge; past that, the ground begins to tilt up. At the tilt there is a cottage — low stone below, timber above, moss on the lee side, a small vegetable plot and a rack of drying herbs. A **lean badger** is outside when they arrive, or working just inside the door with the shutters open. He looks up, reads them in a glance — *where they have come from today, how they moved on the track coming up* — and says something short. *"You're looking for me, or you got lost. Which."*

He does not make small talk. He also does not rush. A party with an honest errand will get a clear answer within a minute; a party wasting his time will be pointed back down the hill.

## How he reads at a glance

- A **lean Badger in weathered leather** — not new leathers, not fashionable; the leathers of a man who spends his life outdoors and re-oils the same pieces. Patched at the knees and elbows where the work wears.
- **A walking stick with notches carved along its length.** The notches mark routes — a count-system he invented as a younger man and never stopped using. He does not lean on the stick hard; he uses it for measurement and rhythm.
- **Eyes that read terrain before they read people.** A party meeting him outside will feel his gaze check the weather, the track behind them, and the direction they came from *before* it lands on their faces.
- **A small cloth pouch at his belt containing rowan berries** — dried, seasonal-refreshed. Old country superstition; he believes in it the way a man believes in the practical tools he carries. It is not decoration.
- **Slow, specific speech.** Short sentences; the words chosen. A valley guide who has had bad weather answer his casual words once too often and now means what he says.

## Visible patterns the party will notice

- **At the cottage in the early morning** — tending the garden or re-cutting a notch on the stick. The day's work starts later than a farmer's; guides' days run on the traveller's schedule.
- **On the valley paths most afternoons.** A circuit he walks for his own — check the creek fords, mark the fallen trees, replace the trail-markers that winter knocked down. Not paid work; the work the valley would not have if he stopped.
- **At Bridget's Inn a few evenings a week.** Specific corner, specific seat, one drink extended. He listens to the merchants; the traders who have come up from Salmonswell or Linar are his information net for the roads outside his own.
- **Never near Laurent's downed city.** The party will notice this once they know enough to notice — where his circuit goes, where it conspicuously does not.
- **A quiet short nod to Wilfred and Bridget** whenever he passes them. Clan-greeting. Not remarked on by either party.

## At the cottage — his own making

Where the party most reliably finds him. The back wall is set into the hillside; the front faces down into Elmsfield's valley. He built the stone lower course himself over two summers; the upper floor came later, when he decided he was staying.

- **A map-table.** A low table inside the door with a set of thin charcoal sketches of the valley's routes, drawn on oiled linen and kept rolled. Not a formal map; his route-notes, understandable to him and anyone who has walked the land with him.
- **A rack of walking staves** — not his working one, but spares, partly-shaped, and a few he has carved as gifts over the years. A guest-stick for a visitor whose foot he judges the right size.
- **A small shrine-shelf** with a bowl of rowan berries, a bundle of knotted grass, and a carved Badger-clan sign. He does not advertise the shelf; a party looking around the cottage will see it. Asked about it, he is frank: *"Old habits. Some of them worked."*
- **A hearth that burns all year.** The cottage is above the valley floor and cold even in summer evenings; the fire is maintained as a matter of practical routine.

## On the valley paths — his circuit

The tracks that run across the hills and up to the forbidden slopes. He walks them weekly, sometimes daily, keeping them usable without ever calling it a job.

- **He marks trees.** Small notches at shoulder height on specific oaks and birches, in a pattern only he and the handful of valley folk who learned it from him read.
- **He knows where every ford is at every water-level.** A party walking with him in spring will cross in places that would be impassable in autumn, and vice versa.
- **He reads the weather off the ridge-line first.** If he says the weather is changing, it is changing; if he says the weather is holding, it is holding.
- **There is a specific ridge he never crosses** — the line above which Laurent's downed city begins to lie. He walks *to* the ridge; he does not walk *over* it. A party accompanying him will see him stop at the same point every time and wait.

## At the Inn — listening, not drinking

See [`../places/elmsfield_inn.md`](../places/elmsfield_inn.md). He is there a few evenings a week, seated at a corner where he can see the door and the trader's table both. Bridget pours his drink without being asked.

- **He is not a story-teller there.** His voice stays down; the merchants tell; he listens.
- **Asked for news he has, he gives it.** Offered news he does not have, he takes it. A party with specific valley questions gets specific answers; a party with outside-world questions finds he has less to say than the Inn's merchants do, and he directs them.
- **He and [Nadine](nadine.md)** have a working acquaintance — she brings him the road news from south; he briefs her on the valley so her cart doesn't come up into a flood.

## On the hill route to Luchebert — when he guides

The **inland route over the hills** that leaves the river behind, climbs across ridges, and descends into Luchebert's vale from above. The route Irna Bean could not find from the water; the one CAMPAIGN.md §"The Monastery Road" flags as the necessary next step.

- **He walks it himself sometimes.** Not often — it is a long trip — but he has not lost the track.
- **He will guide a party for a day or two if the request is reasonable** and the timing is right. *Reasonable* means a clear errand, decent weather, and a pace he can trust.
- **The route actively goes around Laurent's wreck**, not past it. This is not a cost to the route; for most parties it is a feature.
- **He will not take a party *into* Laurent's city.** Ever. A party that wants to bargain for it gets the firmest *no* he ever issues.

## At the threshold — where he will not step

If the party walks with him toward Laurent's wreck from the *outer* side, he will accompany them up to a specific ridge and stop. He will point to the direction they need to go. He will wait. He will not go down.

- **The ridge is a place of small signs.** A patch of rowan he has planted there. A stone cairn he rebuilds each spring. The marks a man leaves when he is telling the land he acknowledges its rule.
- **A party that is gone into the wreck for more than a day will not find him still on the ridge.** He will leave a note under a specific stone and go back to his cottage. *"I will hear about it when I hear about it."*

## What he will tell — and when

| Topic | When he'll speak |
|---|---|
| Valley routes, seasons, fords, weather | Freely. This is his life. |
| The hill route to Luchebert | Freely to a party with a credible errand. Will guide if asked and paid in a reasonable form. |
| Laurent's city — that people have disappeared | Plainly. He will give names if asked. |
| Laurent's city — why *he* won't go | Plainly. His reason is the disappearances and the valley's old stories. He does not dress it up. |
| The rowan and his other small practices | Freely, without making it strange. *"Old country. Some of it works."* |
| The Storm Mage in the hills | Cautiously. He has heard; he has not gone looking. What he can tell is second-hand, and he labels it as such. |
| Badger-clan matters | Carefully. He will acknowledge the clan; he does not speak for it. |
| News from the trade roads | Only what he has picked up at Bridget's. Labelled as such. |
| His own history — family, past work | Once asked. Short answers. No dramatisation. |

## Stories he tells

When the relevant topic comes up and he chooses to answer.

### When asked about the hill route to Luchebert — *the way Irna could not find*

> *"The girl went up the river because the river is the obvious route. The river is wrong. The vale is on cliffs; you can see the monastery from below and not reach it. You go up the ridge on *this* side of the valley, you walk two days along the crest, you come down from above. The monks hold the back gate now. The cliff path is no longer in use. I will walk the first day with you if your errand is clean."*

### When asked about the wreck — *old stones, bad feelings*

> *"Laurent's city is a downed thing older than any of us can work with. People have gone in. Some have come back, changed. Most have not come back. I can name them to you. What kills them is not something I can tell you — only that the pattern is long, it is stable, and it is not random. I respect that the way a fisher respects a stretch of rapids that took his cousin."*

### When asked about the disappeared — *the names I can give*

> *"I can give you six names from my lifetime. A cousin of Bridget's. Two young men from Salmonswell who came up looking for mage-gold. A woman from a valley further east. Two whose names would only mean something to their own people. Six that I know. There are others I do not know. If you would like to go in after what took them, I will draw you the last safe line on the map, and you will cross it without me."*

### When asked about the rowan — *what the stories turned out to be true of*

> *"My grandmother planted rowan around her kitchen door and made me carry berries in my pocket as a boy. I thought it was old-country nonsense. I have lost count of the evenings where I have been on the hill and felt the air change and put my hand on the pouch and said the old word aloud, and the air has changed back. I cannot prove to you that the rowan did the work. I also cannot prove to you that it did not. I carry it."*

### When asked about the Storm Mage — *the hill the sky visits*

> *"There is a ridge east of the wreck where the weather is not the weather of the surrounding valley. Lightning at clear noon; snow in spring. I have walked to the base of that ridge. I have not walked up. People in the valley call what lives there *the Storm Mage*, and I do not argue with them. If you are going up that ridge, I will walk you to the base. I will not walk you up."*

### When asked about his own history — *the cousin who did not come back*

> *(Asked only once trust is earned, and only in private.)*
>
> *"I had a cousin when I was a boy. He went into the wreck one autumn with three friends. Three came out. He did not. The three who came out could not say where he had been lost, and inside a year two of them had disappeared in their own separate ways. The third is still alive in a cottage up the river and does not leave it. I did not learn the valley because I wanted to be a guide. I learned it because I wanted to be a man who would not go in after my cousin. The pouch was my grandmother's. She said the rowan kept her in this world. I did not think I would need it. I needed it."*

## Mannerisms

- **When pressed** — plants the walking stick. Looks at the ground near the stick. Gives a short answer to the simple part of the question first. Does not chase the asker's follow-up until they ask it plainly.
- **When distraught** — walks. Very long walks, alone, returning silent. Nobody in Elmsfield asks him where; Bridget pours the drink without asking and does not remark on the longer-than-usual absence.
- **When happy** — a short laugh, two beats, hands on his knees as he shakes his head. A story he is keeping to himself; a memory that pleases him. He does not share the memory; the laugh is the whole thing.

## If the party does something — what he does

| If the party... | He responds by... |
|---|---|
| Asks him to guide them to Luchebert | Accepts if the errand is plainly legitimate and the weather holds. Sets the pace, walks the first leg, hands them off to the ridge the monks watch. Will not go past the monastery's gate — *"That is their house, not mine."* |
| Asks him to guide them *into* Laurent's city | Refuses. Plainly. No negotiation; no raising voice. *"I will walk you to the ridge. The step off it is yours."* |
| Asks him to lead the climb *up* to the Storm Mage | Same refusal, softer. Will walk them to the base of the ridge. Will wait one night. Will not stay past that. |
| Offers him coin for a long guide-contract | Takes it seriously. Will quote a fair price — usually less than the asker offered — and will deliver exactly what he promised. |
| Brings him news of the disappeared | Very quiet. Asks for specifics; writes nothing down; remembers. The next time he goes to the ridge, the cairn is rebuilt one stone higher. |
| Insults his rowan / his superstition | A slow look. *"You are walking in the wrong part of the valley for that view. Walk it with me a week and tell me again."* He does not argue further. The party's standing with him narrows. |
| Asks him to teach the hill craft | Yes. A student walks with him a week, two weeks, a season. He teaches by doing; he does not lecture. A student who cannot keep pace is returned home. |
| Brings him rowan berries from elsewhere | A real small gift. He will examine them, nod, and add them to the pouch. Standing with him rises one honest notch. |
| Wants him to accompany a party going into Laurent's city anyway | He walks them to the ridge. Waits one day, as described above. Will not follow them in. If they come back, he walks them home. |
| Asks after a Badger clan name he has not volunteered | Careful. Confirms acquaintance if it is safe to do so; names locations only if the named person would not object. |
| Tries to cross the ridge he won't cross, with him watching | He lets them. Stands on the ridge. Does not follow. Does not call out. The lesson is not his to teach. |

## What he can teach

- **Valley navigation.** Reading terrain, reading water, reading the track. Taught by walking the valley with a student over weeks.
- **Ridge-walking.** The specific craft of long-pace movement at altitude — pacing, breathing, footing on slate, reading a cornice.
- **Weather-reading off the ridge-line.** An eye that a lowland farmer will not develop unaided.
- **Basic survival.** Shelter, fire, water in the hills and valleys of the region. Practical.
- **How to not go into a thing that keeps swallowing people.** A habit, more than a skill. Students who stay near him long enough pick it up.

## What he will not say

- **The details of his cousin's disappearance.** He has given the shape of it in the private story; he will not give the date, the exact location, or the cousin's name.
- **Which Badger-clan elders live where.** Bridget's territory, not his; he will not name names without her cover.
- **What he thinks was going to kill him the evening he said the old word aloud and the air changed back.** He does not know. He will not theorise.
- **Exactly how many routes he knows through the hills beyond his working set.** He keeps some in reserve.

## DM notes

- **He is the in-world answer to "how do we actually get to Luchebert."** CAMPAIGN.md §"The Monastery Road" flags this gap; Corné closes it. A DM whose party has saved Irna and been told she needs Luchebert should expect Diederik (or Bridget) to direct the party to Corné's cottage within the first session afterwards. The party arriving at the cottage is the normal pathway; handle the scene with the shape above.
- **The hill route is a one-to-two-day guide contract, not a permanent escort.** Corné walks the first leg, hands the party off to the route, and returns. A party that wants him for the whole trip will be met with a polite *"No. That is not how this works."* Respect the shape.
- **The wreck-refusal is non-negotiable.** He *cannot* be talked into going into Laurent's city. A DM who bends this breaks the character. A party who wants a guide into the wreck needs a different NPC (one of the disappeared's survivors, for instance) or to go without one.
- **He is a possible secondary pointer to the Storm Mage.** CAMPAIGN.md names him as a candidate source. His actual knowledge is thin; he has not climbed the ridge. He can confirm the ridge's location, confirm the weather-anomaly, and refuse to take the party up. That is appropriate — the Storm Mage's file has more of the mechanics.
- **The rowan and the small practices are not quaint decoration.** They are a man's lived defence against a region where things went wrong often enough to teach a grandmother to plant rowan. Play them with that weight.
- **Bridget, Wilfred, Ralph, and Corné are the Badger-clan concentration at Elmsfield.** A DM running any of the four should keep the cross-references honest — they know each other, back each other, and send travelling clan members to whichever of them is the right answer for the errand.

## Background — for the DM

*Reference, not script.*

- **Elmsfield-born Badger.** Grew up in the valley; taught the hills by his grandmother and her brothers, the older generation of clan valley-folk.
- **Lost a cousin into Laurent's wreck** when he was a boy. The loss shaped him; the rowan habit and the refusal to cross the ridge are both downstream of it.
- **Became a de facto valley guide** in his twenties — the one who knew the paths well enough that travellers started paying him. Never formalised; never advertised; the reputation did the work.
- **Walked the hill route to Luchebert** the first time accompanying an older guide, the second time alone, and many times since. It is the one named route he is known for outside the valley.
- **Never married.** He does not dwell on it. The cottage is where he wanted to live; the circuit is what he wanted to do; the rest has not called to him loudly enough to change his mind.

## Open questions

- The specific valley-eastern source of the **Storm Mage** rumour — a fisher's story, a refugee's report, a clan cousin's? Left open for the DM.
- Whether he knows anything about the specific mechanism of the wreck's killing power (he does not *reason* about it; whether he has *heard* theorise from others is DM's choice).
- Whether his relationship with his third still-living cousin (up-river, house-bound) would open up if the party asked — and what that cousin could add to the wreck's history.

## Relationships

- Part of Elmsfield's dispersed **Badger clan** (see `../claude/LORE.md` §"Clans and their shape"). Local concentration with **[Bridget](bridget.md)**, **[Wilfred](wilfred.md)**, and possibly **[Ralph Overhill](ralph_overhill.md)**.
- Known to **[Elder Diederik](elder_diederik.md)** as the town's practical valley-man; Diederik refers the party to him for route questions.
- Working acquaintance with **[Nadine](nadine.md)**; they trade road news for valley news when her cart comes up.
- Has had no direct dealings with **[Laurent](laurent.md)** or anyone inside the downed city, by choice.
