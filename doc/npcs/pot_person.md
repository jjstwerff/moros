# The Pot-People (Buried Tower)

- **Role:** Two surviving iron puppets in the buried tower at the Lake of Tears — one small caretaker, one that *is* the tower's furnace
- **Species:** Pot-people — iron puppets with bound spirits, small and pot-shaped rather than the humanoid style Haven collects
- **Location:** Inside the buried tower, nearest of the six towers around the Lake of Tears
- **Status:** Both active; both cautious; both very old; both a one-off experimental lineage of the mage who built this tower

## Summary

There are **two pot-people inside the buried tower**, not one. Together they are the survival of a mage's experimental side-programme that branched away from the humanoid-puppet tradition most of Haven's inhabitants come from. The experiment worked, against the odds; the programme never scaled; these two are what remain of it.

### The Boy

Knee-height, quiet, meticulous. The **small pot-person** keeps the tower running day-to-day. He sweeps. He straightens. He maintains **the mage's living quarters** inside the tower — rooms his creator used, and which he has kept habitable for centuries as though his creator might come back tomorrow. He is the cautious, methodical, deeply-attached personality the Lake-of-Tears sessions will encounter first.

**Defensive behaviour.** When strangers enter his home, the boy's instinctive response is to **disguise himself as a normal pot** — he pulls his stubby legs under him, sits perfectly still on the nearest surface, and tries to look like an ordinary piece of pottery. He is fast at it. What he *does not think about* is the tools he was holding at the moment he was startled. A broom stays leaning against his side. A small chisel rests next to him. He does not drop them; he does not think to. That is how he is typically noticed: not by moving, but by the arrangement of tools around him being impossible for a pot to have arranged. This is the beat the party is likely to catch him on — and, for a kind party, the beat at which they know to be gentle.

### The Furnace

The *large pot at the centre of the tower is itself a pot-person*. It is the one with stubby legs and a will of its own that campaign notes describe as "not having moved in a very long time." It is more than a furnace — it **is** the forge that would have made more of its kind (had the programme scaled).

**Entry to the tower goes down the chimney, into the furnace.** The buried structure's approach routes are choked by fungus and sand; the chimney above is the only route not sealed. Anyone getting inside the tower is getting inside **through** the furnace, which means the first interaction with the tower's interior is an interaction with this pot-person — passing through a being, not past it.

**The chimney is also the tower's defence against the fungus.** The furnace periodically blasts hot exhaust up the chimney to drive encroaching fungal growth back. Descending the chimney means descending against that breath on a timing the party must read correctly; a mistimed descent catches a full blast. The exhaust is not hostility — it is what the furnace has been doing to keep itself alive — but it is still dangerous to whoever is in the way.

The furnace's exhaust is its breath. Its inertia is not absence of will. It is patience, backed by mass. A party that treats the chimney as plumbing is assaulting the furnace; a party that asks to be let down *between breaths* finds the exhaust pauses long enough for the descent.

### How he communicates

The boy **cannot speak** — a pot has no mouth, and the spirit bound into him was not made with a voice. What he can do is **draw on the ground**: with chalk, with furnace-ash, with a finger in dust. His drawings are how he answers questions and makes himself understood.

The drawings are **targeted**. Specific questions produce specific answers; he does not volunteer a running monologue the way a talking puppet might. A party that asks *which way to the workshop* gets a map on the floor. A party that asks *what happened to your maker* gets a sequence of images — the mage leaving, the door closing, the years passing. Vague or hostile questions get small shapes and silences; he thinks before committing ink to stone.

Conversation with him is therefore **slow**, but not furnace-slow. DMs should lean into the drawing scenes rather than summarising them: a careful description of what the boy chooses to draw, and in what order, is often more informative than any sentence he could have produced. For spirit-aware characters, a **Shamanic** approach bypasses the drawing channel entirely — speaking directly with the bound spirit inside him, in whatever way spirits speak.

## Role in the campaign

**The boy is the social encounter.** Trust-building (Shamanic, Ingenuity, Relaxed, patience) turns him into a level-1 contact. He knows the tower and its furnace-companion intimately.

**The furnace is the threshold.** The party's entry into the tower is a negotiation with it. It has not moved in a very long time, but it has not stopped being willed, and anyone who tries to force their way through without acknowledging it as a person rather than a machine may trigger a response that is slow in arriving and very difficult to dismiss when it does. Approaching the furnace with the same respect the party would offer Haven's humanoid puppets (or the pot-boy himself) is the correct posture.

**Together they are kin.** They were made by the same mage, for the same purpose, out of the same experimental tradition. The boy has lived with the furnace as his one companion for centuries. They do not think alike — the boy is quick-for-a-puppet, if silent (he cannot speak; he draws — see below); the furnace is vast and slow — but they are all each other has.

### They are the key to the first shackle

The six towers around the Lake of Tears are the **six shackles** that hold the bound air elemental in place. Breaking a shackle does not mean destroying a tower; it means **moving the tower out of the ring**. For the buried tower, the mechanism is simple to describe and hard to earn: *get the pot-boy to leave, and the furnace will follow him as its companion*. The furnace has stubby legs and a will; it has not moved in centuries only because the boy was there. Give it a reason — the boy walking away with people it has learned to trust — and the whole tower stands up and goes after him.

This is the *first and easiest shackle to break*. It is easy only in the sense that the mechanism is legible; the execution is still earned trust, genuine care, and the party committing to relocate the boy (and by extension the furnace) somewhere he wants to be. Haven, if they can get him there, is the obvious destination.

Breaking this shackle is also the **first step toward the Last Flight climax-option**: freeing the air elemental enough that Laurent's downed city can rise for its final journey. Each of the other five towers will have its own relational mechanism to break; this one is the party's first lesson that the cage comes apart through understanding, not force.

## Relationships

- Both are creations of the same unnamed mage who ran the buried tower's experimental programme. Creator is long gone (left, died, or was recalled across the ocean; the boy does not know which).
- Both are kin (in construct-type) to **Haven**'s humanoid ironwood puppets, but *not of* them. See `doc/places/haven.md` — the two of them are the last remaining branch of a side-project that Haven's mainstream does not fully recognise.
- The **Hermit Shaman** at another of the Lake-of-Tears towers is the single most likely outside party to know about the boy and the furnace, given proximity. Whether they have spoken across the lake in all these years is a live DM question.

## Open questions

- Do the boy and the furnace *communicate*? On what timescale? A sentence from the furnace might take a season to arrive.
- Does the furnace have its own name, or does it use no word for itself at all?
- Would restoring the furnace to fuller operation allow it to make *more* pot-people? That would be a campaign-scale consequence — a third option alongside Last Flight and Shamans' Plan.

## Role as spirit-shield (partial)

Per `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Spirit Ecology," both pot-people have bound spirits inside, and both have been in place for centuries. Their combined presence at the buried tower reinforces the Lake-of-Tears region's already dense spirit-shield against chaotic-spirit migration. The Lake-of-Tears ring (Felicia, Laurent, the Hermit Shaman, the boy, the furnace) is the densest concentration of mighty-spirit-readers on the continent — this is two of them, not one.
