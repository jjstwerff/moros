# Irna Bean

- **Role:** Daughter of John Bean; exile bound for Luchebert Monastery; current patient in Elmsfield
- **Species:** Boar folk (Tusk Clan), female, 22 years old
- **Location:** Elmsfield — her dog guided her there after she wandered the Lake of Tears region
- **Status:** Alive but gravely ill — feverish, half-blind, crystalline fungal growths on skin and eyes

## Summary

Twenty-two, Boar folk of the Tusk Clan, the only one of John Bean's children to inherit the lineage's strong will. As a child she fell from a tree and was pulled out by her father from a significant distance — an early debt to him that still shapes how she carries herself.

She can **brawl**. Really brawl — Tusk Clan mass, Tusk Clan will, and years on a farm of handling things that did not want to be handled. She will teach someone how to brawl without the conversation ever being framed as teaching: tease her a bit too much and she will deliver the lesson for free, physically, right then, and you will know afterwards exactly how your guard dropped and what landed where. It is how she learned it too, in the family yard with her father and her brother and whichever cousins were visiting. The "free lesson" model is not aggression toward the student; it is the only teaching style she has, and it is surprisingly effective.

### What she actually carries

Her trained capacities, from two backgrounds and the will that drove her into each:

- **Farmer** (childhood and on) — tools, livestock, weather-reading, the patient physical competence of someone raised to handle animals bigger than herself.
- **Miner** (from her forced labour at Clear water) — pick work, ore identification, tunnel-sense, the sort of practical knowledge the crown intended as punishment and which she now owns regardless.
- **Brawl** and **Axes** as her fighting specialisations. Axes from the farm, brawling from the yard; she knows both, and she knows them well.
- **Charge** — the boar-folk forward attack, a mass-at-speed move that ends arguments quickly.
- **Digging** — faster and more efficient than most, a direct inheritance of the mine months.
- **Smell** — the Tusk Clan's sharpened nose. Useful for tracking, for reading whether someone is being straight, for knowing what has been in a room recently.

Most of this is *practical craft*, and she can pass it on to anyone she chooses to teach — with the brawling caveat that "choosing to teach" and "being teased into it" tend to produce the same outcome.

She attacked **Didrich**, the second prince of Allondo, non-lethally. The crown set her to forced labour at Clear water, Allondo's mining town; **Lady Rhianna Linthrope** intervened at Scarlet vale, got her out, and arranged exile to Luchebert Monastery instead — carrying Rhianna's letter asking **Father Elliot** to correct the "valiant spirit" proclamation he made about her brother **Ron**.

She never reached Luchebert. She was looking for a small vale leading past Laurent's downed city toward the monastery, and could not find the entrance. She wandered near the Lake of Tears and was exposed to the crystalline fungus growing outward from the buried tower there — the same fungus fed by the air elemental's struggle against its cage. It left her feverish, half-blind and barely alive. Her grey-muzzled dog guided her to Elmsfield, where she collapsed at the gate.

She cannot currently speak.

## Late childhood — the story Irna will tell

This is Irna's own memory of the months leading up to the attack on Didrich. She can tell it, roughly in her own voice, when she trusts the listener.

In the last stretch of her childhood, she started hearing her mother crying in the night. Never loudly. Never with an explanation. When Irna asked, in the morning, her mother would simply say the wrong thing and change the subject — the kind of wrong thing that told Irna not to ask again. She didn't. She listened instead, for a long time, night after night, getting nothing.

Her father was a different kind of quiet. John turned brooding as the day came closer, the day the crown would come for Ron. The farm kept working — John saw to that, the way he saw to everything — but he stopped speaking at the table, and he stopped meeting her eyes. He was bound, she understood later, by an old promise he had made in exchange for the farm itself, and promises made to Hannes could not be taken back. John knew exactly what was coming and could do nothing about it. That was the worst part for him, and Irna saw it, and she had no way to help him with it either.

She was not planning anything. She knew, without having worked it out in so many words, that the people coming to her family's door were the ones who had broken her mother's nights and her father's days. And when they arrived — Didrich with his two knights, there to take Ron — she could not stop herself. She flung herself at them. She did not know what she thought she could accomplish. She did not accomplish it. But she *did* hurt Didrich, non-lethally, enough to turn a routine enlistment into a political incident and to put herself in a mine.

She did not regret attacking him. She regretted, sometimes, that she had not found some better way. But she had not seen one at the time, and she had not been raised to stand still while the people she loved were taken from her.

## Role as a potential spirit-shield

Per `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Spirit Ecology," a true spirit-shield is a being that reads to chaotic spirits as mighty enough to make them back off — a mage, a shaman, a druid, a priest, a great spirit, or something comparable. **The prophecy that led to the "valiant spirit" proclamation is about Irna, not Ron.** She is the one who has the potential to become a spirit-shield, very likely through the combination of her Tusk Clan lineage's strong will and training she has not yet received.

The interpreters misread it. "Valiant spirit" was translated into *martial* imagery — a warrior, a protector, someone with military might — and the conclusion drawn from that was that the prophecy must be about a Bean son destined for battle. Ron was then enlisted on that reading. It is not what the prophecy meant.

Irna's forced exile to Luchebert, arranged by Rhianna after the Didrich attack, is — by accident or providence — delivering her to precisely the place where her actual potential could be recognised and trained: Felicia's shrine, Father Elliot's priestly order, and the hill country whose quiet spiritual ecology is the single densest region on the continent. Whether Elliot knows this about her, whether Rhianna suspects it, and whether Irna herself can survive long enough to find out, are all live questions.
