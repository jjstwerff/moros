# The Storm Mage

- **Role:** The Lightning Tower's original owner; survivor of both the towers' seizing and a chaos-spirit encounter; the lever that could, in principle, break the Lightning Tower's shackle.
- **Species:** Human (presumed). Very old; nobody including him can say how old.
- **Name:** **Ornalis.** Very few people know this, and he does not use it himself. The name is written in old signed notes and a registry inside his house in the hills. Townsfolk call him *the old storm mage.*
- **Lives:** A **small stone cottage somewhere in the hills** that he returns to between wanderings — nobody in the valley can reliably point a party to it. The rest of his time he is *moving*, and every few months he walks into a hill or valley town to trade for food.
- **First met:** Almost always in a town, not in the hills. A party asking around the valley will hear about him long before they see him.

## What the party meets

The party is in a town — Elmsfield, a smaller hill settlement, a village along the trade road — when the Storm Mage walks in. The baker recognises him immediately and sets aside a loaf without asking. A child on a doorstep waves; he nods back, fails to focus on her, keeps walking. He is **not being handled**; he is being *attended to*, the way a village attends to someone it has decided is owed attention.

He will talk to anybody who speaks to him kindly. What he says is mostly fragments. If the party asks him something concrete, they will get a reply that is *almost* an answer, or is an answer to a different question, or is half a sentence that stops where it should have continued. This is not theatre. It is the shape of his mind.

He will not introduce himself. The townsfolk refer to him as *the old storm mage* or not at all.

## Personal goals — early and later

He cannot hold a goal across a day. The goals are framed for the party — what the campaign is doing *for* him, since he can no longer do it for himself. The fragments, accumulated, point to both.

- **Early-game — find the name in the cottage and read what was done.** Ornalis cannot tell the party what was taken from him; the cottage can. *Where this goes:* through **Corné Dunham**, **Gerhald** at his tower, or older members at **Luchebert**, who know the rough territory; through tracking him back from a town visit (the gentle path); to the cottage itself, where his **signed notes** name him *Ornalis* plainly and where letters from the **old-order mages who took the towers** carry names that exist nowhere else in writing. **Lady Rhianna** or **Elder Diederik** will identify the calming-working he hums as a fragment of pre-rift practice. The cottage gives the party what he cannot: a record of who he was, who took his tower, and the shape of a working tradition the rift broke. This is the campaign's quietest dungeon — a working library the size of a single cottage, where reading slowly is the work and taking nothing is the test.
- **Later-game — give him either rest or restoration; do not leave him as he is.** Two paths, each costing something. *Restoration:* months or years of slow work at **Felicia's shrine** with **Willow** tending and **Father Elliot** ratifying; partial at best; he may come back enough to give names and facts the campaign needs and no further. *Rest:* the **clarity bout** at the **Lightning Tower** is the only voice he has left to use deliberately. *"It is not going to work. I know that. Let it not work."* He said it knowing. The flight north and the crash at the **forbidden woods**' fringe is — for him — the parting his mind has been unable to reach by other means. After the crash he is worse, and the party who took him there carries that. **Andras** (`andras.md`) is the one who would, in time, come for what is left; the gatherers do not collect what is being held by the one they would collect, but Ornalis is no longer holding much. *Where this goes for the party:* to the question their characters are willing to ask — restore him at length, walk him to the tower for the parting, or leave him to the towns that have arranged to feed him. Each is legitimate. None are clean.

## How he reads at a glance

- **An old human**, white-haired, unkempt but not unclean — somebody has been looking after him in the ordinary ways a town looks after such a person. Eyes that **do not always focus** on what is in front of him.
- **Clothes from three or four eras at once.** What he is wearing today is whatever a village handed him most recently. The underlayers are older. Nothing matches.
- **Baubles clicking in his pockets as he walks.** Glass, crystal, bits of worked metal, occasionally something with faint residual workings still in it. He pays with these; he does not distinguish value.
- **A plain walking stick** he sometimes points at the sky — not a gesture people should read anything into, but something his body still does when the weather shifts.
- **Responds to weather before the rest of the room.** The air pressure changes; his head comes up, his eyes narrow, and for a moment he is somewhere else.

## Where to find him — town visits, hill wanderings, the cottage

He is **not unfindable**, but finding him takes one of three approaches:

1. **Wait in a town he is known to visit.** The simplest path. Every few months, less predictably sometimes, he comes down for supplies. Ask anywhere in the valley, or as far as Elmsfield and Luchebert, and a local will say *yes, the old storm mage comes through now and again.* Intercepting him on a supply walk is the gentlest path and most parties take it.
2. **Search the hills for him between visits.** **Corné Dunham**, **Luchebert's older members**, or **Gerhald at his tower** can give approximate territory — but he moves, and the search is real wilderness work.
3. **Find his cottage in the hills, and wait (or look around).** Nobody in the valley can point a party at it reliably; a party has to track him back from a town visit, follow partial directions drawn from a lucid fragment, or stumble on it. The cottage is where his name — and the rest of his old life — is actually written down. See §"At his cottage in the hills" below.

## At a town (where most scenes happen)

Most encounters with the Storm Mage happen in a small public space — a baker's doorway, a market stall, a stone bench against a wall in the square. He comes to towns because he needs food, not company, but he will accept company if it is offered gently.

Three things stand out about a town scene with him:

- **Nobody else in the town is afraid.** Shopkeepers greet him by their own habit; somebody will fetch him water if he looks tired; a child may hand him a stone he examines and pockets. The party should feel they have walked into a **social arrangement that already works** without them.
- **The baubles come out of his pockets as he shops.** He lays them on the counter and lets the shopkeeper choose. Shopkeepers have developed the practice of *taking one that fairly matches what he is buying* and returning the rest. The party watching this may notice the shopkeeper leaving a clearly valuable piece on the counter — and Hood-winking him would be possible, which is why nobody does.
- **Weather outside the window moves him.** A gust of wind, a clap of distant thunder, a sudden cloud across the sun — his head turns, his walking stick shifts, and for a beat he is half-present. The townsfolk know to wait that beat out before continuing a transaction.

## At his cottage in the hills

A small stone cottage, well-built once — a mage's dwelling, not a hermit's hut. Older than the trees around it. A party who has tracked him to it finds it half-lived-in: he returns between wanderings, he leaves again, the cottage stands between his visits.

Three things stand out on entering:

- **Old papers everywhere, in two distinct strata.** Scrolls and bound pages in the older layer — neat, dated, practitioner's-hand. Loose leaves and scrawled fragments in the newer layer — the work of his worse years, on top of the older. A careful party reading both can piece together *what he was* and *what he became*, in the same room.
- **A signed registry and old correspondence** — the one place his name is clearly written. *Ornalis.* A practitioner's seal appears beside the name in several places. Older letters addressed to him use the name plainly. The party who reads the notes learns this; he does not tell it.
- **A broken lock on the door, and tools nobody has used in centuries** on a work-bench by the east window. The lock has been broken a long time; he does not lock things. The tools include a small weather-glass that **still works if tapped** — a pre-rift instrument that should not have survived the centuries but has. There are other fragments of his old craft on and around the bench, half-functional, intact enough to suggest the practice he once held.

A party that spends time in the cottage can learn:

- **His name: Ornalis.** (In the signed notes.)
- **Pieces of his original practice** — the calming-working he still hums, other small workings laid out in his older hand in ways a mage, druid, or shaman student could study.
- **Fragments of the old-order mages' names** — in letters he received around the time of the towers' seizing. Some of this may be the only written record of those names anywhere.
- **Evidence of how his mind went.** A letter or journal entry where the handwriting shifts mid-page is often in the transitional layer. The rift between *Ornalis who wrote the first half* and *the storm mage who finished the paragraph* is visible to anyone who looks.

He will not be offended by a party reading his notes. He may not realise they have, and may not understand the question if asked. *"Did you read — those — yes. Yes, they're old. I wrote them, I think."* A party who takes something from the cottage, though, is another matter; see *reaction triggers* below.

## At his tower — the clarity bout

If the party gets the Storm Mage to the **Lightning Tower**, something unexpected happens.

The moment he crosses into the tower's working-space, he has **a bout of clarity** — not a slow return of lucidity, but a sudden one, the way a mage's mind *snaps into* a working it knows intimately. He whirls around the tower pulling levers, adjusting workings, opening settings idle for centuries. The party will recognise almost none of it; the point is that *he* does, and he is fast, and he is **not asking them**.

During this bout his voice is completely different — short clean sentences, the right words, direct instructions. See §"Fragments he mutters — and the one time his voice returns" below for what he says.

**The tower comes to life.** The lightning arcs steady. The levitation increases. The tower **lifts higher into the air and begins to travel north**.

It does not last. The flight is short. As the tower reaches the **fringes of the forbidden woods** (see `doc/places/forbidden_woods.md`), the chaos spirit's influence **undoes the working** — the same mechanism that once undid the storm mage himself. The levitation fails. The tower **crashes** into the fringes of the woods.

## After the crash — a man more broken than before

The party can follow the tower's flight and find the wreck. The tower is broken open, workings vented. The Storm Mage is **wandering around it** — alive, mostly intact, and **even more confused than before**. His clarity is gone the moment the working was. He is poking the tower from every side, muttering, not placing why he is here or what this object is. The second chaos-spirit encounter has pushed his mind further out of reach than the first did.

Whatever the party was planning for him afterwards — resettling him, handing him to Luchebert, finding his name — now happens in a harder register. He is in pain. His pain is not legible to him.

The crash site is also the party's **in-world introduction to the forbidden woods as a place worth engaging with**, not just a backdrop — and a thing Tod may wander out to investigate.

## Fragments he mutters — and the one time his voice returns

When the relevant trigger appears in a conversation, these are what he says. They are not coherent stories; they are the pieces that still surface.

### When the sky darkens with weather

> *"The frames, the frames. Third one still open. Somebody needs to close the third one."*
>
> *(Looks up, smiles briefly.)* *"Go on, then."*

### When handed food

> *"Thank you. Thank you. I had coin for this."*
>
> *(Rummages, produces a bauble.)* *"This? Or this? You choose."*

### When somebody uses the word *tower*

> *(A long pause. The fragment is almost legible.)* *"I had one of those."*

### When asked his name

> *"Something with an R. Or a T. It was a long time ago."*
>
> *(On a very clear day, rarely:)* *"Ornalis. That was — yes. Ornalis."* *(He will not remember saying it the next time they meet, and if asked again the same day he is likely to give a different answer. The name is **his**, but it is not reliably **in** him. The party can verify it in the cottage.)*

### When asked about the old-order mages (who took his tower)

> *"They came. They moved what was mine. I was not a person at the time. I am also not a person now. I was, in between."*

### When left alone in silence for a long while

> *(He hums three notes, repeated. A mage in the party with pre-rift training, or Diederik, or Rhianna, will recognise a **calming-working** — a small pattern from before the rift that practitioners used to settle nerves. He is working. He does not remember he is working.)*

### The clarity bout — when he crosses into the Lightning Tower

His voice changes in a single step. The fragments are gone. The sentences are whole.

> *"Third frame. Come on. Come on."*
>
> *"The anchor is where I left it. Of course it is. Nobody's touched anything. Nobody could. They moved the tower but they couldn't run it."*
>
> *"Don't stand there. You — yes, you — hold the lever down until I say. Good."*
>
> *(Working fast, giving instructions to the party as he goes. He does not explain. He assumes they will keep up.)*
>
> *"Alright. We lift. We go north. North is where it started and north is where it ends. It is not going to work. I know that. Let it not work."*
>
> *(That last sentence is the one the party will remember. He said it knowing.)*

After the crash, when the party reaches the wreck and finds him wandering, his voice is back to fragments. Worse. The lucid man was there for one scene and will not be there again.

## Mannerisms

- **When pressed** — loses the thread faster. Mutters about something adjacent. May walk away without being rude about it. The conversation has to start again, or wait for later.
- **When distraught** — complete dissolution. Repeats a single word. Points at the sky with the walking stick. The townsfolk, when present, will step in and gently lead him to sit.
- **When happy** — also rare. May hum a small working. May place a bauble in the speaker's hand and close their fingers around it. Smiles without focus, past the speaker's shoulder at something they cannot see.

## If the party does something — what he does

| If the party... | He responds by... |
|---|---|
| Speaks to him politely | Mostly responds, partially. Gives them fragments. May give them a bauble. |
| Demands something of him | Loses the thread faster. May walk away. The demand does not land. |
| Offers him food without being asked | Accepts gratefully. Offers a bauble. The party can take one (fairly) or decline. |
| Tries to manipulate him with a bauble or flattery | The townsfolk notice. If he has any patronage here, the patronage turns on the party. He himself does not notice. |
| Changes the weather near him (magically) | Reacts immediately. *"That's you. That's not the sky."* A rare moment of sharp recognition; passes quickly. |
| Tries to heal his mind (druidic, priestly, shamanic) | **Slow work.** Not a scene; months-to-years of sustained effort, and Felicia's shrine at Luchebert with Willow is the place most likely to help. Will not complete at any one table session. |
| Brings him to the Lightning Tower | The clarity bout happens. The tower flies. It crashes in the forbidden woods. He is worse afterwards. See §"At his tower — the clarity bout." |
| Asks him about the chaos spirit that broke him | Fragments. *"It was near the tower. It was — a shape. I touched what I should not have."* Nothing usable without much longer patience and a lucid window. |
| Threatens him | Confused more than frightened. Does not understand threat as threat. The townsfolk, when present, will remove the party. |
| Tries to stop him during the clarity bout | He does not listen. He is running a working; he will not acknowledge the interruption. The party that physically interferes may break the working and nothing will happen. |
| Follows him into the hills after he leaves a town | He knows somebody is following. Loses them within a day or two if he does not want the company. Accepts them if he does. Neither state is easy to read from outside. A patient follower may reach the cottage. |
| Enters the cottage uninvited (empty) | Nobody stops them. He is not there or does not notice. The notes are readable; *the name is findable here.* A party that reads respectfully takes nothing. |
| Enters the cottage uninvited (he is there) | Confused. *"You're here. Why are you here?"* He does not get angry; he loses the thread. A townsperson later hearing about this will be less welcoming to the party for a while. |
| Takes objects from the cottage | If they take notes, instruments, or personal items, the townsfolk find out through gossip eventually (a party member may wear or sell something recognisably his, or a shopkeeper may ask). Patronage of the party by the valley cools. He himself may or may not notice, depending on what was taken. |

## What he says about other people (rare, fragmentary)

These are the figures he has fragments about. Short on purpose — he does not remember many people cleanly.

| Person | What he says (in fragments, when the name comes up) |
|---|---|
| **The old-order mages** (who took his tower) | *"They moved what was mine. I was not a person at the time."* |
| **Tod / the chaos spirit in the forbidden woods** | *(He recoils slightly at the topic.)* *"Not the one that — not that one. Near the one. Near enough."* |
| **Gerhald** (the Hermit Shaman) | *"Across the water. Still there? Good. Good."* |
| **Laurent** | *(Blinks.)* *"Laurent. Laurent. Is he — no, someone else. Someone else."* (He does not know Laurent is still a demi-lich; the fragment is not acting on current information.) |
| **Narissa** (the First Mage) | *(On a clear day, rarely:)* *"I read her book. Everybody did. It didn't tell us anything useful."* |
| **The townsfolk who feed him** | *(Warm fragments.)* *"Kind. That one is kind. The bread."* |

## What he cannot or will not communicate

- **His name**, reliably. The fragment changes; the name of the day does not survive into the next visit. *(The name is Ornalis. He no longer knows it reliably. It is written in his cottage.)*
- **His age.** He does not know.
- **A coherent memory of the tower-taking.** He was there; he cannot reconstruct it for the party. *(His old notes in the cottage may contain fragments the tower-taking itself never allowed him to say aloud.)*
- **The exact nature of the chaos-spirit encounter that broke him.** Near the tower; something he touched; the rest is gone.
- **What he actually knows about the old-order mages by name**, unless a very long healing process brings some of it back. *(The letters in the cottage may carry some of these names, in the hands of those mages addressing him.)*

## What he can teach

Almost nothing directly. His mind is too fragmented for instruction.

But **observing him is instructive.** A druid, shaman, or priestly student studying the long-term effects of chaos-spirit proximity on a practitioner's mind can learn from him — by tending him, not by asking. Rhianna and Diederik would both understand why he is useful to a student in this way, and would not enjoy saying so.

His fragments, **taken together over months**, occasionally add up to a genuine old-mage-craft insight — the calming-working he hums is one of many. A careful listener collecting the fragments could, in principle, reconstruct several small pieces of pre-rift practice. This is a *long* project. It is not a session.

## DM notes

- **Play him as fragments, not as theatre.** Most of what he says does not make sense and is not supposed to. A DM who turns every sentence into a puzzle to be solved misses the texture. Some sentences are just pieces. The party will learn to listen for the ones that are not.
- **The clarity bout at the tower is the one scene his voice works.** It is the single most vivid moment in his arc, and the line *"It is not going to work. I know that. Let it not work."* should be delivered plainly. He knew. The tower still came down.
- **Ethical weight is real.** He cannot give meaningful consent. A party who takes him to the tower has made a decision *for* him. The campaign should neither punish nor reward this plainly — both Gerhald and Rhianna will want to hear about it if the party comes through them, and the party's explanation will matter.
- **The baubles are generative.** He empties his pockets in every shop. Sometimes one of them is genuinely valuable — a shopkeeper occasionally gives one back; sometimes the party catches one being returned and understands what the arrangement is. If a party member takes advantage of this, the townsfolk react before he does.
- **He is findable without a wilderness hunt.** The town-visit path is the gentle one and the file should steer parties that way by default. The hill-search is available but harder, and is rewarding primarily if the party reaches the **cottage** — where his name *Ornalis* and pieces of his original practice are preserved in writing. The cottage is the *information-source* in his arc; the towns are the *encounter-source*.
- **The name Ornalis is a real secret, gated by the cottage.** Towns do not know it. He does not reliably say it. A party reading his old signed notes learns it plainly; it is one of the few concrete discoveries his arc rewards. DMs should not volunteer the name until the cottage is reached.
- **The Lightning Tower's destruction is campaign-shaping.** Once it has happened, the wreck is at the forbidden woods' fringe and Tod becomes a more concrete presence. The Storm Mage is worse. One shackle is broken. The party carries these consequences forward.
- **Restoring him is months or years of work.** If the party wants to act on his condition, Felicia's shrine at Luchebert is the most likely avenue. Willow will not rush it. Elliot will not oppose it. The restoration, if it happens, is slow and partial.

## Role as spirit-shield (essentially none)

His mage-craft ran through the Lightning Tower's working-space, and without that he is not carrying a working presence that reads as a mighty-spirit-adjacent shield. He *was* one of the continent's spirit-shields, centuries ago. He is no longer. The chaos-warping that broke his mind also hollowed the reading; a chaos spirit near him now would not back off. The townsfolk who tend him carry no illusions about this — whatever protection those towns have, it is not from him.

## Relationships

- The **Lightning Tower**'s original owner and its lever, in the only sense that remains.
- A broken witness to the **taking of the towers** centuries ago. If he can be helped to speak coherently — slow work at Luchebert — he is one of the only living sources on what was done at that event and who the old-order mages were by name.
- Parallels **Narissa** (early mage, spirit-fused, long-lived) and **Laurent** (survivor of an era nobody else lived through). He is a quieter, smaller instance of the same pattern — a person the centuries left behind in a shape they can no longer clean up.
- Known across **the valley and hill towns** by shopkeepers, bakers, and villagers who have developed the practice of attending to him. He is nobody's burden and everybody's occasional visitor.

## Open questions

- The exact nature of the chaos-spirit encounter that broke him — where, when, which spirit.
- Whether restoration is possible or only containment.
- What he would say, after restoration, about the old-order mages — by name — and about what they actually did to the Lake of Tears. (Some of the names may be recoverable from the cottage's correspondence; a full account of the seizing is his to give and he cannot.)
