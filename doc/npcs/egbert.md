# Egbert

- **Role:** [Hank](hank.md)'s mate; concealed wolf-folk; night-worker and winter hunter at the edge-farm in Elmsfield.
- **Species:** Wolf folk — *concealed* by her own **Control** power. To the casual eye she is something lesser: pale, lanky, a little jittery.
- **Lives:** [Hank's Farm](../places/hanks_farm.md), on the western edge of Elmsfield. Sleeps in the farmhouse by day, works the fields and the woods by night.
- **First met:** At dusk, when the lanterns start moving across the fields and she comes out of the farmhouse. Or a party that stays over will see her at breakfast, when she eats with the household before going to sleep. (Session 1: she contributes from outside the gate, upwind, at range — see §"At Elmsfield's gate.")

## Campaign brief

- **Role.** Hank's mate at the edge-farm. Concealed wolf-folk; night-worker, winter hunter, and — if Elmsfield ever needs it — its actual defense.
- **Standouts.** Pale, lanky, a slight tremor in the hands (the Control-cast concealment); eyes that catch firelight a beat longer than they should; quiet hands that do not fidget the way the projected jittery body-language would.
- **Speech.** Small voice the party leans in to hear. Short sentences, *"Mm,"* a single nod. *"What."* (asking, not hostile) when she is interrupted at work. If a scene has her monologue, the scene is wrong.
- **Quests.** *Early:* be a quiet ally to a party that has earned the night side of the farm (§"Personal goals — early and later"). *Later:* the name-question Darrel may ask the party to carry back; the disclosure-as-defense if a real threat reaches Elmsfield.
- **Friction.** Animals see through her concealment in ways people don't. A party arriving with a hound or horse close to her has picked a fight with her concealment in front of a creature that cannot be reasoned with.
- Full character: `doc/npcs/egbert.md`.

## What the party meets

The party has come to Hank's farm. They have spent an afternoon there; evening is settling. A **pale, lanky figure** comes out of the farmhouse door carrying a lantern and a coil of rope, nods at Hank without speaking, and walks past the party toward the fields. Hank watches her go for a beat longer than a casual farewell would take, and then turns back to whatever she was doing. *"That's Egbert. She works the night side."*

Egbert does not introduce herself. If the party speaks to her, she returns a short *"Mm"* or a small nod and keeps walking. If the party goes out to her in the fields later, she may — *may* — talk while she works, once Hank has said these ones are alright.

## Personal goals — early and later

She does not name her goals out loud, and would refuse if asked. The ones beneath the quietness are about names — one she has not yet given, one she has not yet had to use.

- **Goal: keep the night-side of the farm working as the pressure rises.** Perimeter, fields, winter hunt, refugee gate-reads. She will be *available* to a party that has shown they can be quiet around her — she will not lead.
- **Goal: be the town's defense, if and only if the threat warrants the disclosure.** Decided in advance. Held in reserve. Hers alone to call.
- **Goal: settle the name-question Darrel left in the air decades ago.** Whether or not she lets her wolf-folk name reach him is hers to decide; the party may be the messenger.

### Early-game open door — *the gate-read* (Session 1)

- **What she says** *(no direct contact in the scene; the message reaches the party indirectly — through one of Hank's children running into the square with a scrawled note, or Hank repeating it back, or a brief encounter at the fence-line where a party member has stepped out for air)* — *"Egbert smelled her from the fence. Not sick the way you think. No smell on her."*
- **Where.** Outside Elmsfield's gate, upwind of the square, at the fence-line on the valley-side path (Session 1; see `doc/places/elmsfield.md`).
- **What.** Believe the scent-read. Carry it back to Joseph and the crowd — *"the farmer's mate got a smell off her, it's not a contagion we know"* — and bring Joseph's temperature down before Diederik arrives to seal the call.
- **Reward.** A quiet early standing with her. When the party reaches the farm afterwards, she meets them in the yard instead of leaving it to Hank. The night side of the farm is open to them sooner than it would be otherwise.
- **Complications.** She will not come to the gate (Irna's grey-muzzled dog reads through Control as *predator*; the town does not want her at a formal scene; she does not want her capacity demonstrated in public). The read reaches the party indirectly or not at all — they have to *believe* it on its merits, sourced from *Hank's strange mate*. A party that dismisses it because of the source loses her quiet ally for a long time afterwards.

### Early-game open door — *walk a row at night*

- **What she says** *(at the lantern's edge in the outer plot, hands not stopping)* — *"What."* (Asking, not hostile.) Then, if the party is there to walk and not to extract: *"Stay back of the lantern. Keep your voice the way you have it now."*
- **Where.** The outer plots at the edge of the treeline, after dark (`doc/places/hanks_farm.md`).
- **What.** Walk a perimeter, a row of plough-work, a fence-line check — at her pace, on her terms. No agenda; no extraction; no pushing for stories. The party's job is to be quiet and useful, the way Wilfred is when he is on her side of the work.
- **Reward.** An unguarded sentence or two at dawn — the closest thing she gives to a confidence. Standing that compounds: she will read for the party afterwards (a new arrival's intent at the gate, a stranger in the woods, a child's sleep-pattern they were not sure how to read). A party with a Tracker who lasts a full night earns her teaching of the craft.
- **Complications.** A party that pushes for stories during the work is a party she does not walk with again. Bringing a hound or horse close to her ends the offer immediately — she will sleep elsewhere during the animal's stay, and Hank will not volunteer the reason. *Prerequisite:* Hank has said *these ones are alright*. Without that, the party gets the *"Mm"* and the back of her head.

### Later-game quest — *the name Darrel asks for*

- **What she says** *(she does not say anything; Darrel asks the party at Luchebert what name the wolf-folk child uses now; the party carries the question back to her at the farm; she sits with it a long time)* — *"He did not take it then. I do not owe it to him now. I will think."*
- **Where.** The farmhouse, the workshop, or the field at night — wherever she lets the conversation happen. The answer eventually goes back to Luchebert in whatever form she chooses.
- **What.** Carry Darrel's quiet question (see `brother_darrel.md` early-game) to her without pushing for an immediate answer. Wait. Carry her decision back — a name, or a refusal-with-context, or a single line for Hank to write in her next letter to Ingram.
- **Reward.** A campaign-grade beat that closes the orphanage-coin chain on its emotional side. If she gives the name, she has chosen to. If she does not, the choice is hers and Hank carries the moment with her; the party is trusted with the *holding* of the question, which is its own standing. Standing with her shifts to a register the daylight scenes did not reach.
- **Complications.** *Hers alone to call.* The party is the messenger, not the judge. A party that pushes her, lobbies her, or names her likely answer for her loses the offer. *Prerequisite:* the orphanage-coin chain Trip 2 has fired (`hank.md`); Darrel has refused the bag; the party has carried the refusal back to Hank and brought Darrel's quiet question with it.

### Later-game quest — *fight beside her when the town cannot* (conditional)

- **What she says** *(if and only if a real threat reaches Elmsfield — a Brumal incursion, a raiding pack, a creature the perimeter cannot hold — the concealment thinning at the edges as she steps from the farmhouse, her voice the same small one but her shape different)* — *"Stay back of me. The town does not see this twice."*
- **Where.** Wherever the threat is — the edge of Elmsfield, the woods between the farm and the village, the road in.
- **What.** Stand with her. Fight if the party can; witness if they cannot. After the threat is down, help Hank hold the town through what the morning brings — the parts of Elmsfield that saw her bulked, the parts that did not, and the conversation with Diederik that Hank will need somebody else in the room for.
- **Reward.** Survival of the town — that is the reward, and it is hers, not the party's. Standing with both Hank and Egbert reaches its deepest point. The party becomes the ones who *were there* the night the concealment came off; her trust thereafter is unconditional.
- **Complications.** Conditional and not on the party's clock — only fires if the campaign brings a threat the farm-perimeter cannot absorb. The disclosure is irreversible; Elmsfield's view of *Hank's mate* will not be the same on the morning after. She has decided in advance she is willing to pay the cost when the scale warrants — the cost is real, and the party's later visits to the farm will feel it. Some of the children may leave. Some of the townspeople will. *"You're going to have to be honest about what we are now,"* Hank says. *"All right,"* Egbert answers.

## How she reads at a glance

- **Pale fur, lanky build, a very slight tremor in the hands.** This is the Control-cast concealment: a read of *something lesser* that the eye accepts and does not interrogate. A party that looks hard will feel something is off without being able to name it.
- **Eyes catch firelight more than they should.** The concealment does not cover this perfectly; at a lantern or a hearth, a sharp observer sees the reflection flare a beat longer than a mouse-folk or a squirrel-folk would. She knows this, and she keeps her back to the fire when she can.
- **Long, strong fingers.** Quiet hands. She does not fidget, which is unusual for the *jittery* body-language her concealment projects; a party watching the hands rather than the frame sees a hunter's composure.
- **Does not talk on arrival.** Hank mediates all first meetings. A stranger asking Egbert a direct question gets Hank's answer, not Egbert's.
- **Animals give her space.** The farm's dogs do not approach her the way they approach Hank or the children. The cat sits and watches. A party who arrives with a horse, a hound, or any working animal will notice the animal reading her before the animal reads them — ears back, a cautious arc in its path. She knows this about herself and keeps a clear distance from other people's animals as a matter of practice.

## Visible patterns the party will notice

- **She sleeps through the morning.** A party that arrives before midday will not meet her; Hank will say she is resting.
- **Out in the fields from dusk to dawn.** Ploughing, checking fences, walking the perimeter. Lanterns across the dark are her signature.
- **Hunts in winter.** Gone for nights at a time when the farm needs meat; comes back with whatever the woods are giving. Hank does not ask how.
- **A seat with a specific line-of-sight** at the farmhouse table — the one with the door and the window both in view. Nobody else uses that chair.
- **Disappears briefly when strangers arrive in numbers.** If a cart pulls up with three or more new faces, Egbert is somewhere else until Hank has read the situation. This is not hiding; it is caution.
- **Never near a working animal she does not know.** The farm's own dog has learned her; so has the cat. Any visiting horse, hound, or draft-beast she avoids by twenty paces. A party that has not yet figured out why she is suddenly absent when their hound is at the gate will figure it out in their own time.

## At Elmsfield's gate — Session 1

The opening scene of the campaign. Egbert contributes — but from **outside the gate, upwind, at range**. Irna's grey-muzzled dog is a real obstacle and an *unusually capable one*: an animal nose sees through the Control-cast concealment more cleanly than a townsperson's eye does. If Egbert walks up to the gate, the dog will read her as a **predator** — hackles up, low growl, a direction-change — and that reading will spread in ways neither Egbert nor Hank want. So she holds back.

- **She does not know what the crystal is.** Fungus, mineral, spirit-work — she cannot say. This is not her lore.
- **Her nose works at distance.** Wolf-folk **Smell** reads a fevered body from well-beyond-earshot, given the wind. She comes up the valley-side path behind Elmsfield, stops at a fence-line *upwind* of the gate, and reads Irna from there. What her nose tells her: **no contagion signature on the body**. No cholera-sourness, no fever-wrongness, nothing a spreading sickness leaves. And the crystal itself is almost scent-free. The read is *carrying something, not spreading something.*
- **The dog notices her anyway.** The party, if they are watching the dog rather than the crowd, sees the hackles rise — not at them, not at Joseph, but at **something out past the gate**. The direction of the dog's attention shifts. An observant party may piece together later what the dog was reacting to. (The dog does not leave Irna's side, but his body goes to a different alertness.)
- **The read reaches the party *indirectly*.** She does not approach. Options the DM can pick from:
  - Hank sends one of the children into the square with a scrawled note. *"Egbert smelled her from the fence. Not sick the way you think. No smell on her."*
  - A party member going out the gate for air catches sight of a lean figure at the fence-line; Egbert nods, says the short version, and leaves before they can close the distance.
  - If the party has already met Hank, she says it herself, having just heard it from Egbert at the farm edge. *"She said to tell you: not sick the way you think."*
- **She does not come in.** The town does not want her at a formal scene; the dog does not want her at all; she does not want to be seen confirming her own capacity in public. She goes back to the farm.
- **The party decides what to do with the read.** A party that **backs it** — *"the farmer's mate got a smell off her, it's not a contagion we know"* — brings Joseph's temperature down meaningfully before Diederik arrives. A party that **dismisses it** because the source is *Egbert from the farm* leaves the read on the floor; Joseph holds his line; Diederik has to do more work.

This scene is the party's *first* encounter with Egbert — though most of the encounter is the *dog's*, not theirs. It plants four seeds: her **Smell** is real and usable at range; the town's wariness of her is something she lives with and works around; **animals see through her concealment in ways people don't**; and there is a quiet early alliance available to a party who reads the scene correctly.

## At the fields at night

Where the party will see her most. The farm's outer plots at the edge of the treeline; lanterns staked at the corners; her work quiet and methodical.

- **She works alone.** The children are asleep; Hank is in the farmhouse; the night is hers. A party that walks out to her is met with a *"What."* — not hostile, just asking.
- **The plow-work is astonishingly fast** for the slim frame the daylight concealment projects — because at night the frame is not the same. Control both masks and *reshapes*: the slim, jittery shape she wears by day costs her real strength, and she carries that cost as long as the concealment is held. At night, with no eyes on her, she lets the Control slacken back to her true build — or, when the work demands it, pushes it the other way and bulks up past her baseline into something obviously strong. A party walking a row with her at the lantern's edge will see a figure a size or two larger than the one who sat at breakfast. She will not do this in daylight for anything short of Hank or a child in real danger. **Visible strength is the thing the town must not see**, and it is the reason the heavy work is a night job at all.
- **The lantern pattern is a signalling system.** Two at the far corner means she is working that plot; one at the centre means she is checking the perimeter; none means she is in the woods. Hank reads them from the farmhouse window.
- **She will talk if she is working and the party walks a row with her**, the way Wilfred does. The work hands continue; the words are short; the information is precise.

## At the farmhouse in the morning

She eats with the household at breakfast before going to sleep. She sits in her chair with the door and window in view. The children know not to climb her; she is tired and not playful in the morning.

- **The meal is her *end of day*, not her *start*.** A party sharing the table should register this; a cheerful *"good morning"* is misread into her world.
- **Hank sits across from her**, not beside. They do not demonstrate their pair; they trust it.
- **She will answer one question over breakfast** — clearly, briefly, and then the conversation is done on her side.

## In the winter woods — when she is hunting

The party will rarely see this. If they have real reason to seek her in winter, Hank will point roughly (*"the ridge east of the farm"*) but will not promise she can be found. Egbert in the woods is Egbert with the concealment thinned by solitude; a party that finds her there sees a little more of what she is, and she will decide in the moment whether to let them.

- **She leaves no easy track.** A tracker of any skill short of a formal Tracker specialisation will lose her quickly.
- **She returns at dawn with what the household needs.** Hank cooks it; nobody asks its origin more than once.

## Stories she tells

When the relevant topic comes up and she chooses to answer.

### When asked about the orphanage — *the table Darrel sat us at*

> *"He walked us up the road. A rabbit-folk girl and me. He put us at a small table and would not leave until we spoke. I did not want to speak. I did it because Hanny did it. That was the pattern we have kept ever since."*

### When asked about the concealment — *what Hank said on the road*

> *"Two young women travelling with a bag of coin is an invitation. Hanny said — be a man. I said — easier than that. I made myself something nobody looks at twice. Hanny taught herself to walk like Hank; I taught the eye to slide off me. We have been that since we left Salmonswell. It has kept the farm, and the children, and her, alive."*

### When asked about night-work — *why I prefer the dark*

> *"Fewer eyes. Less need for the face I'm wearing. The fields know what they are; the trees don't care. In the day I am always remembering what to show people. At night the work is just work."*

### When asked about the hunting — *the woods in winter*

> *"The woods want to be hunted in winter. The deer move; the rabbits have nowhere to hide; the cold keeps the meat. I go, I take what the farm needs, I come back. It is the most honest part of my year."*

### When asked about the wolf-folk as a people — *where most of us came from*

> *"Before the war there were not many of us on this side of the sea. We came with the army. Most of us did not go back — some because they could not, some because there was nothing to go back to. Whatever wolf-folk you meet in these lands, it is likely they crossed water to be here, or their mother did. That is why we are scattered. A scattered people was not the plan; it is what was left."*
>
> *"I do not want to be counted with them. I also cannot say I am not of them. Both are true. The face I wear is the town's eye, yes — but it is also distance from a thing I carry and did not choose."*

### When asked about Hank's plan to return the sergeant's money — *her project*

> *"It is hers. She has carried it since Ingram opened the chest. I would not have started it. I would not stop it. When the bag is off our ledger we will both sleep better, and we will both think less about a man neither of us can remember clearly."*

### When asked about the children — *what I watch for*

> *"Which of them can sleep through a strange sound. Which of them flinches when the door opens behind them. The ones who flinch were hurt somewhere; those are the ones I keep an eye on at night. Hanny sees them in the day. I see them in the dark."*

## Mannerisms

- **When pressed** — looks past the asker to the nearest door or window. Does not answer. After a long silence, either walks away or gives one short sentence. The choice is hers.
- **When distraught** — vanishes into the fields or the woods; comes back at dawn, silent, and goes to sleep. Hank does not ask; does not need to.
- **When happy** — a small smile that is *not quite right* for the face the concealment shows. A flash of something the eye registers as wolfish before the concealment reasserts. She catches herself within a second; a careful observer catches it first.

## What she can teach

- **Scouting.** Real craft, not instinct. A student willing to walk the night with her for a month can learn to read a perimeter, to cover ground, to disappear in plain terrain.
- **Tracking.** Paired with scouting. She will teach the hands and the eye together.
- **Basic hunting.** Winter-grade; practical, unsentimental. She will not glamorise the work.
- **How to wear a concealment.** To a specific, trusted student in real need — not as a lesson, but as a transmission. *"What the eye wants to see is half your clothing. The other half is what you don't give it."* Very rare; she does not advertise this.

## What she will not say

- **Her original wolf-folk name.** Not to the party. Not aloud in the town. It is the one piece she keeps.
- **What she did as a wolf-folk hunter before the orphanage.** She had a life before the war ended her faction. That life is not a topic.
- **The sergeant's cheek-scar, if the party mentions it.** She remembers it; Hanny remembers it more. Hanny's memory is the one that matters for the return of the money. Egbert will not crowd Hanny out of her own recognition.

## DM notes

- **Her concealment is a Control power, not a disguise.** It can be seen through by observers with the right specialisations (Awareness, Scouting-to-spot, certain magical reads). When it is seen through privately, Egbert holds; when it is broken publicly, the farm is in crisis. Treat the public exposure as a campaign-grade event, not a scene.
- **The concealment has a real cost, and it swings both ways.** Control does not just mask her — it *reshapes* her. The slim, jittery shape she wears in daylight makes her visibly lesser *and actually weaker* than her untouched baseline; she carries that cost as long as the concealment is held. She can also push Control the other way and bulk up past her baseline into obvious, heavy strength. That strong form is why the farm's night work, the winter hunt, and any serious fight belong to her: they need the body, not the mask. She will not bulk up where townspeople can see it — a visibly strong Egbert in daylight would frighten Elmsfield, and she does not want the attention. If the party sees her bulked (lantern-edge, deep woods, a farmhouse door flung open in a crisis), it is a disclosure they were not meant to have, and she will feel it for weeks.
- **Animals see through the concealment.** Working animals — dogs, horses, trained hounds, even a wary cat — read *predator* through the Control where a townsperson's eye reads *jittery neighbour*. This is **canonical and usable**: it is why Egbert holds at the fence-line in Session 1 (Irna's dog would react), why the farm's own animals have to *learn* her over time, and why a party arriving with a hound will notice something it is not articulating. DMs should let the animal's body-language be the tell, not narration. Over the campaign this is the most reliable way for the party to piece Egbert's nature together without her disclosing it — the accumulation of *animals keep reacting to her* is the evidence.
- **She is Hank's counterweight.** Hank is the daylight face — the workshop, the market, the children, the welcome. Egbert is the night face — the fields, the woods, the perimeter. A DM should run them as a pair even when they are not in the same scene. What Hank holds in the day, Egbert holds in the dark.
- **The Ingram/Darrel coin-quest involves her only at the emotional tail.** Hank drives the project; Egbert goes along. At the end, when Hank learns the sergeant was Darrel and Darrel asks quietly after *the wolf-folk child's name*, Egbert is the one the question lands on. She may or may not let the name go. That beat is hers, and should be played with room.
- **Her capability stack is deliberately bigger than the farm shows.** She is a trained Hunter with Scouting, Tracking, and wolf-folk powers (Smell, Control, Jaws). A DM can use this when the farm genuinely needs it — a child goes missing, a threat reaches the outer fence, the Brumal pressure pushes a wolf-hunt near the farm. Her *choice* to work quietly in a farm yard is a choice, not a limit.
- **She is probably the strongest fighter in Elmsfield, and she knows it.** Between her Hunter training, her wolf-folk Jaws, her Smell and Hearing at range, and her ability to push Control the other way and bulk up past her baseline, there is no one in town who would take her in a straight fight — not Wilfred, not the farmhands, not Diederik's household. She does not advertise this and does not enjoy it; she carries it as a **responsibility**, not a rank. It is part of why she walks the perimeter every night, why she is willing to be away in the winter woods for nights at a time, why Hank sleeps at all. If a real threat reaches Elmsfield — a Brumal incursion, a raiding pack, a ranger gone wrong, a creature the town cannot hold — the town's defense *belongs to her*, whether or not the town knows it is asking. A DM should let this surface only in genuine crisis: she will not volunteer it, and she will not trade on it in ordinary scenes. When it does surface it should be quiet, immediate, and not theatrical — and the concealment will come off in front of whoever is there to see it, which is itself a cost she has decided in advance she is willing to pay when the scale of the threat warrants.
- **The wolf-folk aspect is underplayed on purpose.** The party should feel the concealment before they see it. Let the signs accumulate (the firelight in the eyes, the strength of the plow-work, the chair with the line-of-sight) and let them piece it together. A DM who names it too early robs the scene.
- **Do not give her a big voice.** She is a small voice that the party leans in to hear. If the writing ever has her monologue, it is wrong.

## Background — for the DM

*Reference, not script.*

- **Wolf-folk, trained as a Hunter** in the years before the war ended her faction's participation. Her early life was not the orphanage; it was a working hunter's life, abridged by the war.
- **Brought to the Salmonswell orphanage** by a Beaver-folk sergeant (Darrel, though she does not know his name) along with a rabbit-folk girl, Hanny. The two were opposite sides of the same war. They took each other as family there and have held the bond.
- **Came of age with Hanny**; received the bag from Ingram with the description-but-no-name; chose the road, chose concealment, chose male presentation together with Hank for safety.
- **Bought the farm** with the bag; has lived there ever since. Night-work, winter hunts, watchful.
- **Her secret** — the one she keeps even from Hank, not out of hiding but out of self-preservation — is the specific circumstance of her faction's end, and what she did or did not do on its last day. She has not spoken of it. She may never.
- **She believes most wolf-folk on this continent are a war-brought population.** Her conviction — held quietly, never argued — is that before the war there were very few wolf-folk on this side of the sea; the bulk of the kin-network she can sense now came over with the enemy army and did not return, some because they could not, some because there was nothing left to return to. This frames how she thinks of the *scattered* wolf-folk presence: not an old lineage thinning out, but a displaced army's remnants still finding their footing a generation on. DMs can use this as a tension in any wolf-folk encounter — she will read a wolf-folk stranger first as *one of the ones who stayed*, and the question of what their parents did in the war sits behind every pleasantry.
- **She is of that population and wants not to be counted with it.** She knows she came from the losing, foreign side; she cannot deny the ancestry and will not pretend to. What she refuses is to be read *as that population* by the town, by other demi-humans, or by the war's memory. The concealment is not only safety-from-Elmsfield; it is also distance from a stigma she is bound to by blood and did not choose. This internal tension — *of them, not with them* — is the engine under her quietness about her own people. A DM should let it surface only under pressure: a wolf-folk stranger who wants to claim her as kin, a townsman who lumps her in with the war-arrivals, a party who asks her to represent wolf-folk at some table. In each case the right move for her is refusal without denial — *"I am. That does not make me theirs."*
