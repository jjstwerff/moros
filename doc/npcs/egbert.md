# Egbert

- **Role:** [Hank](hank.md)'s mate; concealed wolf-folk; night-worker and winter hunter at the edge-farm in Elmsfield.
- **Species:** Wolf folk — *concealed* by her own **Control** power. To the casual eye she is something lesser: pale, lanky, a little jittery.
- **Lives:** [Hank's Farm](../places/hanks_farm.md), on the western edge of Elmsfield. Sleeps in the farmhouse by day, works the fields and the woods by night.
- **First met:** At dusk, when the lanterns start moving across the fields and she comes out of the farmhouse. Or a party that stays over will see her at breakfast, when she eats with the household before going to sleep.

## What the party meets

The party has come to Hank's farm. They have spent an afternoon there; evening is settling. A **pale, lanky figure** comes out of the farmhouse door carrying a lantern and a coil of rope, nods at Hank without speaking, and walks past the party toward the fields. Hank watches her go for a beat longer than a casual farewell would take, and then turns back to whatever she was doing. *"That's Egbert. She works the night side."*

Egbert does not introduce herself. If the party speaks to her, she returns a short *"Mm"* or a small nod and keeps walking. If the party goes out to her in the fields later, she may — *may* — talk while she works, once Hank has said these ones are alright.

## How she reads at a glance

- **Pale fur, lanky build, a very slight tremor in the hands.** This is the Control-cast concealment: a read of *something lesser* that the eye accepts and does not interrogate. A party that looks hard will feel something is off without being able to name it.
- **Eyes catch firelight more than they should.** The concealment does not cover this perfectly; at a lantern or a hearth, a sharp observer sees the reflection flare a beat longer than a mouse-folk or a squirrel-folk would. She knows this, and she keeps her back to the fire when she can.
- **Long, strong fingers.** Quiet hands. She does not fidget, which is unusual for the *jittery* body-language her concealment projects; a party watching the hands rather than the frame sees a hunter's composure.
- **Does not talk on arrival.** Hank mediates all first meetings. A stranger asking Egbert a direct question gets Hank's answer, not Egbert's.

## Visible patterns the party will notice

- **She sleeps through the morning.** A party that arrives before midday will not meet her; Hank will say she is resting.
- **Out in the fields from dusk to dawn.** Ploughing, checking fences, walking the perimeter. Lanterns across the dark are her signature.
- **Hunts in winter.** Gone for nights at a time when the farm needs meat; comes back with whatever the woods are giving. Hank does not ask how.
- **A seat with a specific line-of-sight** at the farmhouse table — the one with the door and the window both in view. Nobody else uses that chair.
- **Disappears briefly when strangers arrive in numbers.** If a cart pulls up with three or more new faces, Egbert is somewhere else until Hank has read the situation. This is not hiding; it is caution.

## At Elmsfield's gate — Session 1

The opening scene of the campaign. Egbert arrives **second** — after [Farmer Joseph](farmer_joseph.md) has raised the first fear-voice, and before [Elder Diederik](elder_diederik.md) arrives with the full diagnosis. Hank sent her, or she heard the commotion from the farm; either way, she is the first competent pair of hands at the gate, and she is the one the town is most uneasy about.

- **She does not know what the crystal is.** Fungus, mineral, spirit-work — she cannot say. This is not her lore.
- **Her nose works.** Wolf-folk **Smell** applied to a fevered body reads, at close range, the specific *wrongness* of a contagious sickness — cholera-sourness, fever-stink, the body's alarm of *spreading something*. She gets none of that from Irna.
- **The crystal is almost scent-free.** It has no smell a wolf-folk nose would flag. This is, the campaign will later confirm, part of *why* Irna could get infected — nothing in the body's natural warning system told her to stay away from the Lake-of-Tears fungus. Egbert is the first person at the gate to notice this absence, and she reads it correctly: *carrying something, not spreading something.*
- **She says it short.** *"She's not sick the way you think. No smell on her."* She does not elaborate. She does not stay.
- **The town listens to her despite themselves.** She is the neighbour they are wary of; she is also the one who has just given a concrete answer a frightened man can hold. A party that **backs her on the read** brings Joseph's temperature down meaningfully before Diederik even arrives. A party that **dismisses her because the town is wary of her** leaves the read on the floor; Joseph holds his line; Diederik has to do more work.
- **She is gone before Diederik is speaking.** The town does not want her at a formal scene and she does not want to be at one. She goes back to the farm. The party who backed her will find her easier to approach at Hank's that evening.

This scene is the party's *first* encounter with Egbert, almost certainly earlier than they would otherwise meet her. It plants three seeds: her **Smell** is real and usable; the town's wariness of her is something she lives with openly; and there is a quiet early alliance available to a party who reads the scene correctly.

## At the fields at night

Where the party will see her most. The farm's outer plots at the edge of the treeline; lanterns staked at the corners; her work quiet and methodical.

- **She works alone.** The children are asleep; Hank is in the farmhouse; the night is hers. A party that walks out to her is met with a *"What."* — not hostile, just asking.
- **The plow-work is astonishingly fast** for the size of the frame projecting the concealment. A party watching closely will notice the contradiction — a jittery, slight figure doing the labor of somebody half again her size. The concealment masks her; it does not power her body, and what her body actually is does the work.
- **The lantern pattern is a signalling system.** Two at the far corner means she is working that plot; one at the centre means she is checking the perimeter; none means she is in the woods. Hank reads them from the farmhouse window.
- **She will talk if she is working and the party walks a row with her**, the way Wilfred does. The work hands continue; the words are short; the information is precise.

## At the farmhouse in the morning

She eats with the household at breakfast before going to sleep. She sits in her chair with the door and window in view. The children know not to climb her; she is tired and not playful in the morning.

- **The meal is her *end of day*, not her *start*.** A party sharing the table should register this; a cheerful *"good morning"* is misread into her world.
- **Hank sits across from her**, not beside. They do not demonstrate their pair; they trust it.
- **She will answer one question over breakfast** — clearly, briefly, and then the conversation is done on her side.

## In the winter woods — when she is hunting

The party will rarely see this. If they have real reason to seek her in winter, Hank will point roughly (*"the ridge east of the farm"*) but will not promise she can be found. Egbert in the woods is Egbert with the concealment thinned by solitude; a party that finds her there sees a little more of what she is, and she will decide in the moment whether to let them.

- **She leaves no easy track.** A tracker of any skill short of a formal Tracker specialisation will lose her quickly.
- **She returns at dawn with what the household needs.** Hank cooks it; nobody asks its origin more than once.

## What she will tell — and when

| Topic | When she'll speak |
|---|---|
| Farm work, night shifts, the plots, the fences | Freely, while working, if the party is walking with her. |
| The winter hunting | Briefly. *"The woods give what the woods give."* |
| The orphanage in Salmonswell | Once the topic is raised and Hank has not shut it down. Short. |
| The sergeant who brought them | Carefully. She remembers differently than Hanny; less vivid, harder-edged. |
| Her old name (pre-Egbert) | Never, without very deep trust and Hank's tacit sign-off. The road-name is who she is now. |
| Her concealment | Never openly. A party that has read her will find her unmoved — neither confirming nor denying — until they have earned the disclosure. |
| Her wolf-folk training from before the orphanage | Only once she has decided to teach or to fight alongside. Not a topic of conversation. |
| Hank's plan to return the money | Supportive without being enthusiastic. *"Hanny's project. I'll see it through with her."* |
| The children at the farm | Watchful opinions; kept short. She sees which ones are carrying things they haven't said. |

## Stories she tells

When the relevant topic comes up and she chooses to answer.

### When asked about the orphanage — *the table Darrel sat us at*

> *"He walked us up the road. A rabbit-folk girl and me. He put us at a small table and would not leave until we spoke. I did not want to speak. I did it because Hanny did it. That was the pattern we have kept ever since."*

### When asked about the concealment — *what Hank said on the road*

> *"Two young women travelling with a bag of coin is an invitation. Hanny said — be a man. I said — easier than that. I made myself something nobody looks at twice. Hanny taught herself to walk like Hank; I taught the eye to slide off me. We have been that since we left Salmonswell. It has kept the farm, and the children, and her, alive."*

### When asked about night-work — *why I prefer the dark*

> *"Fewer eyes. Less need for the face I'm wearing. The fields know what they are; the trees don't care. In the day I am always remembering what to show people. At night the work is just work."*

### When asked about the hunting — *the woods in winter*

> *"The woods want to be hunted in winter. The deer move; the rabbits have nowhere to hide; the cold keeps the meat. I go, I take what the farm needs, I come back. It is the most honest part of my year."*

### When asked about Hank's plan to return the sergeant's money — *her project*

> *"It is hers. She has carried it since Ingram opened the chest. I would not have started it. I would not stop it. When the bag is off our ledger we will both sleep better, and we will both think less about a man neither of us can remember clearly."*

### When asked about the children — *what I watch for*

> *"Which of them can sleep through a strange sound. Which of them flinches when the door opens behind them. The ones who flinch were hurt somewhere; those are the ones I keep an eye on at night. Hanny sees them in the day. I see them in the dark."*

## Mannerisms

- **When pressed** — looks past the asker to the nearest door or window. Does not answer. After a long silence, either walks away or gives one short sentence. The choice is hers.
- **When distraught** — vanishes into the fields or the woods; comes back at dawn, silent, and goes to sleep. Hank does not ask; does not need to.
- **When happy** — a small smile that is *not quite right* for the face the concealment shows. A flash of something the eye registers as wolfish before the concealment reasserts. She catches herself within a second; a careful observer catches it first.

## If the party does something — what she does

| If the party... | She responds by... |
|---|---|
| Reads her as wolf-folk, privately, to her | Goes still. Does not confirm; does not deny. *"Keep it there."* The conversation does not continue at the table. |
| Reveals her as wolf-folk publicly in Elmsfield | The concealment does not crack; she does. Leaves. Is gone for three days. The farm closes around Hank and the children. The party has broken something that will take seasons to rebuild, if it rebuilds. |
| Threatens Hank | The concealment frays at the edges. Fingers flex. She does not raise her voice. *"Leave."* If the threat continues, the concealment drops entirely and the party is dealing with a wolf-folk hunter in her own yard. |
| Threatens a child at the farm | Same as above, with less warning. |
| Asks to learn scouting or tracking | Tests over time. A student who walks the night with her once without complaint is kept; a student who wants the craft in the day, in Elmsfield's eye, is sent to somebody else. |
| Follows her into the woods at night | Noticed immediately. If the intent is to learn, she will turn and set a pace that tests; if the intent is to catch her at something, she vanishes and the party will not find her until morning. |
| Brings her news of the wolf-folk elsewhere | She will sit down for that. Short questions, careful. Wolf-folk are a scattered people; what news exists is news. |
| Brings her the sergeant's description | Listens. Repeats nothing back. Finds Hank afterwards and tells her in her own way. |
| Insults wolf-folk in her hearing | One warning, soft. *"Don't."* If repeated, she leaves the scene and the party's standing at the farm drops audibly. |
| Offers to walk the night with her, no agenda | Accepted if the party has shown they can be quiet. She will not explain the work; she expects them to watch. A party that makes it through a full night with her earns something she does not give otherwise — an unguarded sentence or two at dawn. |
| Backs her smell-read at the gate in Session 1 | She notices. She says nothing at the time. When the party comes to the farm later, she meets them in the yard instead of leaving it to Hank. An early, quiet standing with her — earned in the one scene where her wariness-from-the-town was on full display and the party chose her anyway. |
| Dismisses her smell-read at the gate in Session 1 | She notices. She says nothing at the time. At the farm afterwards she lets Hank do all the talking; the party will not speak directly with Egbert for several visits. Recoverable, but slow. |

## What she can teach

- **Scouting.** Real craft, not instinct. A student willing to walk the night with her for a month can learn to read a perimeter, to cover ground, to disappear in plain terrain.
- **Tracking.** Paired with scouting. She will teach the hands and the eye together.
- **Basic hunting.** Winter-grade; practical, unsentimental. She will not glamorise the work.
- **How to wear a concealment.** To a specific, trusted student in real need — not as a lesson, but as a transmission. *"What the eye wants to see is half your clothing. The other half is what you don't give it."* Very rare; she does not advertise this.

## What she will not say

- **Her original wolf-folk name.** Not to the party. Not aloud in the town. It is the one piece she keeps.
- **What she did as a wolf-folk hunter before the orphanage.** She had a life before the war ended her faction. That life is not a topic.
- **The sergeant's cheek-scar, if the party mentions it.** She remembers it; Hanny remembers it more. Hanny's memory is the one that matters for the return of the money. Egbert will not crowd Hanny out of her own recognition.

## DM notes

- **Her concealment is a Control power, not a disguise.** It can be seen through by observers with the right specialisations (Awareness, Scouting-to-spot, certain magical reads). When it is seen through privately, Egbert holds; when it is broken publicly, the farm is in crisis. Treat the public exposure as a campaign-grade event, not a scene.
- **She is Hank's counterweight.** Hank is the daylight face — the workshop, the market, the children, the welcome. Egbert is the night face — the fields, the woods, the perimeter. A DM should run them as a pair even when they are not in the same scene. What Hank holds in the day, Egbert holds in the dark.
- **The Ingram/Darrel coin-quest involves her only at the emotional tail.** Hank drives the project; Egbert goes along. At the end, when Hank learns the sergeant was Darrel and Darrel asks quietly after *the wolf-folk child's name*, Egbert is the one the question lands on. She may or may not let the name go. That beat is hers, and should be played with room.
- **Her capability stack is deliberately bigger than the farm shows.** She is a trained Hunter with Scouting, Tracking, and wolf-folk powers (Smell, Control, Jaws). A DM can use this when the farm genuinely needs it — a child goes missing, a threat reaches the outer fence, the Brumal pressure pushes a wolf-hunt near the farm. Her *choice* to work quietly in a farm yard is a choice, not a limit.
- **The wolf-folk aspect is underplayed on purpose.** The party should feel the concealment before they see it. Let the signs accumulate (the firelight in the eyes, the strength of the plow-work, the chair with the line-of-sight) and let them piece it together. A DM who names it too early robs the scene.
- **Do not give her a big voice.** She is a small voice that the party leans in to hear. If the writing ever has her monologue, it is wrong.

## Background — for the DM

*Reference, not script.*

- **Wolf-folk, trained as a Hunter** in the years before the war ended her faction's participation. Her early life was not the orphanage; it was a working hunter's life, abridged by the war.
- **Brought to the Salmonswell orphanage** by a Beaver-folk sergeant (Darrel, though she does not know his name) along with a rabbit-folk girl, Hanny. The two were opposite sides of the same war. They took each other as family there and have held the bond.
- **Came of age with Hanny**; received the bag from Ingram with the description-but-no-name; chose the road, chose concealment, chose male presentation together with Hank for safety.
- **Bought the farm** with the bag; has lived there ever since. Night-work, winter hunts, watchful.
- **Her secret** — the one she keeps even from Hank, not out of hiding but out of self-preservation — is the specific circumstance of her faction's end, and what she did or did not do on its last day. She has not spoken of it. She may never.

## Relationships

- Mate of **[Hank](hank.md)** (Hanny); raised together in the Salmonswell orphanage after the war, from opposite factions. Darrel — who brought them there — knew and used Hanny's name; he never took Egbert's. She was enemy-faction, and he would not let himself.
- Beneficiary of a Beaver-folk sergeant's bag of coins at the orphanage. Same public-relative/private-doubt story as Hank, same description from **[Ingram](ingram.md)**, same absent name. Supports Hank's long project of returning the money; would not have started it herself.
- Connected loosely to the scattered **wolf-folk** (see `../claude/LORE.md` §"Clans and their shape"). No active clan ties she acknowledges publicly.
