# I Didn't Do Nothing

I didn't do nothing!

Egbert was pulled from behind the big machine by his tail. The hand that gripped him was the
size of a small tree. He could see the full frame of the bull against the hot metal glow of
the furnaces — the one who had hunted him through the factory to this point.

Don't they all say. What have you pilfered today, young one?

The bull's grip did not loosen.

Well, it is over now. The mayor has finally seen reason and has ordered your kin out.

What is happening here?

A badger in a full leather apron had entered the room. Master Darius — the forge smell on him
was old enough to be part of his skin. He took in the scene without hurry.

Master Darius. The bull's tone shifted only slightly. This rat will not bother you any longer.
He is at the start of his journey out of this city, together with his extensive family.

Darius looked at Egbert. Then back at the bull.

But why? Egbert has only been helpful here. He did jobs that others were unwilling to do.

Not my call. The bull's voice was not unkind, exactly. It was the voice of someone who had
thought about it and reached a conclusion and was done thinking about it. He may be the honest
outlier, but most of them are not. And none of them will put their honest work above the needs
of a cousin, or a cousin's cousin. No. The city of World Edge will not be their home anymore.

Darius said nothing else. The apron moved slightly as he breathed.

Egbert was still dangling by his tail.

---

They walked him out through the factory floor. The other workers watched or didn't — most
found reasons to look at their hands, their machines, the middle distance. A young fox near
the cooling vats met his eye for a moment and then looked away. They had eaten lunch together
most days for two years.

The street outside was already busy with it. Families with bundles. Children who did not
understand the pace they were being asked to keep. An old rat woman sitting on a cart that
held everything she owned, quite still, watching the city she was leaving with an expression
that Egbert could not read and did not try to.

He had a mother somewhere in this. Two brothers and an uncle and a sprawl of cousins that
extended further than he had ever fully mapped. He understood, in the abstract, what the bull
had meant about cousins. He had covered for them. More than once. He had told himself each
time that it was a small thing, that it was family, that it was what you did. He had not
thought about what it looked like from the outside.

He had also crawled into spaces in that factory that no one else would enter, fixed things in
the dark by touch alone, worked hours that appeared on no record because Darius had needed it
done and there was no one else small enough or willing enough to do it. That also didn't
appear on any record. It didn't count toward anything today.

Master Darius appeared at the factory door behind him.

Where will you go?

Egbert hadn't thought about it yet. There was a lot he hadn't thought about yet.

South, maybe. My uncle has talked about the river settlements. He says the work is different
there.

Darius reached into his apron. He produced a folded piece of paper and held it out.

That is a letter. It says you are a capable worker and an honest one, and that the person
reading it should give you a fair chance. My name on it will mean something in three or four
places I can think of.

Egbert took the letter. It was warm from the apron.

He wanted to say something about the years, about the lunch breaks, about the machine work in
the dark. He couldn't find the shape of it.

He put the letter in his coat and walked toward the end of the street, where the rest of his
family was waiting with their bundles, already facing away from the city.
