# John Bean

I'm not going.

What? Why? Getting cold feet suddenly?

Look at me. John's voice was flat. I'm half the man I used to be, barely able to stand
straight. How can I be anything other than a hindrance? You can scout that camp without me.

Felix leaned against the doorframe, arms folded, his ringed tail moving slightly behind him
in a way that usually meant he was already three steps ahead of the conversation. That is
quite an exaggeration. Yes, losing an arm is painful, but you are still a much better fighter
than me. Any stray soldier from that camp could put me down without a second thought. I would
feel considerably safer with your sturdy shadow behind me.

Rhianna! Felix raised his voice toward the back of the cart. Please talk some sense to your
patient.

Leave her out of it. She would not be so coldhearted as to send me into certain danger.

Felix looked at John for a long moment.

Look at you. One day you brave the world, climb into any place you don't belong, and then
one setback and suddenly you can't stand still and watch a friend's back. You disappoint me.

What is happening? Rhianna appeared at the back of the cart, small and unhurried, a white
mouse who gave the impression of having seen most things and been impressed by few of them.
John? Is Felix trying to get you into trouble?

Evening, Rhianna. Sorry to bother you but —

Silent, Felix. I asked John something. I already know your position without you blabbering
on about it.

John did not know where to look. He had never quite managed to explain to anyone how it was
possible that a white mouse half his size could tell a raccoon off where a pig of John's
particular build had spent years struggling to. He reached absently to scratch his arm and was immediately aware, again, that
it wasn't there anymore.

He still didn't fully understand what had happened in those ruins. He had been following
one of Felix's leads, which in hindsight should have been its own warning. Some old stone
building on the edge of the blasted lands, long abandoned, the walls so weathered they
looked like they had always been there. The main doors had been sealed by whoever last used
the place, and nothing John had with him was going to persuade them — but Felix, with a
raccoon's eye for the kind of opening a sensible person would leave alone, had found a low
slot at ground level, wide and flat, meant for feeding something long and heavy into the
building. John had not known what, and he still did not. Whatever was inside had been worth
more than its owners had bothered to come back for.

The machine had been just inside the entrance. Enormous, low to the ground, crouched in the
dark like it had been placed there and forgotten. No markings he recognised. No obvious
mechanism. He had looked at it for a long time with his lamp and decided it was inert — the
dust on it was old enough to be archaeological — and started to squeeze past it toward the
passage beyond.

It woke the moment he touched it. Not a grinding start-up, not a mechanical warning. An
immediate, purposeful waking, the way a sleeping animal wakes when something crosses into its
space. The blade was already moving when he registered the sound. It was designed for
ironwood — the hardest material in the world, the stuff that resisted every tool ever made —
and it went through his arm like his arm was nothing at all.

He had made it out. He wasn't entirely sure how. The machine had not followed him past the
threshold, which he had noted carefully and was not grateful enough for, and he had tied off
what needed tying and walked back to camp in a state of focused calm that he suspected was
mostly blood loss. Rhianna had not said much while she worked. That had been the worst part.

Well. He said it slowly. Felix has a point. We can hardly sit around and wait until that
army guts Prince Hannes of Allondo.

If you're already in with Felix, why bother me about it? Go and lose that other arm too.
See if I care.

No, Rhianna — Felix stepped forward. Don't do that. I know what's on that cart behind you.
More than you need for your travels.

Nothing is safe when you're around. She was quiet for a moment, looking at John. Okay. I'll
be honest. I may have gotten my hands on something to help you. It looks like a wooden
sculpture, but it is more than that. She reached behind her and brought it forward carefully,
turning it in the firelight. It is a piece of the old work — from the age when there were
walking statues made of this material. Ironwood. With this attached correctly, you gain back
more than you can imagine. It moves with your will.

John looked at it for a long time. The grain of it caught the light in a way that ordinary
wood didn't — something deeper in it, like the material was lit from inside.

Ironwood, he said.

She held his gaze. I know.

He didn't say what they were both thinking. That ironwood had taken his arm. That she was
proposing to give him ironwood back in its place. He wasn't sure if that was darkly funny or
something else entirely.

He held out what remained of his shoulder.

---

John struggled in his captor's arms. The wolf's grip was certain and practised, and he was
steered to a mirror propped near the roaring campfire at the edge of the tree line, another
soldier pressing him forward to look.

The mirror did not show his face.

An old man stood in it — fancy clothes, slightly bent, leaning on a staff. Looking back at
him with an expression of mild professional interest, as though John were a problem he had
solved before. John felt something move behind his eyes at the sight of the man, something
that prowled slowly along the inside of his thoughts, searching for the edge of something.

It found the arm instead — the one he no longer had, still there in his mind, shaped by
habit and by forgetting. He felt it recoil.

He did the only thing available to him. He reached out with his missing arm and pulled.

Hard.

The pain that had been constant since the field surgeon's tent was gone. The mirror showed
his own face. Somewhere very distant — he could not have said how he knew it — something
fell, in the middle of bloody shards of glass.

The wolf behind him had gone very still.

John flexed the hand that was not supposed to be there, and turned around.
