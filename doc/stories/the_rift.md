# The Rift

Did you feel that too?

Laurent walked into Felicia's room without warning, flooding it with sunlight. She had been
working for three days on the arrangement laid out across her worktable — threads of iron
and condensed shadow, painstakingly drawn into a pattern that did not tolerate disruption.
The light hit it wrong. She felt the whole thing come apart.

She looked up at him with eyes watering from the brightness, capable of cutting down a mountain.

It is gone.

What is gone, my dear?

The city.

Now Felicia felt it too. Something tearing at her — not at her body, not at the spirit she
carried, but at the connection that ran beneath both of those. It existed between all mages.
Most of them never spoke about it, some refused to acknowledge it at all, but it was there: a
thread that made you aware, without being able to say how, that you were not the only one. She
had felt it once before when a mage she had known since childhood died suddenly. That had been
a single thread going quiet.

This was not that. This was the whole cloth pulling.

Should we go there? Laurent, can we help them?

Felicia had only seen Laurent cry once in her life. He was not a man who performed composure —
he simply did not often feel things that required it. Tears were gliding along his cheeks now.

No. We would fall out of the sky the moment our castle came near it.

They stood at the window together. The white city was on the horizon and even at this distance
it was wrong — a dark glimmer moving between the towers, something that did not belong to
any element Felicia could name. The towers themselves, which she had always known as clean
and pale and certain, were swaying. Not in wind. In something else. None of them were
untouched.

The fires she could also see. They were enormous but they were ordinary. Ordinary fire meant
people were still alive to cause it, or at least had been recently. Most of the people in that
city were not mages. The mages might already be gone — might have been the first to go, given
what was moving there — but the others could still run. The roads out of the city were wide.
The fires lit the way.

We cannot do nothing. There are so many people.

Laurent was quiet for a moment. She could see him arrive at the decision the way she had
always been able to read him — a particular stillness, a slight straightening, the expression
that meant he had found the shape of what he was going to do and was now only considering
the words.

I cannot save them all. But I will do everything I can to guide them to safety.

He turned from the window.

Everyone will be welcome. Humans and all the peoples that our previous leaders were so quick
to dismiss — the large and the small, the ones they called too simple or too wild or too
difficult to count. This castle can carry many, and let it be a bridge to better places for
as long as it is needed.

Felicia thought of her worktable. Three days of work, gone because he had opened a door.
She would rebuild it when there was time.

I hope more than us are willing to step in, Laurent continued. But one way or another, this
marks the end of our nation. That city was what held it together. Without it, every mage will
do whatever they please — until they encounter what seems to be emerging from over there.

He looked back at the horizon. The dark glimmer had spread. A tower that had been swaying was
no longer swaying.

The fools somehow managed to bring unnatural things into this world.

He said it without heat. That was the part that stayed with Felicia afterward, through all the
days of the evacuation, through all the people they carried and set down in better places and
carried again. Not the anger she might have expected. Something worse. The tone of a man who
had already moved past what could not be changed and was now only looking at what was left.
