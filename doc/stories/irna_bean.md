# Letter on the Body of Irna Bean

She had made it as far as the edge of Elmsfield. No further. She was slumped against the
post of the first gate, her cloak drawn close against a fever that had clearly been with her
for some time. Beside her was a large dog — grey-muzzled, planted squarely between her and the street. It
had already driven off two people who had come close to look. A third was standing at a
careful distance, uncertain what to do next. The letter was folded inside her coat, which no
one had yet managed to reach.

Whoever she had meant to reach, she had not been on the road that led there. The dog had
found a path of its own choosing, and this was where it ended — holding the gate against
anyone who might help, and anyone who might not.

---

Father Elliot,

I hope this finds you well.

The young woman carrying this letter is Irna, daughter of John Bean. She was held in a mine on
account of a mishap involving the second prince. The root of her trouble appears to be that her
brother has been proclaimed some manner of valiant spirit the country is said to urgently need.
That fable seems to have originated from your Abbey, and so I send her to you — to have the
error corrected at its source. Her brother is as ordinary as his kind comes. I urge you to
rectify the trouble you have, no doubt without intention, caused.

With kind regards,

Rhianna Linthrope
