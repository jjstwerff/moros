# Linar Harbour

- **Type:** Small independent harbour town at the east-coast foot of the coastal mountain range, **at the mouth of the Clear Water river** where it meets the ocean. The start of the main inland trade road.
- **Region:** East coast; the trade road disappears west into a pass through the range's southern terminus. The coast stretches north toward World's End and south toward the pirate nest. **The Clear Water river arrives at the harbour from the inland north-west** — its full course runs from the foothills east of Scarlet Vale (where Clear Water mine sits at its head) → north into the Lake of Tears → out past Luchebert Monastery → through the hills → out here (see `doc/places/geography.md` §"Rivers (inventory)"). Whatever travels downstream — fish, silt, mine run-off, the occasional drowned letter — eventually arrives at this harbour.
- **Officially:** Independent. Neither Allondo nor Brumal holds claim here. Self-governed through practical arrangements nobody has rewritten in a generation.
- **In practice:** A working trade hub whose **working population is descended from the overseas soldiers who captured it in the war and never went home**. The town has spent a generation making itself the place where those descendants and the surrounding kingdoms can do business without either side having to explain anything.
- **Status:** Stable. The peace is practical, not warm. Brumal's collapse and the prospect of a second war puts the harbour's neutrality under pressure it has not yet been asked to refuse.

## What the party meets

The party arrives either **by sea** (if they have a reason to be on a boat) or **by road from the pass**, which is the overwhelming majority. The road descends from the coastal mountain range's southern terminus and empties into Linar harbour along its landward side. The town is below the pass, terraced down to the water.

They come out of the pass and see the harbour below them: a working curve of quayside, ships at the moorings, warehouses along the wharves, the fisher-quarters climbing the slopes above, and the sea beyond. The road down takes twenty minutes on foot. **Long before they reach the dock they will hear the voices — and the voices will not sound like the interior.**

## How it reads at a glance — the 2–3 standouts on entry

- **Where the mountain meets the sea.** The southern end of the coastal range rises directly behind the harbour. The cliffs are visible from every corner of the town. The trade road through the pass is the only overland way in or out; the harbour is the only way to the sea from this side of the range. **The town is at a hinge**, and everyone in it knows.
- **Overseas rigging and overseas accents everywhere at the dock.** The working population is visibly and audibly of overseas origin, a generation on. **Rope patterns, sail cuts, fish-preservation methods, songs at shift-end, first names** — all carry the mark of a continent the party has never seen. A traveller from the interior feels it within the first few minutes: the town *reads foreign* in small specific ways, even though nobody here was born overseas.
- **What nobody says.** A pattern in the silences. Strangers are not told who is descended from which side of the war. Families intermarried; the line is softening; **nobody raises it with outsiders, and everyone knows who is descended from whom.** A party that listens for the pattern can learn it in a day. A party that doesn't, doesn't.

## At the approach — down from the pass

The road from Elmsfield and the interior reaches Linar harbour through a cut in the coastal range. The pass is high and narrow; the descent is switchbacks down to sea level over a mile.

- **The last switchback gives the full view.** The harbour is below; ships at the moorings; the curve of the bay; the sea past the range that runs up the coast as far as anyone can see.
- **Caravans form at the pass-foot.** Teams load and unload here on the landward side, where the road opens to space wide enough to park. A party arriving by road will walk past caravans being readied for the inland run.
- **No gate. No guard.** Linar harbour does not garrison itself. The road enters the town as a road enters any settled street.
- **The caravan-drivers speak mixed accents.** A party passing them hears the first sample of the harbour's voice — overseas vowels in one driver's speech, interior consonants in the next. The line the town does not discuss is audible before the party has reached it.

## At the docks — first contact

The working centre of Linar harbour. Stone quay along the bay's curve; warehouses at the landward edge; fishing boats at one end, deep-sea traders at the other, lighters moving between them.

- **Longshore crews are almost all overseas-descended.** Watch them work for a minute. Hear them call to each other. The crews know each other by house-name across generations — the net-mender's son works the crane his grandfather set up a year after the war ended.
- **Harbour clerks at desks near the pier-foot** tally goods in and out. They are courteous. The fee schedule is posted. A clerk will ask the party's business without making the question feel like an interrogation. The answers go into a ledger.
- **The deep-sea ships are fewer than a party expects.** Coastal trade is frequent; the overseas route is rare and not on the open market. A party asking about passage to the overseas continent will be told *"we don't really do that run"* by every captain on the quay — and the answer is the truth as far as strangers are allowed to hear it.
- **A party that helps unload a cargo on arrival** signals something. The longshore crews accept it the way Salmonswell's wharf accepts crate-carrying — as a gesture, and doors open a little faster.

## At the fisher-quarters — where the overseas community lives

Up the slope behind the docks, the fisher-quarters climb in terraces. Small stone houses, shared yards, net-drying racks between. Most of the working population lives here.

- **Songs at shift-end.** A party passing through at the right hour hears songs in what is clearly not a local language — or in local language with phrasings that came from somewhere else. Nobody translates for strangers unless asked.
- **Specific crafts.** Net-making patterns that interior eyes have never seen. Fish-preservation that uses salts and woods the Elmsfield smokehouses would not recognise. **Material culture overseas** in small ways across the quarter.
- **Letters from far away.** In some houses, in careful drawers, letters addressed in scripts that don't match this continent's alphabets. The letters are not secret within the quarter; they are private to strangers. A party with earned standing may be shown one.
- **Children playing in the yards** speak local with overseas lilts. The generation after them will not. The softening is happening visibly.
- **The older residents** are the most cautious with outsiders. They remember the occupation. They remember being occupiers. Most of the people their age on the other side of the line are gone; the older residents carry the war's memory in ways the younger ones do not.

## At the chandleries and warehouses — the trade backbone

Behind the quay, the working buildings of the inland trade. Rope and tar; salt and dried fish; cask-makers, sail-menders, caravan supply. Most of these crafts are mixed — local families and overseas-origin families doing the same work side by side for a generation.

- **Goods transfer here.** Ships' cargo comes off, is weighed at the clerks' desks, moves to warehouses, and leaves a few days later on the caravans bound inland.
- **Every craft has a house-name.** Crews of three or four families have cornered particular trades: one family does the rigging, another the rope, another the caskage. A party looking for specific goods or services will be pointed at a house, not a shop.
- **Bridget's old road ends here.** If Bridget is with the party, she knows this end of the trade road from three years of working it. Named families, specific cheats and honesties, the particular habits of the Linar clerks. See `doc/npcs/bridget.md` §"Her old road."
- **Merchants from the interior pass through constantly.** Most of the ambient gossip in the harbour comes from them — news from Elmsfield, from Scarlet Vale, from Salmonswell, from further afield.

## At the taverns — where the gossip lands

Linar harbour has several taverns; the ones worth knowing are on the landward side of the quay, where longshoremen drink after shift and merchants wait for their caravans to be ready.

- **Mixed rooms.** An Allondan merchant and a Brumal courier can pass each other in the same tavern without remarking on it. The bartenders enforce nothing; the patrons have a generation of practice.
- **Sea-travel stories.** Every merchant who has come off a coastal boat has one. The stories all stop at the same point: **past Linar harbour, south, is where colleagues disappeared**. Captured by the pirates, set to work for them, never seen again. Any merchant will tell a new traveller this if it comes up — the pattern is open knowledge in the taverns. What the merchants will *not* say over a casual drink is whose cousins are in the nest.
- **Song about a shimmering city** sometimes, from a traveller just in from the desert road. A party alert to the **Fata morgana** thread can pick it up here. See `doc/npcs/nadine.md`.
- **Nadine drinks here when she is in town.** Her merchant group has contacts at the dock and in the fisher-quarter. A party that has earned her trust can meet her at the harbour in a way they cannot anywhere else. Her **lament for Linnet** — and the intelligence she has compiled on the pirate nest — lands here in personal form. See `doc/npcs/nadine.md` §"The song for Linnet."
- **Private conversations happen upstairs.** Several of the taverns have back rooms or upstairs rooms the owners rent for the evening. Corven/Penelope, if the campaign opens that thread, can meet in such a room without either court's eyes reaching here.

## At the southern coast road — the pirate pointer

A track leaves the harbour's southern edge and runs along the coast. For a mile or two it is a working road — fishers use it, the occasional local traveller. **Past that distance, it empties out.**

- **No regular traffic south.** Captains do not offer passage. Riders do not offer guidance. A party asking why gets the merchants' answer: *the pirates are down there*.
- **The fishers watch the south.** Boats working the southern bay-arms keep an eye on the coast beyond. A fisher who trusts the party may describe what they have seen — a smoke signal pattern that is not theirs, a sail-cut that is not local, a returning boat that moved as if it did not want to be watched.
- **The nest is reachable.** A party with a boat, a guide, and a reason — and ideally with Nadine's intelligence — can find it. Without those, the route is long, exposed, and the nest is not unprepared for visitors.
- **The fishers' mixed loyalties.** Some of them are cousins of people in the nest. That does not make them pirate-sympathetic; it makes them complicated. A party asking a Linar fisher to help *burn* the nest will not find help here. A party asking for help *getting someone out* is a different conversation.

## Visible patterns

- **Overseas-descended longshore crews work the dock** every shift. Their presence is the town's most visible fact.
- **Mixed rooms wherever people gather.** Taverns, markets, the quayside. Two kingdoms of interior traveller and the overseas-descended locals in the same space without incident.
- **Caravans forming at the pass-foot** most days — east to west; deep-sea goods inland; interior goods to the deep-sea ships.
- **The clerks' ledgers run constantly.** Fees collected, goods tallied, families tracked across decades. The documentation is obsessive in a way that appears, accurately, to be what keeps the town independent.
- **The southern coast is avoided.** A fisher rowing south does so for a reason they can name.
- **Overseas news arrives unpredictably.** A letter. A cousin. A rumour off a boat. The town knows things about the overseas continent that no official channel on this side has — and does not volunteer them.
- **Songs at shift-end.** Most evenings, somewhere in the fisher-quarters, a group of longshoremen's families is singing. The songs travel.
- **Old patched architecture in specific places.** A few buildings along the quay and in the fisher-quarter show clear signs of occupation-era damage patched after: a different stonework style where a wall was rebuilt, a line where a roof was raised. Locals do not point these out.

## What you learn here, and when

| Topic | When the place gives it up |
|---|---|
| That the working population is overseas-descended | Immediately obvious at the dock. |
| That the pirate nest exists to the south | Freely, from any merchant in any tavern. The stories are open. |
| **Intelligence on the nest** (names, schedules, kin connections) | Gated. Nadine gates this; see her file §"The song for Linnet." The fishers know fragments; they release them on earned trust and to people who will *get the captives out*. |
| That overseas-descended families have private correspondence with the overseas continent | With earned standing, not casually. The correspondence is not secret but not advertised. |
| The war, as elders remember it | Carefully gated. Older residents talk about it on their own time; a stranger must have standing. |
| Which families are descended from which side | **Not told.** Learnable by observation; nobody names it for outsiders. |
| Where specific ship captains run and for whom | Over time. A party that drinks in the taverns a few evenings will assemble this. |
| Overseas political questions (who succeeded Thorgal, the current leadership) | Fragments from letters; uncertain. The town knows more than the courts do, and less than it needs. |
| The Corven/Penelope meeting arrangements (if active) | Closed-room topic. Only to parties who are already inside that thread. |

## If the party does something — what the place does

| If the party... | What happens |
|---|---|
| Helps unload cargo on arrival | Registered as arrival-gift; longshoremen read them as willing; doors open faster. |
| Asks about the pirate nest in a tavern | Gets stories. Merchants' disappearance-tales are open knowledge. Specific intelligence is not. |
| Pushes a hard "overseas are enemies" stance in a public space | **The room goes quiet.** Nobody argues; nobody agrees. The party is not welcome in that tavern again. Word spreads faster than they can walk. |
| Brings news from the overseas continent | The town gathers to listen. Longshoremen come off shift to hear. The party is received as important visitors for as long as the news lasts. |
| Asks for passage to the overseas continent as a stranger | Declined across the quay. *"We don't really do that run."* A party with earned standing — and a reason that makes sense to Linar — can eventually arrange something, but not from a cold ask. |
| Asks for coastal passage north toward World's End | Readily available. Captains run this regularly. |
| Asks for passage south toward the pirate nest | Declined by every captain. A party that explains *why* (Linnet extraction, a person to pull out) may get a different answer eventually — but through Nadine and the fisher-quarter, not through the dock. |
| Arrives with Nadine | Her Finch-merchant contacts open. The fisher-quarter's information flow becomes available in a way it otherwise is not. |
| Arrives with Bridget | Her old-road history opens specific houses. Named families welcome her; some do not. |
| Arrives flying Allondan royal colours | Received politely. Doors close slightly. Overseas-descended longshoremen watch carefully; nothing happens that a stranger would notice. |
| Writes a letter to send overseas | Possible, for a fee, through private channels. A party that has standing in the fisher-quarter may send it confidentially; a party without that standing sends it publicly, through a captain who will read it. |
| Meets Corven or Penelope (or both) in a back room | Possible if arranged discreetly. The town provides the *setting*; the *arrangement* is Corven's and Penelope's. Linar harbour hosts; Linar harbour does not remember. |
| Investigates a specific old-war damage mark | Learnable with patience. An older resident may talk about what happened at that wall; most will point to someone else who knows better. |
| Asks a fisher to help burn the pirate nest | **No.** The fishers will not help destroy a place some of their cousins live in, captives and all. A party that presses the ask loses their standing here. |
| Asks a fisher to help get captives out of the nest | Different conversation. With the right framing and earned trust, intelligence flows. This is the Linnet thread. |
| Causes a public disturbance | Handled collectively, without garrison. The offending party is asked to leave the town. Word travels up the trade road; Elmsfield and Salmonswell will have heard. |

## DM notes

- **The silences are the texture.** Do not narrate them as absence — narrate them as choice. The town's stability is built on *what is not said*. A DM who surfaces this as a live pattern (the older resident glancing at another family's doorway; the bartender changing the subject from the war) gives players the chance to learn the harbour's rules by watching.
- **The pirate-nest breadcrumb is ambient.** Any merchant in any tavern. The story of colleagues who sailed south and did not come back is common knowledge and should surface whenever the party engages with sea-travellers. This keeps the pirate thread visible without requiring a specific named scene.
- **Nadine lands here personally.** When the party has earned standing with her and the thread is open, the harbour is where the Linnet intelligence becomes concrete. Do not release the details before Nadine has reason to release them.
- **Hard-line stances break the room.** If Hannes (or any NPC or player) pushes an "overseas are the enemy" frame here, the town does not argue — it *closes*. Let the closing be visible: fewer conversations, slower service, doors that were open now shut. The message is *this is where that frame does not work*, and the DM should make it felt.
- **The Corven/Penelope option is available.** If the arc opens, Linar harbour is a plausible discreet venue — a room upstairs at a quiet tavern, arrangements made by a merchant who forgets names. The town hosts without remembering. This makes it the cleanest place to run such a scene.
- **Overseas is *porous, not open*.** The town is the most porous point on the continent to overseas news. It is not a diplomatic channel. A party that understands the distinction can use the porosity; a party that treats Linar as a formal contact point will be quietly disappointed.
- **Captains who "don't really do that run" sometimes do.** Deep-sea passage exists. It is not on the open market. Earning it requires real standing — usually several visits, named friends in the fisher-quarter, a reason Linar can verify, and an acceptance that the crossing is long and dangerous. Treat this as a multi-session unlock, not a purchase.
- **Brumal's collapse lands here sideways.** Allondan preparations for the border war reach Linar through merchants and caravan-drivers; Brumal refugees reach Linar through overland traffic from Salmonswell's direction. The harbour absorbs both kinds of news and volunteers neither.
- **No named NPCs assigned yet.** The file treats the town collectively — longshoremen, clerks, fishers, captains, older residents. DM-fill named NPCs as the campaign's needs surface them: a harbour-clerk, an overseas-descended fisher-elder, a ship captain with a specific loyalty, a tavern-keeper. Let names emerge at the table.

## Open questions

- Which overseas species settled here, in what proportions? (Bound to matter when the overseas thread opens.)
- Is there a visible community leader among the overseas-descended population — a harbourmaster, a boat captain, a respected elder — who would be a DM-facing named NPC when the time comes?
- What does the *official* Linar harbour governance look like (council, harbourmaster, something else)? The file assumes an informal council-and-clerks arrangement; a name and a seat can be added when the campaign asks.
- How closely do the fishers watch the pirate nest further south? What intelligence could they give to a party that earned it?
- What is the state of letters-from-overseas right now — what are the most recent rumours the fisher-quarter has received about Thorgal's succession, the current overseas leadership, and the continent's famine?
