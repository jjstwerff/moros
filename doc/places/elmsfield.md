# Elmsfield

- **Type:** Small independent border town in the wilderness east of Allondo.
- **Region:** On the Allondo side of nothing, specifically — the Great Swamp separates it from the kingdom, and no royal writ reaches here. Elder-led, not crown-ruled.
- **Built around:** The great ironwood tree at its centre. The town is the tree's apron.
- **Status:** Small. Hardy. Currently taking in Brumal refugees as the southern crisis pushes them this way.

## What the party meets

The party arrives by the main trade road from Linar harbour, or up from the south, or along the small river from the swamp. The road narrows; the woods thin. The town is suddenly *right there* — no walls to speak of, no gatehouse, just a low palisade and an open gate that was reinforced after the last war and never fully unreinforced.

Then they see the tree.

For Session 1, the first thing they meet *at* the gate is a sick stranger and a frightened crowd — see `doc/claude/CAMPAIGN.md` §"Session 1" and `doc/npcs/irna_bean.md` §"What the party meets — Phase 1". For later arrivals, the gate is open and the town is at work around them.

## How it reads at a glance — the 2–3 standouts on entry

- **The great ironwood tree at the centre** of the town — enormous, old beyond easy estimate, its trunk hollowed into a building's worth of rooms. **Bridget's Inn is inside it.** The whole town is arranged around the tree the way a camp is arranged around a fire. On first sight a visitor understands that the tree is not decoration; the tree is *why Elmsfield is here*.
- **The wilderness at the edges.** Elmsfield has no defensible walls and no garrison. The **Great Swamp begins a short walk north**; hills rise east; the **trade road runs south toward Allondo and east toward Linar harbour through the coastal pass**. **South of Elmsfield is the trade artery and, beyond it, the broken lands**; the wilderness with the swamp and the Lake of Tears is to the north. The town is deliberately *in* the wilderness rather than against it, and it shows — farms run up to the treeline, paths go where the ground allows, no one has tried to impose a grid on the place.
- **The gate, reinforced once and never fully undone.** The wooden palisade that rings part of the town is modest; the gate itself carries the marks of the Overseas War — heavier timbers than the walls they sit in, scorch marks on one post, an iron bar at the base that does not match the rest of the ironmongery. The town's defensive habit is visible. The town's other defensive habit — hiding what matters inside the tree — is not.

## Visible patterns the party will notice

- **Diederik at the tree most mornings**, hands on the trunk. Townspeople walk around him. (See `doc/npcs/elder_diederik.md`.)
- **Bridget behind the bar in the morning**, doing books; Nadine at the trader's corner when she's in town. (See `doc/npcs/bridget.md`, `doc/npcs/nadine.md`.)
- **Children running between Hank's farm and Bridget's Inn** — some are Bridget and Wilfred's; some are orphans from Hank's place being sent in on errands.
- **Farmers in the fields from dawn** — Joseph on his plot to the north, Wilfred on the farm east, Hank on the larger edge-farm west.
- **The trade road through town** — merchants pass daily; caravans stop overnight; the Inn is the default lodging.
- **The town's elder governs quietly.** No courthouse, no assembly hall. Diederik's treehouse is where decisions are brought; most are settled between neighbours without him.

## At the central square and the great tree

The heart of town. The square is not formal — it is the packed-earth space the tree permits around its roots. On market days it carries stalls; on ordinary days it carries people passing through.

The **ironwood tree** is the place that matters most, and the rest of the town defers to it:

- **Bridget's Inn is inside the hollow** (lower floor + partial cellar; upper floors boarded and tree-gripped, reopenable by labor). See [`elmsfield_inn.md`](elmsfield_inn.md) for the space and [`../npcs/bridget.md`](../npcs/bridget.md) for the Innkeeper.
- **The tree hid the town's wartime hoard** during the occupation; the overseas raiders could not open it with any tool they had. Parts of that hoard are still inside. Bridget alone keeps the inventory.
- **The tree is a fertile female ironwood** — sterile because there are no males on this continent. Very few people in Elmsfield understand this; Diederik does, and the question of what to do about it drives much of what he is quietly preparing. See `doc/npcs/elder_diederik.md` §"The errand only the party can run".

## At Diederik's treehouse

A druid-grown dwelling in a substantial living tree at the edge of town — dwarfed by the great ironwood at the centre, unmistakable as his work. See `doc/npcs/elder_diederik.md` §"At his treehouse".

## At Hank and Egbert's farm

On the edge of Elmsfield. An orphanage-of-sorts as well as a working farm — see `doc/places/hanks_farm.md` for the full scene treatment, and `doc/npcs/hank.md` and `doc/npcs/egbert.md` for the residents.

## At the other farms

- **Wilfred's farm** — east of town. Bridget's husband, a Badger farmer, runs it. Their children split between the farm and the Inn. See `doc/npcs/wilfred.md`.
- **Joseph's farm plot** — north of town. Joseph the Raccoon farmer recently bought this plot from Hank, exhausting his savings; he is land-rich and cash-poor, and it has made him the town's loudest post-war voice of fear. See `doc/npcs/farmer_joseph.md`.

## At the river-mill — a short walk north

The small river that leaves Elmsfield **northward** toward the Great Swamp does not run through the village itself; the town sits a *bit away* from the bank. A short walk north of the gate, where the ground falls toward the water, sits the town's **grain-mill** — a single modest building on the river's edge. It is small. It is also the only one Elmsfield has, and the bread on every table in town comes through it.

- **A low timber-and-stone mill house with a single under-shot wheel.** The wheel is older than anyone living and has been re-bladed in pieces over generations. The race is short — a stone channel cut into the bank, narrowed where the flow needed slowing — and the wheel turns at the unhurried pace a small flow allows. *You hear the wheel before you see the mill.*
- **The miller is a Badger named [DM-fill] who has held the post since long before the Overseas War.** Quiet, flour-dusted, slow with strangers and patient with everyone. Trades by weight, not by coin where the farmer would rather pay in grain; the books are kept by Bridget's accounting hand, not the miller's.
- **Wagon-tracks on the path from the gate** — the rhythm of farmers bringing grain in and flour out is the town's other slow heartbeat, alongside Diederik at the tree. Joseph, Wilfred, Hank, the farms further out — all of them go through this one mill.

**When something breaks** — a worn gudgeon, a slipped sluice gate, a tooth gone from the gear ring, a flood-year that puts silt where the race used to run clear — the town notices within a week, because the bread comes from somewhere worse and costs more. The miller will accept help from any character with **Wright**, **Carpentry**, **Blacksmith**, *Ingenuity*, or *Hand*. **Master Darius**'s forge at World Edge is the regional source of the heavier iron parts (`doc/npcs/master_darius.md`); **Diederik** can advise on suitable replacement timber from the woods around Elmsfield without cutting anything load-bearing. A breakdown that lasts more than a few weeks puts pressure on Hank's farm capacity — the orphanage feeds itself off the same flour as the Inn does.

## At the gate

The town's Session 1 scene location. Low palisade, heavier gate, scorch marks from the war. The gate is staffed only loosely — a townsperson in rotation checking who passes, no formal guard.

The gate is also where **Brumal refugees arrive** as the southern crisis pushes them east. The pattern is recent and still increasing. The town's capacity to absorb is limited; Hank's farm has absorbed more than anyone else. See `doc/claude/CAMPAIGN.md` §"The Neighbour's Crisis".

| Topic | When the town gives it up |
|---|---|
| That Elmsfield is not part of Allondo | Immediately — any local will say so if asked. |
| That Diederik is the elder | Within an hour of arriving. Everyone knows. |
| That the tree is ironwood | Only with Diederik's conversation. Most locals think it is "stone wood." |
| The wartime hoard inside the tree | Only through Bridget, and only in fragments. |
| Joseph's specific post-war trauma | Once asked of anyone who knew him then. |
| Hank's druidic training and hidden book | Only with earned standing (see her file). |
| Egbert's wolf-folk concealment | The town has hunches; nobody confirms; Egbert will not. |
| The Badger clan's local concentration | Briefly, from any Badger (Bridget, Wilfred, Corné, possibly Ralph). |

## If the party does something — what happens

| If the party... | What the town does |
|---|---|
| Tries to cut into the tree | Stopped immediately by whoever is nearest; town turns hostile fast. The tree is sacred in practice even when people cannot say why. |
| Starts trouble at the Inn | Bridget handles it (see her file); if it escalates, half the town arrives, because the Inn *is* the town's common room. |
| Challenges Diederik's authority directly | Fails. The town's consent is his authority; nobody votes with the challenger. |
| Mistreats a Brumal refugee publicly | Joseph's fear-voice speaks first (and loudly); Hank and Diederik push back quietly. Both factions watch the party's side. |
| Shows sustained respect across visits | Gains standing that opens later layers — Diederik's grave invitation, Bridget's trader network, Hank's hidden skills, Corné's hill route, the tree's deeper history. |
| Brings news of the wider world | Welcomed. Nadine is the usual channel, but a party with real news — Brumal movements, Scarlet Vale rumours, overseas reports — will be heard at the Inn and passed around within a day. |
| Threatens the town openly | The town does not fight well, and it knows this. It *disappears* — inhabitants retreat to the tree, the farms, the hills. Diederik, if forced, can call on his druidic and spirit-shield capacities, but will not unless the alternative is losing the town. |

## DM notes

- **Elmsfield is the campaign hub.** Most sessions pass through it; most NPCs either live here or are reached from here. A DM running a first campaign should assume the party will be in Elmsfield more than anywhere else, and treat the small details (who is where on any given day, what the latest refugee situation looks like, which farmer just lost a sheep) as the ambient material of ordinary scenes.
- **The town has no crown.** No royal writ reaches here; Allondo's formal authority stops at the swamp. What replaces it is the elder's quiet consent-based governance plus the network of clans and neighbours. This is a *feature* for the campaign — Elmsfield can shelter what the kingdoms cannot, and the party's actions here have different political weight than they would in Scarlet Vale.
- **Diederik is the spirit-shield.** Per `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Spirit Ecology," a resident druid holds chaotic spirits at a distance. If Diederik leaves, dies, or is driven out, Elmsfield's cover thins within months. The ironwood tree may provide secondary cover; Diederik does most of the work. See his file for the full mechanic.
- **The wartime hoard inside the tree** is a slow-release thread. Bridget knows what's still there; the town mostly doesn't; the party may learn fragments across visits. Items worth having are *plausibly* inside. The DM can place one or two there that serve the campaign, leaving the rest unspecified.
- **Brumal refugees are the live pressure.** Session 1's Joseph is the opening manifestation of this, but the thread grows: more people arriving, less capacity to absorb, the town's fault lines widening. See `doc/claude/CAMPAIGN.md` §"The Neighbour's Crisis".
