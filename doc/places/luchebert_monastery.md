# Luchebert Monastery

- **Type:** Small monastery in a remote hill vale, on top of cliffs above a curve of the river east of the Great Swamp.
- **Region:** The hills east of the Lake of Tears, beyond Laurent's downed city.
- **Officially:** Saint Luchebert's Abbey. The saint himself was one of the first human priests on this world, who built his religion out of **an ancient holy book carried through the portal from the world the first humans came from** (see `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Saint Luchebert"). The monastery's **library** is the living continuation of that work — ancient books being copied and translated, page by page, to keep knowledge from the other side of the portal alive on this one.
- **In practice:** A monastery whose true devotion is **Felicia**, tended through **Willow**'s hands at a *cave chapel* just off the cloister grounds. The ancient-knowledge work and the great-spirit devotion run in parallel, not in competition — Saint Luchebert's tradition was always flexible enough to receive what came after.
- **Status:** Reduced from former scale; half the side buildings shuttered; still functional and still gathering people who need to disappear.

## What the party meets

The party reaches Luchebert via the **hill path** — Corné Dunham's route, or one they worked out themselves. They have not come up from the river; that path is no longer in use (see §"The cliff path"). The hill route brings them *descending* toward a small modest portal set in a stone wall — the **back gate**, which has become the monastery's main entrance now.

Just before the portal, the path passes a **cave chapel** set into the hillside on their right. They may not realise it is a chapel — the entrance is dim, half-grown over, and a worn wooden plank leans against the lintel as if to say *do not enter casually*. (This is Felicia's shrine; entering uninvited is Willow's affair — see `doc/npcs/willow.md`.)

Through the portal, the path opens into the **maze garden**. This is the only way in for visitors today, and walking it is part of the act of arrival.

## How it reads at a glance — the 2–3 standouts on entry

- **The maze garden between the gate and the central building.** Visitors walk it before reaching the cloister proper. The path winds in figures it takes a moment to recognise — a depiction of life, in the order's tradition. **Stone benches sit in the corners** for older monks to rest along the route. **Herb and vegetable plots line the path** on both sides — somebody, generations ago, decided the symbolic walk should also feed the kitchen.
- **The shuttered side buildings.** The central building is in use; the half-dozen smaller buildings around it are mostly *not*. Doors closed. Moss along the sills. Whatever made the monastery shrink — plague, schism, attrition — is not advertised, but the empty dormitories are the most visible fact about the place. The order **was bigger once**.
- **The cliff edge at the back, and the broken path below.** Past the central building, the ground falls away. The river bend lies far below; a **narrow track**, crumbled in places, runs along the cliff face. No one walks it now. A party who looks down understands at once why someone following the river from below could never find this place.

## At the maze garden — the entry route

The maze is the *first* part of the monastery a visitor experiences inside the wall. Walking it is not optional; it *is* the path from the gate to the central building.

- **The route** is gravel, edged with low boxwood. The figures it traces represent life as the order understands it; the design is clearer in retrospect, or from above, than from inside the path itself.
- **The benches** are set into the inner corners. A travel-tired party will be invited to sit. Whoever is in the garden when they arrive — usually a lay brother or sister — will sit with them.
- **The herb and vegetable plots** are practical care that lasted past whoever first laid them. Beans climb a trellis at one corner; rosemary in another; medicinal herbs at a third, where the lay community tends most carefully. The garden feeds the kitchen and the infirmary.
- **The walk ends at the cloister door** — visitors are usually walked the last few yards by whoever met them at the gate.

## At the central building — the cloister proper

The main building of the monastery, surviving from when the order was larger. Plain stone, wood-shuttered windows. A central hall opens onto the saint chapel, the library, the dining hall, and the abbot's quarters.

For Father Elliot's specific scenes inside — the saint chapel, the library, the abbot's quarters — see `doc/npcs/father_elliot.md` §§"At the saint chapel," "At the library," "At the abbot's quarters."

In passing through, the party will also notice:

- **A dining hall** in use but quieter than its size suggests — long benches sit half-empty at every meal.
- **A small kitchen** open to the herb garden. Whoever has the cooking shift can be reached through the garden door.
- **A stair** to upper rooms once used as cells, now thinly occupied.

## At the cave chapel — Felicia's shrine

Set into the hillside just below the maze garden's portal, on the path *back down* toward the hills. Visitors pass it on entry without necessarily noticing it. **Willow tends it**, descending from the cloister at dawn and at dusk.

The chapel is a place in its own right and has its own file: see **`doc/places/felicia_shrine.md`** for the cave's standouts, the shrine, the spring, the necklace, and the rules of approach. For Willow's gating of it, see `doc/npcs/willow.md` §"At Felicia's shrine" and §"The draining curse."

## At the side buildings

Most are shuttered. Three or four are still in use — for the sheltered (see DM notes), for stores, for the infirmary; the rest are closed.

A party that asks to look at the empty buildings will be allowed, with a brother accompanying. Inside they find dormitory bunks under sheets, dust, the smell of long-closed wood. Some rooms look as if they were left in a hurry; some as if they were left carefully, ready for occupants who never came.

This is where the monastery's history lives most visibly. *Why* it shrank is a question Father Elliot will answer if asked plainly; he was not abbot when it happened.

## At the cliff path — defunct

The original entrance to the monastery, before the cave-portal route became standard. A narrow track cut into the cliff face above the river, descending in switchbacks to the water below.

It has not been maintained for many years. The track is **broken** in two places where rockfall has taken sections out; elsewhere the cut has weathered to a ledge no wider than a foot. **Serious climbing skill, with ropes for the rest of the party**, is required to traverse it now.

The monastery does not maintain it because the river-side approach is no longer used; visitors come over the hills instead. A party that *wants* to descend to the river from here can do so, but it is a real climb. A party trying to ascend from the river will find the track and recognise it — but they will not get up without preparation.

## Visible patterns

- **Monks walk the maze daily.** The path is never not in use during daylight.
- **Herbs are tended through the morning.** A lay brother or sister is usually in the garden until midday.
- **Willow descends to the cave chapel** at dawn and at dusk, with flowers and candles. She comes back up. She does not invite company.
- **The dining hall fills lightly at meals**, no more than a quarter occupied even at the busiest.
- **The shuttered buildings stay shuttered.** Their doors are not opened casually.

## What you learn here, and when

| Topic | When the place gives it up |
|---|---|
| Saint Luchebert's official rites | Immediately — visible at the saint chapel. |
| The library, what it holds | Once asked. Father Elliot will walk a visitor in. |
| The maze garden's meaning | Freely given by any monk asked. |
| Why the side buildings are empty | Once asked of Father Elliot or a senior brother. Plainly. |
| Felicia, the true devotion | **Only with earned trust.** The cave chapel is Willow's; she gates the topic. |
| The cliff path's history | Once asked. Anyone will say *"we come up the hill, not the river, for as long as I've been here."* |
| The disappeared the monastery shelters | Carefully. Names never given. |

## If the party does something — what happens

| If the party... | What the place does |
|---|---|
| Tries to enter the cave chapel without invitation | Willow intervenes (see her file). The shrine itself is not booby-trapped; the curse is hers, not the chapel's. |
| Picks from the herb or vegetable plots without asking | A lay brother politely intercepts — *"those feed us."* If they ask first, they are given a cutting. |
| Tries to descend the cliff path without preparation | Falls. The path is genuinely dangerous now. Endu/Health damage, possibly worse. |
| Asks to look in a shuttered side building | Allowed, with a brother accompanying. The brother will not narrate the history; the party may ask. |
| Brings a refugee — Brumal, overseas, or otherwise — to the gate | Received. Father Elliot and the senior community will not ask too many questions. The shelter is real. |
| Loiters in the maze without walking it through | The monks notice. After a while, one sits on a nearby bench and quietly asks if they're well. |
| Tries to climb up to the monastery from the river side | A serious endeavour. A party that succeeds gets respect from Elliot — who knows the path himself — but also arrives *at the back*, near the cliff edge, rather than through the maze garden. |

## DM notes

- **The shrine is the cave chapel below the gate**, not a room inside the cloister. Visitors pass it on entry without necessarily noticing it. Willow's two-trips-a-day to the chapel is a visible pattern the party will see if they stay any length of time.
- **The maze garden as scene-arrival.** Walking the maze takes time. The conversation that begins on a bench in the maze is often the conversation that decides what kind of welcome the party gets. Treat it as a *first scene*, not a corridor.
- **The monastery is smaller than it was.** The DM can place the reason where it serves the campaign: a generation-old plague, a schism with the urban church, attrition after the rift, a slow loss of the order's young. Whatever fits. The visible fact — half the buildings shuttered — is what matters at the table.
- **The cliff path** is a rope-and-climbing scene if the party wants to use it. As a route *into* the monastery from the river, it is harder than the hill route by a wide margin and gains nothing tactical. As a route *down* to the river, it is occasionally useful (a fast descent during an emergency). The DM should make the climb expensive enough that the party does not do it casually.
- **The library is a campaign-significant resource** — see `doc/npcs/father_elliot.md` §"At the library" and DM notes there. It is also a credible reason for the party to *return* to Luchebert after their first visit.
