# Haven

- **Type:** Settlement of surviving iron puppets from the mage era. Humanoid wooden-bodied constructs, metal-reinforced, spirit-bound, most of them close to a thousand years old.
- **Region:** Beside the shifting lands, to their west. Reached either by crossing the shifting lands with a guide, or by a long circle around their western flank.
- **Officially:** Not on any map. Not claimed by either kingdom. Not widely known to exist.
- **In practice:** A working settlement of patient, deliberate people who have seen what the last generation of power-seekers did to their kin and have stayed selectively invisible since. **Hidden by preference, protected by geography, kept alive by trust never given cheaply.**
- **Status:** Stable but under threat. The buyers currently offering extraordinary prices for spirit-bound artefacts (see `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"The Shifting Lands") have not located Haven yet. Haven intends to keep it that way. The party's discretion — if they earn the right to know the location — becomes part of that defence.

## What the party meets

The party has either crossed the shifting lands with a guide who knows the rock formations and river bends (the only landmarks that persist there), or circled around along the lands' western flank — a long detour with no road. Either way, **they arrive at Haven because they intended to**. Incidental visitors do not.

They come up over the last rise — or out of a pass if they approached from the circle-around — and the settlement is below them. It takes a moment to read. **Low buildings of ironwood and stone, scaled for person-sized inhabitants, with strip-metal details at the joints.** No movement at first. Then — slowly — a figure in the middle distance rotates toward them. A humanoid shape, ironwood body, metal strips at the elbows and knees. It does not hurry. It does not wave. It looks.

Others, further away, also turn their heads in the party's direction across the course of a minute. **Haven has seen them coming for most of a day, and is now letting them know it has.**

## How it reads at a glance — the 2–3 standouts on entry

- **Low buildings emerging from the landscape.** Ironwood and stone, person-scaled, no walls, no gate. **Nothing announces itself.** The settlement is simply *there* once the party is close enough to recognise it as a settlement. A casual traveller could walk past on a middle-distance track and not see it for what it is.
- **Figures that could be statues.** Scattered through the buildings and the open ground between them, humanoid shapes in ironwood and strip-metal. Some are stopped. Some are moving — at a pace the eye has to adjust to, then confirm. A party will **miscount the inhabitants**, because what they registered as a statue in one sweep has changed position in the next.
- **The western edge.** Toward the setting sun, the ground *changes*. A visible line past which the earth stops holding what is placed on it — markers weather off, cairns lean and fall overnight. Haven's buildings end where the shifting lands begin. **The settlement sits with its back to something that won't let a settlement exist.**

## At the approach — finding the way

The last leg to Haven is not a trail. It is a matter of following features the land cannot move: a specific rock formation, a particular river bend, the silhouette of a ridge against afternoon light. **A guide says it aloud every few minutes, because the guide knows how easily a party stops paying attention.**

- **No way-markers on the path.** Cairns do not last. Blazed trees erase themselves overnight. The shifting lands' reach extends onto the approach.
- **The guide becomes load-bearing.** A party that loses their guide before reaching Haven will struggle to find it. A party that loses the guide *after* reaching Haven will not find their way back out easily; Haven's inhabitants give the return route as sparingly as the arrival route.
- **Landmarks that do persist** are the ones the party should memorise. Haven's people will test that memorisation on departure by asking casually what the party remembers. They are checking whether the party can return — and whether they should be allowed to.

## At the outer buildings — first contact

A few low buildings stand where the approach ends. They are working buildings — storerooms, a smithy-shape, what looks like a kitchen with no smoke coming from the chimney. **A puppet waits in the middle of the open ground between them.**

- **The greeting is slow.** The puppet raises a hand; the hand takes three seconds. It asks a question; the question takes twelve. The party will feel the urge to speak in the pauses. Speaking in the pauses is permitted; interrupting the puppet's answer is not. The pacing is the conversation's *shape*, and the party will be assessed on whether they can match it within the first few exchanges.
- **There is no welcome speech.** The puppet asks who sent them, what brought them, and what they know. The answers matter. A party that was sent by a known friend (the pot-boy, a shaman the puppets remember, Wilder on a specific referral) will be seated faster than a party who arrived by their own curiosity. A party who arrived by accident is asked to leave — politely, slowly, but firmly — and given enough guide-information to get back out.
- **The puppet who greets is not *senior*.** Greeting is a role; decisions will happen elsewhere. A party pressing for more than the greeter's remit will be told, slowly: *"That is not my question to answer. Sit."*

## At the workshop yards — Haven's quiet work

Past the outer buildings, the settlement's working life becomes visible. Open yards between shops; puppets at benches and anvils; projects in progress at the glacial pace Haven operates at.

- **Maintenance is centuries-long.** A puppet repairing a joint on another puppet will work for a season on one elbow. The work is thorough. Nothing at Haven is half-repaired.
- **Ironwood everywhere, aged but whole.** Door frames, walls, puppet bodies. Grain patterns that took centuries to develop the deep dark colour of old ironwood. Nothing is rotting.
- **Metal scraps saved and sorted.** Every piece has a reason — a future repair, a joint that will need it, a reinforcement for a body that is thinning at the shoulder. **Nothing is waste.**
- **Tools the party has never seen.** Old mage-era instruments, modified for the puppets' own hands. A party that has met the pot-people (whose tools are similar in origin) will recognise the kinship immediately.
- **The work is visible but private.** A party may watch; they may not touch or pick up. A greeter will usually accompany them. If the greeter trusts the party, the range of access widens.

## At the council stones — the heart

At the centre of Haven is a flat stone circle. **Not a building — a place.** A dozen ironwood posts driven into the ground at intervals, marking where the old ones stand when a decision needs to be made.

- **Decisions at Haven are not fast.** The stones are used when a question is significant enough to ask the oldest puppets to assemble. "Significant" at Haven's pace can mean *this season*, not today.
- **Brought here, the party is being taken seriously.** Only a visitor whose presence has already passed the greeter's filter and whose business genuinely concerns Haven as a whole reaches this circle. Trade does not come here. Individual requests do not come here. The questions that reach the stones are: *should this person be trusted? should we move? should we speak to someone we have not spoken to?*
- **The oldest puppets are distinguishable.** Deeper grain; joints that have been repaired more times than the youngest puppets have been alive; a stillness that takes ten minutes to resolve as the puppet deciding to speak. One or two carry **original pre-Rift markings** on their chests — guild or workshop signs the white city's foundries used.
- **What happens here takes multiple visits to conclude.** A party hoping for an answer within one meeting will be disappointed. Haven will say *"come back when we have decided,"* and the party will have to leave and return on Haven's schedule, not theirs.

## At the western edge — the shifting lands' boundary

Walk far enough west and the settlement ends. Walk further and the ground is no longer ground.

- **A visible line.** Within a few steps, the difference is clear: earth that holds footprints versus earth that smooths them out within minutes; stones that stay where they fall versus stones that have rotated since the party last looked.
- **A few test-structures stand at the edge.** Small, experimental, built by the puppets over generations to understand what the shifting lands will and will not destroy. Most are rubble at various stages of rejection. A few older ones have lasted and become study-objects themselves — *why did this cairn survive and its neighbour not?*
- **Guides to the shifting lands are trained here.** A puppet who knows the lands' logic walks with apprentices along the edge, teaching which shapes persist. The party may be allowed to watch; they will not be allowed to accompany a serious crossing on the first visit.
- **This is where Haven is vulnerable.** Anyone who reached Haven via the shifting lands reached it along the edge the puppets know best. Anyone who reached Haven via the long circle-around did not. Haven watches both approaches; the edge is where the guides are.

## Visible patterns

- **Glacial pace of everything.** Greetings take minutes. Decisions take seasons. Walking across the settlement takes longer than the distance should require.
- **Miscounting the inhabitants.** Statues become moving figures; moving figures stop and become statues. The count the party makes today will not match the count they make tomorrow.
- **Conversations with long silences.** Silence is not hesitation; it is thought. A party that fills the silence is teaching Haven something about themselves. A party that sits with the silence is being taught something about Haven.
- **Everyone knows everyone's name.** The puppets of Haven have had a thousand years to learn each other. They will learn the party's names too, slowly, and use them exactly once per encounter to confirm the name belongs.
- **Guest accommodation is dusty.** Rooms exist for visitors. They are not often used. A party sleeping in one will find old furniture that has been waiting for them — kept usable, not recently used.
- **Metal and ironwood are *saved*, not discarded.** The party will not find broken pieces lying around. Everything has a future use.
- **The shifting lands are watched.** A puppet is always looking west. They rotate the watch; the watch never stops.
- **Nothing is in a hurry except the weather.** Storms come and go; the puppets do not hurry for them. The buildings were built to withstand weather slowly.

## What you learn here, and when

| Topic | When the place gives it up |
|---|---|
| That Haven exists and is real | Immediately on arrival. Hiding in plain sight ends when the party has walked in. |
| That the puppets are spirit-bound constructs | Within the first conversation — they do not hide this. |
| That the buyers of spirit-bound artefacts are a known threat | Freely, once the topic comes up. Haven knows the buyers exist. Haven will ask what the party knows. |
| That the pot-people in the buried tower are kin-not-kind | Once the pot-people are mentioned. A party that has met them is welcome to tell Haven what they know. |
| The white city, as remembered by the oldest puppets | **Earned trust, multiple visits.** Available from specific senior puppets, slowly. |
| How the puppets were treated pre-Rift | Carefully. The topic is still painful to them. |
| The route to Haven, for return visits | Given to trusted parties as the party leaves. Withheld from parties who failed the greeter's filter. |
| The black iron fortress beyond the shifting lands | Haven has heard of it. They do not know what is there. They are careful about speaking of it. |
| That Laurent remote-puppets never-bound bodies at his wreck | Not Haven's knowledge until the party brings it. **Their reaction shapes the Haven-Laurent arc.** |
| Spirit-binding technique and mage-era puppet-craft | Only with deep trust across many visits. This is Haven's most closely held knowledge. |

## If the party does something — what the place does

| If the party... | What happens |
|---|---|
| Approaches slowly and matches the pace | Welcomed. The greeter's filter opens further each time the party respects the pause. |
| Tries to hurry a puppet | The puppet does not change pace. The conversation continues at Haven's rhythm. Repeated hurrying closes the greeter's filter; the party will not reach the council stones. |
| Attempts to trade — metal, goods, currency | Possible, but Haven prefers **labour trades**. A party who fixes a gate, helps with a repair, or runs a delivery earns more goodwill than one who offers coin. Haven has what it needs of metal. |
| Offers to leave a map of Haven behind | Declined politely. *"We do not keep maps of ourselves."* |
| Tries to map Haven covertly | The puppets notice — they are always looking — but do not react at first. A sustained attempt loses trust quietly; the party will find the council stones out of reach. |
| Mentions the pot-people | Deep interest. Haven will ask every detail the party has. If the party has met them, Haven will gently press whether they are well, whether the furnace still runs, whether the boy is cared for. |
| Mentions the buyers of spirit-bound artefacts | **The mood shifts.** The puppets nearby stop whatever they are doing. Haven will ask what the party knows, who the buyers are, how close they are, and whether the party has been approached. This topic is existential. |
| Brings news of Laurent and his alcove puppets | **Long, careful conversation.** Haven has not known this. The puppets will want to understand whether the alcove-bodies were ever *intended* to be spirit-bound. Their views on remote-puppeting those bodies will be strong and specific. See also `doc/places/laurents_city.md` §"If the party does something". |
| Brings the pot-boy to Haven | **Set-piece scene.** The oldest puppets will rise from the stones to meet him. Haven will claim him as kin immediately. Whether he stays, returns to his tower, or becomes an ambassador between is a multi-visit conversation. |
| Offers to help hide Haven more effectively | **Welcomed gratefully.** Haven knows it is at risk. A party that brings skills (concealment, disinformation, counter-intelligence on the buyers) will be valued beyond trade. |
| Betrays Haven's location to outsiders | **Haven leaves.** Not immediately — it takes them a season to move. But they have a plan; they have moved before (once, long ago, well before this settlement was founded). The party returning to the spot will find ruins that already look centuries old. Haven will not be found again. |
| Tries to steal — tools, metal, a puppet-part | The puppets will surround the thief without speaking. No violence. The party will not leave the settlement while carrying stolen goods. The greeter's filter closes permanently. |
| Tries to free a bound spirit from a puppet | **Absolutely offensive.** The offer itself may end the visit. Haven's inhabitants *are* their bound spirits; they do not want to be "freed" any more than a living person wants to be killed. A party that acts on this is acting against Haven. |
| Asks to stay overnight | Allowed if invited. The guesthouse is dusty but habitable. The silence at night is profound; the party will not sleep well the first night. |
| Asks to learn spirit-binding or puppet-craft | **Deep-trust gated.** A student who returns across multiple visits, who has earned standing, who has a purpose Haven can support, may be taught fragments. Not a single-conversation matter. |
| Arrives with a shaman | The shaman is read immediately. Shamans who have worked respectfully with spirit-bound things in the past are recognised; shamans who have not are watched carefully. Haven's relationship with shamans is neutral-but-cautious. |
| Arrives with a buyer's agent by accident | The party is asked to leave *with* the agent, immediately. Haven will not engage further until the party returns alone and can explain. |
| Asks where Haven might go, if it had to move | Not answered directly. *"Where we went last time."* |

## DM notes

- **Pace the scene literally.** Haven's pacing is part of the place. At the table, let pauses be pauses. Puppet-speech should take genuinely longer than player-speech. A DM who rushes Haven through a scene loses what Haven *is*.
- **Trust is multi-visit.** The greeter's filter, the workshop access, the council stones, the spirit-binding lore — each is a further threshold. A single visit reaches one or two. A party that returns across the campaign unlocks more.
- **Secrecy is existential.** Haven is not shy out of preference; they are hiding from buyers whose prices suggest extractive intent. Make the stakes real. The DM should treat any party decision involving Haven's location as a live security question, and let consequences follow.
- **The pot-boy scene is a set-piece.** If the party brings him, clear time for it. Haven meeting a cousin they did not know existed is one of the campaign's highest-emotional moments. Play it slow, play it warm, give the council stones to it.
- **The Haven-Laurent arc opens naturally.** Once the party has been to both places, the question of Laurent's alcove puppets (humanoid bodies, kin in construct-type to Haven, never spirit-bound, currently remote-puppeted by a demi-lich) will come up. Haven has views. This is a real thread the campaign can develop.
- **The last flight option reaches here.** If the party frees the air elemental and rises Laurent's city for one journey, Haven is a destination Laurent's city *can* reach where a ground expedition cannot. The puppets would get off the ground for a one-way journey (a new site, further from the buyers; an arrangement with the shamans) if the offer were right. Whether they accept depends entirely on the party's standing.
- **No named NPCs yet.** The file treats Haven collectively. If the campaign reaches deep into it, a named elder or two will emerge naturally — the oldest puppet at the stones, the guide who trained the current edge-watchers. Let those names surface at the table rather than being assigned in advance.
- **Labour trades are the currency.** Haven does not need coin. Fix something. Run a delivery. Help with the edge-watch. These open doors that money does not.
- **The shifting lands' edge is a permanent teaching context.** Any party travelling with a Haven member learns a great deal about the shifting lands from the walk along the edge. This is a resource the campaign can use; it is also a reason to bring a Haven member along on a shifting-lands-adjacent arc.
- **Destroying a Haven puppet is destroying a person.** The DM should make this explicit if the party is considering violence here. Haven's inhabitants have had a thousand years to become what they are. Treating them as constructs is the mistake their pre-Rift makers made.

## Open questions

- How many puppets remain at Haven? Dozens? Hundreds? Small enough to know each other by name, per the council-stones ritual.
- Are any of Haven's inhabitants old enough to have walked the white city *before* the Rift, or are they all post-Rift survivors who emerged from the ruins?
- Is there a named elder — a spokes-puppet — who speaks for Haven when a senior answer is needed? The file allows one to emerge at the table; the question is whether canon should commit.
- Has Haven ever sent an emissary out into the world, or have they been strictly in-place for the thousand years since?
- Where did Haven live before *this* location? They moved once. When? Why did they stop moving?
- What do the pre-Rift guild markings on the oldest puppets' chests actually say? A mage-historian could read them; none has visited.
