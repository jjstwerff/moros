# The Elmsfield Inn (inside the great ironwood)

- **Type:** Inn — lodging, taproom, and the public heart of Elmsfield.
- **Location:** Inside the hollow of the great ironwood at the centre of Elmsfield.
- **Run by:** [Innkeeper Bridget](../npcs/bridget.md), with her daughters **Elly** and **Nina** working the floor.
- **Structure:** Lower floor and partial cellar inside the tree's main hollow. Upper floors exist but are boarded and tree-gripped; reopenable by labor (see below).

## What the party meets

The party walks in from the square looking for a bed, food, or a place to sit. The door is plain; what happens when they cross it is not. They are no longer inside a *building*. They are inside a *tree*. The walls curve the way a trunk curves. The ceiling is one continuous piece of grown wood. Whatever they expected an inn to feel like, this one feels like something else.

[Bridget](../npcs/bridget.md) is behind the bar doing two things at once; her daughters Elly and Nina are running plates; a few locals are at their tables; a merchant or two may be in the trader's corner. The room moves without anyone obviously directing it.

## How it reads at a glance — the three things that hit you at the door

- The **walls and ceiling are grown wood, not built**. The grain curves through corners no carpenter could lay. The structure is *the inside of a tree*, hollowed out and lived in. Old wood layered with hearth-smoke and whatever Bridget is serving today.
- The **hearth fits a knothole-recess** that came pre-made; the smoke goes up a flue that simply *is* the tree's old central channel. Look up the flue and you can see the knots of the trunk going up, impossibly, a long way above you.
- A **staircase goes up from one corner and stops at a boarded ceiling**. Once there were upper floors. The boards are old and fitted carefully — and the ironwood has **gripped the planks**, bark curled over the edges, grain pressing each plank tight from all four sides. Nothing special about the tools it would take to pull them out; everything special about the *work*.

## The bar floor

A wide oval cut into the hollow, hearth at one end and bar at the other. The **trader's corner** has the best light — a knothole-window high in the wall drops a shaft of daylight onto one specific table. Merchants gravitate there without being told.

The **cellar door is behind the bar**, and Bridget keeps the only key.

Above the bar hangs Bridget's **slate** — names and tabs in a hand only she fully reads. The slate's full meaning belongs with her (see `../npcs/bridget.md` §"How she reads at a glance" and §DM notes). In short: it is the Inn's public tab-ledger *and* a private record of what is hidden in the cellar walls, encoded in the same shorthand and sitting in plain view.

## The cellar

A small lower chamber, dug where the tree's roots allowed. Stores the Inn's kegs, drysalts, cured meat, and cold storage.

What the cellar is *really* for, the party will not learn on entry. **Parts of the wartime hoard are hidden in the cellar walls**, behind fitted panels that look like shelving. The ironwood has pressed those panel-fits tighter over the years, which is also why nobody has stumbled on them by accident. **Bridget goes down alone once a season with a lantern, for an hour.** She is not mysticking; she is doing inventory, reconciling what is behind the panels against the non-tab marks on her slate.

## The staircase — and the upstairs problem

The staircase ends at a boarded ceiling. Past this ceiling, a **second floor exists, large enough to double the Inn's capacity**. The blocker is not material; it is grip.

- **Labor.** The ironwood has tightened around each plank's edges over decades. Ordinary iron tools suffice to prise them out, but it is days of brutal overhead crowbar work, without splitting bark. A party offering this labor is offering genuine work.
- **Closure.** Bridget will not shut the Inn for several days of hammering overhead. A workable plan has to keep the room running through the work — night shifts while rooms are empty; staged plank-by-plank over weeks; a way to muffle or tent the work area.
- **The line.** Damage to the *tree* itself (splitting bark, chopping, burning) is the line that triggers town response, not just Bridget's. See `../npcs/bridget.md` §"If the party does something" for her reaction rows.

## Beyond the upper floor — the shaft and the compartment

Once the planks are off and the upper floor is opened, one wall of the room holds a feature nobody living currently knows about:

- A **narrow vertical shaft** runs up inside one of the tree's great branches. A Badger-sized body can climb it with work; a human needs ingenuity (and may not fit without widening the shaft — which the tree will not forgive).
- At the top: a **small secret compartment inside the branch itself**, fitted by whoever lived in the tree before the town existed.

**Bridget does not know the shaft is there.** Her slate-ledger does not refer to this compartment — the slate tracks only the wartime hoard she hid at cellar level. The shaft-compartment is a separate, older cache:

| Cache | Where | Age | Bridget knows |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Wartime hoard** | Cellar walls, behind fitted panels | ~one generation; hidden the night before the overseas force arrived | Yes. Tracks it on the slate. Visits quarterly. |
| **Secret compartment** | Top of the branch-shaft | Older than the town | No. The party's discovery to make. |

Do not let these two blur.

## Long-arc — the tree-hut and terraces

The same branch that holds the shaft is thick enough to carry a structure. Whoever lived in this tree before the town *used the height* — Bridget's own line foreshadows it (see `../npcs/bridget.md` §"the upstairs problem"). A party committed across many sessions, with Elmsfield's eventual help, can open the branch out into a proper **tree-hut with terraces overlooking the town**. Visible from every approach to Elmsfield. A public mark that the party changed the shape of the place.

This is Bridget's unspoken wish. She will not name it aloud until the upper floor is already under way.

## DM notes

- **Three-stage reward.** (1) Open the upper floor → a practical doubling of Inn capacity, and the relationship with Bridget pivots. (2) Find the shaft → an older mystery enters the campaign. (3) Climb to the compartment → contents are the DM's to place; the natural fit is something older than the war, not more war-hoard. Let each stage be earned; let the party's care for the tree be what opens the next stage.
- **Tree-hut is campaign-scale.** Do not offer it on a plate. Once built it is a permanent feature of Elmsfield's skyline and a visible sign the party changed the place.
- **Ironwood-grip is the physical mechanism throughout this tree** — the boarded ceiling above, the fitted cellar panels below. The same slow tightening protects both.
- **Relationship with Bridget's file.** Bridget's NPC file carries *her* (voice, mannerisms, reactions, the slate as personal prop, the hoard-ledger encoding). This file carries *the space* (physical layout, mechanics, caches, long-arc). When the party does something in the Inn, the mechanics live here and Bridget's response lives in her file's §"If the party does something."
