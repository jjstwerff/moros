# Hank's Farm

- **Type:** Working farm on the western edge of Elmsfield; orphanage-of-sorts built onto it over the years.
- **Region:** Edge of Elmsfield, outside the palisade, at the treeline.
- **Officially:** A farm owned by Hank (Hanny) and her mate Egbert.
- **In practice:** A sanctuary that takes in young people the kingdoms will not formally shelter — war-orphans from any side, Brumal refugees, the occasional stranded overseas-demi-human child. Clan-logic applied at scale.
- **Status:** Expanding slowly; respected by half the town, resented by the other half.

## What the party meets

The party reaches Hank's farm by following the western road out of Elmsfield until the palisade falls behind them and the cultivated fields open up. The farm is **bigger than a farm for one household has any right to be**; this is the first thing they notice. Then they see *who* is working it.

**Many young people, of many different folk.** Rabbit-folk, raccoons, boar folk, a young wolf folk or two, at least one human. They are doing the work of a farm — weeding, mending, carrying feed, hauling water. They are dressed in sturdy mended work-clothes that all came from the same hand. They glance at strangers carefully before going back to what they were doing.

**Hank is somewhere** — usually in the fields, sometimes in the kitchen, sometimes in the workshop sewing more clothes. She is not hard to find if asked for; the children will fetch her without a fuss. **Egbert is usually not visible during the day.** She sleeps when the farm is busy and works at night, when fewer eyes are watching her concealment.

## How it reads at a glance — the 2–3 standouts on entry

- **The scale.** The farmhouse is modest, but the cluster around it is not. New outbuildings that are clearly *new*: a second dormitory put up in the last year, a workshop that used to be a barn, a kitchen annex doubled in size. The land itself extends further than one household could plausibly work. Someone has been **expanding on purpose**, and the evidence of absorption — of *more people* arriving and being accommodated — is the farm's most visible fact.
- **The children, and what they are wearing.** The orphans are of mixed origins — war-orphans from both sides of the last war, Brumal arrivals, a few who drifted further. They are *all* wearing the same quality of sturdy, well-mended work-clothes. The shirts, trousers, coats are different sizes but the *hand* that made them is obviously the same. A party with any eye for craft will see it immediately: **somebody on this farm sews, constantly, and is good at it.**
- **The crop rotation.** A party with any farming knowledge — or Irna, if she is travelling with them — will recognise that the fields are laid out unusually well. Four-field rotation, properly done, with soil-care that most of Elmsfield's older farmers do not bother with. Yields here have been better than the neighbours'; the town attributes it to luck. A party who knows better will suspect someone on this farm has been *studying*.

## Visible patterns the party will notice

- **The children work and are unsupervised in the light way that suggests they have already been trusted.** Nobody is shouting at them. Nobody is standing over them. Hank walks through, checks, moves on.
- **Hank sews in the workshop most afternoons** — the clothing output for the children is how half the town knows her. A child passing the workshop door may leave a torn garment and come back later to find it repaired.
- **Egbert appears at dusk.** She plows the plots at night — the party may see lanterns moving across the fields after dark — and hunts in winter when the farm needs the meat.
- **Food comes from the kitchen in large, practical meals** — more vegetable than meat; Hank's rotation produces enough to feed the household.
- **New arrivals are settled without ceremony.** A cart pulls up with a refugee or an orphan; Hank meets them, asks three questions, shows them where to sleep. The farm absorbs.

## At the farmhouse

The original building, plain stone lower, timber upper. Hank and Egbert's private space is on the upper floor, small and private. The lower floor is the kitchen, the common room, the household's daily life.

- **A book tucked somewhere only Hank touches.** A farming manual she bought on the road to Elmsfield and has been reading in pieces for years. The source of the crop rotation. Hidden because Elmsfield notices when a farmer reads. See `doc/npcs/hank.md` §"Quiet accumulations."
- **A drawer with a leather purse, full again.** The original bag from the Salmonswell orphanage — left there by a Beaver-folk sergeant whose name Ingram would not give them — was spent to start the farm. Over ten years Hank has been replacing the sum coin by coin. The drawer now holds the full amount, ready to be carried back. She does not know the sergeant's name. She has the description Ingram gave her at eighteen, and a decade of resolve. See `../npcs/hank.md` and `../npcs/ingram.md`.

## At the dormitory outbuildings

Two, now — the original and the newer one. Rows of simple bunks, wool blankets, personal effects in small boxes at each bed. Clean, but lived-in. The children maintain their own spaces; the younger ones are helped by the older.

The party that looks will notice **the children's origins do not match the usual pattern for one region**. Brumal accents mixed with local ones. A rabbit-folk child next to an ox-tauren child next to a human. Clan-networks and refugee routes converge here.

## At the workshop

Hank's clothing-work is visible here — bolts of wool and linen, cutting tables, needles in cushions, a stack of repaired garments waiting for return. The party will find her here most afternoons.

She will talk while she works. Teaching the craft to an interested student is one of the ways she builds standing with new arrivals; anyone competent enough can pick up needlework here without being formally taught.

## At the fields

The crop rotation is visible if you know what you are looking at. Four plots in four phases of the cycle; a legume band where her neighbours would have a fallow; a vegetable patch close to the kitchen run by the older children.

A party that asks Hank about the fields will get a short honest answer about rotation. A party that presses for *how she knows* will find her shift the conversation — the book is not openly shared.

## What you learn here, and when

| Topic | When the farm gives it up |
|---|---|
| That this is an orphanage as well as a farm | Immediately — the children make it obvious. |
| Where the children come from | Carefully. Hank will describe patterns (war, Brumal crisis, clan networks) without naming specific individuals unless the child volunteers. |
| How the farm gets new children | Quietly. Word travels on clan networks; Hank does not advertise. |
| The clothing craft | Freely; Hank will teach any interested student. |
| The farming system | Once asked; less freely. Hank will say *"rotation"* and not much more without trust. |
| Hank's druidic training | **Only with earned trust.** Very few in Elmsfield know. |
| Egbert's concealment | Never confirmed openly. The farm works around it; the party may piece it together. |
| Sergeant Darrel's role in their origin | Hank does not know. A party who has learned it elsewhere should think carefully about telling her. |

## If the party does something — what happens

| If the party... | What the farm does |
|---|---|
| Offers to help with the work for a day | Accepted gratefully. Hank watches how they work. A party that takes it seriously earns real standing. |
| Brings a new child or refugee to the gate | Hank asks her three questions and settles them in. The farm absorbs. |
| Tries to take food from the kitchen without asking | A child intercepts, politely. *"That's for the house."* Hank hears about it within the hour. |
| Investigates Egbert at night | Egbert notices first. She will not confront; she will disappear for the remainder of the party's stay, and the household will be tense. |
| Repeats Elmsfield's scorn at Hank's face | Hank answers calmly. *"I know what you think. I'm not going to argue."* Does not retreat; does not fight. Some party members will feel the weight of what they said and adjust; others will not. |
| Threatens the children | The farm closes around them — older orphans first, then Hank and Egbert both. Egbert's wolf-folk capacity comes out; concealment ends for the duration. The party should not recover from this easily. |
| Brings Sergeant Darrel back to the farm after finding him at Luchebert | He will not identify himself. Hank will feel something she cannot name. The scene is the party's to shape. |
| Offers to teach the children something worth teaching | Hank accepts if the skill is one she judges useful (literacy, music, practical trades). Refuses if the skill is fighting-for-hire or theft. |

## DM notes

- **The farm is a soft place in a hard campaign.** It is where characters come to breathe. DMs should resist putting too many threats here; the farm works best as *the place that is currently holding*, which makes it meaningful when something later threatens it.
- **Hank's druidic training is a campaign-scale asset.** Her crop rotation is the visible face; her actual druidic capacity is closer-held. If the campaign needs a second druid in the setting (for Diederik's overseas plan, for any ritual needing two), Hank is a candidate. The party who earns her trust across visits opens this.
- **Egbert's concealment is fragile.** The farm's scorn-edge in Elmsfield is not random; the town has hunches about her. A careless party could settle the question for the wrong audience. Egbert's file details what she actually is and what happens if the concealment breaks.
- **The orphans are the future pressure-valve of the campaign.** As Brumal's crisis worsens, more children arrive; the farm's capacity is finite; the town's willingness to absorb is finite. At some point Hank will either need the party's help in a concrete way, or the farm will fail, or the farm will expand in a way Elmsfield cannot metabolise. Which of those happens is the campaign's.
