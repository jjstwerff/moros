# The Southern Desert

- **Type:** Continental band of chaos-pressed desert south of the blasted lands. The spirits that migrated out of the portal settled here and made the desert what it is now.
- **Region:** Runs from the south edge of the blasted lands to the far southern mountains (Gap city's range). Touches Allondo's southern flank; Brumal's southern border is actively being lost to it.
- **Officially:** Claimed by none. Crossed by few. Survived by one city (Fata morgana, inside) and one town (Gap city, on the far side).
- **In practice:** **A graveyard of human settlement under sand.** Since the rift, the desert has crept over pre-rift mage dwellings, post-rift settler homes, and fallen castles. The creatures living in those ruins now are not the humans who built them.
- **Status:** Expanding. Steadington lost recently. The edge is moving.

## What the party meets

The party comes off the southern trade road — which skirts the blasted lands and straightens into the desert's northern edge. Way-markers thin; traffic thins. At **Lastwater**, a caravan stop at the desert's edge, they are among travellers who are mostly not going further.

Past Lastwater, the road is a suggestion. Sand takes over. **Walking into the desert is walking into settled country now covered by dunes.** The first ruin they pass — a farmhouse, drifted to the lintels — reads as *recent*, not ancient. Brush at the sand and the door is still intact. Nobody has lived here in four hundred years.

Within half a day of Lastwater, the chaos-pressure starts. Workings stutter. Minds fray. Time reads wrong. A party without a mighty-spirit-reader (shaman, druid, priest) takes real damage on the crossing; with one, less.

## How it reads at a glance — the 2–3 standouts on entry

- **Sand at every lintel.** The first dozen ruins the party passes show the same pattern — doors still standing, sand halfway up the frame, a family name still readable in script on the door-post. **The loss is not ancient.** Pre-rift ruin is mixed in with post-rift settler ruin is mixed in with fallen castle, all under the same moving sand. The party cannot assume what the next ruin is, but they can assume it was someone's.
- **Fata morganas.** Shapes on the horizon that dissolve when looked at directly — reflections of distant places the traveller has seen or wanted. The city they are from. A figure from their past. A market. Looking past the vision makes it hold; looking directly unmakes it. **Unprepared parties chase them** and lose time, water, and position.
- **The chaos-pressure.** Not air; not weather; the *quality of the medium*. Workings stutter with distance from Lastwater; minds get tight by afternoon. A party carrying a shaman feels the difference immediately. A party without one learns by paying.

## At Lastwater — the desert's edge

A caravan-stop on the last reliable well before the desert. A low cluster of stone buildings around a deep well. Not a village — a waystation run by a rotating handful of families who take long turns operating it.

- **The well is the town.** Deeper than ordinary; protected by a small priest-attended working that keeps the water clean. Caravans pay for water by volume; travellers drink free.
- **A map-trader operates from a corner.** Maps of the desert that are *honest about what they do not know*. A route to Fata morgana marked in strong ink; routes to specific ruins marked with caveats; routes past the first day's travel marked with the warning *sand has moved*.
- **Travellers bound the other way** come through here — refugees from Brumal, scholars returning from Fata morgana, merchants from Gap city on rare runs. A party buys stories for the price of a drink.
- **Nobody offers to guide a stranger further south.** Guides exist; they work on arrangement, not walk-up.
- **An old desert nomad** sits in one of the low buildings most evenings — a regular at Lastwater for decades, between his crossings. The keeper's wife knows his name; the rest of the town calls him *the one who has seen the rooftop*. *Char* and *Will* over an evening's drink earns the story: years ago, on a wrong-route between Half-Tower and the Anchor's ridge, he came over a sand-step and saw *a stone roof rising out of the dunes that should not have been there*. He did not investigate; he turned for known water and made it back. He has never gone back. He will tell a respectful party where he saw it. (The **Lethran dwelling** — `doc/places/lethran_dwelling.md`.) He does not know whose house it was.

## The crossing — travel under chaos-pressure

Past Lastwater, travel is the scene.

- **Workings stutter at gradient.** Near Lastwater: barely. Half a day in: noticeable. Deep desert: real drag. Shamans adapt faster; mages and druids feel the strain.
- **Minds fray in predictable patterns.** Irritability, bad dreams, misread distances. Sleeping near a known ruin is worse than sleeping in open sand — the ruins are where the chaos thickens.
- **Maps do not hold.** A map sketched at noon no longer matches the dunes at dusk. Stable landmarks (rock outcrops, a specific ridge, a specific ruin's highest point) persist; shifting ones (paths, recent tracks, cairns) do not.
- **Fata morganas compound fatigue.** A party that chases even one loses a day. Chasing two in a week is dangerous.
- **Water discipline is the constraint.** Ordinary calculation of *how much we are carrying, how far to the next well* is everything. A party that has not done desert work before underestimates.

## At the Half-Tower — a fallen castle

Due south of Lastwater, two days' walk, a single tower rises above the dunes. **Cracked, partly collapsed, but standing.** Visible for half a day before reaching it. A party approaching from the north sees it as a landmark; locals avoid it.

This was a mage-era flying castle that crashed here when the rift unmade its working. The main body lies buried — ballroom, great hall, the lord's quarters, servants' wings. The Half-Tower is the portion that stayed above ground.

### What the ruin is

- **One tower still standing**, cracked from base to mid-height on the north face; sand drifted up the south side. Three floors of internal rooms still intact.
- **Buried structure underneath**, accessible through two entrances a party can identify with patience: a side-door the sand has half-cleared, visible at the foot of the tower; and the old courtyard-well, which drops straight down into what was the kitchen.
- **Preservation holds inside.** Mage-work on the stone kept the interior from dissolution. The buried rooms are *dry, dark, intact* — cloth, paper, and wood have not rotted.

### What lives there

- **A chimera lineage** nests in the half-buried great hall. Three or four adults; generations have lived here, inbreeding has narrowed them to a consistent form — lion-eagle-serpent composite, large, patient. They are **territorial, not predatory**. The hall is their home. A party moving through with respect for *distance* passes. A party moving aggressively is met.
- **Air elementals** circulate the tower's broken galleries. The castle's collapse is still happening, slowly; elementals are the collapse given form. They are not hostile by default; they respond to **noise and speed**. A quiet approach moves past them. A loud one provokes.

### What is there to find

- **The lord's personal quarters**, sand-sealed. Clothes in the wardrobes; a seal-ring on the desk; a mirror that has not aged. The room holds what the lord was using the day of the fall.
- **A journal of the fall** lies open on the study desk. The last entries, in a shaking hand, describe the working giving way, the lord's awareness he was going to land badly, **the names of his wife and children who were with him at the fall**. A party reading it gets the castle's personal face. **An earlier entry, dated months before the rift**, reads: *"Visited the Lethran household across the ridge today; pleasant evening; the child is bright; their work is the kind of work I hope my own children will live to see finished."* — one of the few surviving outside references to the family at the **Lethran dwelling** (`doc/places/lethran_dwelling.md`).
- **The library** holds pre-rift mage-work — scrolls, working-diagrams, notes in the lord's own hand. Rare material; a *Mage* specialist recognises the value immediately.
- **The children's chamber** is reachable off the lord's quarters. Toys; a small bed; a drawing on a slate that has kept its marks. A party that chooses to enter this room finds it hard to leave quickly.

### Combat framing — emotional investment

- **The chimeras are home-defenders, not the villain.** A fight here is *clearing a nest of creatures living in someone else's fallen house*. The DM should name this aloud through what the party sees — eggs or young near the engagement, the creatures' clear familiarity with the terrain.
- **The air elementals can be read as the castle's still-falling working.** A party that treats them as the house *settling* rather than *attacking* can move through without triggering.
- **The journal is the emotional fulcrum.** A party that reads the lord's last entries — naming his family — and then loots the children's chamber carries that decision. A party that reads and leaves the children's chamber alone carries something else. Both are legitimate. The DM should let the weight land.

### Approach options

- **Quiet and respectful.** Low chance of combat. High chance of passing through with what the outer rooms hold.
- **Negotiating the chimeras.** Difficult; they are not naturally talking creatures. A *Druid* can attempt; a **sphinx's** introduction (see Three Walls, below) carries weight with chimeras who recognise the older intelligence's vouching.
- **Aggressive clear.** Costly. Four adult chimeras are serious opposition; the air elementals escalate with noise; the hall is not favourable terrain for the party.
- **Digging a third entrance.** Possible. Requires *Ingenuity* or engineering work. A party that digs straight down into the lord's quarters from above avoids the hall entirely. The sand is heavy work; two days.

## At Three Walls — a sanded settler village

Four days' walk south-east of Lastwater, a half-buried settlement. The name is what the place *is* now: three standing walls of the largest building, plus scattered smaller ruins drifted around them. The village was abandoned four or five centuries ago; the sand has eaten most of the rest.

### What the ruin is

- **A central square**, mostly buried, with a **well at the centre**. The well still holds water — bitter, drinkable, not attended by any working. The sand did not take the water; the chaos took the crops. The settlers left because they could not eat, not because they could not drink.
- **The three walls** are part of what was the community building — a hall, a meeting-place. The roof and the fourth wall are gone. What remains shelters both creatures and party members who pitch camp there.
- **Smaller houses** radiate out from the square. Some are collapsed; some are drifted but whole. Stepping into one is stepping into someone's recently-abandoned home.

### What lives there

- **A serpent matriarch** nests under the three standing walls. Large; coiled; patient. She has **clutches** — a dozen young at various ages, in different rooms of the half-buried hall. She will defend the nest absolutely. The current matriarch is not the original; the lineage has held the site since not long after the settlers left.
- **Two sphinxes**, mated or mother-and-young, watch from the village's edge. They are **not hostile**. They are witnesses. They have been here since *just after the settlers were not*. They will speak to a party that addresses them respectfully, in a mode that drifts between conversation and riddle. They remember the village. They remember the people.

### What is there to find

- **Personal things in the houses.** A comb in a bedroom drawer. A child's wooden toy at a doorway. A family's hearth with its cooking implements still laid out — someone left in the middle of preparing a meal and did not return. **This is the register to lean into.** The houses are not dungeon rooms; they are the last minute of family lives.
- **A cache at the bottom of the well.** Sealed ceramic jar. The settlers hid something valuable before they left, hoping to return. They did not. **Contents are DM-fill**: heirloom jewelry, a deed to land in a village that no longer exists, a sealed letter, a small sum in old coin.
- **Names everywhere, if the party looks.** Door-posts. Wall markers. A carved family record in the community hall. A party that spends time can learn *who lived here*.
- **The sphinxes know what's in the well** and will tell a party that earns their trust — which is a test of priorities. Would the party rather *retrieve* the cache, or *be told* what it is?

### Combat framing — emotional investment

- **The serpent matriarch as mother.** A party respecting distance from the nest can move through the village. A party approaching the clutches — even by accident — meets her full response. **The combat choice belongs to the party**: escalate, respect, negotiate (through a sphinx's mediation).
- **The sphinxes are not combat.** They are conversation. Their mode is **grief-adjacent riddle rooted in ancient world lore**: *"what did the first humans bring through the portal that they would not have come without?"*; *"what does a chaos spirit lack that you have?"*; *"whose work sleeps under sand and waits, and whose work was the sand?"*; *"the rift opened a door — what was the door for, before the chaos came through it?"* (See `doc/claude/LORE.md` for the world's deep lore the sphinxes draw from.) They ask; the party answers; the quality of the answer shapes what they share. **Honest *"I do not know"* is always a true answer.** Pretending to know is the wrong answer; thinking aloud is the right one.
- **The emotional fulcrum is the personal things.** A DM who describes the comb, the wooden toy, the cooking-implements in specific detail lets the place weigh what it is. A party that pockets a named heirloom is carrying something home that has a real owner (a descendant — findable at Fata morgana, Gap city, or back through Brumal).

### Approach options

- **Pass through respectfully.** The sphinxes may step out and speak. The serpent matriarch ignores. Personal things can be observed and left.
- **Camp in the three walls.** Shelter is real; the serpent knows the party is there; the sphinxes watch. A night's rest is possible if the party carries themselves well.
- **Address the sphinxes.** A riddle-conversation. Good answers earn real knowledge — village history, the well's cache, the route to Fata morgana or Gap city, names of descendants. **A party that asks specifically about *Lethran* or *the Pair* or *a household near the Half-Tower's ridge*** receives the **Lethran dwelling**'s direction in the sphinx's register (`doc/places/lethran_dwelling.md`); the sphinxes have been watching that ridge as well, across the same generations they have watched here. *Char* and *Will* test for the trust that earns the directions in the right detail.
- **Retrieve the well's cache.** Possible with rope and patience. The serpent matriarch will not interfere with the well itself — it is outside her defended perimeter. The water is bitter; the jar is sealed.
- **Fight the matriarch.** Serious opposition. Possible. The clutches die with her if the party follows through; the sphinxes will leave silently and will not speak to that party again.

## At a mage-dwelling — DM-fill location

Scattered across the northern desert where the blasted lands bleed into it, individual pre-rift mages built solo work-retreats. Most are too buried to find; a few still poke up enough to be visible. Each was **one mage's workspace**, built for the specific workings that mage practised.

- **Inhabitants vary by mage.** A working-chamber that focused on air-work will hold **dust elementals** made of the specific residue. A retreat that bound creatures will hold their **chimera descendants**. A dwelling whose value warranted attention will have **a sphinx guarding it**.
- **Treasure is specific.** Pre-rift mage-work in the niche the original mage practised — air-working, spirit-binding, preservation, elemental negotiation, illusion. Rare material. Deep value to a student of that niche.
- **Emotional hook varies with the specific dwelling.** A mage who worked alone for decades leaves a specific kind of room behind — notebooks, a preserved meal that was never eaten, a chair at a window, a single named spirit bound in a jar and still patiently waiting.
- **DM-fills this as the campaign needs.** When a party's arc wants a mage-dwelling — for a specific working, a specific kind of knowledge, a specific emotional beat — the DM places one in their path and develops it from this register.

Within this region: Fata morgana is the surviving case, the deployment of mighty-spirit-readers that holds a city where no other settlement has held since the rift. Parties crossing the desert will *see it*, eventually — a shimmer on the horizon that resolves into walls and green.

## Gap city — the far side

**Cross-reference (brief).** On the southern side of the desert, in the mountains. Not part of either kingdom. Crossed to by rare travellers; corresponds with Fata morgana; worth the crossing for parties going far south. Currently no full place file; DM-fill as the campaign reaches.

## Visible patterns

- **Sand moves daily.** The landscape the party sees at dusk is not the landscape they saw at noon. Stable landmarks (rock, a specific ruin's highest point) persist.
- **Fata morganas appear in clusters at dawn and dusk.** Midday heat dissolves them.
- **Creatures are at ruins, not in open sand.** Open-desert travel is monotony; ruins are where the life is.
- **Names on doorposts persist.** Sand scratches stone slowly; inscriptions a century old are still legible. A party that brushes a lintel can read *who lived here*.
- **Chaos-pressure thickens at ruins.** The desert itself is bad; the ruins are worse. Sleeping near a ruin is a decision.
- **Bones at specific ruins.** The humans who did not leave. A party stopping to observe learns that some families tried to stay.
- **Water costs everything.** Decisions in the desert are water-decisions.

## DM notes

- **The desert is loss, not villainy.** Every ruin is somebody's former home. Every creature is a tenant of that home. Every combat is a fight that happens *in* someone's house. Lean into this. A DM playing the desert as dungeon-corridor loses the register.
- **Chaos-pressure as pacing.** Short visits are bearable; long ones are real cost. Structure desert arcs in passes — in, do the thing, get back to Lastwater or Fata morgana, recover. A party that lingers pays.
- **The creatures are *species*, not monsters.** Chimeras as lineage; serpents as matriarchal; sphinxes as intelligence; air/dust elementals as the desert's voice. A DM's description should carry this — their ecology is a real thing, not a bestiary entry.
- **The Half-Tower is a fight-or-avoid decision.** The party has real choices. A party that avoids the chimeras loses some treasure and gains some respect; a party that fights gains some treasure and loses some emotional ground. Both are legitimate.
- **Three Walls is primarily a conversation scene.** The sphinxes are the centre. The combat option (with the serpent matriarch) is the ugly fallback for a party that escalates.
- **The journal and the personal things are the whole point.** If a party finishes either location without registering *who lived here*, the DM has not surfaced the location's heart. Make the lintels readable. Make the children's toys visible. Make the hearth-implements specific.
- **Descendants in Fata morgana and Gap city can receive returned heirlooms.** A party carrying a named item from Three Walls to Fata morgana finds a family there — second or third generation — who carries the name. The reunion is a *scene*. The DM should let it land.
- **The mage-dwellings are a currency.** Do not spend them on first crossings. Save them for when the campaign needs a specific piece of pre-rift knowledge; then place one in the path.
- **Fata morgana as resupply and recovery.** A party that makes Fata morgana a base for deeper desert work does well. The city can absorb a shaken party for a week while workings settle and minds unwind.
