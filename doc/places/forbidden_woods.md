# The Forbidden Woods

- **Type:** Forested region; live magical hazard
- **Region:** North of the two kingdoms; south of Raft city
- **Political status:** None — no polity claims it, and no polity wants to

## Summary

A forest that still holds a chaos spirit. The spirit's presence warps the trees inside — growth patterns bend, bark behaves wrongly, the forest itself is not quite stable from one season to the next. This is why the woods are *forbidden* rather than simply *avoided*: passing through is a known, ongoing risk, not a historical legend.

The forbidden woods are also the barrier between the settled south and **Raft city** to the north. Travellers who want to reach Raft city either pass through the woods (quickly, knowledgeably, and with reasons to take the risk) or find a long way around them. The river through the great forest (which Raft city sits on) runs through this territory.

Whether the "forbidden woods" and the broader "great forest" are the same thing, or whether the woods are a specific warped section of a larger forest system, is currently open.

## The chaos spirit

Per `doc/claude/LORE.md`, chaotic spirits are rare on this continent and more common overseas — they are part of why mage workings unravel here where they do not unravel there. The one in the forbidden woods is one active, local example. There are others:

- **The blasted lands** south of the main trade road also hold chaotic spirits, and some of them have migrated into the southern desert where they are currently making the desert much more hostile than natural (see `fata_morgana.md`). The forbidden-woods spirit and the blasted-lands spirits may be the same kind, related, or merely similar in category; not yet established.

### Tod — the spirit's accessible face

The forbidden woods' chaos spirit is **the most accessible chaos spirit the party can meet**, because it has done something the others have not: it has taken a body and made itself talkable. A **young fox boy** walked into the woods ignoring the warnings and died there. The spirit inherited the body. It walks around in him now — not naturally, but in the way the spirit *thinks a boy walks*, which is close enough to fool a glance and deeply wrong if you watch for a minute.

It has given itself a name: **Tod**. Simply "fox" — the creature the body used to be. The spirit does not use (or does not know) the boy's real name. It has picked a word that describes what it is wearing and adopted it as identity. That is characteristic of how it thinks.

Tod is a piece — a *small piece* — of the full spirit. The main bulk remains diffuse in the woods and in the warped trees; the Tod-fragment is what the spirit condenses into when it wants to walk and talk. Conversations with him are partly coherent, partly alien. He will ask questions a boy would not ask. He may be hungry without knowing what for. He may remember the boy's life and also not quite know what a life is.

Tod is not lethal to encounter. He is not trying to trick anyone. He is not even opposed to this world's rules — he is from elsewhere and does not yet know them well enough to move through this world without accidentally bending it (see LORE.md §"Why the chaos"). The danger is subtler: prolonged contact with him is contact with the chaos spirit's underlying nature, and prolonged contact with a chaos spirit's nature is what makes magical workings unravel and makes minds fray at the edges. Short visits are safe. Long ones teach the party things their own spirits start to resent. Patient conversation, though, is something the shamans routinely use to *teach* chaos spirits to approximate this world better — and is one of the few ways the chaos is slowly reduced, piece by piece.

**Campaign function.** Tod is the setting's gentle introduction to the chaos-spirit ecology. A first scene with him — sympathetic, uncanny, educational — prepares the party for the larger stakes: Fata morgana's arrangement, the blasted-lands pressure, the shamans' long plan. Meeting him safely first is how the party learns what the rest of the setting is protecting itself against.

See `doc/npcs/tod.md` for his full entry.

### The spirit's shape — large, slow, settled

As chaos spirits go, the one in the forbidden woods is **one of the larger ones**. It is also **one of the least active and least mobile**. It is more like a deep pond than a stream — mass without movement. That shape has three practical consequences:

1. **It is hard to influence.** A small, active chaos spirit can be taught, redirected, or nudged by a patient shaman. This one cannot. Its sheer inertia is what makes conversation with Tod informative but conversation with the *whole* spirit largely impossible; the mass responds too slowly to be led anywhere.
2. **It does not spread.** Partly because of the mighty-spirit-readers ringing the region (druids, Luchebert's priests, Felicia's diffuse presence, possibly the ironwood tree) — which a chaos spirit backs off from — but partly just because this one does not move.
3. **It *lashes out* when threatened.** Slow to respond, but violent when it does. It cannot distinguish small from large threats cleanly, but it reads *active provocation* (major workings performed in its territory, destruction of its warped trees, deliberate binding attempts) as threat and responds disproportionately. The danger is real when it comes — the spirit has mass behind it — but it is triggered only by significant acts.

**A normal party is not a threat to it.** Visiting the edges of the woods, talking to Tod, leaving without doing anything ritual is entirely safe. The spirit simply does not register them as a danger, because they are not one. This is what makes Tod the party's *gentle* introduction: the door is open; the creature on the other side of it is inert and patient so long as the party doesn't try to pry the door off the frame.

### Why the woods' spirit has not spread

Spirits respect mightier spirits and back off from them. Chaos spirits obey the same rule, and they cannot tell the difference between actual spirits and powerful mages, shamans, druids, or priests. The forbidden woods are ringed, in effect, by mighty-spirit-readers: the druidic presence around Allondo, Luchebert Monastery and its priests in the hills south of the woods, possibly Felicia herself as a great spirit whose works still run the hill country, and possibly the ironwood tree of Elmsfield. The woods' chaos spirit *is* wild and *could* migrate — but wherever it might go, something more powerful (to its reading) is already there. It stays. Its own inertia reinforces the containment: even if the shields around the region thinned, this particular spirit would be slow to take advantage.

Open questions:

- The fox boy's original name and village of origin — and whether his family is still looking for him.
- Whether the spirit *knows* it is wearing a dead boy, or whether it thinks this is simply what it is now.
- Is the spirit related to the blasted-lands spirits, or independent?
- Exactly *which* mighty-spirit-readers are holding the main spirit in place? (Useful to know before any of them leave or fall.)

## The crashed Lightning Tower

At the **fringes of the forbidden woods**, a **mage-era tower has recently crashed** — the **Lightning Tower**, once part of the cage-ring at the Lake of Tears (see `doc/npcs/storm_mage.md` §"When he enters his tower"). A broken-minded Storm Mage, returned to his old tower by the party, had a brief bout of lucidity there, activated the tower's workings, flew it north, and the working failed as the tower crossed into the woods' influence. The wreck is now here.

**For the party, this turns the forbidden woods from backdrop into destination.** Investigating the crash means standing at the edge of Tod's territory. The Storm Mage is still at the wreck, wandering around it, even more confused than before. The tower itself is salvageable in parts — workings, levers, materials — for a party willing to camp in the chaos spirit's shadow. Tod may wander out to investigate the event.

This is the in-world **entry point to the forbidden woods thread** for most parties: they follow a tower they were responsible for flying here, and the woods are where the tower came down.

## Campaign hooks

- **A route problem, not a destination.** The woods obstruct travel to Raft city (and possibly to Salmonswell, if the rivers align that way). Any reason the party has to go north makes the woods a live scene.
- **A living example of a chaos spirit.** If any session wants to make the setting's magical ecology concrete, the forbidden woods are the cheapest way to show it — the warping is physical and visible.
- **A territory nobody owns.** Refugees, outlaws, and those who want to disappear might already be using the edges of the woods. Useful for hiding or for dark secrets.
