# The Blasted Lands

- **Type:** Post-rift ruin of what was once the continent's agricultural heart. Pre-rift: vineyard terraces, grain fields, thickets, communal demi-human halls feeding the white city. Now: chaos-pressed, creature-held, slowly reclaiming itself in shapes the living community no longer recognises.
- **Region:** South of the main trade road, which skirts the northern edge. North of the southern desert, which bleeds into the lands' southern fringe. The **portal** sits at the centre, inside the ruins of the white city.
- **Officially:** Nobody's. The kingdoms' maps mark it as *do not enter*. Scavengers go in anyway.
- **In practice:** **A landscape of broken agreements.** Every dangerous place was once a *helpful* one. Every creature is still performing a role whose reason has been gone for a thousand years. The horror is *reciprocity betrayed*, not wilderness.
- **Status:** Stable in its ruin. The portal's old residue keeps the region in a slow, warped equilibrium that neither heals nor worsens. The creatures and the warped spirits keep their places; the ruins stand; the chaos-pressure thickens toward the centre.

## What the party meets

The party comes off the southern trade road at its skirting point. Most travellers do not turn south here; the road curves east to keep them on safe ground. A party that *does* turn south descends into the blasted lands through a treeline that is still a treeline — the thickets that bordered the pre-rift fields are rooted, alive, the exact size they were planted. Beyond the thickets, the slope opens.

**The first thing they see is cultivation still readable under ruin.** Terraced vineyard-hills where no vines grow any more; grain fields gone to a thin strange grass; a large stone building on a ridge where the community's press-house used to be. Not a farmhouse. A *press-house* — tall, open-roofed, the stone vats still visible. **Nobody lived alone here.** A party that has read the southern desert reads the blasted lands differently immediately: this was not homes. This was **shared work**.

Within the first hour, something will be heard or seen that **is still going through its motions**. A bell that rings the harvest-hour in an empty tower. Smoke rising from a press-house chimney where no fire has burned in centuries. A figure on a distant field, walking the harvest-line at the right pace, carrying nothing to harvest. The land still *works*. It just works *wrong*.

## How it reads at a glance — the 2–3 standouts on entry

- **The cultivation is still readable.** Vineyard terraces under scrub; grain fields gone to weed in ordered rectangles; thickets too regularly spaced to be wild. A party's first read of the landscape is *this was farmed*. Not by individuals; by community. The field-shapes are too large for any single household to have worked.
- **Communal halls on the ridges, not houses in clusters.** Large stone buildings visible from distance: press-houses, granaries, eating-halls, storage-barns. No individual dwellings between them. **Demi-humans lived together here**, slept together, ate together, worked shared land. A party familiar with Haven recognises the construction — stone-and-ironwood, community-scale, built to last centuries.
- **Something still performing its old role.** Within an hour of crossing in, the party sees or hears one: a harvest-bell ringing at the correct hour; a figure walking a field-line with no crop present; smoke from a cold chimney. **The motions continue. The reason is gone.** A party reads this as haunting; a shaman reads it as chaos-held duty.

## At the trade road's skirt — the northern edge

Where the main southern trade road curves east to avoid the blasted lands. A party turning south here is choosing the region deliberately.

- **No way-markers past the curve.** The road-tenders do not maintain anything past the last wayfarer's stone. A stone remains where the road *used to continue* southward before the rift; the stone marks *where the rift broke what was a road*, and has been left as a memorial.
- **John Bean's ironwood mill** lies somewhere along this edge — the abandoned building where John lost his arm to the cutting-machine inside. See `doc/npcs/john_bean.md`. A party that visits it learns the blasted lands' *industrial* edge: this was not just agriculture, it was also the continent's centre of ironwood-working.
- **Wilder's hill** is at the western flank. See `doc/npcs/wilder.md`. A party approaching from Salmonswell's direction passes close; Wilder notices them and will intercept if he judges it worth the walk down.
- **Scavengers on the trade road** sometimes. Ruin-crawlers heading in or out, often avoiding each other. A party taking directions from them gets names of specific ruins, warnings about specific creatures, and a clear sense that most ruin-crawlers *do not go near the centre*.

## The crossing — workings that complete but warp

Chaos-pressure in the blasted lands is a different flavour from the desert's.

- **Workings complete, but they return warped.** Unlike the desert — where workings *stutter* and fail — the blasted lands let workings *finish*, and what comes back is a version of what was asked for that is slightly wrong. A healing working heals but leaves a mark. A light working lights but in a wrong colour. A divination returns an answer that is true but keyed to the wrong question. A shaman can read this gradient; the closer to the centre, the more drastic the warping.
- **Workings that touch spirits are *especially* vulnerable.** Calling a spirit, binding one, speaking with one — all higher-risk here. The spirit that answers may not be the spirit asked for; may be a *warped-local version* of the spirit called.
- **Minds fray in *community-shaped* patterns.** The party dreams of people they have never met — demi-humans at a harvest supper, children at a communal cradle, workers at a threshing. The dreams do not harm; they *populate*. A party waking in the blasted lands has more people in their head than they did going to sleep.
- **Time marks wrong.** Specifically: the agricultural calendar still *insists*. A party will notice the urge to *plant* in spring, to *harvest* in autumn, in places where they had no such intention. The land's pre-rift rhythm is still running in its bones and trying to pull travellers into its pace.

## At Silvertrace — the stream-temple and the swallowing ground

A half-day's walk south of the road-skirt, along a shallow valley that used to hold a stream.

### What the place is

- **A small stone temple** stands intact on the near bank of what was a stream. Carved lintel: **"To Silvertrace, who gives the field."** Inside: an altar, a shallow basin, offerings still piled at the rim — coins, carved tokens, a specific ritual spoon, items of thanks from a community that no longer exists. The temple's preservation is not accidental; Silvertrace was a bound-spirit of genuine weight, and the temple was built to last.
- **The stream-bed is dry.** Not dry *in a drought sense* — dry in the sense that **the water has been warped and is no longer water**. The bed is now **ground that swallows**. Hard to see; the old waterline was marked by reeds and river-sedge, both long gone. The bed reads as continuous with the surrounding grass until a foot is on it, and by then the foot is going down.
- **A wooden man's arm and part of a head** protrudes from the ground a few paces past where the water would have been. Ironwood, old but intact. A party pulling at the arm finds **no body under the ground any more**. It has been moved. Where it went is a mystery the party can follow.
- **The spirit is still there.** Silvertrace did not die; it *warped*. It can be sensed by a *Shamanic* character standing at the temple. Its register has gone from *helping* to *pulling down*. It does not intend harm in the way a creature does; it is doing what it has been warped into doing.

### Approach options

- **Respect the line.** A party that reads the terrain carefully — which requires *Hunter* or *Druid* or patience — can identify the old waterline (subtle ground-colour change, the specific angle of the slope) and walk around the swallowing ground. Safe passage; no combat; the temple can be visited from the near bank.
- **Negotiate with the warped spirit.** Very difficult, but possible. Requires a shaman of real standing, the temple's offering-rituals observed correctly, and an honest address of the spirit's warped state. A party that convinces Silvertrace of the community it has lost — names the people who gave it the temple — may lessen the swallowing for a single crossing.
- **Attempt to *un-warp* the spirit.** A campaign-scale project, not a scene. Would require multiple returns, multiple offerings, a working the shamans' long plan aligns with. A real arc. See DM notes.
- **Try to cross the swallowing ground regardless.** Dangerous. The ground takes — it is *how the warped spirit now holds its shape*. A party attempting the crossing without preparation loses someone.

### Treasure and emotional weight

- **The offerings on the altar.** Dozens of small items, centuries old, still waiting for the help that has not come. Pocketing them is easy; doing so is a choice with weight. These are a community's thanks to a spirit that has turned against them.
- **The wooden man's arm.** Ironwood craftsmanship, identifiable to a party who has met the pot-people or Haven as **the same workshop-lineage**. The arm is a puppet's arm. The question is where the rest went.
- **The spirit's name.** *Silvertrace*. A shaman carrying the name can attempt to reach the spirit elsewhere in the region; a priest can pray with the name; a mage can research the name at Luchebert's library.
- **The emotional fulcrum** is the lintel. **"To Silvertrace, who gives the field."** The past tense lives in the present-tense carving. A party that reads it has the register of the whole region in a sentence.

### The moved body — a campaign-hook

The wooden man's body is gone. Somewhere. A party that walks other ruins in the blasted lands may find **parts of it** surfacing at different sites — an arm at one ruin, a torso at another, legs at a third. The chaos is *arranging* something with the puppets it has taken. *What* it is arranging is a DM-level open question. Possibilities: the parts migrate toward the portal; they are being reassembled by something at a specific location; they are being *held* somewhere a visitor has not yet reached. **Haven would want the parts back.** A party returning pieces of lost kin to Haven is doing something real.

## At the Great Thresh — the harvest-works

South of Silvertrace, at the foot of a wide-fielded ridge, stands a large open-sided stone building. The **Great Thresh**: the communal harvesting-works where the region's grain was processed before moving to the white city's granaries.

### What the place is

- **The building is partially intact.** Roof gone; walls standing; the central threshing-floor visible. **Great stone wheels** — the grinding-works — still in place. The mage-working that powered them is still *present* in the stones.
- **An iron elemental walks the threshing-floor.** Large — human-sized or larger — shaped from the iron and ironwood of the harvest-machine. Not a puppet; an **elemental**, a being of the machine's bound spirit still performing the motions. Walks a specific circuit: the approach to the grinding-stones, the motion of feeding the stones, the turn to fetch more. Over and over. Centuries.
- **There is no grain.** The elemental is performing the work without material. What it grinds is empty air.
- **Old grain stores under the threshing-floor.** Cellars preserved by a mage-working that has not unravelled. Grain from before the rift, still edible, in quantities sufficient to feed a party for months.

### Approach options

- **Watch the circuit and step around it.** The elemental's path is predictable; a party can move through the building without engaging by timing the gap between passes.
- **Interrupt the work.** Standing in the elemental's path is engagement. It will *push through*, politely at first — the way a worker moves past a person in the way — and less politely if the person does not move. A party that stands firm is met with the elemental's full attention.
- **Bring it grain.** The elemental was built to grind grain; bringing it grain lets it *complete a working* for the first time in centuries. The elemental's register shifts — from mechanical performance to *genuine engagement*. This is the **relational path** (parallel to the volcano tower's fire elemental; see `doc/places/volcano_tower.md`). A party that does this earns real trust.
- **Ask it about the work.** A *Shamanic* character can speak to it. It remembers — dimly, mechanically — the community that used to feed the stones. Names; voices; specific workers. A party that listens carefully learns more about the pre-rift community here than from any other source.
- **Try to free it from the binding.** Possible, and *offensive* if done wrong. The elemental *is* the binding; it has become a person through centuries of operation. Freeing it without its consent is the Haven-analogue mistake. A party that proposes freedom *to it* may get a *yes* or a *no* — the elemental does not know yet what it wants.

### Treasure and emotional weight

- **The grain-stores underneath.** Real food in practical quantities. A party crossing the blasted lands will eat for weeks from what is here.
- **The mage-working in the grinding-stones.** A *Mage* or *Ingenuity* specialist can read the working's structure. Rare pre-rift mechanical mage-craft; valuable to any student of that niche.
- **The elemental's memories.** A shaman who engages it over multiple visits can assemble a **community roster** — names of the workers, the families, the specific demi-humans who fed these stones. Possibly names kin to people the party has met (Haven, specific demi-human villages in the hill country).
- **The emotional fulcrum** is the circuit. The elemental has been walking the same path for a thousand years. Watching it for an hour is its own scene — and the decision of whether to interrupt, engage, or pass by is the character-reading moment of the location.

## At Commons House — the cerberus's empty duty

Further south-east, at the centre of what was one of the largest demi-human working-communities in the region, stands a large stone building that **looks intact**. Two storeys. Wooden shutters still closed. A path of flagstones leads to a double door. The grass around the building is kept low, as if walked — though nothing walks it.

### What the place is

- **A cerberus guards the door.** Three-headed; massive; patient. It is not a dog, though it has dog-shape. It is the *bound guard-spirit* of Commons House, still on duty. Its three heads watch the path, the door, and the ground. A party approaching within fifty paces is noticed. A party approaching the door without address is challenged.
- **The building looks intact. It is not.** From outside, a party cannot see this; the windows are shuttered. **The cerberus cannot see it either** — its duty is to protect the building from intruders, not to report on the building's state. A party that convinces the cerberus they have lawful entry may find what is inside.
- **Inside: the hall has been emptied.** Not by scavengers. The great tables are gone; the cooking-stones cold; the wood-panels that lined the walls with community records have been **removed, cleanly, and taken somewhere.** The hall is empty in a way a merely-ruined hall is not. Someone has *taken* the community's memory and moved it.
- **The cerberus does not know.** Its duty is the door. It cannot perceive the emptying. A party that tells it what they found inside is telling it something it cannot process in the ordinary sense.

### Approach options

- **Address the cerberus in its register.** A shaman, a priest, or a character with formal training can speak to a bound guard-spirit in the mode it recognises. State name, state business, state claim to lawful entry. The cerberus will assess. A credible claim — even a partial one — earns passage.
- **Show the cerberus an old token.** If the party has recovered anything from Silvertrace or the Great Thresh with the community's marks on it, showing it to the cerberus registers as *one of us*. Passage becomes likely.
- **Fight the cerberus.** Three-headed; large; a genuine combat. Possible to win; costly. The cerberus is not a mindless guard — it is an old spirit doing its job, and *killing it is killing the last active member of the community*. A DM should let this weight land if the party commits to it.
- **Tell the cerberus the truth about the hall.** Risky. The cerberus cannot process *the building is empty* in the ordinary sense. What happens when a bound guard-spirit learns that what it guards is gone is a DM decision — it may discharge its duty and fade; it may become enraged at whoever carried off the panels; it may refuse to accept the news and double down on the door.

### Treasure and emotional weight

- **The missing wall-panels** are the real treasure of this location. A party that *pursues* them — where did they go? who took them? — is following the blasted lands' deepest mystery, connected to the moved body at Silvertrace and to whatever is arranging things at the centre. The panels held names of every demi-human who lived and worked here: births, harvests, deaths, a full community record.
- **Haven would want the records.** Some of the names on the panels are ancestor-names of Haven's iron puppets. Returning the panels — or even copies of them — is a real gift.
- **A shrine to the house's smaller spirit** remains on the upper floor: an eating-place deity, a granny-figure to whom the community's children were presented. Still tended, somehow — a single candle lit in a lantern that has burned for a thousand years. This is the *other* spirit of Commons House, smaller than the cerberus, not bound to defend, still simply *here*.
- **The emotional fulcrum** is the cerberus itself. **A guard who does not know what he guards has become.** A party that earns his trust and then has to decide whether to *tell him* or *leave him in his duty* carries a decision with real weight.

## Wandering creatures and environmental hazards

Not location-bound; encountered across the region.

- **Jackalopes** — small horned hares in the overgrown pastures where livestock was once kept. Skittish; impossible to hunt properly (arrows land, but the creature "isn't there" when the arrow arrives); they bleed wrong colours. The livestock the region used to raise, chaos-warped. Not dangerous; not useful. A *signal* that the land is still producing its old outputs in broken form.
- **Hippogriff** — flies over from time to time. Looks like a mount. Will descend *close enough to look useful* — and will not answer any call. A party exhausted in the field, seeing it, will feel the specific cruelty of help-in-shape-without-substance. If it is chased or shot at, it leaves easily; if it is approached respectfully and *not asked for*, it may sometimes land near a party and walk with them for a stretch, without ever becoming a mount.
- **Spoken grain-voices.** In the overgrown fields, on the wind, a party sometimes hears what sounds like speech — not words, but the cadence of conversation, at the pace of workers moving along a harvest-line. No source. Ends when the party pays direct attention. This is the community's *voices* still in the wind, not a creature per se.
- **Warped spirits of smaller places.** Wells that do not return what is dropped in; ovens whose smoke burns cold; cradle-shaped stone niches where the community's children were blessed, now subtly wrong to stand near. Not every ruin has one; many do.
- **Swallowing ground at other sites.** Silvertrace's warped stream is the clearest example; a party will encounter others. The pattern: places where water used to flow, now warped to pull down whatever stands on them. Readable with terrain-skill.

## The white city — the centre

**Cross-reference.** At the centre of the blasted lands lie the ruins of the **white city** itself — and the **portal**, still glowing, still shimmering, the physical structure the first humans built to stabilise their arrival. The region's chaos-pressure is highest here. Most ruin-crawlers do not reach the centre; those who do rarely return.

The white city's full place file is not yet written. For lore, see `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"The Rift" and §"The White City." For the shamans' long-plan arc toward the portal, see `doc/npcs/wilder.md`.

## Visible patterns

- **The harvest-motions continue.** A party in the region will see or hear something performing its old role daily — a bell, a figure on a field, smoke from a cold hearth.
- **Workings return warped.** Healing heals with a mark; light lights with a wrong colour; divination answers a different question. A shaman can read the gradient; the centre is worse than the edges.
- **Dreams populate.** A party sleeping in the region wakes with people in their head — demi-humans at harvest, children at cradles, workers at threshings — they have never met.
- **The agricultural calendar insists.** An urge to plant in spring, harvest in autumn. The land's rhythm pulls travellers into its pace.
- **The iron elementals, the cerberi, the warped spirits are stationary.** They do not hunt. They do not leave their sites. They are *there*, performing their role; leave the site, leave the creature.
- **The wandering creatures do not follow.** Jackalopes scatter; the hippogriff leaves. Nothing pursues. The region is not predatory in the usual sense.
- **The centre is worse.** A party approaching the white city's ruins feels the pressure thicken noticeably within a day's walk.

## DM notes

- **The register is *reciprocity betrayed*.** Every encounter is an old helper now turned. The desert is archaeological grief (someone's home, taken); the blasted lands are relational grief (someone's friend, warped). A DM who runs the blasted lands as *dungeon with weird monsters* loses the register entirely. The creatures are still performing roles. The relationships are still there, in broken form.
- **The communal scale is the whole point.** This was not a land of individual homes. Lean into the communal buildings, the shared work, the collective memory. A party that registers *nobody here lived alone* is reading correctly.
- **The warping is *completing-with-warp*, not stuttering.** Distinguish from the desert's pressure at the table. A working done here *finishes* — and comes back wrong. The difference matters for pacing: the party cannot tell their working has warped until the consequence lands. Let the landing be the scene.
- **The moved-parts mystery is a real arc.** The wooden man at Silvertrace, the emptied wall-panels at Commons House, other findings across the region — these connect. *Something* is gathering community-traces and moving them toward a location. The DM can name the location (the centre, a specific ruin, a hidden site), or leave it as open as the campaign wants.
- **Haven as the natural terminus.** If the party returns any iron-puppet parts they find to Haven, they are doing work that matters at a campaign scale. This is the blasted lands' connection to the wider iron-puppets / Haven / pot-people thread.
- **Iron-elemental-as-befriendable being.** Parallel to the volcano tower's fire elemental. The two-path structure — ignore, befriend — carries.
- **Cerberus as moral-weight scene.** The DM should treat the cerberus not as guard-combat but as *a person still doing their job a millennium after the job should have ended*. Fighting it is possible; addressing it is better; telling it the truth is hardest.
- **The dreams are flavour with payoff.** A party that dreams of a specific worker at the threshings may later *recognise a name* at the Great Thresh's elemental-roster. These are not random. The DM should thread them.
- **The hippogriff is the sharpest cruelty the region offers.** A tired party seeing what looks like help and being refused — this is the blasted lands' spine in a single encounter. Use it at least once when the party is genuinely tired.
- **The centre is hard-limit territory.** Do not let first-visit parties push to the white city and the portal. The chaos-density there is campaign-climax material; saving it for the shamans' long-plan arc preserves its weight.
- **Silvertrace can be un-warped.** Across a campaign, with real shamanic work and specific offerings, the warped stream-spirit can be *restored* to its helpful role. The stream comes back. The temple is honoured again. This is a quiet-good the party can pursue. Wilder or a Luchebert priest will notice the regional reading softening if it happens.

## Open questions

- **Where are the moved puppet-parts going?** The migration-destination. DM-fill: a specific ruin, the white city's centre, or a hidden site arranged by the chaos.
- **What happens when Commons House's cerberus learns its hall is empty?** Discharge and fade; enraged pursuit; refusal. DM decision at the scene.
- **Can Silvertrace be un-warped by a party across a campaign?** The file allows it; the mechanism is a long-term shamanic project. Worth specifying if the campaign pursues it.
- **The community-roster at the Great Thresh.** Specific names, specific kin-connections to Haven and to hill-country villages. DM-fill as the party engages.
- **The wall-panels' specific contents.** Names, dates, ceremonies recorded. DM-fill when the recovery happens.
- **Named specific ruins in the region.** The file names three (Silvertrace, Great Thresh, Commons House) — the blasted lands hold many more. A DM can draft additional sites as the campaign needs them, following this register.
- **Cerberus lineage elsewhere.** Are there other cerberi in the region, at other communal buildings? Probably yes. Each is its own scene.
- **The hippogriff's nature.** Is it a single being, or a kind? Is it warped-from-bound-mount, or a chaos-spirit wearing the mount-shape? The file does not commit.
