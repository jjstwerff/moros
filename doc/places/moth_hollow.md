# Moth Hollow — the crash-graves

- **Type:** Burial ground; post-crash spirit site
- **Region:** Hills near the Lake of Tears, in the broader terrain around Laurent's downed city — a separate hollow, not inside the wreck itself
- **Political status:** None; unclaimed; felt-rather-than-known
- **Inhabitants:** Large slow-moving white moths

## Summary

A quiet hollow in the hills, not signposted on any road, **where the dead of Laurent's crash were buried**. Not everyone who fell with the city died — Felicia's work during and after the fall saved more than anyone had a right to expect (see `doc/npcs/felicia.md`) — but not everyone survived either. The bodies were brought here and buried after the crash by **the survivors and whatever hands remained** — not by Felicia herself; she was already dead by then and could not do the work of burying. What she did was come to the hollow afterwards, in spirit. **She clung to the place** because it held the people she had failed to save, and she has attended it since. Like the wells and the boundary stones and the quiet pacts she made for the living, this is one of her places — not because she shaped it with her own hands, but because she *stayed*. The graves are old now. The ground has closed over them. The hollow has not stopped meaning what it was.

Locals do not come here. Most do not know it exists as a distinct place; those who do know it as one of the hill pockets you do not walk into — part of the same folk-memory that keeps them clear of Laurent's wreck (see `doc/npcs/corne_dunham.md` §"Clear of the fallen city"). It is not dangerous the way the wreck is dangerous. It is dangerous the way a great spirit's own quiet garden is dangerous: by mattering enough to her that disturbing it gets her attention.

## The moths

The hollow is filled with **big, slow-moving white moths**. Pale, soft-winged, hand-sized when they stretch, moving at a pace closer to drifting than flying. They **cling to anyone entering** — landing on shoulders, hair, sleeves, packs — without aggression but without any visible concern for personal space. A party walking in quickly finds itself carrying a small white crowd on its clothes. The moths do not bite. They do not buzz. They arrive and stay.

The specific meaning of being covered in them is DM-fill — plausible readings:

- **Witness, not aggression.** The moths are how the dead here register a living visitor. Being covered is being *noticed* by the site.
- **Felicia's attention.** The moths are a quiet expression of her drive — her fear for the people she tried to save, expressed here where some of them are buried. A visitor carrying moths is a visitor Felicia is now aware of, the way she is aware of her arrangements generally.
- **A spirit-ecology marker.** The moths read to chaotic spirits as a mighty-adjacent presence (see LORE.md §"Spirit Ecology"), which is part of why the hollow has stayed undisturbed for centuries.

A party that leaves slowly, respectfully, and without taking anything finds the moths drift off over time. A party that runs, shouts, or damages the ground finds the moths **do not come off** — the clinging persists past the hollow's edge, and for as long as it does the visitor is carrying the site with them.

## Angering Felicia

The moth hollow is, in practical terms, **the one place in the campaign where players can make Felicia actively angry at them**. Normally she cannot be offended — she is a great spirit driven by fear for her people, not a person choosing to take offence. The hollow is the exception. It holds the specific dead her drive is built around. Disturbing them — looting graves, defacing markers, desecrating the ground — is a direct violation of the thing her drive attaches to, and her drive responds.

What follows is not wrath in the human sense. It is her drive turning against the violators at the level of her arrangements and channels:

- **The moths do not come off.** The marker of her attention persists past the hollow's edge and continues wherever the violators go.
- **Her arrangements turn against them** in the hill country: wells are less potable for them; boundary stones do not grant the quiet passage they grant other travellers; the pacts she made for the living will not shield them.
- **The necklace fails to condense her** near Laurent. The party's route to calming him is cut. They can still carry the necklace — it is still a physical object — but Felicia will not step through it for a party she has marked.
- **Willow senses it.** Felicia's closest tender knows without being told that these particular visitors have crossed the hollow's line, and her response (see `doc/npcs/willow.md` §"Willow as witch — when the shrine is crossed") may escalate from refusal to active defence.

The anger is **reversible but not cheap**. A great spirit is not a god who holds grudges, but her drive has registered a violation, and satisfying it again — returning what was taken, making restitution in a way the hollow itself can register, perhaps a condensation ritual where she can finally say what she means — is the work of multiple sessions, not a single apology.

## What is here to find

A revelation layer, not a combat scene. What a careful party can learn:

- **The names of the dead.** If the graves were marked — by Felicia, by the demi-humans she settled nearby — those markings are still readable to someone who takes the time. The names are of specific demi-humans who fell with Laurent's city, and some of their descendants are people the party has already met in the hill country and Elmsfield's surrounding villages. Finding an ancestor's name is a moment of personal connection for a player whose character has hill-country roots.
- **Felicia is closer here.** Like her shrine at Luchebert, the hollow is a place where her presence is more reachable than the general hill country. A condensation ritual (see LORE.md §"Speaking with a great spirit directly") performed here might be easier or more reliable than elsewhere — though the specific trigger for the *Laurent encounter* remains the necklace (see `doc/places/laurents_city.md`).
- **The story Laurent does not quite tell.** Laurent narrates his downfall freely (see `doc/npcs/laurent.md` §"What he will tell") but rehearses the heroism of the evacuation more easily than the losses. The hollow is where the losses are. A party that has heard his story and then stands in this hollow has the emotional counterpoint the narration cannot provide.

## Discovery paths

- **Willow** knows of the hollow — Felicia's work, Felicia's grief, her ongoing presence here. To a party that has earned her trust (see `doc/npcs/willow.md` §"Closest to Felicia"), she will point them toward it, though not casually.
- **Laurent**, in the deeper parts of his narration, will mention that "they" were buried — in the hills, near but not inside the wreck. A party listening carefully can pursue the thread from there.
- **Corné Dunham** vaguely knows the hollow as one of the hill pockets he avoids, part of the same folk-memory that keeps him clear of the wreck. He will not guide the party to it but can point at a general direction without walking the last stretch himself.
- **Demi-human villagers** whose ancestors fell may know family stories about where an ancestor was buried; pursuing those stories can lead a party here.

## Open questions

- The exact nature of the moths — passive witness, Felicia's active presence, something between.
- Whether a condensation ritual for Felicia performed here is materially easier, or just emotionally appropriate.
- Whether any of the buried here have left traces that can be reached (a shaman speaking with a specific dead, for example).
- How many people are buried here — dozens? A few hundred? The scale shapes how much the hollow holds.
