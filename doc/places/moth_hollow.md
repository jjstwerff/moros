# Moth Hollow — the crash-graves

- **Type:** Burial ground from the crash of Laurent's flying city; a place Felicia tends.
- **Region:** A hollow in the hills near the Lake of Tears, in the broader terrain around Laurent's downed city. Not inside the wreck itself; a separate pocket.
- **Officially:** Unclaimed; unsignposted; not on any road.
- **In practice:** One of **Felicia's own places**. She did not shape it with her hands — she was already dead when the bodies were brought here. She *stayed* after the burial, the way she stays at wells, at boundary stones, at the quiet pacts she made for the living. Moth Hollow is where her drive attaches to the dead.
- **Status:** Undisturbed for centuries. Locals do not come here. The hollow is **the one place in the campaign where players can make Felicia actively angry**.

## What the party meets

The party has come here by reading Laurent's narration carefully, following Willow's pointer, listening to a demi-human villager's family story, or following Corné Dunham's vague *"a pocket in the hills I don't walk into."* None of these paths are signposted. The last of them involves tracking the shape of the hills by memory and guess.

They step over a low ridge and into the hollow. It is **smaller than they expect** — a bowl in the hills, gently sloped, grassed, with a few stands of birch at the edges. The ground has the settled look of earth that has not been disturbed in a long time. No path leads in; no path leads out. **The hollow does not feel empty.**

Within a minute, the first moth lands. Within five, the party is carrying a small white crowd.

## How it reads at a glance — the 2–3 standouts on entry

- **The moths.** Hand-sized, pale, soft-winged, moving at a pace closer to drifting than flying. They arrive as the party enters — not in a swarm, in ones and twos, taking their time. They **cling** — to shoulders, hair, sleeves, packs — and they do not startle. A party walking deeper into the hollow accumulates moths on every surface that will hold one. The moths do not bite or buzz. They arrive and they stay.
- **The stillness.** No birds. No wind patterns. The grass does not move quite right. A *Druid* reads this as *held ground* — terrain a mightier presence is attending to. A casual party feels *something* and cannot name it.
- **Markers, half-hidden.** Small stones at intervals through the grass — not obvious on first entry, visible on a careful second look. Some are roughly carved; some are worn almost flat. **The hollow is a cemetery.** A party that kneels to look can read some of the markings.

## At the entrance — the first moths

The moment of crossing into the hollow is its own scene. The ridge is low; the step over is nothing physical. But the air changes.

- **Moths arrive immediately.** The party will feel the first landing as a soft weight on a shoulder before they see anything land.
- **The temperature drops a degree or two.** Not cold — *attended*.
- **The light has been waiting.** Whatever hour the party entered, the hollow's light reads as *late-afternoon*, regardless of the actual sun. This is Felicia's steady state; it has held for centuries.
- **Any chaos-spirit residue on the party (from the forbidden woods, from the desert, from long exposure to Tod) bleeds off here.** The hollow is mighty-spirit-attended in a way that chaos spirits cannot touch.

## At the graves — reading the markers

The markers are scattered. Not in rows; not grouped by kin. They were set by survivors and Felicia-in-spirit, in whatever order made sense to them at the time.

- **Names are readable, if the party takes time.** Some are in languages the party knows; some are in scripts the party does not recognise; a few are in the older forms the white city used. Demi-human names predominate — the passengers Laurent's city was carrying when it fell.
- **Some names matter to people the party knows.** A player whose character has hill-country roots may find an ancestor. The demi-human villages around Elmsfield and the Lake of Tears have descendants buried here; a party that has spent time in those villages will recognise family names.
- **Children among the dead.** Small stones; small names. The fall killed without selection. This is the emotional weight the hollow carries; the DM should not soften it, and should not lecture on it.
- **Felicia tends the markers.** Some have been re-cut across centuries — her doing, done through whatever channels a great spirit has. The party may notice a stone that has clearly been re-inscribed at a date long after the burial.
- **The number is not small.** Dozens, certainly. A careful count across a full visit would put it at more.

## At the centre — the place Felicia stays

A slightly lower point in the bowl. No marker; no structure. The grass is fractionally thicker here. The party will find themselves drawn to it without knowing why.

- **This is where she stayed.** A spirit-aware character (*Shamanic*, *Druid*, a priest of Saint Luchebert or of Felicia) feels her presence here as clearly as at her shrine. A casual party feels *quiet* — the specific quiet of a place someone is in.
- **A condensation ritual performed here** may be easier or more reliable than elsewhere in the hill country. The emotional register is *right* — she is at her most reachable where the dead she grieves are. See `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Speaking with a great spirit directly."
- **The necklace trigger does not work here.** The necklace condenses her *near Laurent*, not near the graves. At Moth Hollow she is already present; the necklace would not be asked to do anything.
- **Sitting still at the centre** is the right behaviour. Speaking is allowed. Respectful addresses find her.

## At the edges — the birch stands and the boundary

The hollow's perimeter is marked by small stands of birch. The trees have been here as long as the graves have.

- **The trees are attended.** Dead wood is cleared; new saplings come up in specific positions. The care is not human-regular; it is *over-time*, and the ground remembers.
- **The moths extend to the tree-line and stop.** A party walking the perimeter will find the moths drift off at the edge as if a boundary is visible to them that is not visible to the party.
- **Crossing the boundary** ends the hollow's specific quiet. The hills become ordinary again within a dozen paces. A party leaving respectfully feels the moths *release* as they cross.

## Visible patterns

- **The moths arrive within a minute of entry.** No matter the time of day or season.
- **The light is always late-afternoon.** Dawn, noon, dusk outside — late-afternoon inside.
- **Chaos-spirit residue bleeds off** inside the hollow.
- **The grass does not move with outside wind.** It has its own subtle motion that is *attended*, not meteorological.
- **Markers cleared of debris.** Whatever falls — leaves, branches — is absent from the markers themselves. Something keeps them visible.
- **No animals.** No birds; no insects other than the moths; no mice in the grass. The hollow is not infested; it is *held*.
- **Dreams that night.** A party sleeping within a day's march of the hollow, after visiting it, will usually have vivid dreams involving people they have never met. The dreams do not harm; they are *introductions*.

## What you learn here, and when

| Topic | When the place gives it up |
|---|---|
| That this is a burial ground | Within a minute, once the first markers are noticed. |
| That Felicia stays here | Felt immediately by spirit-aware characters; inferable by others within a scene. |
| The specific names of the dead | With time and care. The readable markers take attention. |
| An ancestor's name, for a party with hill-country or demi-human roots | Possible on a careful sweep. The reveal is personal. |
| That the place has been tended across centuries | From the re-cut markers, the cleared debris, the birch stands' positioning. |
| How to perform a condensation ritual for Felicia | Not taught here. The hollow is *where*, not *how* — see Willow (`doc/npcs/willow.md`) and LORE for the mechanic. |
| Why the moths come | Inferable — witness, attention, or spirit-ecology marker. The file does not commit; the DM fills at the table. |
| That angering Felicia is possible | Felt by any spirit-aware character the moment they consider taking something. Stated plainly by Willow if the party asks her later. |

## If the party does something — what the place does

| If the party... | What happens |
|---|---|
| Enters respectfully, sits, looks | Moths cling; the hollow holds its quiet; names can be read; Felicia attends without intervention. |
| Kneels at a marker and names the person | The moth-count around that kneeling party member increases subtly. A moment of recognition, not a reaction. |
| Leaves an offering (a stone, a flower, a written name) | Received. The offering is not disturbed. A party returning later finds it still there, often with other small things left alongside. |
| Leaves slowly, respectfully, without taking anything | The moths drift off at the boundary. The hollow releases them. |
| Runs, shouts, or damages the ground | **The moths do not come off.** The marker of Felicia's attention persists past the hollow's edge. The party carries the site with them until they make restitution. |
| Loots a grave or defaces a marker | **Felicia is angered.** See §"Angering Felicia" below. |
| Attempts a condensation ritual here | More reachable than elsewhere. A spirit-aware character leading the ritual may feel her step forward sooner. The conversation that follows is intimate and freighted with the dead present. |
| Brings a descendant of someone buried here | Rare, emotionally heavy, welcomed. The moths gather specifically on the descendant. Felicia attends. The dead, through the hollow's channel, may be felt. |
| Attempts to speak with a specific dead (a shaman speaking-to-the-dead working) | Possible with real skill. The dead are reachable here in a way they are not elsewhere in the hills. Each attempt costs the shaman; sustained attempts cost more. |
| Camps in the hollow overnight | Discouraged by the hollow itself — the light stays late-afternoon; sleep is difficult; dreams are vivid. A party that insists on it is not harmed but is *changed* by the morning. |
| Tries to map the hollow or note marker positions | Possible. The map will agree with itself the next time the party visits. The hollow does not hide from people who will return respectfully. |
| Tries to catch or kill a moth | The moth will not be caught. It drifts away. Attempting again escalates toward the anger described below. |
| Asks Willow about the hollow after returning | Confirmed. Willow will say what she knows about Felicia's attention and the dead buried here, with the care she carries for the shrine. See `doc/npcs/willow.md` §"Closest to Felicia." |

## Angering Felicia

Moth Hollow is the exception in Felicia's normal *cannot-be-offended* state. Her drive — fear for the people she tried to save — attaches directly here, to the specific dead. A violation of the hollow registers as a violation of her drive. The response is not human wrath; it is her drive turning against the violators at the level of her arrangements and channels:

- **The moths do not come off.** The marker persists wherever the violators go.
- **Her arrangements turn against them in the hill country.** Wells are less potable for them; boundary stones do not grant the quiet passage they grant others; the pacts she made for the living will not shield them.
- **The necklace fails to condense her near Laurent.** The party's cleanest route to calming him is cut. They can still carry the necklace — it is an object — but Felicia will not step through it for people she has marked.
- **Willow senses it.** Felicia's closest tender knows without being told. Her response (see `doc/npcs/willow.md` §"Willow as witch — when the shrine is crossed") may escalate from refusal to active defence.

The anger is **reversible but not cheap.** Returning what was taken, making restitution in a way the hollow itself can register (sitting long enough, performing a condensation ritual where Felicia can say what she means, working restoration into the markers) is multi-session work. A great spirit is not a god that holds grudges; her drive is her drive, and satisfying it means doing the work that repairs what was damaged. Until it is satisfied, the marked party lives without her channels.

## DM notes

- **Do not explain the moths.** The file offers three possible readings — witness, Felicia's attention, spirit-ecology marker. Pick one silently and run with it. Players will read their own meaning into the image; do not flatten.
- **The hollow is revelation, not combat.** Nothing here attacks the party. What is here is *weight*. Emotional weight for a descendant; moral weight for any party that stops to read; spiritual weight for anyone spirit-aware. Run it as a quiet scene and trust the quiet.
- **Felicia-anger is a campaign-scale consequence.** Not a fight; a slow tightening of the hill country's rules against the violators. The DM should narrate the tightening over sessions — wells that turned, strangers who refuse food, paths that feel longer. Do not lecture on *why*; the party figures it out.
- **Willow's role.** Willow is the human side of Felicia's attention. A party that has earned her trust hears about the hollow from her directly; a party that has not must find it through the harder paths. Either way, Willow's response to a violation is worth flagging (see her file).
- **The hollow is the counterpoint to Laurent's narration.** Laurent tells his downfall freely; the losses are harder to feel in his hall than the heroism. Moth Hollow is where the losses live. A party that has heard his story and then stands here has the emotional frame his narration cannot provide.
- **An ancestor's name is a gift to give a player.** If a character has hill-country or demi-human roots, the DM should consider planting a relative here. The moment of recognition — kneeling at a stone with a familiar family name — is the kind of scene Moros rewards return visits for.
- **No named NPCs live here.** Felicia is present; no one else. The dead are many; the specific names become known through marker-reading, family stories, and slow work.

## Open questions

- **Exact nature of the moths** — passive witness, Felicia's active presence, spirit-ecology marker, or something between. The file refuses to commit; the DM fills at the table.
- **Whether a condensation ritual here is materially easier**, or just emotionally appropriate. The DM can calibrate against the campaign's pacing — easier for a climactic Felicia scene; equal for a mid-campaign check-in.
- **Whether any of the buried here have left traces a shaman can reach directly.** A scene where a specific dead speaks through a shaman visiting the hollow is possible; canon leaves the threshold open.
- **How many are buried here.** Dozens? A few hundred? The scale shapes how much the hollow holds.
- **Whether Felicia-angered parties can be *un-marked* through an act other than restitution** — a third-party intervention by a mightier friend, a great spirit's vouching. Probably not, but the file does not forbid it.
