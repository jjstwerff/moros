# Moros — Relative Geography

**This is not a map. It is a constraint list for one.**

Everything recorded here is a *relative* position (X is south of Y, X is on the river that feeds Z), not an absolute one. Distances, exact coastlines, and precise river courses are deliberately *not* specified — those are for the real map when it exists. What is written here should stay true on that map.

Cardinal directions are used as a convenience. They describe layout on a future flat map; they are not claims that real-world compass points correspond to anything in particular in the setting.

**Note on `doc/Untitled.png`:** that image is an **external visual inspiration**, not a canonical Moros map. Pieces of it informed the geography documented here through the author's confirmations (Rakeville south-west of Elmsfield, Clear water north of Rakeville, Linar harbour visible on the east, the swamp surrounding the Lake of Tears, the coastal mountains hard to traverse, Luchebert's river-bend positioning, Lastwater's adjacency to the blasted lands). Those confirmations are canon. The image itself is not. A future Moros world map should respect what is written here; it need not match the inspiration pixel-for-pixel, and its labels were not meant to be adopted directly.

## The two kingdoms

**Allondo** and **Brumal** are the region's two kingdoms. **Brumal lies south-west of Allondo**, and the two share their common border on that south-western side.

### Allondo

Allondo is the central reference point of the map. Brumal sits south-west of it, Elmsfield east of it, the desert further south (past Brumal), and the northern features (forbidden woods, mountains, World's End) to its north.

Settlements (from RULES.md Places of Origin):

- **Scarlet vale** — capital
- **Blackwood freehold** — agricultural town; the **Bean farm** is here
- **Clear water** — mining town; where **Irna** was sent for forced labour; visible north of Rakeville on the draft map
- **Chatter Creek** — northern border town
- **Rakeville** — village *towards* Allondo; sits south-west of Elmsfield on the draft map, south of a second still-unnamed western settlement

Internal positions of these settlements inside Allondo are not yet fixed.

### Brumal

**Brumal lies south-west of Allondo**, and its southern edge is being eaten by the advancing desert.

Settlements:

- **Cliffside hold** — capital
- **Bockthicket** — mining town
- **Steadington** — village, **already lost to advancing desert**

Steadington has fallen; the capital and the mining towns still stand, but the country is thinning. Brumal's crisis is therefore both *political* (shared border with Allondo, war brewing) and *geographic* (desert closing in from the south, shrinking the land that can still be farmed, mined, or lived in).

### Elmsfield

**Elmsfield lies east of Allondo but is not part of it.** The Great Swamp (with the Lake of Tears at its centre) forms a natural barrier between Allondo proper to the west and Elmsfield to the east. That barrier is not just inconvenient — it is large enough that the kingdom's formal reach stops on the Allondan side of it. Elmsfield sits in the wilderness beyond: an independent town in the current wilderness, elder-led (Diederik) rather than crown-ruled, effectively its own small polity even though the canonical list still treats it as a frontier settlement.

It is still the canonical "border town around a huge tree" (per RULES.md): the border it sits on is the one between the known kingdoms and the wilderness that includes the Lake of Tears hills, the forbidden woods, and the coastal mountains beyond. From Allondo's perspective, Elmsfield is *over there* — at the wilderness edge, not reachable by royal writ, a place Allondan law quietly stops short of.

- Built around the ancient **ironwood tree** at its centre. The tree is a **female**, and ironwood is dioecious — she cannot bear fruit without pollen from a male. Every male ironwood on this continent was felled by the ancient mages for their puppet work; the overseas continent still has its male population (see `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Ironwood itself"). The Elmsfield tree is therefore one of the last candidates on this side of the ocean for producing a new generation of ironwood, if cross-pollination could ever be arranged. Small enough that a stranger arriving attracts notice. The campaign starts here.
- A **small river** flows **north** out of Elmsfield into the **Great Swamp**, the broad wetland that surrounds the Lake of Tears at its centre (per the `Map of Elmsfield and the great swamp` item). Swamp and lake are one region, not two separate features. **South of Elmsfield is the trade route, then the broken lands** (see §"The main inland trade route"); the swamp lies to the *north*.
- **Hank and Egbert's farm** sits on Elmsfield's edge; it has grown into an orphanage-of-sorts that takes in young people from anywhere the war and its aftermath have broken.
- In the Overseas War, Elmsfield was captured quickly — **but after Linar harbour fell first**. The invasion force landed at Linar harbour on the east coast, then pushed inland along the main trade road, and Elmsfield was the next major stop. Before the town fell, locals hid a large amount of valuables inside the hollow of the ironwood tree (now Bridget's Inn). The raiders could not open it; the ironwood defeated their tools. Parts of that hoard may still be inside.

## The Lake of Tears region

North of Elmsfield, along the river that flows out of the village toward the lake:

- **The Great Swamp** — broad wetland region surrounding the Lake of Tears. Marshland, slow water, bogs, reedbeds. **Fed from the south by two named rivers**: Elmsfield's small river (a short tributary entering from the south) and the **Clear Water** from the foothills east of Scarlet Vale (entering from the south, carrying whatever the mine's wash-tables let into it — see `doc/places/clear_water_mine.md`). The swamp is the terrain a party travelling **north** from Elmsfield toward the lake actually crosses; it is the environment the mother-bear encounter in Session 2 takes place in; it is where the crystalline fungus spreads outward from the lake into the water and growth around it. **Whether the Clear Water's recent ramped-up output has shifted the swamp's chemistry** — and whether that is making the fungus worse — is a thread the campaign can open through Diederik or Wilder. **The lake's outflow** is the Clear Water continuing eastward past **Luchebert** (the cliff-bend the monastery sits above) and then **south-east through the hills** to **Linar harbour**, where the river meets the ocean. Pollution starting at the Clear Water mine therefore travels not just into the swamp but eventually into the harbour at Linar.
- **The Lake of Tears** — lake at the centre of the Great Swamp, in the depression the river feeds. Ringed by **six wizard towers** (seized from their original owners by old-order mages and moved there centuries ago to capture the air elemental that once held Laurent's flying city aloft). Each tower is one of the **six shackles** holding the elemental; a shackle breaks not by destroying its tower but by **moving that tower out of the ring** through the tower's own relational lever — the buried tower's lever is the pot-boy (the furnace follows him), the Hermit Shaman's tower likely breaks when they leave, and the other four have their own keys. The towers stand in the swamp-and-lake complex, each partly soft ground, each partly approachable only by knowing the marsh.
- **The buried tower** — one of the six, closest to the lake, almost consumed by crystalline fungus. Contains the **Pot-Person** and an old furnace for making pot-people.
- **The hills east of the lake** — a belt of hills immediately east of the Lake of Tears. They hold **Laurent's downed flying city** (the vast ruin where **Laurent** persists as a demi-lich) and, in a vale beyond the downed city, **Luchebert Monastery** — home to **Father Elliot** and the shrine of **Felicia** (the great spirit). The monastery sits **in hills above a bend in the river**, and it is **hard to reach from the river side**: the obvious approach (following the water from Elmsfield and the swamp) does not yield up the vale easily, which is why Irna could not find the entrance. Reaching Luchebert typically means approaching from another direction — over the hills themselves, or via a known path that avoids the river-side cliffs.
- **The five other towers** — scattered around the lake at intervals. Each has its own character:
  - **The Hermit Shaman's tower** — inhabited; the Hermit Shaman is probably a surviving original owner or heir.
  - **The Lightning Tower** — flies about six metres off the ground, not unreachable (climbable with rope or the right approach) but not sitting on it. Metal spikes jut from its sides; lightning arcs between them occasionally, lighting the night sky in visible flashes from a distance. Whatever workings the tower originally housed involved storm-spirits or lightning-channelling, and those workings are still visibly active centuries on. Its original owner — a **Storm Mage**, now of unknown age and broken mind — still lives in the hills nearby (see `doc/npcs/storm_mage.md`); he is the tower's lever, though reaching him and helping him is a campaign arc in its own right.
  - *(Three other towers still to be characterised.)*

Directional relationship: Elmsfield → small river *north* → Great Swamp → Lake of Tears (at the swamp's centre) → (the lake's outflow runs *east* past) Laurent's downed city in the hills → Luchebert Monastery (in the hills above the river bend) → (the river continues *south-east* through the hills) → Linar harbour (where the river meets the ocean) → (the coastal mountains run from Linar northward as the eastern spine of the continent). The whole eastward stretch is the **Clear Water river**'s course past the lake; the river originates south, in the foothills east of Scarlet Vale, and flows north into the lake before continuing this eastward arc.

### The Lake as rain-buffer

The Lake of Tears is **not part of the neighbour's ecological crisis**. On the contrary: it acts as a **rain-buffer against the advancing desert**. The evaporation and weather patterns it sustains keep the surrounding inland area — Elmsfield, the settled parts of eastern Allondo, and the hills around the lake — stable in the face of the desertification pushing up from the south.

However, the **hills east of the lake** (with Laurent's city and Luchebert) **block that rain from reaching further east**. The **coastal mountain range** beyond the hills — the long north-south spine that runs from Linar harbour up to World's End — sits in the rain-shadow and does not benefit from the lake's moisture at all. The east coast itself (Linar harbour and further north) has its own sea-weather; it is not parched, but it is not watered by the lake either.

**Campaign implication:** anything that would disrupt the lake — freeing the bound air elemental incautiously, the fungus consuming more of the tower system, the lake's weather-workings failing — also disrupts the buffer that is holding the desert off this region. The obvious-feeling "right thing to do" (free the trapped spirit) has unintended ecological consequences. Worth weighing before the party acts.

**The trade, if the party makes it:** freeing the air elemental is also the act that lets **Laurent's downed flying city rise for one last journey**. The same spirit once held the city aloft. Released, it can lift the city again — briefly, not permanently, a few sessions of player-led flight rather than a revival. The party gains a flying platform for a single meaningful trip (to the overseas continent, to the shifting lands, wherever is worth the last flight). The region loses its rain-buffer and the desert starts reaching inland. That is the shape of the bargain, if the campaign ever reaches for it.

## The desert and Gap city

The **desert** is not a confined region of southern Brumal. It is a **continental-width band** running **south of the blasted lands across the full width of the continent**. Both Brumal (south-west of Allondo) and **Allondo's southern flank** are bounded by it; Brumal is simply the first kingdom the desert's advance has started to eat. The desert reaches its southern limit at the **mountains of Gap city**, which stop its further spread; beyond those mountains lie the shifting lands, and beyond *those*, rumours speak of habitable lands that no reliable traveller has confirmed.

**What drives the desert's hostility:** it is not purely natural. **Chaotic spirits** from the nearby **blasted lands** have spilled into the desert and made it *much more hostile than it once was*. The Lake-of-Tears rain-buffer is unrelated; the desert's aggression is a spirit problem. The chaotic-spirit migration from the blasted lands is the underlying driver of Brumal's collapse — and the slower, quieter pressure on Allondo's southern edge too, though that pressure has not yet become the visible crisis Brumal's is.

### South-continent geography, stacked

Moving south from the main trade road, the layers run roughly like this:

1. **The blasted lands** — hazardous ruin-country, source of the chaos spirits, contains the portal.
2. **The desert** — continental-width band, spirit-infested, advancing northward. Fata morgana is inside it.
3. **The mountains of Gap city** — the southern limit of the desert; Gap city is in the steep gap through them.
4. **The shifting lands** — beyond the mountains, the zone that rejects all built structure.
5. **Rumoured lands beyond the shifting lands** — told to be habitable by rare travellers; nothing is verified. The rumoured black iron fortress (LORE.md §"The Shifting Lands") is in this unconfirmed territory.

### Notable places in this band

- **Gap city** — last settlement before the shifting lands; built into the steep mountain gap that forms the desert's southern boundary. Belongs to **neither kingdom**. May know things about the desert, the mountains, and what may or may not lie further south that neither Brumal nor Allondo does.
- **Fata morgana** — **big city, inside the desert**. Fata morgana has developed **unique protection against the chaotic spirits** over time — enough to survive and sustain itself inside a desert that should, by now, have consumed any city. That protection is *limited*: there are things it cannot do and things it will not risk.
- **Lastwater** — travel stop on the southern trade road, **beside the Blasted Lands**. Positioned as the last reliable water before the desert, it is the caravan stop travellers use while the road is skirting the blasted lands (the road goes around, not through). Named for exactly what it is.

## The coasts

### The northern coast (World's End)

Beyond the volcanoes lies the northern sea. This coastal region is **World's End**, and it is defined by volcanic activity and mining:

- **World edge** — mining city near the ocean, in the World's End region; **Master Darius**'s forge district is here. Volcanic-region metals explain why it is a forge city. The region is also home to important (non-royal) human houses — most notably the **Leatherman house**, from which **Queen Fienna of Allondo** comes. The Leathermans are *outwards-facing*: they are actively trying to build a **railroad** from World's End, with **two intended directions**:
  1. **Near-term: toward Raft city** — a commercial connection to the great river's major port town, unlocking the continent's largest river trade for the northern coast. The forbidden woods sit between World's End and Raft city, and no one has yet solved how to lay track through chaos-spirit territory (or how much further the route would have to run to circle the woods entirely). The Leathermans do not know whether reaching Raft city is actually possible.
  2. **Longer-term: southward, toward Fata morgana and eventually Gap city** — bridging the homelands to the southern independent cities across the chaos-infested desert. This direction would require Fata morgana's chaos-spirit protection applied along the track, making it a later-campaign project rather than a near one.
  Both directions face terrain, chaos-spirit interference, inter-polity politics, and cost. The Leathermans are *not* aware of Laurent's crashed flying city; their plans do not include it. The railroad has not been built. It may never be. But it is a real commitment the house is pursuing, and it gives the northern coast a slow, industrial-scale plan for breaking its own isolation — and, potentially, a later-campaign force the party will have to engage with.
- **Rosepond** — mining village near World edge, in the same region.
- **The estuary of the great river** (see above) — also on this northern coast, north of the volcanoes.

### The east coast

- **Linar harbour** — small independent harbour town on the **east coast**. It is the **start of the main inland trade route**: goods come ashore here, then travel overland west/south through Elmsfield, past Allondo, and eventually into the desert.
- Linar harbour was **captured in the Overseas War** — the invasion landed here. It was the first place to fall, and from it the overseas force pushed inland along the trade road, capturing Elmsfield next. When the war ended with Thorgal's death, most of the occupying demi-human soldiers did not go home (their continent could not feed them). **They stayed.** A generation later, most of the working population of Linar harbour — fishers, dock workers, net-makers, chandlers — is descended from those overseas settlers. The harbour's independence is partly demographic: neither kingdom fully trusts a port whose longshoremen are the children of the soldiers who captured it, and Linar harbour has grown comfortable in that ambiguity. See `linar_harbour.md` for detail.

## The north: forests, mountains, volcanoes, estuary

The region north of the Lake of Tears is a chain of distinct features, stacked in order:

1. **The forbidden woods** — immediately north of the Lake-of-Tears hills. A forest that still holds a **chaos spirit** living within it. The spirit warps the trees inside the woods, which is why the region is called forbidden and why travellers avoid it when they can. This is a live hazard, not a legend. The woods are a barrier that must be crossed (or circled around) to reach anything further north.
2. **The mountains** — *north-east* of the Lake of Tears, **beside the forbidden woods** (woods and mountains are adjacent, running roughly parallel). These are the central stretch of **a single continuous coastal mountain range** that runs the full length of the east coast — **from the southern end near Linar harbour all the way to World's End in the far north**. The range is the eastern spine of the continent and is **hard to traverse**: no casual crossing, no through-route in the middle stretches. The trade road uses a specific pass at the range's southern end (near Linar), and travellers attempting to cross the mountains elsewhere are taking on a real expedition.
3. **The volcanoes of World's End** — north of the mountains. The **most active** volcanoes of World's End. Treat **World's End** as the northern coastal region; **World edge** (the mining city, per RULES.md) and **Rosepond** (the nearby mining village) are in this region — volcanic mining country.
4. **The estuary of the great river** — above (further north than) the mountains, where the great river meets the sea. The sea, therefore, lies to the north, past the volcanoes.
5. **Raft city** — on that same great river, *behind* the forbidden woods (per the earlier canon that Raft city is reached past the woods). Where exactly along the river — closer to the estuary, in the forest interior, or on an upriver stretch — is open. Home of **Nadine** (the Finch trader).

And connected to the great river indirectly:

- **Salmonswell** — independent small city *well upriver*, on a river that is a **tributary of the great river**. Salmonswell's stream eventually joins the great river somewhere along its course; the salmon that run up through the Upwelling every season are doing so as part of the great river's larger migration pattern, taking the branch that leads them toward Salmonswell's spawning grounds further upstream.

  **The tributary is not practically navigable.** It is dangerous water — **many rapids**, fast currents, narrow channels, and surrounding terrain that is **hostile to anyone stranded**. Salmon can manage it; boats and travellers cannot. So while Salmonswell's waters do eventually reach the northern estuary and therefore share an ocean outlet with Raft city, **there is no usable river route between them**. Salmonswell is reached overland, not by water — which is part of why it sits where it does: the fish come to it without any human route needing to be practical.

## Trade, sea routes, and inland alternatives

**Most trade moves by sea.** Bulk goods, long-distance trade, and contact between regions (and with the overseas continent) all rely on ships. The sea is the fastest way to move things in Moros.

The sea is *hazardous*, but not always directly:

- **Pirates.** The stranded overseas soldiers who turned pirate after the Overseas War hold a nest well to the south. They are *not* immediately astride the main trade lanes — they live further south than the routine trade coasts — but their presence is constant enough to push cautious cargo off the sea entirely, especially for anything going to or past the kingdoms.
- **Storms.** A persistent hazard, separate from the pirates and not fixable by negotiation. Losses at sea are an expected cost of doing business.

### The main overland trade road

Because of the combined pressure of pirates and storms, a lot of trade that could in principle go by sea goes overland instead. The backbone of that overland traffic is the **main trade road**, which **starts at Linar harbour on the east coast**, crosses the **southern end of the coastal mountain range** (which runs from near Linar all the way north to World's End — the range's southern terminus is where the trade road finds its way through), runs inland **through Elmsfield**, and continues south/southwest:

- Goods arrive by sea at **Linar harbour** and are transferred to overland caravans there.
- From Linar harbour the road runs inland to **Elmsfield** — the first major inland stop.
- Traffic bound for **Allondo** continues along this road to reach the capital and the agricultural towns.
- Traffic going further — deeper south into the desert — follows the same road past Allondo's reach.
- Refugees (from wherever the current pressures push them) use this road too.

The road is *well established*: it has a chain of known **resting places** along its length, built up over generations of trade. **Lastwater** is the southernmost of these known stops, sitting beside the blasted lands as the road skirts them, and earning its name as the last reliable water before the desert. Traders take their time on the road, stage goods at the rest stops, and treat the journey as routine rather than adventurous. That infrastructure is what makes the southern road a viable alternative to the sea in the first place.

The road does *not* cut through the blasted lands — it skirts them. Over the full journey between Elmsfield and the southern desert, the road curves to keep travellers on safe ground, and only once the blasted lands are fully behind them does the road straighten out and lead into the desert proper.

This is why Elmsfield matters commercially out of proportion to its size: the main southward trade artery starts from it, and the town profits (and suffers) from being the gate onto that artery.

### The blasted lands

**South of the southern overland road** sits the **blasted lands** — a *large* hazardous region with its own dangers, distinct from the desert further south. The abandoned ironwood mill where **John Bean** lost his arm was on the edge of the blasted lands; the land has enough old machinery, old buildings, and old wrongness in it to reward (and punish) ruin-crawlers.

The blasted lands are also the **origin of chaotic spirits** — because they are where the **portal** sits. At the centre of the blasted lands stands a structure the first humans built to stabilise the portal on this side; the portal itself is a glowing field that occasionally shimmers and briefly reveals views into dimensions unknown (not worth risking your mind for). See `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"The portal itself." The chaotic spirits that now infest the region came through here; they still migrate outward, which is why the neighbouring desert has become so much more hostile than it used to be.

**The current spirit population of the blasted lands** is a mix of two kinds. The **bigger chaos spirits have migrated away** (into the desert most notably, but also further afield); what remains are **smaller chaos spirits** and **highly influenced original spirits**. The second category is a specific, local phenomenon: local spirits native to this world that have lived alongside chaos for so long they have been warped by the proximity. They are not chaos spirits — they still belong to this world — but their behaviour, their shape, and their willingness to follow this world's natural rules have all been pulled out of true. See LORE.md §"Why the chaos" for the underlying mechanism; the blasted-lands influenced spirits are what "prolonged contact with chaos-spirit approximations" produces in local spirits specifically.

Reaching the portal at the blasted lands' centre is a journey, not a visit. The region's hazards and the ongoing chaotic-spirit pressure (plus the warped local ones) make unescorted travel into the interior an extremely bad idea. The shamans' long plan (see LORE.md) requires getting a ritual team there alive — one of the reasons the plan is still conditional on circumstances aligning.

The trade road skirts around the blasted lands rather than crossing them, which is why the journey south is a multi-rest-stop haul rather than a straight run: the detour is built in, and it is big. The road only enters the southern desert once it is fully past the blasted lands. Travel off the road into the blasted lands is not casually done.

### Inland alternatives more broadly

- **Rivers** — the great river (through the forbidden woods and past the mountains to the northern estuary), Elmsfield's small *northward* river to the Lake of Tears, the **Clear Water** (the spine river: foothills → lake → past Luchebert → ocean at Linar harbour), Salmonswell's salmon river, and any others.
- **Overland caravans** — for cargo that cannot risk storms, or routes that do not want to engage with the sea at all.
- **Salmonswell** as an inland alternative — its economic importance (smoked salmon, salt, river trade) comes partly from being a reliable inland node for traffic that prefers rivers to coasts.
- **Linar harbour** — a small *independent* harbour town; its neutrality has commercial value of its own.
- **Elmsfield** — the obvious refugee-entry by land from Brumal, and the gate onto the main southern trade road.

### Water-powered industry — a regional feature

**River-side towns each carry a water-powered industry**, sized to the water they have access to. Players will hear the wheel before they see the town — the rhythm of a saw-frame at Salmonswell's cutting-yard, the steady turn of Elmsfield's grain-mill a short walk *north* of the village, the wheels at Clear Water mine that pump and stamp and feed the bloomery, similar features at any other river-side settlement the campaign opens. **Monasteries, hill-shrines, and farms-with-only-a-stream are not towns and do not carry this feature**; the systemic point is *settled communities at workable water*, not every place that hears running water.

The industries are present *as scenery* most of the time — a sensory standout on entry, a working crew the party may meet, a rhythm the town keeps time by. They become **active scenes** when something breaks: a stripped gudgeon, a snapped mill-shaft, a silted race after a flood-year, a cracked sluice gate. Breakdowns are **crafting-quest seeds** — the millwright accepts help from any character with **Wright**, **Carpentry**, **Blacksmith**, *Ingenuity*, or *Hand*, and from a party that does not have those, will accept hauling, salvage, and timber-felling work. Heavier iron parts trace back to **Master Darius**'s forge at World Edge (`doc/npcs/master_darius.md`); replacement shaft-timber traces back to **Diederik**'s ironwood and the woods around Elmsfield (`doc/npcs/elder_diederik.md`).

A DM adding a new river-side town to the campaign should give it one such industry — small or large per the water — and *let the players notice it on entry*.

## Spirit ecology: why chaos spirits are held off

Spirits in Moros are **wild**. They migrate, they move freely, and there is no natural law preventing them from showing up in new territory. What there *is*, is an unwritten social rule: **spirits respect each other**. When a spirit encounters a mightier one, it backs off.

**Chaotic spirits obey the same rule** — with one critical twist: they cannot distinguish what is actually a spirit from what merely *reads* as one. Any sufficiently powerful mage, shaman, druid, or priest reads to a chaos spirit as a mighty spirit. The chaos spirit backs off regardless of whether the being in front of it is, strictly speaking, a spirit at all.

This is the mechanism behind every piece of protection in the setting:

- **Fata morgana**'s unique protection is generations of mages, shamans, druids, and priests arranged so that every part of the city reads as mighty-spirit territory. That is why the protection is real; it is also why the protection is *limited* (reach, staffing, failure modes).
- **Luchebert Monastery** and the hills around it read as mighty-spirit territory because **Felicia** is actually a great spirit and **Father Elliot** is a priest. Two layers of cover.
- **Laurent's downed city** reads as mighty because Laurent himself is a demi-lich — a spirit-adjacent being the chaos spirits would back off from. He is, in his own way, protecting the hills he has colonised without intending to.
- **Elmsfield** is held partly by **Elder Diederik** (druid-trained) and partly by the ironwood tree's standing strangeness. The tree itself may read as something; whether it does is an open question the party might answer.
- **The forbidden woods** are not a chaos spirit that has *spread* — they are a chaos spirit held *in place*. Whatever makes the edges of the woods read as mighty-spirit territory to it (neighbouring settlements, druidic tradition, the natural spirits of the surrounding land, or all three) is what has kept it from migrating further. If those hedges waned, the woods' spirit would move.
- **Brumal's southern edge** broke because its mighty-spirit cover was not dense enough to hold the blasted-lands chaos spirits from crossing into the desert. Steadington lost its protectors (or never had enough), and the country is thinning inward.

**Practical consequence for play:** every region the party cares about has one or more mighty-spirit-adjacent figures as its de facto shield. If a priest is killed, a mage driven out, a druid excommunicated and not replaced, a great spirit angered, a demi-lich destroyed — the chaos spirits will notice, and the region will stop being defended. The party's standing with those figures, and whether those figures stay where they are, is itself a threat surface.

**The chaos spirits are a constant threat, not a settled one.** They are still pressing into the world and still capable of making life almost impossible wherever cover is thin. The **shamans have a long-running plan** — generations in the making — to reopen the portal deliberately and push the chaos spirits back through it into the ether that spawned them (see `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"The shamans' long plan"). Rift-scale risk; potentially campaign-ending if attempted. Real, slow, conditional. Worth knowing in the background of any session that touches the spirit world.

**Most people don't know this.** Ordinary urban folk have lost track of who actually protects them, and the practitioners who still care — senior shamans, working druids, serious priests, retired powerful mages — mostly live *outside* the cities. The result is that the countryside and the frontier are often better protected than the capital, and that major cities (Scarlet vale, Cliffside hold, World edge) are thinner shields than their inhabitants assume. See `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Spirit Ecology" for the detail.

**Clans cut across place.** Demi-human clans are small and dispersed across nearby towns rather than concentrated in any single settlement (see LORE.md §"Clans and their shape"). That matters for two things in particular: (1) a demi-human figure's political reach often tracks their clan network rather than their town — Lady Rhianna's standing at the Allondan court, for example, represents mouse folk across Scarlet vale and surrounding villages at once — and (2) any conflict between the two kingdoms will cut *through* clans rather than between them, because most clans have kin on both sides of the border. The political form of a kingdom matches the human mode of belonging more cleanly than the demi-human one; in any serious crisis, demi-human kin-networks will act on their own logic.

## Overseas (the next continent)

Across the ocean lies the **next continent**, where mages rule largely unchecked because chaotic spirits are rare there. Its land is poorly cultivated; its people struggle to grow enough food. Its mages fought the **Overseas War** against the two kingdoms a few years ago; some of their soldiers became the **pirate nest** along the southern coast. The old-order faction that crashed Laurent's city is *not* the same body as the current overseas leadership — the old order has diminished — but some current overseas leaders still carry its heritage.

Also on the next continent: more ironwood trees than on this one (including the surviving *male* population — see LORE.md §"Ironwood itself").

**The overseas continent's current political structure is deliberately blank.** No one on this continent knows who succeeded Thorgal, how his dissenters fared, or what the leadership looks like today. The continent went quiet after the mirror broke; there is no diplomatic channel across the sea. When a campaign actually moves to the overseas side, the DM running it fills in the political landscape that makes the arc work. Until then, "what the overseas looks like now" is an intentional blank — exactly as it appears to characters on this side.

## Rivers (inventory)

Known named or described rivers:

1. **The small river from Elmsfield** — short tributary flowing **north** out of the village into the Great Swamp. Drives Elmsfield's grain-mill a short walk *north* of the village. Joins the swamp's water alongside the Clear Water; *not* the same river as the Clear Water.
2. **The Clear Water** — the campaign's **spine river**. Rises in the foothills east of Scarlet Vale (where the **Clear Water mine** sits at its head — the river is what makes the mine viable, and what the mine's wash-tables empty into). Flows **north into the Lake of Tears**, entering the swamp from the south. The lake is the river's expanded middle. **The lake's outflow is the Clear Water continuing east** past **Luchebert Monastery** (which sits in hills above the river bend the cliff path descends to). The river then runs **south-east through the hills** and **empties into the ocean at Linar harbour**, on the east coast. The full course: foothills east of Scarlet Vale → north → Lake of Tears → east past Luchebert → south-east through hills → ocean at Linar harbour. Drives the Clear Water mine's pumps, stamping mill, wash-tables, and bloomery bellows. **Pollution from the mine travels the full course** — through the lake, past Luchebert, and out at Linar.
3. **The great river** — Raft city sits on it, beyond the forbidden woods. Flows roughly north-westward past the forbidden woods; its estuary is on the northern sea, past the mountains and the volcanoes of World's End. Passes through territory warped by the chaos spirit somewhere along its course.
4. **Salmonswell's tributary** — a branch of the great river system; Salmonswell sits on it, well inland. The salmon that run up to the Upwelling take this branch off the main course on their way further upstream to spawn. Waters from Salmonswell eventually reach the northern estuary via the great river — but the tributary itself is **not navigable**: rapids and hostile terrain make the route impassable for anything that isn't a fish. Salmonswell is reached overland. Drives Salmonswell's cutting-yard at the rapids' first lift.
