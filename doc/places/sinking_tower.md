# The Sinking Tower

- **Type:** One of the six towers ringing the Lake of Tears. A shackle on the bound air elemental. Two halves joined at the waterline — antechamber above, lived-in living quarters below, reached by sinking.
- **Region:** Floating on the Lake of Tears itself, near the centre of the ring.
- **Officially:** Nobody's. The original owner is dead.
- **In practice:** **His spirit is bound here.** He stays in the lower half, which is air-sealed and preserved, and he has been waiting for a very long time.
- **Status:** Active. Sinks in response to a climber. Sinks nightly on its own.

## What the party meets

The party reaches the lakeshore and sees the tower out on the water — **a slim stone shaft rising from the surface**, mid-lake, with no visible foundation. It looks, at first glance, like a tower built on piling. Closer examination — from the shore, with a careful eye, or from a boat circled around — shows that the tower is buoyed, not pinned. It rides on the water.

**If the party watches at night, it sinks.** Every night, the tower descends slowly straight down until it is fully submerged, **giving off a visible glow on the water the whole way**. The glow persists until the tower reaches the lakebed and whatever it is settling onto there. Then the glow fades. Before dawn, the tower rises again.

**If the party tries to climb onto it, it sinks too.** The upper half goes straight down the moment weight comes onto it — a controlled descent, not a collapse. A climber who does not hold on is left swimming. A climber who *does* hold on goes under with the tower.

That holding on is the point.

## How it reads at a glance — the 2–3 standouts on entry

- **A tower on the water.** Stone, narrow, slim profile, visibly not built on anything. The shape should be impossible; it isn't.
- **The nightly glow, visible from the shore at night.** A steady pale light descending through the water, following the tower's sink. **From Gerhald's hill above the lake, the pattern is unmistakable** — and Gerhald will tell the party what he has seen (see `doc/npcs/hermit_shaman.md` §"What he sees from the hill"). He does not know the specifics; he knows the pattern, and he knows the glow ends where the tower *reaches its counterpart*.
- **The sink-response.** Any touch from above causes the tower to descend. A party that reaches out for a hand-hold and does not commit to climbing is warned. A party that does commit goes down with it. The tower is teaching its own entry mechanism.

## At the lakeshore — first observation

The tower is not reachable from the shore. Eye distance only.

- **The water around it is deeper than the shore-edge suggests.** Shallow at the shore, deep at the tower. The lake has a sudden drop a hundred yards out.
- **No boats tied to it.** No evidence of recent visitors. Locals do not row out; the sink-defence has discouraged every curious traveller for centuries.
- **The glow at night.** From any shoreline point, visible if the party watches. A steady pale light descending through the water, fading only when the tower is at the lakebed.
- **Gerhald's hill is the best vantage.** The hill-tower is the highest point on the ring. A party asking Gerhald what he has seen gets the full pattern — the time each night, the direction of the glow, the phrase *reaches its counterpart*. See `doc/npcs/hermit_shaman.md`.

## At the upper half — the antechamber

Reaching the upper half requires a boat. The climb itself is a decision point.

- **Approaching by boat is safe.** The tower does not sink until weight comes *onto* it from above. A boat alongside, with the party in the boat, is unmolested.
- **Touching the tower triggers the descent.** Any weight — a hand grip, a rope over the top, a climber's foot — starts the sink. The descent is *controlled*, not a crash; the tower goes down at walking pace.
- **The upper half is the *antechamber***. Its interior, reached during the descent if the party is holding on, is a single circular room. Windows that look out into the water as the tower goes down. Old stone. Nothing lived-in; the upper half was a passageway, not a living space. A preserved desk at one side holds papers that dissolve if handled.
- **The descent is the scene.** A party holding on through the sink watches the water rise outside the windows, then the surface above them, then the lakebed below resolving into view. The glow the shoreline sees is *from their tower*, descending with them. **The whole descent is perhaps thirty seconds.**

## At the lakebed — the lower half

The tower settles on the lakebed with a soft grind. The antechamber's lower door, previously sealed, now opens onto the lower half — **an air-filled sealed living space** that has been here for centuries.

- **The living quarters are intact.** A main room with a preserved hearth; a sleeping alcove with bedding long dried; a library corner with books protected by wards; a working-table with implements laid out as if mid-use. **This was the owner's home.** The preservation is old-mage-work and still holds.
- **The air is breathable.** Old, dry, slightly cold. A party can stay as long as they want to.
- **The lakebed floor is visible through a window.** One wall of the lower half faces the bed; silt, small fish, the rooted stems of the underwater plants Laurent's fall probably drowned.
- **The binding's seat.** Somewhere in the lower half — at the hearth, at the working-table, in the library corner — is the specific object or place where the owner's spirit is anchored. A *Shamanic* character can locate it on a careful check. Moving it, disturbing it, or breaking the binding is *not* the lever here — see the spirit below.

## The owner-spirit

He is present the moment the party steps into the lower half. Not as a figure at first — as a *presence* in the air, the sense of a room that is *attended*. After a moment, he forms — **a translucent figure at the working-table, in robes a generation older than the party has seen, looking up from papers that do not turn**.

- **He speaks when the party speaks.** He has had centuries to prepare conversation and very little practice. His voice is dry and precise. He asks who the party is and what brought them down.
- **He is not hostile.** He is aware; he is waiting. He has been waiting a very long time for someone to come through the sink.
- **He wants something.** The specific content is a DM-fill detail — a lost object, a person he wants to hear from, a ritual he could not complete before he died, a belonging of his that a surviving heir now keeps elsewhere. What matters: it is **shaped like a fetch quest** that the party can *bring him* through sustained work, not resolve in a single conversation.
- **He holds his name** until the party has earned it. Once given, it is the key — he can be addressed as himself rather than as "the owner."
- **He knows the tower.** He built parts of it; he inherited parts from the original construction; he knows what the old-order mages did when they seized and moved it. His testimony is first-hand on the ring's construction, and he is willing to share it with a party he trusts.

## The lever — floating down to the sea

Bring the bound owner-spirit what he wants, and he will release the tower — **but in a direction the other towers cannot match.**

- **The tower floats down the small river to the sea.** The upper half lifts; the lower half follows on the same buoyancy that has held them together. The structure travels with the water current out of the lake, down the valley, and eventually into open ocean.
- **The shackle breaks the moment the tower crosses out of the ring.**
- **The owner-spirit goes with it.** He is heading wherever he is going. The fetch-quest resolved has given him a reason to move — a destination, a person to meet, a ritual to complete. The tower is his vehicle.
- **Unique exit direction.** The pot-boy walks out with the furnace following. The volcano tower lifts on fire-elemental energy. The darkness tower moves by mosaic mechanic. The Storm Mage is brought back to the Lightning Tower. Gerhald consents. **The Sinking Tower floats away by water.** This matters for the campaign's sense that each shackle has its own logic.

## Hidden in the sinking tower

The original mage hid pieces here before the seizing (see `doc/claude/ITEMS.md` §"Where items come from"). The cache is **water-and-buoyancy-aspected**, in style with the tower, and the descent-glow that lit the lakebed once a night is part of why the seizing mages did not look closely:

- **A piece of deepwater glass** — bound to the tower's nightly descent for so long it has taken some of the working in; functions as a focus for water-aspected powers.
- **A windbone-and-water clasp** — small lift-aspected accessory; a Bracelet Material variant for a power-user already carrying a buoyancy or balance trade.
- **A sealed scroll in waxed cloth** — the original mage's notes on the working that lifts the upper half and lowers the lower half; a Wright or Druid student can read pieces of it.

Diving the lower half during the right hour of the descent is when the cache is reachable. After the tower floats away, what was not retrieved goes with it.

## Role as spirit-shield (partial)

Per `doc/claude/LORE.md` §"Spirit Ecology," a bound owner-spirit inhabiting his old home for generations reads to chaotic spirits as a mighty-adjacent presence. The Sinking Tower contributes, quietly, to the Lake region's unusual shield cover. When it leaves — by the river, to the sea — that contribution departs with it.

## Visible patterns

- **Nightly sink with visible glow.** Always, reliably. Clock-regular across centuries.
- **Upper-half descent in response to weight.** Always. A touch is a commit.
- **The lakebed position is consistent.** The tower sinks to the same spot each night.
- **The glow's colour is pale-white.** Not warm, not cold. Steady.
- **The lower half's preservation holds.** Air dry; wood unwarped; papers intact under wards.
- **No aquatic life inside the preservation field.** Fish do not swim through the air-seal; the protection works.
- **The owner's working-table papers do not turn.** They were set centuries ago. They are what they are.

## If the party does something — what the place does

| If the party... | What happens |
|---|---|
| Watches from the shore at night | Sees the glow. Observes the pattern. Gains half the information for free. |
| Asks Gerhald what he has seen | Gets the full shoreline pattern. *"Reaches its counterpart."* A hint; not the solution. |
| Rows out and circles the tower in a boat | Safe. The tower ignores them. Observation possible. |
| Touches the tower from a boat | The tower starts to sink. The boat is pushed off; the party can row clear. Warning delivered. |
| Climbs onto the tower and does not hold on | The party member is left swimming. The tower disappears below them. |
| Climbs onto the tower and *holds on* | **The descent.** Thirty seconds. The antechamber opens. The lower half becomes reachable. |
| Breathes out the wrong moment on the way down | Gets a mouthful of water, then air as the antechamber closes. Uncomfortable, not fatal. |
| Enters the lower half disrespectfully (loud, handling things, looking into the library) | The owner's presence forms quickly. He asks them to sit. The conversation that follows is stiff and short. |
| Enters the lower half respectfully (standing, waiting, speaking only when spoken to) | The owner forms, greets them, asks their business. The conversation begins well. |
| Tries to break the binding | The owner registers the attempt calmly. *"That is not what you came here for."* The sink-up (the tower's rise before dawn) happens whether the party is on board or not; a party that has angered him may find itself *returned to the surface alone* without the conversation resuming. |
| Offers to undertake the fetch-quest | Accepted, once the owner trusts them. Specifics laid out. |
| Returns with the fetched object | **The lever activates.** The owner releases the tower; the structure floats down-river; the shackle breaks. The owner goes with it. |
| Asks him for his name | Given once trust is earned. Not before. |
| Asks him about the ring's construction | Answered in detail, once trust exists. First-hand testimony. |
| Asks him about Laurent | He knows of Laurent — they were contemporaries, not allies. Opinions exist. |
| Tries to take something from the lower half | Declined calmly. The items are his. A party that insists finds the tower has risen early, with them still aboard, and they are now on the lake's surface in the upper half with nothing. |
| Tries to map the lower half | Possible. The room plan is stable and the party can draw it. |
| Stays in the lower half past the nightly sink-up | They rise with the tower. The party is briefly inside a tower that is breaking the surface. The sink-up is gentle; the party disembarks at the top. |

## DM notes

- **The cling-to-descend is the puzzle's core.** The party will work it out — through observation, through Gerhald's hint, through trying and failing — but the DM should let *the tower teach it* rather than explaining. The first sink-response is a warning; the second is the answer. A party that never commits to holding on never reaches the lever.
- **The owner-spirit's name is the trust-gate.** Holding his name back until the party has listened and shown care gives the DM a specific beat — the moment he says the name changes the register of every following conversation.
- **The fetch-quest is DM-fill.** The file leaves it open because the content should match the party and the campaign. A lost object carried by a descendant the party already knows is cleanest. A ritual the party can learn to complete is richer. A person the owner wants to hear from — and hearing from them requires travel or letter-sending — is the most narratively generative.
- **The lever is a *departure*, not a destruction.** The tower floats away. The owner goes. The party loses both — and gains the shackle-break. The scene should feel like a parting, not a victory.
- **The water-exit matters for the ring's geometry.** Each of the six shackles has its own logic. The DM should preserve that distinctness — the sinking tower is *the water one*. A DM who homogenises the levers into variations of the same mechanic loses what the ring is.
- **Gerhald's hint is valuable, not complete.** His *"reaches its counterpart"* is the kind of thing that makes a party keep thinking. Do not let him say more than he has seen.
- **The preservation field is mage-era work.** A *Mage* examining it learns something about pre-Rift workings; a *Shamanic* character reads the spirit-binding anchor. Either is a genuine learning moment worth playing.

## Open questions

- **What the owner-spirit wants.** DM-fill. The file sets the shape (fetch-quest) and trusts the campaign to fill the content.
- **Is the lower half's air-seal metaphysical or mechanical?** A party considering salvaging the tower later may care.
- **Does the spirit stay bound to the tower after it reaches the sea, or is he released by the journey?** The file leaves this open; a mercy arc can free him if the campaign wants.
- **The original owner's name, species, and pre-Rift role.** DM-fill as the party engages.
- **What he and Laurent thought of each other.** Contemporaries, not allies — the specifics are a conversation the party can have.
