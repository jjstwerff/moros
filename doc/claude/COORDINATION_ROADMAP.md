# Coordination Roadmap — enhancing the campaign with cooperative setpieces

A planning document. **Not implementation.** This doc catalogues where the campaign should have coordination-requiring scenarios and what each should contain, so that the cooperative register Moros rewards is threaded through every session rather than concentrated in the survival setpieces.

The **reference example** is Session 1's Irna-at-the-gate scene (see `doc/claude/CAMPAIGN.md` §"Session 1 — An Unexpected Guest"). That scene is the campaign's opening worked example of what coordination *feels* like. This roadmap extends its pattern across the whole campaign.

## The campaign's spine — tension moments as the shape of Moros

**The core principle of campaign-shape in Moros.** Every other section of this document, and every session-planning decision, follows from it.

**Tension moments shape the campaign. Individual-character tasks do not.**

A tension moment is a coordinated group effort — a setpiece where the party works together under rising tension to resolve a scene that no single character can carry. The Session 1 Irna-at-the-gate scene is the reference. The shackle towers are the repeated pattern. The non-dungeon crises at familiar places are the distributed variant. SURVIVAL.md's hostile-biome setpieces are the test-cases.

### What tension moments do — the two functions

**1. They *resolve* story lines.**

A thread that has been tracking across sessions — the Bean arc, the scattered-kin search, the last-flight preparation, the Brumal-collapse refugee question, the Corven/Penelope secret — closes *inside* a tension moment. Not in a private conversation between one character and an NPC. Not in a GM-announcement that the quest is done. **In a coordinated scene the party navigates together**, where the resolution only lands because everyone was present and contributed.

This is how Moros closes threads: through group scenes where nobody can claim they alone resolved it.

**2. They *open* new story lines.**

The **consequences** of a tension moment are what the campaign's next arc is made of. Who was in the room when the decision was made. What was agreed to. What was seen. What opened up that had been closed. What closed that had been open. **The new threads require the group's focus** — they are not errands a single character goes off to pursue between sessions. They are hooks that demand the party's coordinated engagement at their next peak.

This is how Moros opens threads: by the group's resolution of one thing creating the conditions for another.

### What this means structurally

- **The campaign progresses through tension moments, not through individual quests.** A series of individual-character errands between peaks is not campaign progression. The peaks are.
- **An NPC-task or solo-errand is connective tissue.** It gets the party from one tension moment to the next; it is not what the campaign is *about*.
- **Planning a session** = picking which tension moments will occur in it, in what order, with what stakes. The connective scenes follow from the moments, not the reverse.
- **Character arcs exist** but hang off the group moments rather than driving them. A character's growth happens through the coordination they participate in — through being the shield-bearer at the chimera-mother scene, the voice at the Scarlet Vale audience, the clinger who held on at the sinking tower. Their individual development is a record of the coordination they shared.
- **A session without a tension moment** is a session of *movement and preparation*. Legitimate; necessary; not *campaign progression*. Three such sessions in a row is a signal the DM has lost the spine.
- **A DM planning ahead** picks two or three tension moments per arc and designs the surrounding scenes as **approaches to them**. Travel leads to a crisis; a conversation surfaces a decision that a coordinated scene will test; a market visit reveals the cost that the next peak will collect.

### What this rules out

- **Campaigns organised around quest-chains** that one character could complete alone.
- **Sessions where a single character's specialty resolves everything** while others spectate. If the druid handles the warped spirit while the rest wait; if the rogue picks the lock while the rest loiter; if the priest blesses the sick while the rest make camp — the tension moment is not actually happening. It is one character being watched.
- **Story progression delivered through NPC exposition** rather than through scenes the party earns. When Rhianna explains what needs to happen next at her hour-glass, *that is preparation*; the needing-to-happen is the next peak, and the peak is what progresses the campaign.
- **Arcs that close by announcement.** The campaign does not close threads by the DM saying *"and so the prophecy was resolved."* It closes threads by a scene where the party resolves it *together*.

### What this rewards

- **Sessions where everyone had a role.** The measure of a session's quality is whether each party member can name what they did at the peak — and whether those contributions were genuinely different.
- **Scenes that could not have resolved the same way without all parties present.** A peak where a missing party-member's absence would have changed the outcome is a peak done right.
- **Story arcs that close by the group agreeing on something hard together.** The Gerhald consent-gate is the canonical example: the last shackle opens only when the party, collectively, can answer his three specifics in a register that satisfies him. That is how Moros resolves.
- **New arcs that open because the group's resolution changed the world around them.** Un-warping Silvertrace opens a thread with Wilder noticing the regional softening; breaking a shackle with violence at the volcano tower opens a thread Gerhald is now weighing; returning a Haven-kin closes one arc and opens the question of the next scattered kin. The peaks *generate* new peaks.

### The campaign's shape — in one sentence

**Moros is a sequence of coordinated tension moments; each closes old threads and opens new ones; every moment requires the group's focus together. Between the moments, the party moves, learns, prepares, and deepens their relationships. The moments are the campaign.**

### How this doc serves the spine

Every section of this roadmap is an *implementation* of this principle:

- **The pattern** (below) extracts how a tension moment works, from the Irna entrance.
- **The scenario-by-scenario plan** ensures tension moments cover every Moros scenario type.
- **The shackle-tower coordination coverage** and **Dungeons as tension-peaks** make dungeons into reliably peak-shaped tension moments.
- **Non-dungeon tension-peaks** distribute peak-moments across familiar places so the campaign's rhythm has highs available anywhere.
- **Session-by-session inventory** and **priority tiers** translate the principle into scheduling.
- **The writing template** and **design principles** ensure every implemented setpiece carries the spine's register.

Every setpiece written from this roadmap should pass one test: **did a thread close here, or did a new one open, or both — and could it have happened without the group?** If no thread moved, the setpiece is a side-scene; if a single character resolved it alone, the setpiece is not a tension moment in Moros's sense. It should be redesigned until both conditions are met.

## The pattern — extracted from the Irna entrance

Session 1's opening has five simultaneous tasks, all in shared space at the gate:

1. **Calm the dog** — Hand-check or *Druid* — so someone can reach Irna.
2. **Manage Farmer Joseph's fear** — *Relaxed* or *Politics* — so his panic does not infect the crowd.
3. **Diagnose Irna** — Healer specialization or *Druid* — so the group knows what they are dealing with.
4. **Calm the crowd** — *Relaxed*, *Politics*, or *Musical* — so the town does not turn against helping.
5. **Retrieve the letter** — once past the dog, read the Luchebert-and-Elliot thread.

**What makes this scene coordination-requiring:**

- **Multiple simultaneous tasks** — none can be sequenced without cost. A party that tries to do them one at a time loses standing with the crowd (or loses Irna) while they work.
- **Different powers apply to different tasks** — no single specialist can cover them all. A Char-heavy character who can calm the crowd cannot simultaneously diagnose the disease.
- **Real consequences for unattended tasks** — the dog attacks, Joseph spreads panic, Irna's condition worsens, the crowd turns hostile.
- **Shared space** — all five tasks happen at the gate, visible to each other. Per RULES.md §"Party scope and cooperative specialization," this is specialization-in-shared-space, not geographic separation.
- **Resolution requires distribution** — four party members can cover four tasks. A party of two has to make hard choices. A party of one cannot win this scene the way it is designed.

**This is the template.** Every coordination setpiece in the roadmap follows this shape:

- A **setup** — what the party sees and the stakes.
- A **task table** — named tasks, what they prevent, who performs them, how.
- A **round-by-round structure** — 3–5 rounds typically, each with its own pacing beat.
- An **outcome framework** — full cooperation / partial / failed, with specific consequences.
- A **"what cooperation looks like at the table"** note — how the DM should run it in practice.

## The enhancement goal

**Not every scene** needs to be a coordination setpiece — that would exhaust the mechanic. The goal is **1–2 coordination setpieces per session at key story beats**, with coverage spread across all 10 scenario types over the campaign.

The current state (after SURVIVAL.md and the party-scope rule):

- **Covered**: camp (×3 biomes), forage (blasted lands), travel (system-level). Session 1 Irna entrance (implicit).
- **Uncovered as formal setpieces**: combat, stealth, negotiation, exploration, repair, cure, market. These need one coordination setpiece each, written to the template.

## Scenario-by-scenario plan

Each slot below names *what the setpiece is*, *where in the campaign*, *which session(s)*, *which task-types*. Implementation is deferred; this is the roadmap.

### Combat

**Setpiece: Laurent's audience — puppets, fire, and the looting-question**

**Where:** The audience hall at Laurent's downed city (see `doc/places/laurents_city.md` §"At the audience hall" and `doc/npcs/laurent.md`).

**Session:** Mid-campaign, 7–10. When the party has reached Laurent.

**The situation:** The party has entered Laurent's audience hall. They do not have the necklace (or a party member has crossed him in another way). Alcove puppets begin to activate. Laurent throws fire. The party must reach the dais, hold their position, and either *complete a conversation* or *withdraw alive*.

**Tasks in shared space:**

| Task | What it prevents | Who performs it | How |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hold the edge** | Puppets close the retreat line | A Might or Shield character | Specific positioning at the hall's open arches |
| **Drop puppets** | Puppets accumulate faster than the party can manage | A character who can reach the verticality (*Climber*, *Ingenuity*) | Over edges the party has identified |
| **Catch Laurent's fire** | Direct damage to a protected party-member | A *Shield* or *Fur* character at the dais approach | Positioning |
| **Speak to Laurent** | The conversation never happens | A *Politics*, *Clan*, or Char character | Through the noise and the heat |
| **Control the looting-instinct** | Party-members grabbing things escalate Laurent's response | Any character — often *Relaxed* | Group discipline, made explicit |

**Round structure:** 4 rounds. R1 establish positions. R2 first puppets animate. R3 fire escalates. R4 conversation possible or withdrawal.

**Outcome framework:**
- **Full cooperation**: Laurent speaks. The party withdraws intact with information.
- **Partial**: Laurent half-speaks. The party loses a round's Endu to fire and has to retreat with fragments.
- **Failed**: Full city activation. Combat as defined in Laurent's file; party loss is real.

**Priority: Tier 2.** Load-bearing for the Laurent arc; will see use when the party reaches him.

### Stealth

**Setpiece: The pirate nest extraction**

**Where:** The pirate nest south of Linar harbour (see `doc/npcs/nadine.md` §"What she has compiled" and `doc/places/linar_harbour.md` §"At the southern coast road").

**Session:** Late mid-campaign, 10–14. When the party has earned Nadine's intelligence.

**The situation:** The party has been set down by a Linar fisher on a beach a half-mile from the nest. They have Nadine's intelligence — names, schedules, where Linnet is held, the specific building. The task is extraction, not assault. They have hours, not days.

**Tasks in shared space** (the nest and its approaches — one or two world-grid tiles):

| Task | What it prevents | Who performs it | How |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Perimeter watch** | The nest waking before the extraction | *Lookout* or *Hearing* | Eyes outward; ears for the specific sound-patterns |
| **Silent approach** | Giving away the group's position | *Adaptation*, *Night*, or *Scurry* | Matched pace; shared shadow; quiet footing |
| **Distraction** | The patrol crossing the extraction path | *Scolding* or *Sly* | A specific fire, a small animal released, a false sound |
| **The extraction itself** | Losing Linnet | Healer, *Hand*, or whoever can open the hold without noise | Inside the hold, at the right moment |
| **Rear-guard, on the beach** | The boat leaving without the party | The party's slowest member | Holds the return line |

**Round structure:** 5 rounds, each ~half-hour. R1 approach. R2 distraction. R3 extraction (the load-bearing round). R4 withdrawal. R5 beach departure.

**Outcome framework:** Linnet extracted clean → Linnet extracted but wounded → the extraction fails but the party escapes → partial capture.

**Priority: Tier 3.** Major arc, late in the campaign. Worth writing fully when the campaign has committed to the Linnet thread.

### Negotiation

**Setpiece: The Scarlet Vale audience — Irna's exile and the Bean bargain**

**Where:** King Hannes's audience chamber (see `doc/npcs/king_hannes.md` §"At the audience chamber" and `doc/places/scarlet_vale.md`).

**Session:** Mid-campaign, when the party has reached the capital with Rhianna's introduction and either Elliot's corrected proclamation or the Clear Water document.

**The situation:** The party has an audience. The king is on the raised chair. Rhianna is in the room. The court is watching. The party must negotiate: Ron's release, Irna's formal rehabilitation, or the crown's acknowledgement of the party's work. Every courtier is reading them.

**Tasks in shared space** (the audience chamber — one tile):

| Task | What it prevents | Who performs it | How |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Formal address** | The king dismissing the party for poor court-manners | A *Politics* or Char character | Speech, kneel-and-rise, precise language |
| **Produce the evidence** | Hannes deflecting on procedure | A Hand or *Ingenuity* character with the document | Presenting at the right moment, sealed and verified |
| **Vouch through kin/standing** | The court doubting the party's legitimacy | A *Clan* or *Religion* character | Kinship-invocation, or Luchebert-authority |
| **Hold the emotional register** | The scene tipping into a quarrel | A *Relaxed* character | Presence — unhurried, unrattled |
| **Read the lie** | Accepting a partial concession that is not what it seems | A *Perc* character with a called-shot | At the moment Hannes offers |

**Round structure:** 3 rounds — approach, exchange, conclusion. Court-pacing: each round is a formal exchange, not a minute of real time.

**Outcome framework:** Full agreement → partial (some concession, others delayed) → refusal → combat (rare, costly if it happens).

**Priority: Tier 2.** Shows what a cooperative negotiation looks like and anchors the Bean-arc resolution.

### Exploration

**Setpiece: Commons House after the cerberus has stood aside**

**Where:** Commons House interior in the blasted lands (see `doc/places/blasted_lands.md` §"At Commons House").

**Session:** Mid-campaign, once the party has addressed the cerberus.

**The situation:** The cerberus has stood aside. The hall is open. The party steps inside. They have perhaps an hour before dusk and the cerberus's shift-change to work with. The hall holds: the long tables (empty), the cooking-stones (cold), the missing wall-panels (taken), the upper-floor shrine (Willow would want news of), and hidden details only careful reading reveals.

**Tasks in shared space** (the hall and its upper floor — one tile):

| Task | What it prevents | Who performs it | How |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Read the emptying pattern** | Missing the clue about who took the panels | A *Perc* or *Lookout* character | Examining the removal-marks, the cleanness |
| **Tend the upper-floor shrine** | Offending the lesser spirit | A priest or *Relaxed* character | Adding an offering, lighting a candle |
| **Map the layout for return** | Future visits navigate blind | A *Labyrinth* or *Travels* character | Sketching, noting passages |
| **Listen to the cerberus** | Missing what it will say now that the door is open | A *Shamanic* character at the door | Addressing it in its register |
| **Watch the light** | Being caught in the hall past the shift-change | Any character | A simple watch on the sky and the doorway |

**Round structure:** 3 rounds of an hour each. R1 enter and disperse. R2 work the tasks. R3 reassemble at the door before the cerberus's shift-change.

**Outcome framework:** Full cooperation → all clues gathered, shrine tended, return-path noted → the migration-pattern of Commons-House's missing panels becomes legible; scattered-kin arc deepens.

**Priority: Tier 1.** Blasted-lands setpieces are load-bearing; the commons house visit benefits most from an explicit coordination framing.

### Repair

**Setpiece: The buried tower's furnace — restoration against the fungus**

**Where:** Inside the buried tower at the Lake of Tears (see `doc/claude/CAMPAIGN.md` §"Session 3 — The Buried Tower" and `doc/npcs/pot_person.md`).

**Session:** Session 3 or the return visit.

**The situation:** The party has reached the pot-boy and the furnace. They have earned enough trust to attempt the furnace's restoration. The fungus is closing in from outside. The furnace is low; the exhaust is weak; the mage-era tools are mostly unfamiliar. A full restoration requires multiple hands working in parallel.

**Tasks in shared space** (the furnace chamber — one tile):

| Task | What it prevents | Who performs it | How |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Diagnose the furnace-being** | Making a repair that injures the pot-person | A *Shamanic* character communicating with the furnace directly | Addressing it, asking where it is failing |
| **Fuel and feed** | The furnace dying during the repair | A Hand or *Portage* character | Moving fuel, supporting the firebed |
| **Replace degraded mechanisms** | The repair not holding | *Ingenuity* or Machinist specialization | Fabricating replacement parts on the spot |
| **Direct the exhaust** | The fungus closing on the tower while the work is slow | *Hand* **improvise tool** or *Ingenuity* | Redirecting the exhaust toward the densest growth |
| **Translate for the boy** | The pot-boy's contributions being lost | A character paying attention to his drawings | Reading the boy's chalk-and-ash guidance as it appears |

**Round structure:** 4 rounds, 20 minutes each (small scale — this is urgent). Pot-boy contributes a drawing per round that reveals a specific next step.

**Outcome framework:** Full restoration → the furnace regains strength; the exhaust drives the fungus back for a season. Partial → the furnace holds but weakly; the party has to return. Failed → the furnace dies; the fungus closes; the pot-people are endangered.

**Priority: Tier 1.** Session 3 scenario. Repair is the campaign's weakest scenario-type; this setpiece shows what a proper cooperative repair looks like.

**Alternate repair setpiece (Tier 2): The Ironwood Mill's cutting machine.** See `doc/places/ironwood_mill.md`. If the party chooses to restore the mill to working order — with Haven's interest as the incentive — the same cooperation pattern applies with different task-mix.

### Cure

**Setpiece: The Brumal plague outbreak at Hank's farm**

**Where:** Hank's farm (see `doc/places/hanks_farm.md`), mid-late campaign when Brumal refugees arrive carrying illness.

**Session:** Late mid-campaign, 10–14. The Brumal collapse has accelerated; refugees reach Elmsfield faster than Hank can absorb; one arrives carrying what will become an outbreak.

**The situation:** Hank's farm has begun to fill with sick children. Diederik diagnoses it as a communicable fever — not the fungus, a different threat, faster-moving. The farm is an hour from Elmsfield town centre. Local healers are already stretched. The party must stabilise the outbreak and produce a working cure.

**Tasks in shared space** (the farm — one tile, potentially with one member in the adjacent tile for gathering):

| Task | What it prevents | Who performs it | How |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Triage and stabilise** | Critical patients dying during the search | Healer specialization or *Blood* | At the farm's infirmary |
| **Isolate and contain** | Outbreak spreading to Elmsfield | *Politics* or *Relaxed* | Establishing the quarantine rules, managing the refugees' fear |
| **Identify ingredients** | Compounding the wrong cure | *Druid* or *Smell* | At the farm's herb plots, the surrounding fields, or (one tile away) the hills |
| **Compound the cure** | Delivering contaminated medicine | Healer specialization or Hand **improvise tool** | At the stable, set up as workshop |
| **Manage the community** | The refugees panicking, hoarding, or fleeing into Elmsfield carrying the illness | *Relaxed*, *Musical*, Char **rallying cry** | At the farm gate |

**Round structure:** 6 rounds of an hour each, spanning a day. R1 assess. R2 stabilise. R3–4 ingredient-gather (the adjacent-tile round, if needed). R5 compound. R6 administer and outbreak resolution.

**Outcome framework:** Full → all sick recover; Elmsfield does not catch it; Hank's farm strengthens. Partial → some recover; Elmsfield sees a few cases; the party has contained it. Failed → a child dies; Hank grieves; Elmsfield goes on alert. The emotional weight is real.

**Priority: Tier 2.** Revisits the Session 1 Cure scenario at larger scale; connects the Brumal-collapse campaign thread to hands-on cooperative work.

### Market

**Setpiece: The Linar harbour triangular deal**

**Where:** Linar harbour (see `doc/places/linar_harbour.md`). An inland-to-sea cargo negotiation across three or four merchant families who do not fully trust each other.

**Session:** Mid-campaign, when the party needs resources for a larger undertaking — say, funding the ironwood-mill restoration, or provisioning for a desert crossing, or paying a Fata morgana visit.

**The situation:** The party needs a specific set of goods or a specific service only available through Linar's complicated merchant-family network. One family has the goods but not the standing; another has the standing but not the goods; a third has to vouch for both without declaring allegiance.

**Tasks in shared space** (Linar's dock-side tavern district — one tile):

| Task | What it prevents | Who performs it | How |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Read the room** | Being played by any of the families | *Perc* **called shot** or *Hearing* | Listening across tables before speaking |
| **Handle the seller** | Price-gouging or quality-substitution | A Jeweler or Hand-specialist | Direct haggle; appraisal |
| **Warm the vouch** | The vouching family withdrawing | *Clan*, *Musical*, or *Relaxed* | Shared ancestry, a song, a drink bought |
| **Block the rival's move** | A competing party arriving mid-deal | *Sly* or Char **strategy** | False tip, discreet distraction, speed |
| **Close the deal** | The paperwork failing at the harbourmaster | *Politics* or *Hand* | The final signature, the witnessed exchange |

**Round structure:** 3 rounds (three tavern-hours). R1 triangulate. R2 close the family-of-standing. R3 close the family-of-goods and paperwork.

**Outcome framework:** Full → the deal lands; the party has what it needs; Linar's standing with the party rises. Partial → part of the deal only; the rest must be found elsewhere. Failed → burnt bridges; the party is not welcome in Linar's tavern district for a season.

**Priority: Tier 3.** Market is adequate without a setpiece; the setpiece is useful when the campaign's stakes require Linar's network and the party is ready for a heavier negotiation.

## Shackle towers — coordination coverage

**Design goal: every shackle-tower visit should give every party member something to do.**

The six towers around the Lake of Tears (the buried tower, volcano tower, sinking tower, darkness tower, Lightning Tower, and Gerhald's hill tower) each break by a distinct lever. The existing place files describe each lever correctly — but most of those levers currently resolve through one or two specialists (*"the brave clinger"*, *"the mosaic-reader"*, *"the fuel-carrier"*). This under-uses the party and cheapens the tower's register.

**The fix at planning level:** each tower gets a **task palette of 5–8 tasks spanning different powers/stats**, so that a typical Moros party (4–6 characters — see `doc/claude/CAMPAIGN.md`) has a role for every member at every tower. The core lever is preserved; what changes is the surrounding coordination demand. Below is the task palette per tower, as design input for when the full setpieces are written.

All task-sets sit in shared space (the tower itself and its immediate surroundings — one tile, at most one task at adjacent-tile reach per RULES.md §"Party scope and cooperative specialization").

### Buried Tower (pot-people) — Tier 1

Three scene-clusters across the Session 3 visit; each with its own palette.

**Chimney descent (entry):**

| Task | Power/Stat | What it prevents |
|---|---|---|
| Read the exhaust timing | *Shamanic* or *Ingenuity* | Descending into an active blast |
| Time the descent | Speed **sprint** | Catching a blast mid-descent |
| Cover the descender from spores | *Fur* or *Hide* | Fungal exposure on the way in |
| Anchor above with rope | Might + Hand | The descender falling |
| Signal across the noise | *Hearing* | Coordination failure during the timing window |

**First approach to pot-people (inside):**

| Task | Power/Stat | What it prevents |
|---|---|---|
| Recognise the pot-boy's disguise | Perc — the tool beside the still pot | Stepping on him or picking him up |
| Address the furnace | *Shamanic* | Treating the being as a machine |
| Hold respectful register | *Relaxed* | Rushing the boy's trust |
| Offer drawing medium (chalk, ash) | Hand | No communication channel open |
| Translate the boy's drawings | Perc or *Druid* | Missing what he tells the party |

**Furnace restoration (repair):**

| Task | Power/Stat | What it prevents |
|---|---|---|
| Diagnose with the furnace directly | *Shamanic* | Injuring the pot-person-being |
| Provide fuel | Hand + *Portage* | Furnace dying mid-repair |
| Fabricate replacement parts | *Ingenuity* or Machinist | Repair not holding |
| Direct exhaust against fungus | *Ingenuity* + Hand **improvise tool** | Fungus closing on the tower |
| Translate the boy's real-time guidance | Perc | Losing the boy's contribution |

Total distinct specialists engaged across the visit: 7+ (Shamanic, Druid, Perc, Ingenuity, Hand, Might, Speed, Fur/Hide, Relaxed, Hearing). Every typical party member has a role.

### Volcano Tower — Tier 2

Relational lever: feed and befriend the fire elemental.

| Task | Power/Stat | What it prevents |
|---|---|---|
| Observe the rim-cycle from distance | Perc or *Lookout* | Approaching during a fire-throw window |
| Carry fuel to the rim | Might + *Portage* | Insufficient offering |
| Address the elemental in heat-patterns | *Shamanic* | Being read as a disposal chute rather than a being |
| Watch the ward-stones at the base | *Mage* or *Ingenuity* | Containment-degradation that the elemental would read as provocation |
| Tend the old offering-stones | *Relaxed* or *Religion* | Missing the respect-register prior visitors established |
| Shield an advancing party-member | *Shield* or *Fur* | Direct fire damage during approach |
| Read the elemental's mood-shifts in the heat | Perc | Missing when trust has been earned |

**Distinct specialists engaged: 7.** Every party member has a role. The elemental's consent — its name, its agreement to lift the tower — is the collective achievement.

### Sinking Tower — Tier 2

The descent and the owner-spirit's fetch.

| Task | Power/Stat | What it prevents |
|---|---|---|
| Gerhald-vantage observation | Perc or *Lookout* (requires an earlier visit to Gerhald) | Missing the cling-to-descend hint |
| Row the boat on the lake's unsafe water | *Swimmer* or Hand | Boat trouble on approach |
| Approach without weight-trigger until ready | *Stealth* powers (*Adaptation*, *Scurry*) | Premature sinking with nobody clinging |
| The cling-and-descent itself | Dex + Might + Endu | Letting go or drowning during the sink |
| Stay topside as anchor | Hand (line to boat) + *Hearing* | Losing contact with the descender |
| Speak with the owner-spirit | *Shamanic* or *Politics* | Misreading his fetch-quest |
| Bring the fetched item (arrives with it) | Any — whoever went to find it | No offering for his release |
| Manage air and pressure during descent | *Ingenuity* or Endu **dig in** | Cold-water trauma, disorientation |

**Distinct specialists engaged: 8.** Every party member has a role. The brave clinger is one among many — not the solo hero the earlier draft implied.

### Darkness Tower — Tier 2

Two-step puzzle: lift the basket, then read the revealed mosaic.

| Task | Power/Stat | What it prevents |
|---|---|---|
| Read the threshold effect before crossing | *Shamanic* or *Mage* | Crossing blind into the life-drain |
| Navigate the blind interior by touch | *Labyrinth* or Perc | Getting lost in the multi-stair layout |
| Find the basket | Perc + *Smell* + *Shamanic* sensing | Spending Endu wandering |
| Lift the basket (heavy; possibly 2-person) | Might + Hand | Suppression continues |
| Speak with the released light elemental | *Shamanic* or *Relaxed* | Missing the ally the release creates |
| Read the mosaic pattern | Perc + *Ingenuity* or *Mage* | Step 2 never completes |
| Walk the mosaic-instructed path | *Druid* or *Travels* (pattern-walking) | The tower not moving |

### Tower-coordination design notes

- **Every tower supports at least 5–8 distinct specialists.** A typical 4-member party has a task for each; a 6-member party has overlap that lets members support each other.
- **Preserve each tower's core lever.** The lever mechanism (feeding the elemental, cling-to-descend, lift-and-read-mosaic, return the Storm Mage, consent) does not change. The coordination palette is what *surrounds* the lever.
- **The buried tower is Tier 1**; it comes early in the campaign and sets expectations. The other five are Tier 2 and can be written as the campaign approaches each.
- **Cross-tower connections matter.** Breaking one shackle establishes tokens, witnesses, and consequences that other towers will reference. Gerhald specifically wants to hear what happened at the others; a party that has done the volcano violently or has lost the sinking tower's owner-spirit is answering a harder version of Gerhald's question. The roadmap's Gerhald task table reflects this — the *party collectively* presents what earlier towers mean.
- **Tier 1 (buried tower) should be written first as the worked example.** When the roadmap's setpieces are implemented, the buried tower's three-scene palette becomes the reference — future tower palettes follow its shape.

## Dungeons as tension-peaks — the universal template

**The shackle-tower palette is the template for every dungeon in the campaign.** Every named dungeon should have the same structural shape — 5–8 tasks spanning different powers, in shared space, preserving the dungeon's core mechanic. **Dungeons are the campaign's tension-rising focus points**; the rest of the campaign runs at lower tension.

### Why dungeons peak the tension

The Moros tension mechanic rises with every aid-action (see RULES.md §"Core Mechanics" and SCENARIOS.md). A coordination-rich dungeon *demands* aid — multiple specialists acting simultaneously, helping each other, sharing information, covering one another. **The aid-density is the tension-density.** Dungeons are where the system's pressure is allowed to peak, because the cooperative work the dungeon demands is what raises the tension.

Outside dungeons, tension stays moderate — conversations, travel, camp in safe country, market visits. The party does not need to work at peak-coordination; the tension mechanic accumulates slowly. **Inside a dungeon, tension rises hard**, because the coordination demand is sustained across multiple scenes. A party enters a dungeon at low tension; by the second scene they are coordinating heavily; by the climax scene they are at maximum tension and any mistake costs. Exiting the dungeon releases the tension.

This rhythm — tension-low → tension-peak → tension-release — is what makes dungeons *feel* different from the rest of the campaign. A DM who applies the coordination-palette template consistently across all dungeons gets the rhythm automatically.

### Design principle

**Every dungeon in the campaign follows this template:**

1. **5–8 tasks per scene-cluster.** Each dungeon may have 1–3 scene-clusters (the buried tower has three: chimney / pot-people / furnace). Each cluster gets a palette.
2. **Tasks span different powers and stats.** No single specialist should cover more than 1–2 tasks in any cluster. A Hunter handles tracking; a Shamanic handles spirit-work; an Ingenuity handles mechanism; a Relaxed holds the register.
3. **All tasks in shared space** per RULES.md §"Party scope and cooperative specialization" — one tile, at most one adjacent-tile task.
4. **Core mechanic preserved.** Whatever the dungeon's distinctive lever (cling-to-descend; lift-the-basket; address-the-cerberus; retrieve-the-document), the palette surrounds it; it does not replace it.
5. **Neglect has consequences.** An untended task compounds across rounds — tension rises, Endu drains, a watcher misses something, a trap engages, a creature escalates.
6. **Tension-peak at the climax scene.** The dungeon's last scene-cluster is designed so the tension mechanic is at maximum. The party should feel the pressure physically at the table.
7. **Exit releases tension.** Leaving the dungeon — or completing it — should visibly lower the register. The DM narrates the exit-breath as a specific moment.

### Worked palette at a glance — what the template produces

When every dungeon follows this template, the campaign has the following structural property:

- **Each dungeon is a tension-peak.** The party enters at moderate tension, rises across rounds, peaks at the climax scene, falls on exit.
- **Every dungeon rewards the full party.** Nobody sits out. No single specialist carries the scene.
- **Dungeons feel distinct.** The core mechanic is preserved per dungeon (cling-to-descend differs from lift-the-basket differs from address-the-cerberus), so each feels like its own place. The *shape* is the same; the *content* varies.
- **The campaign has a rhythm.** Sessions alternate between low-tension stretches (travel, inns, negotiation) and dungeon-peaks (coordination-rich focus scenes). The rhythm is felt without being named.

This is what the shackle-tower palette gives when generalised.

## Non-dungeon tension-peaks — coordination setpieces at familiar places

Dungeons are one kind of tension-peak. **Not the only kind.** Familiar places — towns, farms, Inns, roads, shrines — can also host tension-peak scenes when a crisis event lands on them. A plague at Hank's farm, a night-ride to Corven's secret meeting, a dispute at the Harbourmaster's Office during a salmon-run, a Brumal-border scene in winter: each of these is a full tension-peak without being a dungeon.

**The difference:**

- A **dungeon** is a place the party enters *because the place is the scene*. Its peaks are continuous across the visit; its register is *inside*.
- A **non-dungeon peak** is an event at a familiar place the party already knows. The peak is the *crisis*, not the location. Before and after the crisis, the place is ordinary.

Both apply the same coordination-palette template (5–8 tasks, shared space, different powers). What differs is the *frame*: the dungeon runs at peak for hours of play; the crisis-at-a-familiar-place runs at peak for one scene and then releases.

**Why this matters for the campaign's rhythm:**

- The campaign's tension-peaks cannot all be dungeons — the party would spend every session at peak.
- Crisis-at-familiar-places distributes tension-peaks across lower-pressure content: a market visit, an evening at an Inn, a day at a farm, a travel leg — each of which *can* turn into a peak under specific conditions.
- The familiarity of the place makes the crisis hit harder. Bellrose sleeping on the wood-rack at Bridget's Inn is ordinary; Bellrose missing the morning after a disturbance in the upstairs hallway is a moment of specific dread.

### Design principle

- **Peak on event, not on location.** The place is known; the event is the tension-raiser.
- **Task palette still required** — 5–8 tasks, different powers, shared space.
- **Release visible.** When the crisis resolves, the return to ordinary at the familiar place is itself a scene. The party sees Bellrose back on the wood-rack; the Inn's noise returns; the ordinary is restored (or visibly not — some crises leave the place changed).
- **Familiar-place peaks connect to threads.** A plague at Hank's farm is the Brumal-collapse thread made concrete. A dispute at Salmonswell's Harbourmaster's Office is the fishing-rights political tension under pressure. Each peak is a campaign-thread stress-point.

## Writing template

Each setpiece follows the SURVIVAL.md / Irna-entrance pattern:

### Header
- **Scenario type** (Combat, Stealth, Negotiation, Exploration, Repair, Cure, Travel, Market)
- **Where** — place reference
- **Session** — approximate campaign position
- **Priority tier**

### Setup
- What the party sees
- Who is present
- The stakes
- Time pressure

### Task table
- Task name | What it prevents | Who performs it | How
- 4–6 tasks, all in shared space (one tile; at most one at adjacent-tile reach per RULES.md)
- Tasks cover different powers/stats — no single specialist can do them all

### Round-by-round structure
- 3–6 rounds typically
- Each round has its own pacing beat
- Tension rises; tasks compound if neglected

### Outcome framework
- Full cooperation → specific outcome
- Partial → specific outcome
- Failed → specific outcome
- Connect to campaign consequences

### What cooperation looks like at the table
- How the DM should run it
- How to demand task-assignment explicitly
- How to handle task-neglect

### DM notes
- Anything specific the DM needs to know
- Cross-references to place/NPC files

## Design principles

A few rules that should hold across all setpieces:

- **Tasks in shared space.** Per RULES.md §"Party scope and cooperative specialization". No geographic split beyond adjacent-tile at hour-scale.
- **Different powers to different tasks.** If any two tasks reward the same power, revise — a single specialist should never be able to cover them both.
- **Time pressure, not just urgency.** Each setpiece has a round-count that creates a real clock. Neglected tasks worsen the party's position across rounds.
- **Compassion-first compatible.** Every setpiece has a non-combat path (or at minimum a minimise-violence path). The cooperation is in the *shared work*, not the shared violence.
- **Emotional register held across the scene.** Every setpiece has an emotional spine — the mother and her cub; Laurent's unresolved grief; Hank's farm at risk. Not just a mechanical puzzle.
- **Specific NPC and place ties.** Every setpiece is anchored to named NPCs and named places, already in the campaign docs.
- **Outcome frameworks, not success/failure binaries.** Every setpiece has full / partial / failed outcomes, with specific consequences. This preserves the register that the campaign rewards trying.

## Open questions

- **Pacing density.** Is 1–2 coordination setpieces per session the right target, or should it be higher/lower? The Session 1 density (one setpiece) feels right for the opening; long-haul sessions may need two.
- **Can the tension mechanic handle sustained cooperation?** Moros's tension rises with every aid-action. A coordination setpiece with 5 concurrent tasks generates a lot of aid. The tension mechanic should accommodate — verify at the table.
- **Should SITUATIONS.md and these setpieces merge?** SITUATIONS.md already has scene-seeds. These setpieces are larger. Current call: keep them separate — SITUATIONS.md is creature-centred, this roadmap is scenario-type centred.
- **Where do these setpieces live after they're written?** Probably in a new `doc/claude/SETPIECES.md` or folded into the existing session notes in CAMPAIGN.md. The roadmap suggests the latter — write each setpiece into CAMPAIGN.md's session section, so DMs preparing a session find them in context.
- **Do we need combat-specific coordination mechanics beyond the tension track?** Most existing combat mechanics reward one strong character. A coordinated combat setpiece may strain the system. Test at the table.

## Closing — what the roadmap delivers

If every Tier 1 setpiece is written and folded into Sessions 1–3, and Tier 2 setpieces are written before the mid-campaign sessions reach them, **the campaign will have a coordination-requiring scenario every 2–3 sessions on average, covering all 10 scenario types across its length.**

The Session 1 Irna-entrance is the proof-of-concept. The SURVIVAL.md setpieces are the first extension. This roadmap is the plan for completing that extension across the whole campaign.

**The register the roadmap preserves:** compassion under pressure, specialized work in shared space, resolution through distribution rather than through a hero. Moros's cooperative register made explicit as a design principle, then realised scene by scene.
