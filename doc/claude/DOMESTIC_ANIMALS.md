# Domestic Animals

Named animals that live with the campaign's settled NPCs and places. **Not encounters. Not combat.** Presence — at the Inn's hearth, at the farm's fence-line, at the dock, at the smithy. The compassion-first register of Moros lives here more than anywhere else: how a party treats *somebody's cat* says as much about who they are as any combat choice.

This file profiles a handful of specific animals by place and NPC. Use them as **scene-colour with character** — the dog who recognises the party after three visits, the cat who takes the worst seat in the Inn for herself, the oxen who recognise Wilfred's whistle.

## How to run domestic animals at the table

- **They have names. Learn them; say them.** *"Bellrose is asleep in the wood-rack."* This is different from *"there is a cat."* The party learns the name if they care enough to ask, and they should.
- **They have patterns.** Each animal has a *ritual* they repeat. A party that has returned to a place across sessions starts to *count on* those rituals — and notices when one is missing.
- **They read the party.** A dog who trusts the party by the third visit is *acknowledging return*. A cat that allows a scratch from one specific party member is *choosing*. These readings run one direction (animal to party); the party does not get to decide they have earned it.
- **They remember.** An animal treated well on the second visit extends more goodwill on the fifth. An animal mistreated — even mildly — does not forget; a dog will stand at a different distance permanently.
- **They are not set-dressing.** When something happens to a domestic animal — injury, death, loss — the scene has weight. A DM who lets a bandit kill the farm dog in passing has *permission to kill the farm dog*, and should run the scene as the specific grief it is.
- **They are not magical problem-solvers.** The dog does not track the villain by miraculous scent. The cat does not lead the party to the hidden panel. They are *animals*. Their usefulness is in *being specific beings who know a place*.

## The roll-call

### Bellrose — Bridget's Inn cat

**Where:** Bridget's Inn, Elmsfield. Sleeps variously: top of the wood-rack, on the bar under the last tankards, in the south alcove when the bread-dough is proving.

**What she is:** Grey tabby, older than she looks, half-deaf in the right ear. A specific crook at the end of her tail from an ancient argument with a closing door.

**Patterns:**
- Comes down to the common room at the hour the fire is built. Accepts a scratch from Bridget and not from the cook. Returns to her chosen spot.
- Kills a mouse every two or three nights; presents it at the bar for Bridget to see, does not expect thanks.
- Takes the *best* seat by the fire whenever it is empty; vacates only if Bridget pats the cushion. A guest who tries to displace her does not win; she simply stays and looks past them.
- In the upstairs corridor at dawn, she walks the length and back. Anyone up that early shares it with her.
- Does not accept scratches from strangers on the first visit. On the second, she allows a specific party member — *only one*. On the third, she climbs into that person's lap for a scene.

**How the party meets her:** On entry to the Inn. A party that pays attention to Bridget's quiet glance at the wood-rack learns Bellrose's name in minutes.

**What a party can do:**
- Learn her preferences over visits. Bellrose rewards patience.
- Notice when she is not in her spot — Bridget will say why. ("She's been with the kitchen cat across the lane lately.")
- Carry a sparrow or a fish-head from the wharf; present it. Bellrose accepts, tears it into two, eats both pieces calmly.
- Do not pick her up. Bellrose does not like being picked up. She remembers who tried.

**Weight:** Bellrose is the Inn's domestic heart in a way Bridget's voice is not. A party that is *read* by Bellrose is read by the Inn. If Bellrose ever accepts a particular stranger on the first visit, Bridget will take note and serve them better.

### Shepherd — Hank's farm dog

**Where:** Hank's farm, the orphanage-farm outside Elmsfield. Works the fence-line, sleeps in the byre, accompanies the children to the fields in the morning.

**What he is:** A mixed-breed farm dog, medium-sized, rough-coated, brown and white. One ear permanently forward, one half-back. Moves purposefully, not quickly.

**Patterns:**
- Morning: rounds the fence-line while Hank and Egbert are at breakfast. Finds breaches or new tracks; comes back to Hank when the rounds are done.
- Day: with the children. If the orphans are in the lower field, he is at the lower field.
- Evening: at Hank's feet when the farm sits to dinner. Accepts one strip of meat; does not beg more.
- Night: at the byre door. Alert sleeper.

**Names he knows:** Every orphan by their face and their walk. Hank's step; Egbert's step. He will bark at a known voice from the road before the person reaches the gate.

**How the party meets him:** On first approach, he is at the fence-line, watching. He does not bark unprompted but he *stands*. A party that approaches politely — walking, hands visible, speaking to Hank — is registered. A party that rushes or shouts is marked.

**What a party can do:**
- Speak to him. He listens and moves his ears in response. He does not reply, but he will look at a character who is speaking respectfully for as long as they speak.
- Bring him a bone from Bridget's kitchen. He accepts; chews it once; takes it to a specific spot by the byre; buries it.
- Play with the orphans. He watches; this raises his trust in the party.
- Follow his rounds on a morning. He does not mind company if the company is *quiet*.

**Weight:** Shepherd is Hank's second pair of eyes and the orphans' companion. An injury to Shepherd is a scene. His death is an event. Hank would grieve quietly and the orphans would grieve loudly, and Egbert — who is closest to the dog without showing it — would take a week to recover.

### Ox and Axe — Elmsfield's smithy oxen

**Where:** Wilfred's smithy, Elmsfield. Stabled in the small shed behind the forge; yard-work; occasional draught work for the town.

**What they are:** A pair of old working oxen, matched in age, one dun-coloured and one nearly black. Long-horned. Slow-eyed. Wilfred has worked them for twelve years and intends to keep them until they retire to pasture.

**Patterns:**
- Morning: Wilfred whistles a specific three-note call at the shed door. They come out in sequence — Ox first (the dun), Axe second (the black), never the other way.
- Work: hauling the forge's heavier stock, turning the grindstone when Wilfred needs a long cycle, carrying a load to a farm on request. They move at one pace; the pace is theirs.
- Evening: return to the shed at dusk without being called. Wilfred waters them and closes the door.
- Rest day (once a week): both in the yard, chewing, watching the town move around them.

**How the party meets them:** On approaching the smithy. If the oxen are working, they are a background presence; if resting, they are *foreground* — two large animals observing everything the yard brings.

**What a party can do:**
- Let them smell the hand offered. They do, slowly. This is the Elmsfield greeting Wilfred will silently note.
- Help with the grindstone on a long cycle. The oxen do the work; the party keeps them oriented.
- Bring them the specific grass they favour (a grass that grows near Diederik's treehouse — Wilfred occasionally gathers some for them).
- Listen to Wilfred's whistle. It is a working signal; mimicking it is *not funny* and the oxen will not come for strangers.

**Weight:** The Pair is part of Elmsfield's infrastructure. A loss would be felt across the town. Wilfred would not easily train replacements.

### Eyes — Diederik's visiting owl

**Where:** Diederik's treehouse, Elmsfield. *Not his pet* — a wild tawny owl who visits the treehouse most nights, perches on the eave, sometimes comes inside when the shutters are open.

**What she is:** A tawny owl, adult. Large pale-ringed eyes. Specifically *specific* — Diederik can distinguish her from other owls by a small notch in her left ear-tuft. He calls her *Eyes* because it is what he sees of her at dusk.

**Patterns:**
- Arrives around dusk. Perches on the treehouse eave for as long as Diederik is at his outdoor stool.
- Sometimes enters through an open shutter and sits on the ridge-beam for a while.
- Does not accept food from Diederik — hunts her own — but will take a mouse carcass left on the windowsill if the alternative is flying further.
- Leaves before dawn.

**How the party meets her:** If they are at Diederik's treehouse in the evening. She is a visible presence on the eave. A character who looks up for her will see her; a character who doesn't will miss her.

**What a party can do:**
- Not approach the eave. She will leave if the treehouse becomes too busy.
- Sit with Diederik at his stool and speak quietly. She tolerates this.
- Listen to what Diederik says about her — she is in his stories (see `doc/npcs/elder_diederik.md`). This is one of his quieter pleasures.
- Leave her a mouse if they have one. She may accept. More often she hunts her own.

**Weight:** Eyes is a sign of Diederik's standing with the wild world. A party that sees her is seeing a small thing most visitors miss. She is not his; she is *with* him in the way the wild world is.

### Roe — the Salmonswell dock-dog

**Where:** Salmonswell, the wharf. Belongs to one of the old salmon-fishing families — the **Lillocks** — but patrols the wharf as if the whole stretch is hers.

**What she is:** A lean mid-sized dock-dog, piebald, bright-eyed, a specific white crescent at her throat. Not old; not young. Hips show the season's work.

**Patterns:**
- Morning: at the Lillocks' door when the family leaves for the boats. Follows old Uncle Geb to the wharf. Sees him off.
- Day: rounds the wharf. Stops at Smokehouse Row's back doors for scraps (not always — only if the families are in a giving mood). Back at the Lillocks' dock by noon. Rests under the pier in summer, in the net-house in winter.
- Evening: back at the family's door when the boats return. Eats with the family's dinner. Sleeps near old Geb's chair.
- Children: knows every child of the fishing families, and a dozen Brumal-refugee children by face now. Tolerates being followed by any of them; kids know they can find her at the wharf.

**How the party meets her:** Arriving at Salmonswell's wharf. She is one of the first specific inhabitants they meet. She does not bark at strangers; she *assesses*.

**What a party can do:**
- Ask a wharf-hand about her. Any will tell the party her name and that she belongs to the Lillocks. Asking about Roe is a trader's polite move and is *noted*.
- Give her a fish-head. She accepts. She has been given many; she knows the register.
- Follow her rounds on a slow day. She notices; she does not object.
- Help the Lillocks with a specific small thing. Roe returns the favour by *choosing* the party's company on later visits.

**Weight:** Roe is the wharf's shape, in a way the harbourmaster's clerks are not. A stranger welcomed by Roe is a stranger Salmonswell will be warmer toward. Her loss — she is middle-aged; there will be a later dog — would mark a year of wharf-emptiness.

### Grey-muzzle — Irna's dog

**Where:** Wherever Irna is. See `doc/npcs/irna_bean.md` for full treatment — the grey-muzzled dog is a recurring physical motif in her file.

**What he is:** A large grey-muzzled dog, old, steady, fierce when he needs to be. Guided Irna to Elmsfield when she was blinded by the fungus. Has been with her since.

**Patterns:**
- Beside Irna when she is still. At her feet when she is at a table.
- Walks the edge of any new place before settling — a *perimeter check*.
- Accepts greetings from strangers only after Irna acknowledges them. A stranger who reaches for him without Irna's signal is warned with a low chest-sound.
- Knows Diederik, Rhianna, Father Elliot, Willow specifically — trusts them on first meeting.

**How the party meets him:** At the Elmsfield gate during Session 1, where he is the obstacle to reaching Irna. He has already driven off two locals before the party arrives (see `doc/claude/CAMPAIGN.md` §"The Arrival").

**What a party can do:**
- Earn his trust by being good to Irna. The *Druid* power or patient *Relaxed* engagement works on him directly. See Session 1 notes.
- Let him set the distance at which a new party-member operates around Irna.
- Travel with him once Irna joins the party. He is a de facto companion on Irna's companion arc.
- Lose Irna's trust and lose his as well — the two are coupled.

**Weight:** Grey-muzzle is Irna's physical self-extension. A loss to him would be a scene for Irna on the scale of the mine. The party should not put him in danger casually.

## Beyond the named six — the broader pattern

Every Moros settlement has its specific domestic animals. A DM can place new ones when a location's scene calls for them:

- **Linar harbour:** an old gull that lands on a specific pier-post every morning; a smokehouse cat with three legs; a caravan-master's mule named *Breath*.
- **Fata morgana:** a small desert lizard who lives in one of the well-copings and emerges at cool hours; a **well-priest's** specific mouse (unusual — the priests permit mice at the wells, which marks a mouse who sleeps at a specific well-coping as *known*).
- **Scarlet Vale:** a palace hound that accompanies the king's walks; a merchant's parrot at the grain exchange who calls prices in the old market-tongue; a specific pigeon at Rhianna's residence who carries her small letters.
- **Luchebert:** a monastery cat who has never been given a name (the order does not name them; Willow has called this one *the kitchen one* for decades); a donkey that carries the herb-baskets; the specific hive of bees at the garden's edge.

**Pattern for writing a new one:**
- Place and NPC tie
- Species, distinctive visual, specific physical trait
- Two or three patterns they repeat
- How they read the party
- Weight of loss

Short. Specific. Named. Load-bearing in the small way that compounds across sessions.

## Closing

Domestic animals are Moros's quiet register. They are what makes a farm feel like a farm; what makes an Inn feel inhabited; what makes a wharf feel like a place where specific people have lived for generations. A DM who populates the campaign with Bellroses and Roes and the Pair creates a world the party returns to with warmth. A DM who treats them as set-dressing loses one of the compassion-first principle's most effective tools.
