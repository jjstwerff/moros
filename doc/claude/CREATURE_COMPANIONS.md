# Creature Companions

A structural guide for running creatures that **travel with the party** across sessions. Different from NPC companions (see `doc/npcs/irna_bean.md` §"On the road with her") in three ways:

1. They communicate in their own register, not in ordinary language.
2. They often cannot enter towns, cross certain thresholds, or be seen by everyone.
3. Their purposes are adjacent to the party's, not identical — a creature-companion usually has somewhere it is going.

This document covers: **what qualifies**, **how the companionship forms**, **daily life at camp and on the road**, **departure conditions**, and **how to run them at the table**.

## What qualifies as a creature-companion

A creature becomes a companion when **three conditions hold simultaneously**:

- **The party has earned the relationship.** Not bought it, not captured it, not commanded it. *Earned*, usually through trust-building over multiple scenes. The volcano tower's fire elemental, the freed light elemental, Laurent's alcove puppets post-accord — each took sustained respectful engagement.
- **The creature has its own reason to travel with the party.** It is not *their* creature. It is going somewhere, or looking for something, or attending to a purpose of its own, and the party's road happens to align for a while.
- **Departure is implicit from the start.** The creature will leave. Both sides know. When and why is the scene.

A creature the party has merely *befriended* and left in place is an ally. A creature the party has *purchased or captured* is property, which the campaign's moral register does not reward. A creature that *travels with the party on the creature's own terms* is a companion.

## How the companionship forms

Four canonical paths:

### Trust-earned

The most common. Across multiple scenes, the party has treated the creature with respect — fed it (volcano tower elemental), released it (darkness tower light elemental), listened to it (Tod is the exception here — see §"Exceptions" below), done what it asked (the sinking tower owner-spirit's fetch). At some point, the creature *chooses* to come.

The invitation is usually the creature's. A DM running this should not prompt the player to ask; let the creature's willingness surface in the scene where trust crystallises. *"I am going east. I would walk with you for a stretch."*

### Request-accepted

A creature asks the party to take it somewhere. The pot-boy wanting to reach Haven. A puppet at a gathering-site wanting to be brought home to Haven. A spirit asking to be moved from a place where it is being warped.

The party accepts or declines. Acceptance initiates the companionship; decline is also valid and does not penalise.

### Release-and-stay

After a working is undone, the creature lingers rather than leaves immediately. The freed light elemental who does not ascend right away. The bound owner-spirit at the sinking tower whose spirit *could* detach and travel with the party when the tower floats to sea. A chimera whose territory has been disturbed, who follows the party for a stretch.

This is the *quiet* form of companionship. No invitation; no request. Presence, with an implicit end.

### Promise-kept

The party has promised something and delivered. The creature has been waiting. Now, having seen the promise kept, it *joins* as both gratitude and its own continuation. A cerberus whose hall the party did not desecrate and who now discharges some of its duty as walking-with. A sphinx whose questions the party answered truthfully and who now accompanies them for a stretch to carry witness.

## Daily life — at camp and on the road

### At camp

- **Where the creature sleeps (or doesn't).** Elementals may not sleep at all; they *still*. A fire elemental takes residence in whichever brazier the party maintains for it. A light elemental goes to a lamp. The pot-boy claims a specific spot near the cooking-fire. A spirit might *not be present* at night — dispersing into the ambient — and return at dawn.
- **What they eat, or instead of eating.** A fire elemental consumes whatever the party feeds it — a branch, oil, pitch. A sphinx does not eat *food* but may accept a specific kind of offering — a coin, a carved token, a sung line. The iron elemental, if it travels, grinds what is offered and returns it as dust.
- **Whether they speak.** In camp, in the quiet hour, some companions become more talkative. Heat-patterns clarify by the evening fire. Drawings come from a pot-boy who was silent in the day. A sphinx answers a question it would not have answered earlier.
- **Watch.** Many creature-companions do not need sleep and will stand the night's watch. A DM should make this a *gift* the party notices — waking to find the companion still at its place, the watch unbroken.

### On the road

- **Pace.** An elemental or a spirit matches the party's pace without effort. A puppet matches if smaller; a larger puppet (Laurent's alcove type) is slower and the party may adjust. A sphinx walks at its own pace, which is slightly slower than the party's; they will sometimes outpace it and wait.
- **Visibility.** Some companions are visible to everyone (the pot-boy, a puppet); some only to spirit-aware characters (a dispersed spirit); some are visible but *read wrong* to locals (a chaos-adjacent creature draws attention in a Luchebert cloister). The party manages its visible companions in social settings.
- **Threshold crossings.** Felicia's shrine, the council stones at Haven, the inside of Luchebert's maze garden, any mighty-spirit-reader's personal space — a companion from a chaos-adjacent register may *not be welcome* or may choose not to cross. A DM should flag these edges in advance.
- **The cart.** Nadine's cart. Bridget's delivery-wagon. A party-owned cart. Some companions travel in them (a small pot-person; a bound spirit in a specific object); some do not (a fire elemental in a cart is a wagon-fire waiting).

### In scenes

- **Do companions enter combat?** Usually yes, at their own register. A fire elemental combats by being-itself in a heat-radius; a puppet combats by shield-style physicality; a sphinx rarely combats at all. A DM should decide per-companion what their combat mode is and stick to it.
- **Do they speak for the party?** Sometimes. A sphinx can vouch for the party with other sphinxes or with desert chimeras. A Haven emissary can vouch with other puppets. A companion's *presence* in a social scene can be the conversation's shape.
- **Do they *decide* things?** Yes. They are companions, not tools. If the party's decision conflicts with the companion's purpose, the companion may disagree, delay, or leave.

## Departure conditions — how companionships end

Companions leave. The *how* is the scene.

- **Purpose fulfilled.** The pot-boy reaches Haven. The fire elemental reaches the mountain range. The scattered-kin puppet is home. The departure is *completion*, and the scene is warm.
- **Purpose misaligned.** The party is going somewhere the companion cannot follow — into the desert's heart for a companion made of ice-water; into a monastery for a chaos-adjacent companion; into an overseas crossing for a companion bound to this continent. The companion stays behind at the nearest place they can. The scene is *parting*, and it is emotionally specific.
- **Party decision the companion cannot condone.** Killing Haven's kin; delivering the mirror to Raul; desecrating Moth Hollow. The companion leaves and does not come back. The scene is *loss*, and the party's standing with that companion's community suffers.
- **Death.** Companions can die. A fire elemental's body can be broken; a puppet can be shattered; a spirit can be disrupted. The DM should treat this weight. A creature-companion's death is not a combat casualty; it is the end of a specific relationship.
- **Nature overcome.** The chaos-pressure of a region overwhelms them (a spirit in the blasted lands' centre). A mighty-spirit-reader's presence displaces them (a chaos-adjacent companion at Felicia's shrine fades). They leave quietly, usually without telling the party why. A shaman may perceive the mechanism.
- **A better place found.** The companion discovers somewhere it wants to stay. A freed light elemental finds a temple; a sphinx finds a kin-circle; a puppet finds Haven. This is *settlement*, and parting is warm.

## How to run companions at the table

- **Pick a register and hold it.** A fire elemental communicates in heat-patterns; do not let it speak fluent common on hard scenes. A sphinx speaks in question-register; do not let it deliver flat answers. Consistency of register is what makes a companion feel real.
- **Give them their own motivation every session.** Before a session, ask: *what does the companion want today?* Usually small: a specific direction of travel, an item noticed in a shop, an instinct about a stranger, a memory surfacing at a landmark. Let it colour the session.
- **Narrate their perception of the party's choices.** Without words. The fire elemental's heat tightens. The sphinx's tail flicks. The puppet steps half a pace back. This is how the companion *acts* when they disagree without yet speaking.
- **Small turn-economy.** Do not give the companion a full action each round. Give them a *presence* — one small contribution per scene in combat, one noticed-thing per scene out of combat. They are companions, not extra party members.
- **Let them leave before the party wants them to.** A DM who holds a companion beyond their natural departure weakens both the companion and the party's relationship with the setting. The parting scene is a *gift*, not a loss.
- **Do not let them solve problems the party should solve.** A fire-elemental companion is not a combat trump card; a sphinx is not a research-shortcut. They contribute in their register; the party does the work.

## Exceptions — creatures that are *not* companion material

Not every creature can be a companion. Specific exceptions worth naming:

- **Tod** — cannot leave the forbidden woods. *"I cannot. I am the shape of the woods. The woods are here. I am here."* (See `doc/npcs/tod.md`.) He is a teacher the party returns to, not a companion they take away.
- **Felicia** — a great spirit. Not companion-scale. Reachable at channels; never a travelling presence. (See `doc/npcs/felicia.md`.)
- **Laurent** — bound to his wreck. Can be *part of the last flight* as the city rises; is not a companion in the normal sense. (See `doc/npcs/laurent.md`.)
- **The mother bear** — a wild creature in a specific situation. Not becoming a companion even if healed.
- **The sinking tower's owner-spirit** — wants to go *down-river to the sea*. Specific destination; not a travelling companion.
- **The buried tower's furnace** — follows the pot-boy, not the party. The boy is the companion; the furnace follows him.

## Worked examples — companions this campaign has seeded

- **The freed light elemental** (darkness tower) — small, pale, steady. Stays until it finds a temple or lantern it wants to settle in. Companion register: *witness and light*.
- **The volcano tower's fire elemental** (post-befriend) — travels in a brazier. Communicates in heat-patterns. Heading east to the mountain range, eventually. Companion register: *warmth and destruction held in check*.
- **The pot-boy** (if party takes him toward Haven) — silent; drawings; a small pot with legs. Heading to Haven. Companion register: *quiet work and curiosity*. See `doc/npcs/pot_person.md`.
- **A scattered-kin puppet** (if reassembled and escorted back) — newly reassembled, shaky. Heading to Haven. Companion register: *returning-home*. See `doc/places/scattered_kin.md`.
- **The hippogriff** (quiet-company variant) — walks with a party that has not asked for help. Does not become a mount. Leaves when an ask is made. Companion register: *unspeakable-promise-withheld*.
- **Laurent's alcove puppets** (post-accord; a small number only) — quiet, useful, remote-animated. Laurent's extension in the field. Companion register: *borrowed help that is not truly here*.
- **A befriended dust-dervish** (desert) — carries small items between ruins; runs a warning ahead. Leaves when a sand-storm calls it home. Companion register: *trickster that keeps its word*.

## Closing — the register the campaign rewards

A creature-companion is not a pet. Not a summon. Not a mount. **A being that has decided to walk with you for a time and has their own reasons.** The party's task is to keep the relationship the companion is lending. The DM's task is to make the departure matter.

When the last companion leaves at the end of a long arc — a reassembled puppet settling into Haven's council stones; a fire elemental sliding east into the mountain dusk; a hippogriff that walked a stretch with a tired party and is gone at dawn — the party should feel the loss specifically, and feel that the loss was *right*. That is the companion register, done well.
